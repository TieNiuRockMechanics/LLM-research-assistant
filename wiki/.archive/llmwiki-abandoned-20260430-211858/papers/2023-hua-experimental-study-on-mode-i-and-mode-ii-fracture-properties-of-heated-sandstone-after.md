# Experimental Study on Mode I and Mode II Fracture Properties of Heated Sandstone after Two Different Cooling Treatments.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates and compares the effects of rapid water-cooling and natural air-cooling on the pure mode I and mode II fracture toughness of sandstone heated to different temperatures, and evaluates the results using T-stress-modified fracture criteria [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question
How do rapid water-cooling and natural air-cooling treatments affect the pure mode I and mode II fracture properties and degradation mechanisms of sandstone heated to temperatures of 25, 100, and 300 °C? [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
- **Material:** Sandstone. Mineral composition from index includes quartz, albite, clinochlore, and gordonite [Hua 2023, pp. 6-7]. Specific geological source of the sandstone is **not confirmed from index fragments**.
- **Specimen Geometry:** Cracked straight-through Brazilian disk (CSTBD) samples with a radius R and thickness t, containing a central crack of length 2a. The specific geometry used was a crack length ratio `a/R = 0.5` [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Sample Conditions:**
    - Intact (room temperature, 25 °C).
    - Heated to 100 °C.
    - Heated to 300 °C.
- **Cooling Treatments:** Rapid water-cooling and natural air-cooling [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Measurements:** Load-displacement data recorded for every sample until ultimate failure. At least five samples were measured per group [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
- **Specimen Preparation:** Sandstone samples were heated in an oven at a rate of 3 °C/min to target temperatures (25, 100, 300 °C) and held for 48 hours [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Cooling Procedures:**
    - **Rapid water-cooling:** Heated samples were placed in water and naturally saturated for 72 hours [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
    - **Natural air-cooling:** Heated samples were first cooled to room temperature in air for 4 hours, then submerged in water for 68 hours to match the total processing time of the rapid cooling treatment. All samples were subsequently dried in air for 240 hours [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Fracture Testing Configuration:** CSTBD samples were used to achieve pure mode I (loading angle β = 0°) and pure mode II (β = 23°) loading based on `a/R = 0.5` geometry [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Mechanical Testing:** Quasi-static or static loading was applied using an electronic material test machine at a displacement rate of 0.05 mm/min [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Fracture Toughness Calculation:** Mode I and mode II stress intensity factors (SIFs), KI and KII, were calculated using analytic expressions derived via the weight function method (Eqs. 1 and 2) [Hua 2023, pp. 2-3].
- **Theoretical Evaluation:** Experimental results were evaluated against several modified fracture criteria that consider T-stress, including the Generalized Maximum Tangential Stress (GMTS) and Generalized Maximum Tangential Strain (GMTSN) criteria [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1, 7-9].

## Key Findings
- **Temperature Effect on Fracture Toughness:** Both pure mode I (KIC) and mode II (KIIC) fracture toughness of sandstone decrease with increasing heating temperature after both cooling treatments [Hua 2023, pp. 6-6].
- **Cooling Treatment Effect:** Rapid water-cooling causes a more significant reduction in fracture toughness than natural air-cooling for the same heating temperature. For example, the deterioration effect on KIC is about 1.40 times, and on KIIC is about 1.38 times, that of natural air-cooling [Hua 2023, pp. 6-6].
- **Quantified Degradation:** After rapid water-cooling from 100 °C and 300 °C, the pure mode I fracture toughness was reduced by 23.9% and 32.1%, and pure mode II fracture toughness was reduced by 24.6% and 33.3%, respectively, compared to the intact sandstone at 25 °C [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1]. Specific values were an intact KIC of ~0.330 and KIIC of ~0.498 MPa·m^0.5 [Hua 2023, pp. 6-6].
- **Peak Load:** For a given temperature, the peak load of sandstone after rapid water-cooling was less than that after natural air-cooling under both pure mode I and mode II loading [Hua 2023, pp. 4-6].
- **Fracture Toughness Relationship:** An excellent linear relation of `KIIC = 1.494 * KIC` was observed for the tested sandstone [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1].
- **Theoretical Prediction Accuracy:** The strain-based Generalized Maximum Tangential Strain (GMTSN) criterion provided more accurate theoretical predictions of mixed-mode fracture behavior compared to stress-based criteria (like GMTS) [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1]. A negative T-stress in the CSTBD sample geometry decreases the predicted crack initiation angle and increases the fracture toughness ratio KIIC/KIC [Hua 2023, pp. 7-9].

## Limitations
- **Limited Temperature Range:** The study was limited to a maximum heating temperature of 300 °C, so the conclusions cannot be directly extended to higher temperature regimes [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Single Rock Type:** The experimental results and the observed KIIC/KIC linear relationship are specific to the tested sandstone and may not be generalizable to other rock types [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1].
- **Sample Size:** All tests used a single crack length ratio (a/R = 0.5), and the influence of crack length or specimen size on the observed phenomena was not systematically varied [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Fixed Cooling Protocol:** The cooling durations (e.g., 72h water saturation, 240h air drying) were fixed, so the effect of varying cooling rates or durations was not investigated [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Heating as Main Factor:** The heating rate (3 °C/min) was considered low enough that temperature was the sole main factor influencing mechanical properties during the heating phase, following Huang et al. [Hua 2023, pp. 6-7].
- **Validated Test Configuration:** The CSTBD sample geometry and loading angles (β = 0° and 23° for `a/R = 0.5`) achieve pure mode I and pure mode II loading, as established by prior work [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM):** The calculation of fracture toughness and the application of T-stress-modified fracture criteria assumes that LEFM principles are applicable to the tested sandstone, and a critical distance (r0) concept is used [Hua 2023, pp. 2-3, 7-9].
- **Quasi-static Loading:** A displacement rate of 0.05 mm/min is assumed to provide quasi-static or static loading conditions, where dynamic effects are negligible [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables:** Heating temperature (25, 100, 300 °C), cooling treatment (rapid water-cooling, natural air-cooling) [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Dependent Variables:** Pure mode I fracture toughness (KIC), pure mode II fracture toughness (KIIC), peak load (P) [Hua 2023, pp. 4-6, 6-6].
- **Key Geometry Parameters:** Crack length ratio (a/R = 0.5), loading angle (β = 0° for mode I, 23° for mode II) [Hua 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Fracture Mechanics Parameters:** Mode I and II Stress Intensity Factors (KI, KII), dimensionless SIFs (YI, YII), T-stress, dimensionless T-stress (T*), biaxiality ratio (B, BI, BII), critical distance from crack tip (r0), crack initiation angle (θ0) [Hua 2023, pp. 1-2, 7-9].
- **Material Property:** Tensile strength (σt) [Hua 2023, pp. 7-9].
- **Key Formula:** `KIIC = 1.494 * KIC` [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1].

## Reusable Claims
1.  For the tested sandstone subjected to thermal treatment up to 300 °C, rapid water-cooling induces a more severe degradation of fracture toughness (both mode I and mode II) than natural air-cooling, with the deterioration effect being approximately 1.38–1.40 times greater [Hua 2023, pp. 6-6]. This claim is valid under the specific heating/cooling protocols of the study.
2.  A strong linear correlation exists between pure mode II and pure mode I fracture toughness (KIIC = 1.494 * KIC) for this sandstone, regardless of the cooling treatment or heating temperature [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1]. The generality of this coefficient for other sandstones or rock types requires further testing.
3.  The strain-based Generalized Maximum Tangential Strain (GMTSN) criterion, which accounts for T-stress effects, provides more accurate theoretical predictions for mixed-mode fracture in sandstone compared to the traditional stress-based GMTS criterion [Hua 2023, pp. 1-1]. This conclusion is based on experimental validation with CSTBD samples where T-stress is negative.
