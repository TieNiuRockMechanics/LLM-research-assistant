# Thermal Damage of High-Temperature Sandstone Subjected to Cooling Shock and Its Effect on Capturing Acoustic Emission Signals During Fracture.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how thermal damage from water cooling shock in 800 °C sandstone weakens mechanical properties and attenuates acoustic emission (AE) signals during fracturing, proposing a preamplifier adjustment to improve monitoring accuracy. [Ge 2023, pp. 1-2][Ge 2023, pp. 12-14]

## Research Question
How does the crack development induced by water cooling shock in high-temperature sandstone affect the capture and attenuation of acoustic emission (AE) signals during rock fracture, and how can AE monitoring reliability be improved for coal-fire control engineering? [Ge 2023, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
- **Sample Type:** Sandstone from coal measure strata. [Ge 2023, pp. 2-3]
- **Sample Condition:** Two groups of sandstone: raw sandstone and high-temperature sandstone heated to 800 °C and subjected to water cooling shock. [Ge 2023, pp. 1-2]
- **Comparison with Field Conditions:** The color change observed in the experiment (gray to yellowish-brown) was consistent with that observed in the unburned and burnt areas of a coal-fire control site. [Ge 2023, pp. 3-5]

## Methods
- **Mineral Composition Analysis:** X-ray diffraction (XRD) was used to measure the mineral composition of raw and cooling shock sandstone. Rock powder was prepared by passing shattered fragments through a 300-mesh sieve and analyzed with a Bruck AXS GMBH diffractometer at an angle of 4-70°. [Ge 2023, pp. 2-3][Ge 2023, pp. 3-5]
- **Microcrack Characterization:** A scanning electron microscope (SEM) was used to record 2D characterizations of rock cracks. Samples were prepared as smooth blocks with a volume less than 0.5 cm³, with a magnification range of 2–1,000,000 times. [Ge 2023, pp. 2-3][Ge 2023, pp. 3-5]
- **Mechanical Testing and AE Monitoring:** A uniaxial compression test was performed with a displacement change rate of 0.002 mm/s. AE signals (amplitude, count, rise time, duration, energy) were monitored simultaneously using a system with sensors placed according to a layout deemed reasonable. The AE preamplifier was set to an amplification value of 40 dB. The experiment was terminated upon the appearance of visible surface cracks. [Ge 2023, pp. 2-5]
- **Fracture Mode Analysis:** The fracture model was assessed using the AE gradient parameter **RA-value** (RA = TR / Amplitude, ms·dB⁻¹), where a small RA-value represents shear fracture and a large one represents tensile fracture. [Ge 2023, pp. 9-11]
- **Signal Amplification Test:** A subsequent test on another set of cooling shock sandstone was conducted with the preamplifier amplification factor enlarged to 60 dB. [Ge 2023, pp. 12-14]

## Key Findings
- **Increased Physical Damage:** Compared to raw sandstone, cooling shock sandstone showed a crack density increase of 8.79% (from 4.04% to 12.83%), a volume increase of 2.69%, and a p-wave velocity decrease of 51.83%. [Ge 2023, pp. 1-2][Ge 2023, pp. 11-12]
- **Reduced Mechanical Strength:** The mechanical strength of cooling shock sandstone decreased substantially. Its elastic modulus was 6.17 GPa, a reduction of 50.68% compared to the raw sandstone's 12.51 GPa, and its peak stress dropped by 25.41%. [Ge 2023, pp. 5-6]
- **Shift in Failure Mode:** The destruction form changed from brittle (raw sandstone) to ductile failure. The primary fracture mode shifted from shear-dominated in raw sandstone to a combination of shear and tensile fractures in cooling shock sandstone, as evidenced by RA-value analysis and macroscopic crack patterns. [Ge 2023, pp. 5-6][Ge 2023, pp. 9-11]
- **AE Signal Attenuation:** Cracks in the cooling shock sandstone acted as barriers, weakening the captured AE signals during compression. The thermal damage variable calculated from the elastic modulus was 50.68%, and an AE attenuation coefficient (α) related to elastic modulus and crack change rate was derived. [Ge 2023, pp. 1-2][Ge 2023, pp. 11-12]
- **Improved Monitoring Feasibility:** Increasing the preamplifier amplification factor from 40 dB to 60 dB significantly improved the AE signal capture for cooling shock sandstone, with a larger number of high-value counts and energy events recorded. [Ge 2023, pp. 12-14]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Model Basis for AE Attenuation:** The AE attenuation variable was calculated using elastic modulus, based on the consistency with p-wave velocity attenuation and the shared nature of ultrasonic p-waves and AE signals as mechanical waves. [Ge 2023, pp. 11-12]
- **Fracture Mode Identification:** The model assumes a mapping relationship where small RA-values (RA = TR / Amplitude) indicate shear fractures and large RA-values indicate tensile fractures, referencing Shiotani et al. [Ge 2023, pp. 9-11]
- **Experimental Conditions:** The uniaxial compression test was performed at a fixed displacement rate of 0.002 mm/s and terminated upon visible surface cracking. The base AE monitoring used a 40 dB amplification and a specific, reasonable sensor layout. [Ge 2023, pp. 3-5]
- **Physical Property Calculations:** Equations for change rates of volume (k_v), crack rate (k_c), and p-wave velocity (k_u) were defined, and a specific expression for the thermal damage variable (D) based on elastic modulus was used. [Ge 2023, pp. 11-12]

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Claim:** In a study on sandstone, heating to 800 °C followed by water cooling shock increases crack density by 8.79% and volume by 2.69%, while decreasing p-wave velocity by 51.83%, indicating substantial thermal damage. [Ge 2023, pp. 1-2]
    - **Condition:** Sandstone is a coal measure strata rock; heating is to 800 °C followed by water cooling.
    - **Evidence:** SEM analysis and p-wave velocity measurements comparing raw and treated sandstone.
    - **Limitation:** Results are specific to this rock type and thermal treatment.
2.  **Claim:** Thermal damage from water cooling shock causes a transition in sandstone’s failure mechanism from brittle failure with dominant shear fractures to ductile failure with a combination of shear and tensile fractures. [Ge 2023, pp. 5-6][Ge 2023, pp. 9-11]
    - **Condition:** Under uniaxial compression at a 0.002 mm/s displacement rate, for sandstone heated to 800 °C and water-cooled.
    - **Evidence:** Stress-strain curve analysis, RA-value analysis of AE signals, and macroscopic crack pattern observation.
    - **Limitation:** Failure mode analysis via RA-value relies on a specific mapping relationship.
3.  **Claim:** The attenuation of AE signals during the fracturing of thermally damaged cooling shock sandstone can be quantified by a coefficient (α) derived from changes in elastic modulus and crack density. [Ge 2023, pp. 1-2][Ge 2023, pp. 11-12]
    - **Condition:** The derivation assumes that AE attenuation can be represented by the same thermal damage variable (D) calculated from the elastic modulus, which showed attenuation consistent with p-wave velocity reduction.
    - **Evidence:** Both elastic modulus and p-wave velocity decreased by approximately 50%, and AE signals were observed to be weak during experiments with cooled shock sandstone.
    - **Limitation:** The exact mathematical derivation of α is linked to the specific change rates (k_c, etc.) defined in the paper.
4.  **Claim:** For monitoring fracturing in cooling shock sandstone with high crack density, increasing the AE preamplifier’s amplification factor (e.g., from 40 dB to 60 dB) is a feasible method to capture more high-value AE events and improve monitoring accuracy. [Ge 2023, pp. 12-14]
    - **Condition:** AE monitoring during uniaxial compression of 800 °C water-cooled sandstone with a preamplifier setting of 60 dB.
    - **Evidence:** A comparison test showed a significant increase in the number of high-amplitude counts (≥3000) and energy events (≥20,000) when amplification was increased to 60 dB.
    - **Limitation:** Confirmed only for this specific rock type and treatment in a lab setting; field implementation details are not covered.

## Connections To Other Work
- The study cites that cracks in cooling shock rock are more numerous than in raw rock, which is consistent with the conclusions of Ersoy et al. and Kumari et al. regarding the lack of space between fracture surfaces on either side of cracks. [Ge 2023, pp. 11-12]
- The finding that p-wave velocity attenuation is close to elastic modulus attenuation is consistent with the view of Zhang et al. [Ge 2023, pp. 11-12]
- The study’s AE signal attenuation analysis connects to general principles established by Asamene et al. (attenuation varies with propagation direction) and Liu et al. (application of attenuation law to rock stability). [Ge 2023, pp. 2-3]
- The use of RA-value for fracture model identification is based on the finding by Shiotani et al. that RA-value maps to fracture mode. [Ge 2023, pp. 9-11]
