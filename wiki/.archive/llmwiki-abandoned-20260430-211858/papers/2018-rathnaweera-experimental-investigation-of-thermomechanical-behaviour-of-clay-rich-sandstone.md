# Experimental Investigation of Thermomechanical Behaviour of Clay-Rich Sandstone at Extreme Temperatures Followed by Cooling Treatments.

## One-line Summary

This study experimentally investigates how extreme temperatures (up to 1000 °C) and subsequent cooling methods (slow cooling vs. quenching) affect the mechanical, mineralogical, and microstructural properties of clay-rich Hawkesbury sandstone under uniaxial compression, motivated by underground coal gasification (UCG) reservoir conditions. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1]

## Research Question

How do extreme temperatures (25–1000 °C) and different cooling treatments (slow air cooling vs. rapid water quenching) influence the thermomechanical behaviour, failure mode, mineralogy, and microstructure of clay-rich sandstone? [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data

The material tested was clay-rich Hawkesbury sandstone. Cylindrical specimens of 36 mm diameter and 76 mm length were prepared using diamond coring and cutting, with end faces ground smooth. Thermocouples were installed at mid-depth. For each experimental condition, four replicates were used and average values reported. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]

## Methods

- **Heating protocol:** Samples were heated in a high-temperature oven (max. 1500 °C) at a rate of 5 °C/min to target temperatures of 200, 400, 600, 800, and 1000 °C, held for 24 hours, to minimize thermal shock. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Cooling methods:** Two cooling treatments were applied: (1) slow air cooling at laboratory temperature, and (2) rapid water quenching in a 20 l vessel at laboratory temperature. Quenched specimens were subsequently oven-dried at 60 °C for 24 hours to remove water saturation effects prior to mechanical testing. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Continuous heating testing:** A separate set of specimens was tested under uniaxial compression while continuously heated at target temperatures (200–1000 °C) inside an environmental furnace, without a prior cooling step. Heating rate was also 5 °C/min, and displacement rate was 0.1 mm/min. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- **Uniaxial Compressive Strength (UCS) testing:** Cooled specimens were tested under uniaxial compression at a constant displacement rate of 0.1 mm/min until failure. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- **Acoustic Emission (AE):** AE signals were recorded simultaneously with uniaxial loading for cooled specimens using a PCI 2-channel system (250–750 kHz band-pass filter, 500 kHz resonant frequency, 60 dB amplification). Two sensors were attached to opposite sides of the specimen. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- **X-ray Diffraction (XRD):** Powder XRD was performed on samples at room temperature, 600 °C, and 1000 °C under both cooling conditions. Quantitative mineralogy used matrix flushing and RIR-derived constants. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- **Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM):** SEM analysis was conducted on 0.3 mm thick rock slices taken after UCS testing, using 1000× magnification and two 3 nm titanium coatings. EBSD was used for phase mapping and crystallographic studies. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 5-7]
- **Digital Image Correlation (DIC):** ARAMIS® system was used (method referenced but detailed results for failure mode from DIC are mentioned). [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11]

## Key Findings

- **Strength threshold at 600 °C:** Regardless of cooling method, UCS, Young's modulus, crack initiation stress, and crack damage stress increase when pre-heating temperature rises from 25 °C to 600 °C, and then decrease with temperatures above 600 °C. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1]
- **Slow-cooled strength gain:** At 600 °C, slowly-cooled samples reached a UCS 22.76% higher than intact strength, while continuously heated samples only increased by 1.3%. Quenched samples showed strength enhancement between 200–600 °C but failed to surpass intact strength. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11]
- **Strength degradation above 600 °C:** When heating temperature increased from 600 °C to 1000 °C, strength reductions were: quenched 49.81%, slowly-cooled 26.87%, continuous heating 13.31%. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11]
- **Young’s modulus evolution:** Under quenching and continuous heating, modulus dropped to 55–62% of initial at 200 °C, recovered near initial at 600 °C, then fell to ~49–54% at 1000 °C. Slowly-cooled modulus gradually increased to 112.58% at 600 °C, then decreased to 65% at 1000 °C. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11]
- **Poisson’s ratio trend:** Under slow cooling, Poisson’s ratio decreased from 25 °C to 600 °C, then increased from 600 °C to 1000 °C. The same trend occurred for quenched samples except at 200 °C. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11]
- **Crack initiation/damage stresses:** Slow cooling: crack initiation stress 12.63→26.1 MPa from 25° to 600 °C; crack damage stress 23.06→48.41 MPa. Quenching: crack damage first dropped at 200 °C (23.06→20.15 MPa) then rose to 29.36 MPa at 600 °C. Both stresses decreased for all cooling methods between 600° and 1000 °C. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12]
- **Mineralogical transformations:** Kaolinite transformed from a hexagonal structure to a fibre-like (needle-type) structure, believed responsible for strengthening. At extreme temperatures, quartz degraded and transformed to hexagonal tridymite; calcite disappeared with dolomite precipitating; siderite showed significant degradation at 1000 °C; barite formed needle-like structures. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1, 7-8]
- **Colour change:** Irreversible change from yellow to reddish-brown above 400 °C, attributed to deoxidation of siderite forming fassaitic pyroxene [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 7-8, 11-12]

## Limitations

- **Specimen size and scale effects:** Laboratory tests on 36 × 76 mm intact specimens may not fully represent field-scale rock mass behaviour with natural fractures and heterogeneities. [未从索引片段中确认]
- **Water saturation removal:** Quenched samples were oven-dried at 60 °C prior to testing to remove saturation effects; the authors note that real field rocks are fluid-saturated, though drying may occur seasonally. Results may not directly translate to fully saturated in-situ conditions. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Single rock type:** Only Hawkesbury sandstone was tested; mineralogy-dependent effects may differ for other clay-rich sandstones. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1]
- **Uniaxial loading only:** No confining pressure was applied, while UCG reservoirs experience in-situ stresses. Previous work suggests confining pressure can suppress thermal cracking. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2]
- **Single heating rate:** Only 5 °C/min was used; different heating rates may influence thermal cracking extent. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Cooling method details:** Air cooling duration until room temperature not explicitly specified; quenching vessel water volume/temperature evolution not described in detail. [未从索引片段中确认]

## Assumptions / Conditions

- **UCG thermal scenarios simulated:** Continuous heating, slow cooling, and quenching represent three distinct thermal regions around a UCG coal seam: continuous heating zone, extreme temperature with thermal shock, and low temperature with slow cooling. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2]
- **Minimal thermal shock during heating:** 5 °C/min ramp rate assumed sufficient to avoid thermal stress fractures during heat-up. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Oven-drying removes quenching effect on strength:** Drying at 60 °C for 24 hours assumed to eliminate water saturation influence, isolating pure thermal+cooling effect. Applicability to field partially saturated conditions is conditional. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Average of 4 replicates representative:** Variability within conditions was addressed by averaging. Specific scatter or statistical significance not reported in provided fragments. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **XRD sampling at three temperatures:** Mineralogical changes were characterized at 25, 600, and 1000 °C; interpolations for intermediate temperatures rely on indirect mechanical evidence. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]

## Reusable Claims

- **Claim:** In clay-rich sandstone subjected to heating and cooling, uniaxial compressive strength, Young’s modulus, crack initiation, and crack damage stress all increase with temperature up to 600 °C and then decrease up to 1000 °C, regardless of cooling method.  
  **Conditions:** Hawkesbury sandstone, 5 °C/min heating, 24 h holding, cooling by slow air or water quenching followed by oven drying. Uniaxial compression (0.1 mm/min). [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1]  
  **Limitation:** Behaviour may differ under confining pressure or fluid-saturated conditions; only one rock type tested.

- **Claim:** Slowly-cooled (air) samples exhibit greater strength enhancement than quenched or continuously heated samples at 600 °C, with a 22.76% increase over intact strength, whereas quenched samples fail to exceed intact strength.  
  **Conditions:** Same as above, slow cooling = air cooling at laboratory temperature. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11]  
  **Limitation:** The reason for slow-cooling strengthening is attributed to kaolinite fibre-structure formation; quantification of microstructure-strength link is qualitative via SEM.

- **Claim:** Above 600 °C, quenching produces the severest strength degradation (49.81% from 600 to 1000 °C), compared to 26.87% (slow cooling) and 13.31% (continuous heating), indicating that thermal shock exacerbates high-temperature damage.  
  **Conditions:** Water quenching from target temperature, subsequent oven drying. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11]  
  **Limitation:** The interaction of quenching-induced microcracks with mineral alteration products (e.g., tridymite, mullite formation) is not separated.

- **Claim:** The hexagonal-to-fibre-like transformation of kaolinite at high temperatures followed by cooling is a plausible micromechanical mechanism for the strengthening observed up to 600 °C.  
  **Conditions:** Observed in SEM for samples heated to 600 °C under slow cooling. XRD confirms kaolinite content does not drastically change in quantity but morphology does. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1]  
  **Limitation:** Causal relationship is inferred, not directly proven by isolating kaolinite morphology effects from other concurrent mineral changes.

- **Claim:** Thermal degradation of quartz, formation of secondary porosity, siderite decomposition, calcite disappearance, and dolomite/tridymite/mullite precipitation are key mineralogical events above 600–1000 °C that collectively reduce rock strength.  
  **Conditions:** Hawkesbury sandstone with initial mineralogy (74% quartz, 12% kaolinite, etc.). Both slow cooling and quenching produce similar mineralogical end-products but different degrees of microstructure damage. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5, 5-7, 7-8]  
  **Limitation:** Quantification of the individual contribution of each mineral change to strength reduction is not provided.

- **Claim:** Irreversible colour change from yellow to reddish-brown above 400 °C can serve as a field indicator of past thermal exposure exceeding that threshold in similar sandstones.  
  **Conditions:** Deoxidation of siderite, formation of fassaitic pyroxene. Observed in both cooling methods. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12]  
  **Limitation:** Sensitivity to exact heating duration and cooling rate not tested.

## Connections To Other Work

- **Sedimentary rock strength at high temperature:** The paper notes contradictory trends in previous studies on sandstone UCS variation with continuous heating, which are summarized in a referenced figure (Fig. 1). [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2] This study attempts to resolve discrepancies by considering cooling methods and mineralogy.
- **Confining pressure effects on thermal cracking:** Siddiqi & Evans observed that high confining pressure diminishes thermal cracking effects in heated rocks, suggesting that the uniaxial results here may represent an upper-bound of thermal degradation compared to in-situ constrained conditions. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2]
- **Siderite decomposition colour change:** Chakrabarti et al. reported similar reddish-brown colour change above 400 °C in sandstone, corroborating the deoxidation interpretation. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12]
- **Quartz-to-tridymite transformation:** Kihara et al. described the formation of α-tridymite from quartz at extreme temperatures, consistent with the XRD findings here. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12]
- **Potential connections (not confirmed in fragments):** Candidate concepts that might relate include [[thermal spallation in rocks]], [[reservoir geomechanics for enhanced geothermal systems]], [[fire-induced rock mass weakening]], and [[cementation changes during diagenesis]].

## Open Questions

- **Microcrack quantification:** The fragments do not provide quantitative crack density or porosity data. The relationship between observed microstructures and measured bulk mechanical properties remains qualitative. [未从索引片段中确认]
- **Full-field strain evolution:** DIC (ARAMIS®) was used to capture failure mode, but specific strain maps, localization onset, and comparisons across cooling methods are not detailed in the provided fragments. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11]
- **Mechanisms at 200–400 °C:** The Young’s modulus of quenched and continuous samples dropped significantly at 200 °C then recovered; the underlying mechanism (e.g., microcrack opening/closure, water release) is not fully resolved. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11]
- **Applicability to other sandstones:** How sensitive are the strengthening/weakening threshold (600 °C) and mineral reactions to initial clay content and cement type? [未从索引片段中确认]
- **Heating rate and holding time influence:** Only one heating rate (5 °C/min) and hold duration (24 h) were used; the kinetic aspects of mineral transformations and their mechanical consequences need investigation. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
