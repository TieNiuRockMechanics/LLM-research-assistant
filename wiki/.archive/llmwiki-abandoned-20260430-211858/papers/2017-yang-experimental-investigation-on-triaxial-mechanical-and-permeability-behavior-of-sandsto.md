# Experimental Investigation on Triaxial Mechanical and Permeability Behavior of Sandstone after Exposure to Different High Temperature Treatments.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates the effects of high-temperature treatment (25–800 °C) on the physical, mechanical, and permeability behavior of Rizhao sandstone, identifying a critical temperature range of 400–500 °C that induces significant changes in these properties [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
How do different high-temperature treatments affect the triaxial mechanical properties and permeability behavior of sandstone, and what is the underlying microscopic mechanism, to aid in geothermal energy extraction from super-critical temperature resources? [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3]

## Study Area / Data
The sandstone material was collected from Rizhao city, China, located near the Yellow Sea in the Circum-Pacific geothermal belt. The primary surface formations in this area include the Paleoproterozoic Fenzishan group, Jingshan group, and the Cretaceous period Wangshi group, consisting mainly of dolomite marble, purple sandstone, and silty mudstone. The Rizhao sandstone is described as a hard stone with good homogeneity and high porosity [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Heating Procedure:** Specimens were heated to target temperatures (100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 700, 800 °C) at a rate of 5 °C/min, held for two hours, and then naturally cooled to room temperature (25 °C) [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3].
- **Physical Property Measurement:** Measurements of length, diameter, weight, and ultrasonic wave velocity (P- and S-wave) were conducted before and after thermal treatment [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4].
- **Triaxial Compression Test:** Conventional triaxial compression tests were performed to obtain strength and deformation parameters [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3].
- **Permeability Test:** A transient pulse technique, coupled with triaxial compression, was used to measure the permeability evolution during the complete stress-strain process. Permeability was calculated using the equations of Brace et al. (1968). This method applies a small pressure increment (Δp₀) on the upstream side and monitors pressure decay between upstream and downstream reservoirs. The method assumes that the compressive storage of the specimen is negligible compared to that of the upstream reservoir [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4].
- **Brazilian Test:** Used to determine the indirect tensile strength of the sandstone [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4].
- **Microscopic Analysis:** X-ray diffraction (XRD) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) were used to reveal the microscopic mechanism of high temperature on the tested behaviors [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
- **Critical Temperature (Tc):** The critical temperature that induces changes in the mechanical and permeability behavior of sandstone was identified as 400–500 °C. A more obvious decreasing trend in physical properties (weight, density, P-wave velocity, dynamic elastic modulus, Poisson’s ratio) is observed after Tc [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Physical Properties:** Below 400 °C, weight decreases slowly; above 400 °C, it decreases sharply (e.g., ~3% weight loss at 800 °C). Volume expansion becomes notable above 400 °C, reaching ~3% at 800 °C. Density decreases are slight below 400 °C and become obvious afterward. P-wave velocity decreases sharply from 400 °C onwards. S-wave velocity slightly increases up to 300 °C, then decreases above 300 °C, with minimal change above 400 °C. Dynamic elastic modulus increases slightly below 300 °C, then decreases sharply from 400 °C onwards [Yang 2017, pp. 4-6].
- **Mechanical Properties:**
    - **Cohesion and Friction Angle:** Increasing temperature leads to an increase of cohesion and a decrease of internal friction angle before Tc; the opposite trends are observed after Tc [Yang 2017, pp. 6-7].
    - **Elastic Modulus:** The average elastic modulus changes minimally from 25 °C to 500 °C (from 26.50 to 26.48 GPa), then decreases linearly to 21.31 GPa at 800 °C, a decrease of nearly 20% compared to room temperature [Yang 2017, pp. 7-9].
    - **Peak Strength (σₚ) and Crack Damage Threshold (σₛ₄):** At a confining pressure of 5 MPa, both σₚ and σₛ₄ increased with temperature up to 300 °C and subsequently decreased up to 800 °C. At higher confining pressures (e.g., 20, 30 MPa), σₛ₄ did not depend on temperature [Yang 2017, pp. 6-7].
- **Permeability:** The initial permeability of sandstone under certain pressure conditions was found to increase with temperature; detailed evolution during the complete stress-strain process was investigated (specific data not detailed in this fragment set) [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Microscopic Mechanism:** The changes in physical, mechanical, and permeability behaviors were revealed using XRD and SEM analysis, though specific microscopic findings are not detailed in this fragment set [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Limitations
- The study is based on experiments on a single rock type, Rizhao sandstone, limiting the generalizability of the quantitative findings to other lithologies without accounting for initial rock features like micro-cracks and microstructure [Yang 2017, pp. 2-2].
- The research uses a post-thermal-treatment testing method (samples heated and then cooled), which may not fully replicate the in-situ conditions of geothermal reservoirs where both high temperature and high pressure coexist [Yang 2017, pp. 2-2].
- Dynamic elastic modulus and Poisson's ratio could not be determined for samples treated at 800 °C because the measured P-wave and S-wave velocities were too similar, violating the condition Vp ≥ 1.41 Vs required by the calculation method [Yang 2017, pp. 5-6].
- Detailed results of permeability evolution during the complete stress-strain process and microscopic analysis (XRD, SEM) are not covered in the provided index fragments [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Permeability Calculation:** The transient pulse method, using equations from Brace et al. (1968), is valid under the assumption that the compressive storage in the rock specimen is negligible compared with the compressive storage in the upstream reservoir [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4].
- **Dynamic Parameter Calculation:** Dynamic elastic modulus (Ed) and Poisson’s ratio (μd) were derived under the condition that P-wave velocity is greater than or equal to 1.41 times the S-wave velocity (Vp ≥ 1.41 Vs) [Yang 2017, pp. 5-6].
- **Test Conditions:** Permeability tests were conducted with pure nitrogen gas as the fluid and after ensuring a single-phase flow state by balancing upstream and downstream pressures at a saturation pressure p₀ before applying a pressure increment [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variable:** Thermal treatment temperature (T), ranging from 25 °C to 800 °C [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Physical Parameters:** Weight (m), Volume (V), Density (ρ), P-wave velocity (Vp), S-wave velocity (Vs), Dynamic elastic modulus (Ed), Dynamic Poisson's ratio (μd) [Yang 2017, pp. 4-2].

- **Permeability Parameters:** Upstream pressure (pᵤ), downstream pressure (pd), initial saturation pressure (p₀), pressure increment (Δp₀), permeability (k), dynamic fluid viscosity (μ), reservoir volumes (Vᵤ, Vd) [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4].

## Reusable Claims
- Claim 1: For Rizhao sandstone, heating to 800 °C and cooling to room temperature reduces the average elastic modulus from 26.50 GPa to 21.31 GPa, a decrease of nearly 20%. The change is minimal up to 500 °C [Yang 2017, pp. 7-9].
- Claim 2: The critical temperature range for significant changes in physical, mechanical, and permeability behavior of the studied sandstone is 400–500 °C, as evidenced by accelerated weight loss, volume expansion, and P-wave velocity reduction above this range [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2, 4-1].
- Claim 3: Based on the linear Mohr-Coulomb criterion, heating sandstone leads to an increase in cohesion and a decrease in internal friction angle at temperatures below the critical range, with the opposite trends observed after the critical range is exceeded [Yang 2017, pp. 6-7].

## Connections To Other Work
- The paper notes that the characteristics of temperature dependency vary with rock types, citing studies on marble, claystone, shale, granite, salt rock, and mudstone. For the same type of rock, the critical temperature is not constant and might result from initial rock features [Yang 2017, pp. 2-2].
- The study's findings on elastic modulus and peak strength were discussed in the context of a compilation of normalized data from various references (e.g., Shao et al., 2015; Kong et al., 2016; Yin et al., 2016) to show the range of temperature effects on different rocks [Yang 2017, pp. 2-2].
- The research connects the thermal decomposition of minerals and water escape (states of attached, bound, and mineral-combined water) to changes in physical properties, referencing Zhang et al. (2016) [Yang 2017, pp. 4-5].
