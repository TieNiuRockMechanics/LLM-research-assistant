# Controls on the Quality of Archean Metamorphic and Jurassic Volcanic Reservoir Rocks from the Xinglongtai Buried Hill, Western Depression of Liaohe Basin, China.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the petrological, lithological, and diagenetic controls on the reservoir quality and heterogeneity of Archean metamorphic and Jurassic volcanic rocks in the Xinglongtai buried hill, Liaohe Basin, China [Luo 2005, pp. 3].

## Research Question
What are the key controls on the quality and heterogeneity evolution of Archean metamorphic and Jurassic volcanic reservoir rocks in the Xinglongtai buried hill? [Luo 2005, pp. 3]

## Study Area / Data
- **Study Area**: Xinglongtai buried hill, located in the western depression of the Liaohe Basin, northeastern China. The Liaohe Basin is a Tertiary intracratonic rift basin characterized by multiple phases of Mesozoic and Cenozoic volcanic eruptions. The reservoir area covers about 30 km² [Luo 2005, pp. 3].
- **Data**: Core samples, thin sections, wire-line logs (acoustic, spontaneous potential, resistivity, density, induction, gamma-ray), and oil production data from exploration wells drilled between 1970 and 1977, and later [Luo 2005, pp. 3, 6-12, 13]. The study examines an Archean metamorphic suite (e.g., granitoid gneiss, migmatite) and a Jurassic volcanic suite (e.g., andesite, tuffaceous sandy conglomerate, volcanic breccias) [Luo 2005, pp. 3, 8, 10, 12]. Regionally, mudstones in the Shahejie and Dongying formations serve as source rocks and seals [Luo 2005, pp. 3-6].

## Methods
The study used integrated petrological (core, thin-section), lithological, and diagenetic analysis, combined with interpretation of wire-line logs and well testing results, to characterize reservoir properties and understand their controlling factors [Luo 2005, pp. 2-3, 6-8].

## Key Findings
- **Vertical Zonation in Archean Metamorphic Reservoir**: The Archean suite is divided into three zones: a weathered top zone with enhanced porosity due to weathering, vugs, and fissures; a middle fracture zone with well-developed structural fractures partially filled by oil or minerals; and a lower tight zone with poorly developed porosity and permeability [Luo 2005, pp. 2-3, 6-8].
- **Impact of Paleogeomorphology**: Reservoir quality is partly controlled by paleogeomorphology. Rocks located in paleohighs and adjacent transitional areas have enhanced reservoir properties compared to those in paleolows, because of more extensive weathering and better development of vugs and fissures [Luo 2005, pp. 2-3].
- **Reservoir Quality Metrics**: The Archean metamorphic reservoir has total measured porosity ranging from 3.0% to 10.8% (average 6.0%) and permeability from 0.04 to 70.4 md (average 22.0 md). The middle fracture zone measures 3.1–10.8% porosity (average 8.1%) and 0.1–70.4 md permeability (average 47.7 md) [Luo 2005, pp. 6-8]. The Jurassic volcanic reservoir’s total porosity in cores and thin sections ranges from 2% to 18% (average 10%) [Luo 2005, pp. 10-12].
- **Volcanic Reservoir Pore Types**: Porosity in the Jurassic volcanic rocks consists of primary pores (vesicles, interbreccia pores, and cooling fractures) and secondary pores (dissolution voids and structural fractures). Vesicles are most common at the top of lava intervals, accounting for 2–12% (average 6%) porosity [Luo 2005, pp. 10-12].
- **Structural Controls**: Major northeast- and east-west-trending faults confine the Xinglongtai structure. Faults, particularly the Lengdong fault, may have acted as conduits for hydrocarbon migration, bringing source rocks from adjacent sags into contact with the metamorphic and volcanic reservoirs [Luo 2005, pp. 3-6].
- **Well-log Characteristics**: Reservoir zones show characteristic log signatures (e.g., high acoustic values, significant negative spontaneous potential, high resistivity), whereas the tight zone shows a mudstone potential baseline, low resistivity, and high density [Luo 2005, pp. 6-8].

## Reusable Claims
- In the Archean metamorphic reservoir of the Xinglongtai buried hill, a vertical zonation into a weathered zone, a fracture zone, and a tight zone is identifiable, with the fracture zone exhibiting the best reservoir properties (average porosity 8.1%, average permeability 47.7 md) [Luo 2005, pp. 6-8].
- Reservoir quality in metamorphic and volcanic buried-hill rocks is strongly controlled by paleogeomorphology: reservoirs in paleohighs have higher porosity and permeability due to more intense weathering and fissuring compared to those in paleolows, which are commonly occluded by mud and iron oxides [Luo 2005, pp. 2-3].
- The primary porosity in the Jurassic volcanic reservoir at Xinglongtai is dominated by vesicles, which are particularly abundant at the top of lava flow intervals and contribute an average of 6% porosity [Luo 2005, pp. 10-12].
- Major boundary faults, such as the Lengdong fault, can act as hydrocarbon migration pathways by juxtaposing source rocks in adjacent sags against the reservoir rocks of the buried hill [Luo 2005, pp. 3-6].
