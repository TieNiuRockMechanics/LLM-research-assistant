# Experimental Study on the Physical and Mechanical Variations of Hot Granite under Different Cooling Treatments.

## One-line Summary
This experimental study compares natural, water, and liquid-nitrogen cooling on heated granite, finding that liquid-nitrogen induces the most extensive crack networks and property deterioration, especially when the rock is heated to 600°C and 800°C [Kang 2021, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
How do different cooling treatments (natural cooling, water cooling, and liquid-nitrogen cooling) affect the physical and mechanical properties, and the associated crack evolution, of hot granite specimens (as a typical Hot Dry Rock medium) [Kang 2021, pp. 1-2]?

## Study Area / Data
The study investigates a coarse-grained granite. The mineral composition at room temperature is reported as Quartz (41.78%), Orthoclase (23.95%), Albite (17.10%), Oligoclase (7.29%), Biotite (4.29%), and other minerals (5.59%) [Kang 2021, pp. 2-3]. The grain size ranges from 0.05 mm to 12 mm [Kang 2021, pp. 3-4]. The source or quarry of the granite is **未从索引片段中确认**.

## Methods
- **Heating-cooling treatment**: Cylindrical granite specimens were heated to peak temperatures of 200°C, 400°C, 600°C, and 800°C in a muffle furnace [Kang 2021, pp. 1-2, pp. 2-4]. Cooling methods included:
    - Natural cooling (NC): cooled slowly in the furnace.
    - Water cooling (WC): quenched in a vessel with flowing water at room temperature.
    - Liquid-nitrogen cooling (LNC): quenched in a Dewar tank with 50L liquid-nitrogen for 2 hours [Kang 2021, pp. 2-3].
- **Physical property measurements**:
    - P-wave velocity (longitudinal wave): measured using a Pundit Lab ultrasonic detector [Kang 2021, pp. 2-3].
    - Effective porosity: tested using Low Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (LNMR) [Kang 2021, pp. 2-3].
    - Permeability: measured using a steady-state method with a single-phase saturated permeability experimental device, estimated via Darcy’s law [Kang 2021, pp. 2-3].
    - Mass and dimensions: measured via electronic scale and caliper to calculate volume and bulk density [Kang 2021, pp. 2-3].
- **Mechanical property testing**: Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and elastic modulus were determined. The specific loading conditions are **未从索引片段中确认**.
- **Crack investigation**: A multi-scale investigation was performed, including visual observation for macro-cracks and **未从索引片段中确认** method (e.g., SEM or thin-section analysis) for micro-structures [Kang 2021, pp. 1-2, pp. 8-9].

## Key Findings
- **Influence of peak temperature**: The effect of cooling treatment on the physical and mechanical properties of granite becomes more pronounced as the peak temperature increases from 200°C to 800°C [Kang 2021, pp. 1-2]. For example, the P-wave velocity reduction for the LNC specimen reaches 93.38% at 800°C [Kang 2021, pp. 5-6].
- **Observable stages of cooling effect**: Based on physical property differences (volume, bulk density, P-wave velocity, porosity, permeability), the influence of cooling treatment can be divided into two stages: an "unobvious stage" from room temperature to 400°C, and an "obvious stage" from 400°C to 800°C [Kang 2021, pp. 5-6]. For mechanical properties (UCS, elastic modulus), a significant difference between cooling treatments is observed earlier, with a temperature threshold of 200°C [Kang 2021, pp. 8-9].
- **Superiority of liquid-nitrogen cooling (LNC)**: In the "obvious stage" (600°C and 800°C), LNC causes the most significant alterations in granite properties compared to NC and WC [Kang 2021, pp. 1-2]. Specifically, at 800°C:
    - **Crack patterns**: LNC creates complex fracture networks with dominant and interconnected secondary cracks, which are more intensive than those from NC and WC. WC shows noticeable dominated cracks only at 800°C, while NC does not show such features [Kang 2021, pp. 4-5].
    - **Physical Properties**: LNC leads to the greatest reduction in P-wave velocity and the highest enhancement of permeability (1220 times increase) and effective porosity [Kang 2021, pp. 5-6].
    - **Mechanical Properties**: The Uniaxial Compressive Strength (UCS) drops to 30.03 MPa for LNC, compared to 38.07 MPa for NC and 34.92 MPa for WC [Kang 2021, pp. 6-7].
- **Mechanism of LNC dominance**: The cold shock of LNC is stronger due to two main factors. First, the Leidenfrost effect, while present, is mitigated by increased surface cracks that enhance heat transfer. Second, the extreme low temperature of liquid-nitrogen (-195.8°C) creates a much higher temperature gradient and associated thermal stress than water (20°C), propagating cracks more effectively [Kang 2021, pp. 9-11].

## Limitations
- The study focuses on a single type of granite; the results may not be directly transferable to other HDR lithologies or granites with different mineral compositions and grain sizes [Kang 2021, pp. 3-4].
- Only three specific cooling treatments (NC, WC, LNC) were examined. Other waterless fracturing fluids or cooling rates are not considered [Kang 2021, pp. 2-2].
- The investigation of the Leidenfrost effect’s mitigation is based on qualitative reasoning about crack-enhanced heat transfer; a quantitative heat transfer analysis is **未从索引片段中确认**.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Thermal treatment assumption**: The heating phase is assumed to have the same effect on the granite as natural cooling in the furnace. This assumption is used to separate the heating contribution from the total damage for quantification [Kang 2021, pp. 9-11].
- **Experimental conditions**:
    - Granite specimens are heated to a target peak temperature before being subjected to a single cooling treatment.
    - Peak temperatures investigated are at discrete levels: 200°C, 400°C, 600°C, and 800°C [Kang 2021, pp. 1-2].
    - The water used for cooling is at room temperature, while the liquid-nitrogen temperature is approximately -195.8°C at 0.1 atm [Kang 2021, pp. 2-2].

## Reusable Claims
1.  For coarse-grained granite, liquid-nitrogen cooling (LNC) induces a larger increase in permeability than water cooling (WC) or natural cooling (NC) after heating to 800°C. Evidence: Permeability increased by 1220 times for LNC, compared to 1099 for WC and 942 for NC for the same rock type and heating condition [Kang 2021, pp. 5-6].
2.  The effect of cooling treatment on the physical properties (e.g., permeability, P-wave velocity) of thermally treated granite is negligible at peak temperatures up to 400°C, but becomes significant and increases remarkably between 400°C and 800°C. This defines an "unobvious stage" and an "obvious stage" for cooling effects [Kang 2021, pp. 5-6].
3.  The Leidenfrost effect, which can theoretically weaken liquid-nitrogen’s cooling efficiency by creating an insulating vapor layer, is mitigated on hot rocks that are already cracked. The cracks increase surface roughness, enhancing heat transfer and allowing the extreme cold shock of liquid-nitrogen to surpass water in creating a complex fracture network [Kang 2021, pp. 9-11].
4.  The Uniaxial Compressive Strength (UCS) difference between cooling treatments becomes pronounced at a lower peak temperature (200°C) than the difference in physical properties (400°C). This suggests that UCS is sensitive to the formation of scattered, unconnected microcracks that are not yet numerous enough to significantly alter bulk physical measurements [Kang 2021, pp. 8-9].

## Connections To Other Work
- The study explicitly situates itself in the context of HDR (hot dry rock) geothermal energy extraction, where understanding fracturing behavior after cooling is critical for reservoir stimulation [Kang 2021, pp. 1-2].
- It relates its findings to the controversial effectiveness of liquid-nitrogen cooling, mentioning that previous studies attributed weaker performance of liquid-nitrogen to the [[Leidenfrost effect]] [Kang 2021, pp. 1-2, pp. 2-2].
- The observed permeability enhancements of over two orders of magnitude at high temperatures (800°C) are noted to be consistent with previous work on thermally treated rocks [Kang 2021, pp. 5-6].
- The study’s mechanism discussion connects the expansion of quartz due to α-β transition at 573°C to a marked increase in thermal stress and crack formation, citing established mineralogical knowledge [Kang 2021, pp. 8-9].
