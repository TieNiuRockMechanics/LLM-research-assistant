# Characterisation of the Basel 1 Enhanced Geothermal System.

## One-line Summary
This paper presents the geological, geomechanical, and hydraulic characterization of the Basel 1 well and its stimulation, together with the analysis of induced microseismicity, to develop an Enhanced Geothermal System in Basel, Switzerland, and to understand the mechanisms and risks of induced seismicity. [Markus 2008, pp. 1-2]

## Research Question
How can an effective hydraulic heat exchanger be created in the crystalline basement beneath Basel, and what are the controlling factors and risks of hydraulically induced seismicity during reservoir stimulation? [Markus 2008, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
- **Project**: Deep Heat Mining (DHM) Project in the city of Basel, Switzerland. [Markus 2008, pp. 1-2]
- **Tectonic setting**: Southern Upper Rhine Graben, a strike‑slip regime with NW‑oriented maximum horizontal stress (\(S_{Hmax}\)). [Markus 2008, pp. 2-4]
- **Well Basel 1**: Drilled through 2,411 m of sediments and ∼100 m of Permian siltstone/weathered granite before encountering crystalline basement at 2,507 m depth. Basement consists exclusively of plutonic rocks; no metamorphic rocks were observed. The open‑hole section extends from 4,629 m to 5,000 m depth. [Markus 2008, pp. 4-5]
- **Data types**: drilling observations, wireline logs (including a VSP in the nearby OTER2 well), low‑rate injection test before stimulation, massive hydraulic stimulation data (injection rate, wellhead pressure, triggered event rate, magnitude), and microseismic monitoring from a dedicated network. [Markus 2008, pp. 1-2, 8-10, 13-16]

## Methods
- **Stress characterization**:  
  - \(S_{V}\) from integrated density log (Eq. 1: \(S_{V}=0.0249z\) MPa, \(z\) in meters). [Markus 2008, pp. 4-5]  
  - \(S_{Hmax}\) and \(S_{hmin}\) estimated by RACOS® (Rock Anisotropy Characterization on Samples) because no hydrofracturing tests were performed. [Markus 2008, pp. 4-8]  
- **Pre‑stimulation hydraulic test**:  
  - Fluid replacement (mud→freshwater), shut‑in pressure build‑up, three‑step low‑rate injection (max 10 L/min), followed by shut‑in pressure decline. [Markus 2008, pp. 8-10]  
  - Interpretation used log–log diagnostic plots under the assumption of radial flow; a bilinear flow signature indicated fracture‑dominated flow. [Markus 2008, pp. 8-10]  
- **Hydraulic stimulation**:  
  - Massive injection of 11,570 m³ of river water starting 2 December 2006. [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]  
  - Transmissibility enhancement factor estimated from pressure and flow‑rate changes during bleed‑off using Eq. 3. [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]  
- **Microseismic monitoring and analysis**:  
  - Network design via numerical simulation of S/N ratio for a \(M_w\) 0.8 event at 5 km depth, including anelastic attenuation, scattering, and geometrical spreading. [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]  
  - Hypocentre locations, seismic `response procedure` (a “Traffic Light System” based on public response, \(M_L\), and PGV). [Markus 2008, pp. 12-13]  
  - Fault plane solutions from P‑wave first‑motion polarities for 27 events (\(1.7 \le M_L \le 3.4\)). [Markus 2008, pp. 16-18]  
  - Multiplet cluster analysis using waveform coherency to identify discrete repeated‑shear structures. [Markus 2008, pp. 16-18]

## Key Findings
- **Stress regime**: Strike‑slip with \(S_{Hmax} > S_{V} > S_{hmin}\); \(S_{Hmax}\) azimuth is 144 ± 14° (consistent with nearby well OTER2). Natural fractures strike 54 ± 14°. [Markus 2008, pp. 4-8]  
- **Pre‑stimulation permeability**: Effective permeability ≈ 1 × 10⁻¹⁷ m², transmissibility ≈ 5 × 10⁻¹⁵ m³, dominated by a few fractures. [Markus 2008, pp. 8-10]  
- **Stimulation effect**: Fracture transmissibility increased irreversibly by a factor of ~400 throughout the bleed‑off phase. [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]  
- **Induced seismicity**:  
  - First events detected ~3.75 h after stimulation start; seismically active volume ≈ 35 × 10⁶ m³, elongated ~1 km in depth and oriented parallel to \(S_{Hmax}\). [Markus 2008, pp. 13-16]  
  - Local magnitudes range from −1 to 3.4; the largest events (\(M_L\) 3.1–3.4) were felt and led to project suspension. [Markus 2008, pp. 13-16]  
- **Fault plane solutions**:  
  - Dominantly strike‑slip mechanisms with a minor normal component; nodal planes strike approximately NS and EW. [Markus 2008, pp. 16-18]  
  - Multiplet clusters (coherent‑waveform groups) trend NS and EW, matching the active nodal planes of contained events, indicating repeated shear on discrete fractures. [Markus 2008, pp. 16-18]

## Limitations
- No hydrofracturing stress measurements were conducted; absolute stress magnitudes remain uncertain and are constrained only through combined indirect methods. [Markus 2008, pp. 4-8]
- Pre‑stimulation injection‑test steps were too short to reach steady‑state conditions; the initial reservoir pressure could not be precisely determined (estimated range 17–26.3 bar at wellhead with freshwater). [Markus 2008, pp. 8-10]
- The transmissibility enhancement factor of ~400 relies on simple pressure/flow‑rate changes during bleed‑off and assumes the pre‑stimulation test as a stable reference; no production‑test data are presented to verify sustained improvement. [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]
- Fault plane solutions are available only for a limited set of events (27) and may not represent the full diversity of rupture processes. [Markus 2008, pp. 16-18]
- The study does not include coupled hydro‑mechanical modelling or long‑term circulation test results; project was suspended after felt events, leaving the long‑term EGS performance unquantified. [Markus 2008, pp. 1-2, 13-16]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The stress regime is strike‑slip (\(S_{Hmax} > S_V > S_{hmin}\)) throughout the open‑hole section; no direct stress measurement confirmed this. [Markus 2008, pp. 4-8]
- Fluid flow during the pre‑stimulation test is dominated by a few fractures and can be approximated by radial flow for permeability estimation. [Markus 2008, pp. 8-10]
- The pre‑stimulation test provides a valid baseline pressure‑flow relationship for the transmissibility ratio calculation (Eq. 3). [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]
- Microseismic hypocentre locations reflect the stimulated fracture volume; the reservoir volume estimation assumes each 25‑m cell containing at least one event contributes to the active volume. [Markus 2008, pp. 13-16]
- Waveform‑similar multiplet clusters represent discrete, repeatedly slipping fractures; the cluster orientation indicates the active nodal plane of the fault plane solution. [Markus 2008, pp. 16-18]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Overburden gradient**: \(S_V = 0.0249z\) MPa (\(z\) in m). [Markus 2008, pp. 4-5]
- \(S_{Hmax}\) **azimuth**: 144 ± 14°. [Markus 2008, pp. 4-8]
- **Natural fracture strike**: 54 ± 14°. [Markus 2008, pp. 4-8]
- **Pre‑stimulation transmissibility**: ~5 × 10⁻¹⁵ m³; **effective permeability**: ~1 × 10⁻¹⁷ m². [Markus 2008, pp. 8-10]
- **Stimulation injection volume**: 11,570 m³. [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]
- **Transmissibility enhancement ratio**: \(T_{F,i}/T_{F,0} \approx 400\). [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]
- **Seismically active volume**: ≈ 35 × 10⁶ m³. [Markus 2008, pp. 13-16]
- **Induced seismicity magnitude range**: −1 ≤ \(M_L\) ≤ 3.4. [Markus 2008, pp. 13-16]
- **Fault plane parameters**: strike, dip, rake for two nodal planes (NP1, NP2); P‑ and T‑axes orientations. [Markus 2008, pp. 16-18]

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim 1**: In a tight crystalline basement EGS with a strike‑slip stress regime, a massive water injection of ~11,570 m³ can irreversibly increase fracture transmissibility by a factor of ~400, as quantified from bleed‑off pressure‑flow changes relative to a low‑rate pre‑stimulation test.  
  *Conditions*: The pre‑stimulation test must provide a reliable baseline; the calculation assumes the same flow geometry.  
  *Limitation*: Only applied to a single well; no proppant or acidising was used. [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]

- **Claim 2**: Induced seismicity during EGS stimulation can be managed/classified using a “Traffic Light System” based on public response, local magnitude (\(M_L\)), and peak ground velocity (PGV), with thresholds pre‑determined from synthetic ground‑motion simulations calibrated with data from a nearby EGS site and local site amplification factors.  
  *Conditions*: Requires an independent real‑time seismic monitoring agency (e.g., Swiss Seismological Service) and prior administrative approval.  
  *Evidence*: The DHM project implemented such a system and suspended operations when events exceeded the agreed thresholds (\(M_L\) 3.1–3.4). [Markus 2008, pp. 12-13]

- **Claim 3**: In a strike‑slip domain with \(S_{Hmax}\) oriented NW‑SE, induced microseismic events cluster along planes parallel to \(S_{Hmax}\) (here ~NS‑trending), and the active rupture planes inferred from fault‑plane solutions match the orientation of waveform‑coherent multiplet clusters, indicating repeated shear on pre‑existing fractures optimally oriented for failure.  
  *Conditions*: Valid for the crystalline basement of the southern Rhine Graben where natural fractures strike ~54°; focal mechanisms derived from at least 27 events of \(M_L\) 1.7–3.4.  
  *Limitation*: Only a few multiplet‑to‑fault‑plane matches were identified (four clusters). [Markus 2008, pp. 16-18]

## Connections To Other Work
- The regional stress field and seismotectonic setting are linked to earlier studies on the Upper Rhine Graben (e.g., Pleneﬁsch and Bonjer, 1997; Deichmann et al., 2000; Kastrup et al., 2004) that established the NW‑oriented strike‑slip regime. [Markus 2008, pp. 2-4]
- The seismic response procedure directly adapts the “Traffic Light System” proposed by Bommer et al. (2006) and used ground‑motion simulations based on induced seismicity data from the Soultz‑sous‑Forêts EGS project, with local amplification corrections from Giardini et al. (2004). [Markus 2008, pp. 12-13]
- The transmissibility enhancement equation (Eq. 3) is taken from Jung and Ortiz (2007). [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]
- On a conceptual level, the work relates to other EGS projects that combine stress analysis, hydraulic stimulation, and microseismic monitoring, such as [[Soultz-sous-Forêts]], and to methodologies for [[large-volume water injection]] in crystalline rock and [[induced seismicity risk mitigation]].

## Open Questions
- The absolute magnitudes of \(S_{Hmax}\) and \(S_{hmin}\) remain poorly constrained because no in‑situ hydrofracturing measurements were made; a robust stress profile is still missing. [Markus 2008, pp. 4-8]
- The long‑term hydraulic performance of the stimulated reservoir cannot be assessed because the project was suspended after felt induced events; no production or circulation test data are presented. [Markus 2008, pp. 1-2, 13-16]
- The physical mechanism by which irreversible transmissibility enhancement occurs (e.g., shear dilation vs. fracture propagation) is not directly proven; the study infers it from microseismic and pressure data. [Markus 2008, pp. 10-12]
- Whether a modified stimulation protocol could achieve the same permeability improvement while limiting maximum magnitude below the felt threshold remains an open operational question.
