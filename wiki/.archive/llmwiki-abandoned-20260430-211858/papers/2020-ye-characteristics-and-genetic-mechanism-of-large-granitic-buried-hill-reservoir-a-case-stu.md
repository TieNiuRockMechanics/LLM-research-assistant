# Characteristics and Genetic Mechanism of Large Granitic Buried-Hill Reservoir, a Case Study from PengLai Oil Field of Bohai Bay Basin, North China.

## One-line Summary
This paper investigates the reservoir characteristics and controlling factors of the large granitic buried-hill reservoir in the Penglai (PL) oilfield, Bohai Bay Basin, identifying lithology, faults, weathering, and burial depth as the primary control factors for the formation of high-quality reservoirs [Ye 2020, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question
What are the reservoir characteristics of the granite buried hill in the PL oilfield, and what are the primary genetic mechanisms and controlling factors (e.g., lithology, weathering, fault activity, burial depth) that govern the formation and distribution of high-quality reservoir zones? [Ye 2020, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
- **Study Area Location:** Penglai (PL) oilfield is situated in the Tanlu fault zone within the Bohai Bay basin, North China [Ye 2020, pp. 1-3]. The Bohai Bay basin is a Cenozoic continental petroliferous basin covering an area of about 20 × 10⁴ km² [Ye 2020, pp. 3-4].
- **Geological Context:** The PL structure is a granite buried hill that was subject to long-term uplift and exposure during the Paleogene, resulting in a present-day burial depth of only about 1500 m, which is unusually shallow for such formations [Ye 2020, pp. 1-3]. It was subsequently overlain by the Miocene Guantao Formation mudstone [Ye 2020, pp. 3-4].
- **Data Sources:** The study utilized data from multiple wells (PL2, PL4, PL5, PL6, PL8, PL11, PL13) including 68.07 m of conventional cores and 283 side-wall cores (each 2.5 cm long). Additional data included principal element analysis (80 samples), whole rock diffraction (9 samples), physical property measurements (188 samples), thin sections (283 samples), and conventional and imaging logging data (19 wells) [Ye 2020, pp. 3-4]. The depth range of core data is primarily between 1289 m and 1655 m [Ye 2020, pp. 4-5].

## Methods
- **Lithology Identification:** Rock lithology was determined using binocular microscope observation of cores and side-wall cores, supplemented by QAP diagram plotting based on mineral content from XRF principal element analysis (using a XRF-1500 spectrometer) and CIPW norm calculations [Ye 2020, pp. 3-4].
- **Reservoir Space Analysis:** Thin sections were impregnated with blue/red resin for petrological observation to identify reservoir space types, including pore and fracture characteristics. Fluorescence was also observed in thin sections to assess hydrocarbon presence [Ye 2020, pp. 3-4,6-7].
- **Reservoir Classification and Evaluation:** Reservoir types were classified using imaging logging (Eclips-5700 series run by COSL) and conventional logging data from 19 wells, combined with physical property measurements (porosity and permeability) from 188 core and side-wall core samples [Ye 2020, pp. 3-4].
- **Fault and Uplift Analysis:** The influence of faults on reservoir development was analyzed by calculating the correlation between reservoir thickness and the distance from the main controlling fault (Northwest Miaoxi fault, F1). The tectonic uplift history was also recovered to assess its impact on weathering [Ye 2020, pp. 1-1,8-9].

## Key Findings
- **Lithology Control:** The dominant reservoir-forming lithology in the PL structure is Mesozoic intrusive granite (primarily granodiorite and monzogranite), as it is more brittle and less resistant to weathering compared to the Proterozoic mica quartz schist. This leads to a unique "saddle enrichment" phenomenon where hydrocarbons are found in granite at lower structural positions, while schist at higher positions is hydrocarbon-poor [Ye 2020, pp. 1-1,8-9]. The abundance of felsic minerals (feldspar, quartz) in the granite facilitates fracture formation and mineral dissolution for pore development [Ye 2020, pp. 8-9].
- **Vertical Zonation of Reservoir Types:** Under the influence of weathering intensity and fault activity, reservoirs are distributed in an orderly, layered fashion from top to bottom: (1) a porous reservoir in the weathered glutenite zone (WGZ), (2) a pore-fractured reservoir in the weathered fracture zone (WFZ), and (3) a fractured reservoir in the inner fracture zone (IFZ, part of the bedrock zone BZ) [Ye 2020, pp. 1-1,7-8]. Reservoir types transition regularly from pore-dominated to fracture-pore to fracture-dominated downwards [Ye 2020, pp. 6-8].
- **Fault Control on Reservoir Thickness:** The northwest-trending Miaoxi fault (F1) is the primary controlling fault. There is an obvious negative correlation between granite reservoir thickness and distance from F1, with significantly greater thicknesses observed within 3 km of the fault [Ye 2020, pp. 1-1,8-9]. Fault-parallel fractures (near NE-trending, parallel to F1's strike) are also dominant in the study area [Ye 2020, pp. 8-9].
- **Physical Property-based Reservoir Typing:** Based on the cross-plot of porosity and permeability, three distinct reservoir types with different characteristic slopes can be differentiated, confirming the three-fold classification from geological observation. The reservoirs have generally low porosity (mostly below 6%, some up to ~14%) and highly variable permeability (0.001–10 ×10⁻³ μm²) [Ye 2020, pp. 6-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Lithology Type:** Granodiorite, monzogranite, and mica quartz schist [Ye 2020, pp. 4-8].
- **Reservoir Thickness:** Related to the distance from the main controlling fault [Ye 2020, pp. 8-9].
- **Distance from Fault:** A key spatial variable, inversely correlated to reservoir thickness [Ye 2020, pp. 8-9].
- **Physical Properties:** Porosity (%), Permeability (×10⁻³ μm²). Main porosity range is below 6%, permeability range is 0.001–10 ×10⁻³ μm² [Ye 2020, pp. 6-7].
- **Burial Depth:** The current depth of the buried hill is ~1500 m, a condition that facilitated strong weathering [Ye 2020, pp. 1-3].
- **Reservoir Zone Classification:** WGZ, WFZ, IFZ (within BZ) [Ye 2020, pp. 7-8].

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** In granitic buried-hill systems, the relative brittleness and weathering susceptibility of lithologies directly control hydrocarbon accumulation positions. Brittle, less weathering-resistant lithologies (e.g., granite) can form reservoirs in structural lows, while more resistant lithologies (e.g., mica quartz schist) may be non-reservoir in structural highs [Ye 2020, pp. 1-1]. **Conditions:** Applicable to buried hills where structural trap closure is not the sole control on accumulation. **Limitations:** Validated in the PL oilfield, where contrasting lithologies of granite and schist are juxtaposed.
- **Claim:** The presence of a major fault creates a laterally predictable damage zone, where reservoir thickness derived from fracture enhancement decreases with distance from the fault plane [Ye 2020, pp. 8-9]. **Conditions:** Observed within ~3 km of the Northwest Miaoxi fault in the PL structure. **Limitations:** The specific distance-decay relationship has not been confirmed from the provided fragments outside this case.
- **Claim:** A vertical reservoir zonation model for granite buried hills can be constructed based on weathering intensity, comprising a top porous-weathered clastic zone, a middle pore-fracture zone, and a deep purely fractured zone [Ye 2020, pp. 1-1,7-8]. **Conditions:** Applies to buried hills with a preserved weathering crust and a long uplift history. **Limitations:** The boundaries between zones are gradational, as evidenced by the logging response.
