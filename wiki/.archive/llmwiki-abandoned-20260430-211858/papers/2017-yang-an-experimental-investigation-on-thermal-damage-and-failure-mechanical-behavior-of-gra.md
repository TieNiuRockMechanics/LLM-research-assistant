# An Experimental Investigation on Thermal Damage and Failure Mechanical Behavior of Granite after Exposure to Different High Temperature Treatments.

## One-line Summary

This study investigates the effect of high temperature treatments (25–800 °C) on the thermal cracking, strength, deformation, and failure behavior of granite using uniaxial compression tests, optical microscopy, and X-ray micro-CT [Yang 2017, pp. 1-3].

## Research Question

How do different high temperature treatments affect the thermal damage, crack damage threshold, strength, elastic modulus, and failure mechanical behavior of granite? [Yang 2017, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data

Samples were taken from a granite material identified as an excellent medium for nuclear waste disposition due to its lower permeability and better integrity [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3]. The locations of 39.5°N and 117.0°E are noted in Table 1, which likely correspond to the sampling origin in Shandong Province, China [Yang 2017, pp. 4-5]. The granite was mainly composed of three mineral grains: quartz (density ≈ 2650 kg/m³), feldspar (density ≈ 2570 kg/m³), and biotite (density ≈ 3120 kg/m³) [Yang 2017, pp. 4-5].

## Methods

Uniaxial compression tests were performed on granite specimens after exposure to high temperatures of 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 700, and 800 °C [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2]. A TAW-1000 servo-controlled rock mechanics experimental system with a 1000 kN capacity was used, applying a constant axial displacement rate of 0.08 mm/min (≈1.33 × 10⁻⁵ s⁻¹) [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4]. Axial and radial deformations were measured using linear variable differential transducers (LVDTs) [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4]. Acoustic emission (AE) counts were continuously monitored with a DS2 full-information AE measuring system to study fracture evolution during deformation [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4]. Thermal damage of uncompressed specimens was analyzed via X-ray micro-CT scanning (Nanotom 160, 30 μm spatial resolution, 130 kV, 90 μA electron current) and optical microscopic observation [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4, 3]. Internal crack distribution after failure was further examined by X-ray micro-CT [Yang 2017, pp. 5-8]. Mechanical parameters like crack damage threshold (σ_cd), uniaxial compressive strength (σ_c), static elastic modulus (E_s), static Poisson's ratio (ν_s), dynamic elastic modulus (E_d), and dynamic Poisson's ratio (ν_d) were calculated from stress-strain curves [Yang 2017, pp. 8-9].

## Key Findings

*   The crack damage threshold, uniaxial compressive strength, and static elastic modulus of granite all increased at 300 °C, before decreasing with temperatures up to 800 °C [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].
*   The dynamic elastic modulus decreased with increasing temperature, but the dynamic Poisson’s ratio did not depend on the temperature [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].
*   The static Poisson’s ratio first decreased, reaching its lowest value at 600 °C, then increased rapidly with temperature up to 800 °C [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].
*   The crack damage threshold strain and peak axial strain always showed an increase when the temperature was increased [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].
*   Increasing temperature results in more ductile failure of granite under uniaxial compression [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2].
*   Thermal cracks (boundary cracks and transgranular cracks within feldspar and quartz grains) could not be observed via X-ray micro-CT at temperatures up to 600 °C, indicating their widths were smaller than the 30 μm resolution. Isolated cracks larger than 30 μm were observed at 700 °C [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3, 5-8]. The α/β quartz transition at ~573 °C caused a substantial increase in microcracking at 600 °C [Yang 2017, pp. 5-8].
*   Crack density and crack aperture thickness increased with temperature from 400 to 800 °C [Yang 2017, pp. 5-8].

## Limitations

*   While X-ray micro-CT was used, its minimum resolution of 30 μm was insufficient to detect the thermal cracks that formed at temperatures of 600 °C and below; these cracks were only visible through optical microscopy [Yang 2017, pp. 5-8].
*   The research focused on uniaxial compression, and behavior under triaxial stress conditions was not addressed in this study [Yang 2017, pp. 3].
*   The investigation is experimental; numerical modeling of the damage and failure processes was not a component of this research. 未从索引片段中确认。
*   The applicability of results to other granite types is constrained by the specific mineralogy of the studied material (i.e., the presence and behavior of quartz, feldspar, and biotite) [Yang 2017, pp. 4-5].
*   Long-term thermal effects or creep behavior were not considered. 未从索引片段中确认。

## Assumptions / Conditions

*   The experiments were conducted on a specific granite material, whose mechanical and thermal damage behavior is assumed to be representative for evaluating rock suitability in applications like nuclear waste disposal and enhanced geothermal systems [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2, 2-3].
*   The test conditions assume a uniform temperature treatment through the specimen.
*   The axial displacement rate was held constant (0.08 mm/min) for all uniaxial compression tests, implying that rate-dependent effects are constant across the temperature comparisons [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4].
*   The Crack Damage Threshold (σ_cd) is defined by the point on the stress-strain curve where volumetric strain switches from compaction-dominated to dilatancy-dominated [Yang 2017, pp. 8-9]. This definition is assumed to accurately represent the initiation of unstable crack growth.
*   The stiffness of the testing machine (TAW-1000, 1000 kN capacity) is assumed sufficient to capture the post-peak failure behavior of the specimens [Yang 2017, pp. 3-4].

## Reusable Claims

*   **Claim 1:** For the tested granite, static elastic modulus values are consistently higher than dynamic elastic modulus values after exposure to different high temperatures. [Yang 2017, pp. 9-10]
    *   **Conditions:** Uniaxial compression on granite after thermal cycling (25–800 °C). Dynamic modulus derived from ultrasonic wave velocity methods. Static modulus from the slope of the linear portion of the stress-strain curve.
    *   **Evidence:** `E_s` values were 37.35, 36.26, 40.73, 32.45, 25.18, 16.88, 4.842, and 2.068 GPa at each increasing temperature step, while corresponding `E_d` values were significantly lower at 12.20, 7.90, 6.81, 5.89, 4.70, 1.87, 0.22, and 0.12 GPa [Yang 2017, pp. 9-10].
    *   **Limitations:** Only validated for a fine- to medium-grained granite. The discrepancy between static and dynamic methods is a common phenomenon in rocks, but the specific ratio may vary across lithologies.
*   **Claim 2:** Thermal damage in granite at temperatures of 600 °C and below is dominated by microcracks with apertures smaller than 30 μm, which cannot be resolved by standard high-resolution X-ray micro-CT. [Yang 2017, pp. 5-8]
    *   **Conditions:** Granite containing thermally expansive quartz (α/β transition ≈573 °C). Heating was performed before loading. The observation condition is a spatial resolution limit of 30 μm.
    *   **Evidence:** Optical microscopy confirmed the presence of abundant boundary and transgranular cracks at 400 and 600 °C, yet X-ray CT scans did not capture these cracks. Cracks larger than 30 μm became visible only at 700 °C [Yang 2017, pp. 5-8].
    *   **Limitations:** This claim is defined by the specific threshold of the equipment used. Cracks observed below 600 °C might be detectable with higher-resolution (e.g., nano-CT) or synchrotron-based imaging. The finding confirms the crucial role of the quartz transition in crack opening width.
*   **Claim 3:** For granite, the static Poisson's ratio (ν_s) shows a non-monotonic trend with increasing temperature, minimizing at 600 °C, whereas dynamic Poisson's ratio (ν_d) is temperature-invariant. [Yang 2017, pp. 1-2, 9-10]
    *   **Conditions:** This refers specifically to static properties measured from axial and radial LVDTs and dynamic properties calculated from sonic velocity measurements, all at room temperature after a high-temperature treatment.
    *   **Evidence:** ν_s decreased from 0.127 at 25 °C to 0.038 at 600 °C, then increased to 0.367 at 800 °C. In contrast, ν_d remained in the range of 0.449–0.462 across all temperatures, with an average of 0.456 [Yang 2017, pp. 9-10].
    *   **Limitations:** The exact mechanism for the ν_s minimum at 600 °C requires further microstructural investigation, though it is likely linked to the α/β quartz transition and subsequent crack-network rearrangement. The claim may only hold for granite with abundant quartz content.

## Connections To Other Work

The study directly connects to previous works on the thermal mechanical behavior of other rock types, providing a point of comparison. Notably, it reports a similar non-monotonic trend in strength and stiffness (initial strengthening at ~300 °C) to that observed by [[Ranjith et al. (2012)]] in Hawkesbury sandstone and by [[Yang et al. (2014)]] in red sandstone [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3]. It contrasts with [[Heap et al. (2012)]], who found that high temperature did not affect the strength of all tuffs, attributing strength reduction to the presence of thermally unstable zeolites [Yang 2017, pp. 2-3]. The study is methodologically connected to other investigations using X-ray CT to analyze damage in rocks, such as [[Suﬁan and Russell (2013)]] on Gosford sandstone and [[Yang et al. (2015)]] on sandstone under cyclic loading, extending such observations specifically to thermally-damaged granite [Yang 2017, pp. 3].
