# A Review on Pore Structure Characterization in Tight Sandstones.

## One-line Summary
This paper critically reviews the techniques for characterizing the complex nano- to micro-scale pore throat structures in tight sandstones, emphasizing that the integration of complementary methods is essential to fully describe pore geometry, size distribution, and connectivity, which are controlled by both depositional and diagenetic factors [Lai 2017, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question
How can the microscopic pore throat structure (including pore geometry, pore size distribution, and connectivity) of tight sandstones be quantitatively evaluated and comprehensively characterized to better understand and predict macroscopic reservoir quality and fluid flow? [Lai 2017, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
The geological context for this review is primarily deep-buried tight sandstone reservoirs. The paper references specific geological formations and basins to illustrate characteristics and methods:
- **Formation Examples:** Upper Triassic Xujiahe Formation in Sichuan Basin, and the Upper Triassic Yanchang Formation in Ordos Basin [Lai 2017, pp. 5-6][Lai 2017, pp. 6-6].
- **Sample Characteristics:** Pore sizes in tight sandstones range from nanometers to micrometers, with pore-throat sizes typically smaller than 2 μm. In the Yanchang Formation, pore sizes are reported to range from 50 nm to 400 mm [Lai 2017, pp. 5-6].
- **Data Sources:** The review synthesizes findings from peer-reviewed papers and the authors' personal experiences [Lai 2017, pp. 1-1].

## Methods
The paper is a literature review that critically assesses the theories and procedures of various testing techniques for pore structure characterization. The methods reviewed include:
- **Imaging and Petrographic Analysis:** Thin sections, Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), X-ray Computer Tomography (XCT), and Field Emission-SEM (FE-SEM) for direct observation of pore types, pore geometry, and clay mineralogy [Lai 2017, pp. 5-6][Lai 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Fluid Invasion Methods:** High-Pressure Mercury Injection (HPMI) for characterizing pore throat size distribution and connectivity across five orders of magnitude (from approx. 350 μm to 2 nm) [Lai 2017, pp. 6-7]. The relationship between capillary pressure and pore radius is governed by the Washburn equation $Pc = \frac{2\sigma \cos\theta}{r}$ [Lai 2017, pp. 6-7].
- **Scattering and Other Techniques:** Small-angle neutron scattering (SANS) and gas adsorption (e.g., N2GA) for quantifying pore sizes, particularly at the nano-scale [Lai 2017, pp. 1-1][Lai 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Numerical Analysis:** Fractal geometry theory for quantitative characterization of pore structure complexity [Lai 2017, pp. 1-1].
- **Supportive Analysis:** X-ray diffraction (XRD) is noted for mineral composition analysis [Lai 2017, pp. 1-2].

## Key Findings
- **Geological Control:** The complex pore networks in tight sandstones are a product of both original depositional attributes (e.g., grain size, sorting, rigid grain content) and subsequent diagenetic alterations (e.g., compaction, quartz and carbonate cementation, authigenic clay mineral formation) [Lai 2017, pp. 2-3].
- **Pore System Dominance:** Tight sandstones are petrographically distinguished by the loss of primary porosity and the dominance of secondary dissolution porosity and micropores associated with authigenic clays [Lai 2017, pp. 3-5]. Slot pores, formed between abutting quartz overgrowth faces, are also characteristic [Lai 2017, pp. 3-3].
- **Pore and Throat Sizes:** There is a wide range of pore and throat sizes leading to high heterogeneity. Pore throats are mainly in the range of 0.1 to 1.0 μm [Lai 2017, pp. 6-7]. Pore throat type diversity includes contracted, flake-like, curved, and pipe-like throats [Lai 2017, pp. 5-6].
- **Porosity-Permeability Relationship:** The permeability generally shows a moderate exponential relationship with porosity. Disconnects, such as high porosity but low permeability, are attributed to poor connectivity and a tortuous pore system dominated by clays and slot-like pores [Lai 2017, pp. 6-6].
- **MICP Insights:** Mercury Injection Capillary Pressure (MICP) curves show that pore throat size distributions in tight sandstones are often bimodal, reflecting the coexistence of larger residual intergranular pores and clay-dominated micropores. High-permeability samples are associated with interconnected, relatively large pore throats [Lai 2017, pp. 6-7].
- **Integration is Essential:** No single technique can fully characterize the entire pore size spectrum. For example, point-counting-derived porosity underestimates total porosity because it cannot count clay-dominated micropores [Lai 2017, pp. 6-6]. A combination of complementary techniques is required to span from nano-scale to micron-scale features [Lai 2017, pp. 1-1].

## Limitations

For instance, point-counting from thin sections cannot quantify clay-dominated micropores, and MICP measures pore throat size rather than pore body size [Lai 2017, pp. 6-6]. ## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Depositional and Diagenetic Framework:** The entire review operates under the assumption that pore structure is a direct result of initial depositional conditions and the subsequent diagenetic history of the sandstone [Lai 2017, pp. 2-3].
- **Deep Burial Context:** The characteristics described are particularly relevant for sandstones that have experienced deep burial (>3.5 km) and elevated temperatures [Lai 2017, pp. 3-3][Lai 2017, pp. 2-3].
- **MICP Analysis:** The application of the Washburn equation for converting capillary pressure to pore radius assumes a specific surface tension, contact angle, and a simplified, cylindrical pore geometry [Lai 2017, pp. 6-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Pore Throat Radius (r):** A fundamental parameter for classifying pore sizes and understanding fluid flow, derived from Pc via the Washburn equation [Lai 2017, pp. 6-7].
- **Capillary Pressure (Pc):** Measured by MICP, used to infer pore throat size distribution [Lai 2017, pp. 6-7].
- **Porosity and Permeability:** Macroscopic parameters used to gauge reservoir quality, often showing a moderate exponential correlation in tight sandstones [Lai 2017, pp. 6-6].
- **Intergranular Volume (IGV):** The volume of pore space between framework grains, used as an indicator of the extent of mechanical and chemical compaction [Lai 2017, pp. 3-3].
- **Pore Type Classification:** Categories such as residual primary intergranular, intragranular dissolution, micropores, and slot pores are used to distinguish genetic pore types [Lai 2017, pp. 3-5].
- **Fractal Dimension:** A quantitative metric used to characterize the geometric regularity and complexity of the pore structure, as mentioned in Section 5 [Lai 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Reusable Claims
1.  In tight sandstones, petrographic point-counting consistently underestimates total porosity because it cannot resolve clay-dominated micropores; the discrepancy between point-count porosity and helium-measured porosity provides an estimate of microporosity [Lai 2017, pp. 6-6].
2.  The pore throat size distribution in tight sandstones, as determined by MICP, is typically bimodal. The right peak corresponds to larger, well-connected residual intergranular pores, while the complex left peak corresponds to multi-scale clay-dominated micropores and dissolution pores [Lai 2017, pp. 6-7].
3.  In deeply buried tight sandstones, mechanical and chemical compaction is the dominant mechanism for porosity loss, evidenced by low intergranular volumes (IGVs) and concavo-convex or sutured grain contacts, whereas quartz cementation further reduces permeability by occluding pore throats and creating narrow slot pores [Lai 2017, pp. 3-3][Lai 2017, pp. 3-5].
4.  A comprehensive characterization of the entire pore structure in tight sandstones requires integrating multiple complementary techniques, as no single method can cover the full size range from nanometers to micrometers identified by techniques like gas adsorption, SANS, and MICP [Lai 2017, pp. 1-1][Lai 2017, pp. 2-3].
