# Laboratory Investigation on Physical and Mechanical Properties of Granite After Heating and Water-Cooling Treatment.

## One-line Summary
This laboratory study investigates how heating (up to 900 °C) followed by rapid water-cooling alters the physical and mechanical properties of granite, finding a significant degradation threshold near 500–600 °C linked to quartz phase transition and microcrack generation [Zhang 2018, pp. 1-2, 4-6].

## Research Question
How do heating and subsequent rapid water-cooling treatments affect the physical properties (thermal conductivity, P-wave velocity, gas permeability, porosity) and mechanical properties (elastic modulus, strength, brittle-ductile transition) of granite, and how are these changes correlated with microstructural evolution? [Zhang 2018, pp. 2-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study uses granite samples, described as initially having an intact microstructure. The geographical origin of the granite is not explicitly stated in the provided fragments, though comparison is made to Yantai granite [Zhang 2018, pp. 6-7]. The number of tested samples is mentioned as three per condition for some tests [Zhang 2018, pp. 6-7].

## Methods
The experimental program involved heating granite samples to target temperatures (25, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, and 900 °C) followed by rapid cooling in water at ambient temperature for 1 hour [Zhang 2018, pp. 2-4]. The average cooling rate ranged from 18.0 to 19.2 °C/min, significantly faster than the ~1 °C/min used in some previous studies [Zhang 2018, pp. 2-4]. The following characterization methods were used:
- **Microstructure analysis:** Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) to observe microcrack generation [Zhang 2018, pp. 2-4].
- **Physical property measurement:** Measurement of density and porosity via water saturation [Zhang 2018, pp. 4-6]; thermal conductivity using a transient state comparative method [Zhang 2018, pp. 4-6]; P-wave velocity using transducers with glycerol as a coupling medium [Zhang 2018, pp. 6-7]; gas permeability using a steady flow method with argon gas [Zhang 2018, pp. 7-8].
- **Mechanical testing:** Uniaxial and triaxial compression tests at various confining pressures (0, 5, 10, 15 MPa) to determine elastic properties and strength [Zhang 2018, pp. 8-11].

## Key Findings
- **Porosity and Microstructure:** Porosity increased from 1.19% (initial) to 6.13% (900 °C), with a significant increase after 500 °C, linked to the α-β quartz transition at 573 °C and the generation of trans-granular cracks [Zhang 2018, pp. 4-6]. Microcrack density and connectivity increased with temperature [Zhang 2018, pp. 2-4].
- **Wave Velocity and Transport Properties:** P-wave velocity decreased significantly with temperature, dropping from ~3343 m/s (sound) to ~594 m/s at 900 °C [Zhang 2018, pp. 6-7]. Gas permeability increased by three orders of magnitude after treatment, from the order of 10⁻¹⁸ m² to 10⁻¹⁵ m² at 900 °C, with a change in evolution rate around 600 °C [Zhang 2018, pp. 7-8].
- **Mechanical Properties:** The initial loading tangent modulus (E₀) continuously decreased with heating temperature, with a more pronounced rate of decrease above 500 °C [Zhang 2018, pp. 8-11]. Peak deviatoric stress dropped drastically above 500 °C under all confining pressures. For example, at 0 MPa confining pressure, peak stress dropped from 130-144 MPa (≤500 °C) to 57.74 MPa at 600 °C [Zhang 2018, pp. 11-13]. The granite exhibited a transition from brittle to ductile behavior at high temperatures, showing obvious elastic-plastic behavior at 900 °C [Zhang 2018, pp. 1-2].
- **Elastic Modulus under Confining Pressure:** Between 200 °C and 500 °C, elastic modulus and cohesion showed no obvious change, but decreased significantly above 500 °C. The rate of decrease was slower under higher confining pressure. The variation may be due to competing mechanisms of thermal hardening and cracking [Zhang 2018, pp. 1-2, 11-13].

## Limitations
- The confining pressures used in the study (max 15 MPa) may be relatively low for some geothermal engineering applications. Further tests with higher confining pressures are needed [Zhang 2018, pp. 8-11].
- The study focuses on a single type of granite, and the results' generalizability to other rock types is not confirmed from the provided fragments.
- The specific number of specimens tested for some mechanical properties and the exact temperature control precision during heating are not detailed in the provided fragments.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Cooling Mechanism:** The rapid water-cooling creates a significant thermal gradient, generating a non-uniform plastic strain field and a self-equilibrated residual stress field, which is a key condition differentiating this study from slow-cooling experiments [Zhang 2018, pp. 11-13].
- **Klinkenberg Effect:** The Klinkenberg effect on gas permeability is considered negligible due to the high injection pressure (1.5 MPa) used [Zhang 2018, pp. 7-8].
- **Material Homogeneity:** The initial state of the granite was assumed to have a nearly intact microstructure based on SEM observations at room temperature [Zhang 2018, pp. 2-4].
- **Elastic Modulus:** The initial loading tangent modulus (E₀) from uniaxial tests is used as the primary measure of elastic property change, valid at very low stresses [Zhang 2018, pp. 8-11].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variable:** Temperature of heating treatment (25, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 900 °C) [Zhang 2018, pp. 2-4].
- **Dependent Physical Variables:** Porosity (φ), saturated density (ρ_sat), dry density (ρ_d), P-wave velocity, thermal conductivity, gas permeability (K) [Zhang 2018, pp. 4-8].
- **Dependent Mechanical Variables:** Initial loading tangent modulus (E₀), elastic modulus (E), Poisson’s ratio (ν), peak deviatoric stress, cohesion, internal frictional coefficient [Zhang 2018, pp. 1-2, 8-13].
- **Control Parameter:** Confining pressure (P_c = 0, 5, 10, 15 MPa) during mechanical tests [Zhang 2018, pp. 11-13].
- **Key Formula for Porosity:** `φ = (m_sat - m_dry) / V × ρ_w × 100%` [Zhang 2018, pp. 4-6].
- **Key Formula for Permeability:** `K = μ Q_m S / (2 h P_m (P₁² - P₀²))` [Zhang 2018, pp. 7-8].

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim 1:** For this specific granite, a heating and rapid water-cooling treatment above a critical temperature window (500-600 °C) causes a dramatic degradation of mechanical strength (e.g., peak stress under uniaxial compression) and physical integrity (e.g., a three-order-of-magnitude increase in gas permeability), driven by the α-β quartz phase transition at 573 °C [Zhang 2018, pp. 4-8, 11-13]. Evidence is from direct comparison of mechanical and physical tests across a temperature range. Limitation: This threshold may be specific to granites with significant quartz content and rapid cooling.
- **Claim 2:** Rapid water-cooling induces a thermal gradient that generates both material hardening (via non-uniform plastic strain) and damage (via microcracking) in granite. The competition between these two mechanisms results in a non-monotonic variation of elastic modulus between 200 and 500 °C [Zhang 2018, pp. 11-13]. Evidence is from the observed initial increase and subsequent plateau of elastic modulus before the final decrease. Limitation: The claim is a proposed explanation ("may be related to two physical processes"), not a directly measured proof of the hardening mechanism.
- **Claim 3:** The rate of elastic modulus degradation at high temperatures (>500 °C) is attenuated by increased confining pressure [Zhang 2018, pp. 1-2, 11-13]. Evidence comes from triaxial tests showing less significant modulus variations at 10 and 15 MPa confining pressure compared to uniaxial tests. Condition: This is valid for the range of confining pressures tested (up to 15 MPa).

## Connections To Other Work
- The study directly positions itself against a background of numerous previous investigations on the thermal treatment of granite, noting that most focused on slow cooling. It cites a wide range of such work [Zhang 2018, pp. 2-2].
- The measured porosity increase from rapid cooling (1.19% to 6.13% at 900 °C) is compared to a slow cooling study by Chaki et al. (2008), which found an increase from 0.68% to 2.85% at 600 °C, highlighting the impact of cooling rate [Zhang 2018, pp. 4-6].
