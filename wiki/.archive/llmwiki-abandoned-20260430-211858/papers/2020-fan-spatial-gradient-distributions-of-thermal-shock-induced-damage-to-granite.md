# Spatial Gradient Distributions of Thermal Shock-Induced Damage to Granite.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the three-dimensional spatial gradient distributions of damage in granite induced by thermal shock (rapid cooling from high temperature) using X-ray computed tomography (CT) and image analysis, revealing that damage is highest near the surface and decreases inward, with the gradient dependent on preheating temperature and becoming negligible at 800 °C [Fan 2020, pp. 1].

## Research Question
How does thermal shock (rapid water quenching) produce spatially non‑uniform damage in granite, and how do the spatial gradient distributions of that damage change with increasing preheating temperature? [Fan 2020, pp. 1]

## Study Area / Data
- **Rock type:** Granite with mineral composition: quartz (37 %), plagioclase (36.2 %), K‑feldspar (20.6 %), mica (2.7 %), calcite (2.5 %), and clay minerals (1 %). Initial specimens showed tightly bonded minerals with no visible microcracks under 50× magnification [Fan 2020, pp. 2‑3].
- **Specimens:** 33 granite specimens divided into six groups by preheating temperature: 25 °C, 200 °C, 400 °C, 500 °C, 600 °C, and 800 °C. For each temperature except 25 °C, three specimens were subjected to rapid cooling (thermal shock) and three to slow cooling for comparison [Fan 2020, pp. 2‑3].
- **Experimental conditions:** Heating rate 2.5 °C/min to avoid thermal shock during heating, held at target temperature for 3 h. Rapid cooling: immediately immersed in tap water. Slow cooling: cooled in the turned‑off oven to room temperature [Fan 2020, pp. 2‑3].

## Methods
- **Thermal shock treatment:** Preheating to high temperatures followed by rapid water quenching, compared with slow cooling [Fan 2020, pp. 2‑3].
- **X‑ray CT scanning and 3D reconstruction:** A selected cubic analysis region (9.6 mm × 9.6 mm × 9.6 mm) was extracted from the specimen centre, and CT slices were reconstructed into a 3D volume [Fan 2020, pp. 3‑5].
- **Quantitative damage assessment:** Damage was evaluated by **porosity** \( P = \frac{V_{\text{Pore}}}{V_{\text{Total}}} \times 100\% \) computed from the segmented 3D images [Fan 2020, pp. 5‑7].
- **Spatial gradient analysis:** The analysis region was divided into five concentric local regions (Region 1 at the centre to Region 5 at the outer edge). The average porosity \( P_i \) of each region was calculated. The gradient of the spatial distribution of damage was evaluated using the slope of a linear fit between porosity and distance from the specimen centre [Fan 2020, pp. 7‑9].

## Key Findings
1. **Rapid cooling induces more damage than slow cooling.** For all preheating temperatures, the porosity produced by rapid cooling was larger than that produced by slow cooling, and the difference increased with preheating temperature [Fan 2020, pp. 7].
2. **Thermal shock creates a spatial gradient of damage.** After rapid cooling, porosity was lowest in the interior (Region 1) and highest near the surface (Region 5). This gradient was clearly observed at 400 °C, 500 °C, and 600 °C [Fan 2020, pp. 7‑8].
3. **The spatial gradient depends on preheating temperature.** The gradient (slope of porosity vs. distance from centre) increased as preheating temperature rose up to 600 °C, then decreased significantly beyond 600 °C. At 800 °C, the spatial gradient was approximately zero and could be ignored, indicating a nearly homogeneous damage distribution [Fan 2020, pp. 8‑9].
4. **Damage evolution with temperature.** Porosity increased slowly up to 500 °C and then sharply for both cooling methods. The sharp increase was especially pronounced after 500 °C [Fan 2020, pp. 7].
5. **Visual 3D evidence.** 3D reconstructions showed that damage became more connected at temperatures above 600 °C, supporting the observed loss of gradient [Fan 2020, pp. 9].

## Limitations
- The study is limited to a single granitic rock type; the results may not directly transfer to other lithologies [未从索引片段中确认，但属常见限制].
- The CT imaging magnification is smaller than that of optical microscopy, so individual microcrack details below the CT resolution are not captured [Fan 2020, pp. 1‑2].
- Damage analysis was performed on a selected cubic sub‑volume (9.6 mm side) rather than the entire specimen, which may not fully represent boundary‑edge effects [Fan 2020, pp. 3‑5].
- Only tap water was used as the quenching medium; the influence of different cooling fluids (e.g., brine, liquid nitrogen) was not tested [未从索引片段中确认].
- Long‑term cyclic thermal shock effects were not considered [未从索引片段中确认].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Thermal shock is primarily induced by rapid cooling:** the heating phase is designed to be slow enough (2.5 °C/min) to avoid thermal stresses during heating [Fan 2020, pp. 2‑3].
- **Homogeneous mineral distribution:** the calculated porosities average over the analysed sub‑volume, assuming the spatial gradient analysis on five concentric regions captures the main radial trend [Fan 2020, pp. 7‑8].
- **Porosity as a damage proxy:** the volume of pores and cracks segmented from CT images represents all significant thermally induced damage [Fan 2020, pp. 5‑7].
- **Constant temperature conditions:** each specimen was held at the target temperature for 3 h to ensure thermal equilibrium before cooling [Fan 2020, pp. 2‑3].
- **Water temperature and environment:** quenching was performed in tap water at room temperature; any variation in water temperature across trials is assumed negligible [未从索引片段中确认水温和室温的具体控制].

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Cooling method:** rapid cooling (water quenching) vs. slow cooling (furnace cooling)

- **Distance from specimen centre:** derived from the local region geometry; used to fit the spatial gradient
- **Gradient (slope) of porosity vs. distance:** used to quantify the spatial distribution heterogeneity

## Reusable Claims
1. **Claim:** For granite, rapid water cooling after high‑temperature preheating produces higher total porosity than slow furnace cooling, and this difference increases with preheating temperature.  
   **Conditions:** Temperatures 200–800 °C, holding time 3 h, heating rate 2.5 °C/min, water at room temperature.  
   **Evidence:** Porosity comparison from CT analysis [Fan 2020, pp. 7].  
   **Limitation:** Only tested on one granite type.

2. **Claim:** Thermal shock damage in granite is spatially heterogeneous: after rapid cooling, porosity near the surface can be significantly larger than in the interior, forming a radial gradient.  
   **Conditions:** Rapid quenching from 400–600 °C.  
   **Evidence:** Porosity of five concentric regions, clearly increasing outward [Fan 2020, pp. 7‑8].

3. **Claim:** The spatial gradient of thermal shock damage in granite is not monotonic with preheating temperature: it increases up to 600 °C and then declines, becoming negligible at 800 °C where damage is nearly uniform.  
   **Conditions:** Rapid water cooling, granite, temperatures ≤800 °C.  
   **Evidence:** Gradient slopes from linear fits and 3D reconstruction images [Fan 2020, pp. 8‑9].  
   **Limitation:** Above 800 °C the trend may not hold; crack coalescence is inferred as the mechanism.

4. **Claim:** A threshold temperature of about 500 °C exists for granite, above which thermally induced porosity increases sharply regardless of cooling rate.  
   **Conditions:** Slow or rapid cooling, 3 h dwelling time.  
   **Evidence:** Porosity–temperature curves [Fan 2020, pp. 7].

## Connections To Other Work
- **CT‑based thermal damage quantification:** This study builds on works that use CT to evaluate thermally induced microcracks, e.g., Isaka et al. (2019) who studied thermal shock‑enhanced pore connectivity and permeability in granite using CT [Fan 2020, pp. 1‑2].
- **Surface micro‑observation techniques:** Earlier studies (e.g., Jin et al., 2019; Rathnaweera et al., 2018) used optical microscopy to observe thermal shock microcracks on surfaces, which showed deterioration of macroscopic properties but lacked 3D spatial information. The current study extends these observations into a 3D volume [Fan 2020, pp. 1‑2].
- **Thermal stress gradients:** The explanation that the highest thermal shock stresses occur at the surface and decrease with depth aligns with general heat transfer principles (e.g., Zhao et al., 2019, on granite’s low thermal conduction) and links to numerical models of thermal cracking [Fan 2020, pp. 9].

## Open Questions
- **Mineral‑specific crack distribution:** The authors suggest that future work should investigate the spatial distribution of cracks induced by different minerals and analyse the cracking mechanism through numerical simulation [Fan 2020, pp. 9‑10].
- **Scale effects:** How does the observed gradient scale from the laboratory specimen to field‑scale rock masses? [未从索引片段中确认]
- **Multiple thermal cycles:** The effect of repeated thermal shock events (cyclic quenching) on the evolution of spatial gradients was not explored [未从索引片段中确认].
- **Influence of cooling rate:** The study used only one rapid cooling scenario (immersion in tap water). The relationship between cooling rate, thermal shock intensity, and gradient formation remains an open question [未从索引片段中确认].
