# Simulation of Ground Stress Field and Fracture Anticipation with Effect of Pore Pressure.

## One-line Summary

This study uses triaxial compression tests and finite element numerical simulation (ADINA) incorporating pore pressure and fault slip to simulate the tectonic stress field of the Kenkiyak pre-salt oilfield and predict fracture distribution based on tension and shear rupture rates [Lian 2011, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question

How does pore pressure influence the tectonic stress field simulation and fracture distribution prediction in carbonate reservoirs? Specifically, the study addresses how to integrate pore pressure effects into finite element models and how to predict fracture density and rupture rates based on the resulting stress field [Lian 2011, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data

The study focuses on the Carboniferous pre-salt reservoir of the Kenkiyak oilfield, located at the nose uplifted structure in the Ural-emba salt dome region of the eastern Caspian Basin [Lian 2011, pp. 2-4]. Core samples for triaxial tests and well data (e.g., Well 7001, Well 8001, Well 234, Well 8016) from this field were utilized for experimentation and model verification [Lian 2011, pp. 4-6][Lian 2011, pp. 7-8].

## Methods

### Triaxial Compression Tests
Eight core samples from the Kenkiyak reservoir were prepared following ASTM and ISRM standards. They were saturated with oil, divided into two groups, and subjected to different confining pressures and pore pressures using HTHP triaxial equipment until failure. The resulting fractures were analyzed through thin section observations [Lian 2011, pp. 2-4].

### Finite Element Numerical Simulation
The tectonic stress field was simulated using the three-dimensional finite element software ADINA, with the rock modeled as a fluid-filled porous medium. The model incorporated five faults, assuming that fault planes could slip under force to release stress. The simulation accounted for the effect of pore pressure on the effective stress state [Lian 2011, pp. 1-2].

### Fracture Prediction
Based on simulation results, two prediction approaches were applied:
1.  **Total Rupture Rate (I<sub>total</sub>):** A combined index of normalized tension and shear failure factors, weighted by their statistical proportions (a = 0.32, b = 0.68) observed from cores in the field [Lian 2011, pp. 6-7].
2.  **Binary Method for Fracture Density:** A function combining rupture rate (I) and strain energy density (A) to forecast fracture density, using a piecewise equation governed by a critical rupture rate value (I<sub>c</sub>) [Lian 2011, pp. 7-8].

## Key Findings

1.  **Experimental Rupture Modes:** Triaxial tests show that as effective confining pressure increases, the rock damage mode evolves sequentially from shear planes and high-angle cracks to conjugate shear cracks and, ultimately, net fractures, causing more severe texture damage [Lian 2011, pp. 2-4].
2.  **Simulation Validation:** The simulated structural displacement map matched the practical formation, and the simulated tectonic stresses were consistent with values measured by acoustic emission testing. The direction of the major horizontal principal stress was also consistent with imaging log interpretation data [Lian 2011, pp. 1-1][Lian 2011, pp. 4-6].
3.  **Stress Orientation:** The simulation results indicate that the major principal stress is vertical; the intermediate principal stress is oriented South-North (S-N), and the minimum principal stress is East-West (E-W) [Lian 2011, pp. 4-6].
4.  **Fault and Pore Pressure Effects:** Stress concentrates near faults, leading to secondary structures. Fault screening can trap fluids, causing pore pressure to increase locally up to 105 MPa, which is higher than the present formation pressure of 80 MPa. This abnormal pressure can generate new fractures [Lian 2011, pp. 4-6].
5.  **Fracture Distribution:** The predicted fracture density varies widely across the simulated region. Cracks are more likely to develop in structurally high positions and near faults due to elevated pore pressure and tectonic extrusion [Lian 2011, pp. 7-8].

## Assumptions / Conditions

- The numerical simulation assumes rock is a porous medium filled with fluid [Lian 2011, pp. 1-2].
- The geological model contains five faults, and it is assumed that two planes of faults could slip with the force to decompress [Lian 2011, pp. 1-1].
- Fractures are assumed to be generated when the total rupture rate, I<sub>total</sub>, is greater than 1 [Lian 2011, pp. 6-7].
- The weighting coefficients for tension and shear failure (a = 0.32, b = 0.68) were obtained from core observations of four specific wells (234, 7001, 8001, 8016) and are assumed to apply to the prediction [Lian 2011, pp. 6-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Effective Conﬁning Pressure:** Key experimental loading parameter controlling rock rupture mode and fracture density [Lian 2011, pp. 2-4].
- **Pore Pressure:** A key factor reducing effective tectonic stress; incorporated in the numerical simulation [Lian 2011, pp. 1-2].
- **Principal Stresses:** Major, intermediate, and minimum principal stress (σ₁, σ₂, σ₃) and their directions [Lian 2011, pp. 4-6].
- **Total Rupture Rate (I<sub>total</sub>):** An index combining tension failure factor and shear failure factor to predict rock failure probability. Defined as I<sub>total</sub> = a\*I<sub>t</sub> + b\*I<sub>n</sub> [Lian 2011, pp. 6-7].
- **Proportionality Coefficients (a, b):** Statistical proportions of tension and shear failure from core observation; a = 0.32, b = 0.68 for the study area [Lian 2011, pp. 6-7].
- **Strain Energy Density (dW/dV):** Calculated from effective stress components (σ), Young's Modulus (E), and Poisson's Ratio (ν). Minima of SED are potential failure locations [Lian 2011, pp. 7-8].
- **Fracture Density (x):** The predicted parameter from a binary method using rupture rate (I) and strain energy (A) [Lian 2011, pp. 7-8].

## Reusable Claims

- **Claim:** In numerical simulation of tectonic stress fields in fluid-saturated reservoirs, incorporating pore pressure is necessary as it reduces effective stress and affects the results; neglecting it introduces calculation errors [Lian 2011, pp. 1-2].
- **Claim:** For carbonate rocks under triaxial compression, the fracture pattern evolves from discrete shear planes to complex net fractures with increasing effective confining pressure, indicating a direct relationship between stress state and fracture complexity [Lian 2011, pp. 2-4]. *Evidence: Based on thin-section analysis of experimentally deformed Kenkiyak cores.*
- **Claim:** Fault planes acting as barriers to fluid flow during tectonic movement can cause localized abnormal high pore pressure, which in turn can induce fracturing in adjacent low-permeability zones [Lian 2011, pp. 4-6]. *Condition: This was observed/ simulated in the Kenkiyak Field with a maximum simulated pressure of 105 MPa versus a present-day pressure of 80 MPa.*
- **Claim:** Fracture density can be predicted by a piecewise binary function of total rupture rate (I) and strain energy density (A), where a critical rupture rate (I<sub>c</sub>) distinguishes two predictive regimes [Lian 2011, pp. 7-8].
