# Physical and Mechanical Properties of Granite After High-Temperature Treatment.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates the physical and mechanical property changes of granite subjected to high-temperature treatment (20–1000 °C), identifying key temperature thresholds (400 °C, 600 °C, 800 °C) for alterations in mineral morphology, wave velocity, strength, and failure mode [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2, 9-11].

## Research Question
The study aims to identify the physical and mechanical properties of granite before and after high-temperature treatment from room temperature to 1000 °C, and to determine the influence of high temperatures on its appearance, mineral morphology, and their intrinsic connections [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2].

## Methods
The study utilized granite rock specimens. The experimental procedure involved:
- **Sample Preparation:** Rock specimens were air-dried for 2 weeks at room temperature to eliminate natural moisture effects [Qin 2020, pp. 3-4].
- **Heating Treatment:** Specimens were heated in a high-temperature furnace at a rate of 3 °C/min to target temperatures (20–1000 °C), held constant for 2 hours, and then naturally cooled to room temperature within the furnace [Qin 2020, pp. 3-4].
- **Physical Property Measurement:** A longitudinal wave tester (DSG-1, 50 kHz) measured wave velocity, and an optical microscope (20×) observed mineral morphologies. Mass, size, and density changes were also measured [Qin 2020, pp. 3-4].
- **Mechanical Testing:** Uniaxial and triaxial compression tests were performed on the specimens following ISRM specifications using a TAW-2000 mechanical tester [Qin 2020, pp. 3-4].

## Key Findings
- **Mineral Changes:** High-temperature effects include oxidation of biotite (color change to red at 800 °C), recrystallization of feldspar (filling fractures near its melting point ~1100 °C), and development of transgranular cracks in quartz above 600 °C [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2, 4-6].
- **Physical Properties:**
  - Mass loss rate increased significantly above 500 °C, reaching 0.102% at 600 °C and 0.256% at 800 °C, attributed to water loss and dehydroxylation [Qin 2020, pp. 6-8].
  - Volume growth rate increased after 400 °C, accelerating above 600 °C due to the α-to-β quartz phase transition, leading to transgranular cracking [Qin 2020, pp. 6-8].
  - Longitudinal wave velocity decreased with temperature, and the wave was fully absorbed, unable to penetrate the specimen, at 800 °C due to severe cracking [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2, 8-9].
- **Mechanical Properties:**
  - Mechanical properties (strength, deformability) change only slightly up to 400 °C, showing initial softening then hardening in stress-strain behavior. At 400 °C, the elastic modulus decreased by 18.3% [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2, 9-11].
  - At 800 °C, plastic deformation characteristics emerge. At 1000 °C, strength and deformability are largely diminished, with the elastic modulus decreasing by approximately 85% [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2, 9-11].
  - Peak strain values fluctuated below 400 °C but increased rapidly above 500 °C; at 600 °C, the peak strain increased by ~68.4%, and at 1000 °C, it increased by ~124% [Qin 2020, pp. 9-11].
- **Temperature Thresholds:**
  - 400 °C, 600 °C, and 800 °C are characteristic temperatures marking three stages of physical and mechanical property change [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2].
  - 600 °C is the threshold for mechanical properties and represents the transition temperature of the granite's failure mode [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2, 9-11].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The melting point of feldspar was assumed to be decreased by the presence of other minerals, leading to melting and recrystallization that filled fractures at high temperatures near 800–1000 °C [Qin 2020, pp. 4-6].
- Natural moisture content effects were assumed to be eliminated by air-drying specimens for two weeks [Qin 2020, pp. 3-4].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Temperature (T):** Treatment temperature from 20 °C to 1000 °C.
- **Mass loss rate (η_m):** Defined as (m1 - m2) / m1 × 100% [Qin 2020, pp. 4-6].
- **Volume growth rate (η_V):** Defined as (V2 - V1) / V1 × 100% [Qin 2020, pp. 4-6].
- **Density reduction rate (η_ρ):** Defined as (ρ1 - ρ2) / ρ1 × 100% [Qin 2020, pp. 4-6].
- **Longitudinal wave velocity:** Measured in m/s to reflect internal damage [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2].
- **Peak strain:** The strain at peak stress, indicating deformability changes [Qin 2020, pp. 9-11].
- **Elastic modulus:** Calculated from stress-strain curves to represent stiffness [Qin 2020, pp. 9-11].
- **Heating rate:** Set constant at 3 °C/min during heat treatment [Qin 2020, pp. 3-4].

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim 1:** For the granite studied, 600 °C is a critical threshold for mechanical properties. Above this temperature, the failure mode changes, and peak strain increases significantly by ~68.4% due to the generation of extensive thermal cracks, indicating a transition towards ductile behavior [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2, 9-11]. (Evidence: Uniaxial compression tests; Conditions: Granite heated at 3 °C/min to target T, held for 2h, and cooled naturally; Limitation: Granite type is not specified in the fragments).
- **Claim 2:** At a treatment temperature of 800 °C, longitudinal wave energy is fully reflected and absorbed due to fused and meshed transgranular cracks, making acoustic wave velocity measurement impossible for rock damage assessment. [Qin 2020, pp. 1-2, 8-9] (Evidence: Longitudinal wave testing at 50 kHz; Conditions: Granite treated above 800 °C; Limitation: Applicability to other wave frequencies or rock types is unconfirmed).
- **Claim 3:** The most dramatic chemical changes in the granite, evidenced by mass loss (from dehydration and dehydroxylation), are most dramatic within the 600–800 °C temperature range. [Qin 2020, pp. 6-8] (Evidence: Measurement of mass before/after heating; Conditions: Heating rate of 3 °C/min; Limitation: The specific minerals driving mass loss beyond "water" were not detailed in this summary).

## Connections To Other Work
- **Corroborating Findings:** The observed initial increase and subsequent decrease in granite strength and elastic modulus at lower temperatures (e.g., ~200–300 °C) aligns with the findings of other scholars such as Shao et al. 2015, Liu and Xu 2015, Chen et al. 2017, and Yang et al. 2017a, b, where initial strengthening is attributed to the closure of primary cracks before thermal damage dominates [Qin 2020, pp. 2-3, 9-11].
- **Microscopic Crack Observations:** The study’s microscopic findings on crack development relate to prior work by Homand-Etienne and Houpert (1989) on crack width changes and Chen et al. (2017) on crack patterns concerning mineral types [Qin 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Contrasting High-Temperature Behavior:** Yin et al. (2016) found that the brittle-to-critical transition temperature is lower for granite tested under high temperature compared to post-high-temperature treatment, highlighting a difference in the mechanical state between in-situ hot rock and heat-treated rock [Qin 2020, pp. 3-3].
- **Rock Burst Energetics:** The study’s context connects to findings by Su et al. (2017), who reported that rock burst energy levels in granite peak at 300 °C and remain high up to 500 °C, then decrease above 600 °C [Qin 2020, pp. 3-3].
- **Data Comparisons from Other Lithologies:** The findings contribute to a broader body of research on the thermal effects on other rock types such as [[calcareous limestone]], [[mudstone]], [[claystone]], [[marble]], [[sandstone]], [[diorite]], and [[tephrite]] [Qin 2020, pp. 2-3].
