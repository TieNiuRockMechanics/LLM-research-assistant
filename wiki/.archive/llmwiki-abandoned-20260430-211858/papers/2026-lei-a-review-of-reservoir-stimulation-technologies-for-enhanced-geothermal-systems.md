# A review of reservoir stimulation technologies for enhanced geothermal systems.

## One-line Summary

This review proposes a unified “Engineering Trilemma” framework to systematically assess hydraulic, chemical, and thermal stimulation technologies, finding that no single method can independently resolve the inherent trade-offs among connectivity, sustainable heat extraction, and induced seismicity in Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) [Lei 2026, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question

How can the conflicting objectives of ensuring effective reservoir connectivity, achieving sustainable heat extraction, and managing induced seismicity be systematically addressed in EGS reservoir stimulation? [Lei 2026, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data

This is a global review of more than 60 major EGS projects initiated worldwide [Lei 2026, pp. 2-3]. Key data are derived from project-specific parameters, including depths of 3–5 km, temperatures typically 100–200 °C (with a recognized future target of 150–250 °C), and predominantly crystalline rock (especially granite) lithologies [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4]. Case studies extensively cited include Fenton Hill (USA), Basel (Switzerland), Pohang (South Korea), Cooper Basin (Australia), Northwest Geysers (USA), and Reykjanes (Iceland) [Lei 2026, pp. 2-7].

## Methods

The study is a systematic review and synthesis. The authors propose a unified analytical framework centered on the “Engineering Trilemma” [Lei 2026, pp. 1-2]. A key methodological contribution is a discrimination framework for inferring the dominant stimulation mode (Opening, Shear, or Mixed) for hydraulic stimulation. This is based on a weighted assessment (Table 1) of three evidence categories: hydraulic pressure data (40% weighting), geological context (30%), and microseismic attributes (30%). A dominant mode is assigned if one tendency score exceeds the other by a ≥ 15-point margin [Lei 2026, pp. 5-6]. The review also analyzes technological roadmaps and their correlation with geological factors like lithology and temperature [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].

## Key Findings

- **The Engineering Trilemma:** The core challenge of EGS is managing trade-offs between effective connectivity, sustainable heat extraction, and induced seismicity [Lei 2026, pp. 1-2].
- **No Single-Technology Solution:** No single stimulation paradigm (hydraulic, chemical, or thermal) can independently resolve all inherent trade-offs [Lei 2026, pp. 1-2].
- **Stimulation Mode Matters:** The dominance of tensile versus shear failure during hydraulic stimulation creates distinct risk profiles. Tensile-dominated stimulation risks thermal short-circuiting, while shear-dominated stimulation poses higher risks of fluid loss and induced seismicity [Lei 2026, pp. 5-6].
- **Technology and Geology are Linked:**
    - Hydraulic stimulation is the foundational method, applied in 84% of reviewed projects [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].
    - For highly acid-reactive carbonate reservoirs, chemical stimulation is the core technology [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].
    - Thermal stimulation as a standalone method is strictly dependent on extremely high reservoir temperatures (e.g., >400 °C), as seen only at Northwest Geysers and Reykjanes [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].
- **Multi-stage Stimulation for Spatial Control:** Cased-hole multi-stage stimulation is a prevailing method to balance connectivity and thermal short-circuiting by creating multiple, diffuse fractures instead of a single preferential flow path [Lei 2026, pp. 6-7].
- **Cyclic Stimulation for Seismic Control:** Periodic injection processes, such as cyclic soft stimulation (CSS), are designed to mitigate induced seismicity by dispersing energy injection over time [Lei 2026, pp. 6-7].
- **Critical Failure Modes:**
    - **Fluid Loss:** Connection to large, regional fault systems can lead to catastrophic fluid loss, as in the Cooper Basin project where >85% of injected fluid was lost [Lei 2026, pp. 4-5].
    - **Thermal Short-circuiting:** Dominated by advection over conduction, this leads to rapid thermal breakthrough and a shortened economic lifespan, as observed in Phase I of the Fenton Hill project [Lei 2026, pp. 4-5].
    - **Induced Seismicity:** The forced termination of projects in Basel, Switzerland, and Pohang, South Korea, demonstrates how this risk is a critical socio-political factor [Lei 2026, pp. 2-3, 4-5].

## Assumptions / Conditions

- The proposed “Engineering Trilemma” framework is a conceptual model for analyzing trade-offs [Lei 2026, pp. 1-2].
- The economic window for EGS is defined by depths of 3-5 km and a temperature range of 100-200°C, where the trade-off between drilling costs and power generation efficiency is optimized [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].
- The effectiveness of a stimulation technology is intrinsically linked to geological factors; lithology determines the effectiveness of the primary method, and temperature serves as an application threshold [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].
- Microseismic monitoring is a standardized, foundational configuration for real-time feedback control in modern EGS hydraulic stimulation [Lei 2026, pp. 6-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Injection pressure / Hydraulic pressure:** relative to minimum principal stress (σ_min), key for determining potential for tensile failure [Lei 2026, pp. 5-6].
- **Tensile Tendency Score & Shear Tendency Score:** Weighted scores (High/Medium/Low) used to infer dominant stimulation mode [Lei 2026, pp. 5-6].
- **Reservoir Temperature:** An application threshold for specific technologies (e.g., thermal stimulation) [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].
- **Reservoir Lithology:** Determines the effectiveness of chemical vs. hydraulic stimulation (e.g., granite vs. carbonate) [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].
- **Fracture Network Geometry:** Complexity and diffusivity of the fracture network, which controls heat exchange volume and thermal short-circuiting risk [Lei 2026, pp. 4-5].

## Reusable Claims

- **Claim 1:** For EGS projects in granite reservoirs, hydraulic stimulation is the preferred physical method to enhance permeability because granite is chemically less reactive, making chemical dissolution less effective. [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].
- **Claim 2:** Using thermal stimulation as the only reservoir enhancement technique is only feasible when the reservoir temperature is extremely high (e.g., 400-450°C), because a sufficient temperature difference is needed to induce a dominant thermo-elastic rock-breaking effect [Lei 2026, pp. 3-4].
- **Claim 3:** In highly fractured geological settings, hydraulic stimulation carries the critical risk of injected fluid connecting to large, regional fault systems, which can lead to massive fluid losses (>85% of injected volume) and prevent the formation of an effective circulation system [Lei 2026, pp. 4-5].
- **Claim 4:** In hydraulic stimulation, a purely tensile opening-mode fracture is prone to creating a single, high-conductivity channel that leads to rapid thermal short-circuiting, whereas a pure shear-mode stimulation creates a diffuse, complex fracture network with a larger heat exchange area but a higher risk of inducing seismicity on large faults [Lei 2026, pp. 5-6].
- **Claim 5:** Cased-hole multi-stage stimulation technology can create multiple, approximately parallel, and diffuse fractures, which increases fluid flow cross-section and tortuosity, thereby extending fluid residence time and suppressing thermal short-circuiting [Lei 2026, pp. 6-7].
