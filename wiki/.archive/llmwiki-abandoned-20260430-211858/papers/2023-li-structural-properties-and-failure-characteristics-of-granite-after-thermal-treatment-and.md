# Structural Properties and Failure Characteristics of Granite after Thermal Treatment and Water Cooling.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how thermal treatment and water cooling alter pore structure, mineral composition, mechanical properties, acoustic emission (AE) characteristics, and failure severity/mode of granite, finding that higher temperature shocks alleviate failure severity but promote crack development and shift failure modes from tensile toward shear [Li 2023, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
How do structural properties (pore size distribution, mineral composition) and failure characteristics (energy evolution, failure severity, micro‑crack development, failure mode) of granite change after heating to 25–800 °C and rapid water cooling? What are the irregular changes in failure characteristics across temperature gradients? [Li 2023, pp. 1-4]

## Study Area / Data
The study area is not geographically specified in the provided fragments. Samples are a granite rock. Five thermal treatment groups were prepared: 25 °C (untreated), 200 °C, 400 °C, 600 °C and 800 °C, all after water cooling [Li 2023, pp. 1-2]. Mineral composition and structural changes were assessed on treated specimens. Uniaxial compression results, AE data, CT pore images and XRD spectra were collected [Li 2023, pp. 4-5].

## Methods
- **Thermal treatment and cooling**: Granite samples heated to 25 °C, 200 °C, 400 °C, 600 °C, 800 °C, then water cooled [Li 2023, pp. 1-2].
- **CT scanning**: Acquired internal structure images to quantify pore volume distribution and reconstruct 3D pore shape; threshold segmentation binarized the images [Li 2023, pp. 4-5].
- **X‑ray diffraction (XRD)**： Powder samples (ground and 350‑mesh sieved) measured with D8 ADVANCE diffractometer; continuous scanning at 3°/min, sampling interval 0.02° [Li 2023, pp. 4-5].
- **Uniaxial compression tests**： MTS815 rock mechanics test system; displacement‑controlled loading at 0.1 mm/min; built‑in displacement sensor accuracy 0.05%; tests taken to ultimate failure [Li 2023, pp. 4-5].
- **Acoustic emission (AE) monitoring**： Four resonant RS‑2A sensors; DS5 AE system; preamplifier gain 40 dB; sampling frequency 4 MHz; lead‑breakage test ensured probe efficiency [Li 2023, pp. 4-5].
- **Failure severity index η**: Defined as η = (U′_e − U′_d) / U′, where U′_e is releasable strain energy at UCS, U′_d is dissipated energy at UCS, U′ is total energy at UCS [Li 2023, pp. 9-11].
- **Micro‑crack classification**: RA value (rise time / maximum amplitude) and AF value (ringdown‑count / duration) used to discriminate tensile (high RA, low AF) versus shear (low RA, high AF) cracks [Li 2023, pp. 11-13].

## Key Findings
- Pore volume proportions >10^6 µm³ are positively correlated with thermal treatment temperature, while proportions <10^5 µm³ are negatively correlated [Li 2023, pp. 1-2].
- Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and elastic modulus decline significantly with increasing temperature: UCS from 225.88 MPa (25 °C) to 85.60 MPa (800 °C); elastic modulus from 24.18 GPa to 6.51 GPa (73.08% reduction) [Li 2023, pp. 5-8].
- AE peak ringing counts increase at 200 °C (+290.29%) and 400 °C (+184.46%) relative to 25 °C, but decrease at 600 °C (−64.60%) and 800 °C (−18.60%); accumulated AE counts generally increase with temperature, indicating larger failure scale and crack numbers [Li 2023, pp. 8-9].
- The failure intensity index η decreases with rising temperature (from 0.94 at 25 °C to 0.68–0.72 at 600–800 °C), showing that thermal treatment alleviates failure severity [Li 2023, pp. 9-11].
- Failure mode evolves: 25 °C, 400 °C, 800 °C = shear‑dominant failure; 200 °C = tensile failure; 600 °C = tensile‑shear failure [Li 2023, pp. 1-2, 13-16].
- Trans‑granular cracks increase with temperature gradient due to thermal shock, contributing to the shift toward shear‑dominant failure [Li 2023, pp. 13-16].
- Quartz α→β phase transition at ~573 °C causes volume expansion and crystal‑energy drop, critically changing energy evolution and AE response near 600 °C [Li 2023, pp. 11-13].

## Limitations
- Only five temperature groups (25 °C, 200 °C, 400 °C, 600 °C, 800 °C) were tested; intermediate temperatures not covered [Li 2023, pp. 1-2].
- Natural variability of granite (anisotropy, heterogeneity) is acknowledged as a factor in irregular failure characteristics, but its quantification is not detailed in the fragments [Li 2023, pp. 13-16].
- Water cooling conditions (e.g., cooling rate, water temperature) and number of cycles are not specified in the provided fragments, though their influence on crack growth is noted [Li 2023, pp. 2-4].
- AE cracking‑type classification relies on the Ohno‑Ohtsu (2010) method; limitations of this threshold‑based classification in thermally damaged rock are not discussed in the fragments [Li 2023, pp. 11-13].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Heat exchange between the rock and external environment during mechanical loading is ignored in the energy balance (first law of thermodynamics assumed) [Li 2023, pp. 9-11].
- The threshold segmentation method on CT images can effectively distinguish pores down to 5.65 µm based on gray‑value contrast [Li 2023, pp. 4-5].
- The Ohno‑Ohtsu (2010) RA‑AF classification is valid for distinguishing tensile from shear micro‑cracks in thermally treated granite under uniaxial compression [Li 2023, pp. 11-13].
- Water cooling induces thermal shocks that generate both inter‑granular and trans‑granular cracks; the competition between thermal hardening and damage affects failure characteristics [Li 2023, pp. 13-16].

## Reusable Claims
1. **Claim**: For water‑cooled granite, the failure intensity index η defined as (releasable strain energy at UCS − dissipated energy at UCS) / total energy at UCS decreases with increasing thermal treatment temperature, indicating that higher temperature shocks alleviate the violence of compressive failure.  
   **Conditions**: Uniaxial compression at 0.1 mm/min; heating to 200–800 °C followed by water cooling. **Evidence**: η values fall from 0.94 (25 °C) to 0.68–0.72 (600–800 °C) [Li 2023, pp. 9-11]. **Limitations**: Tested on a single granite type; intermediate temperatures and cyclic cooling not covered.

2. **Claim**: Under water‑cooling thermal shock, pore volume proportion larger than 10^6 µm³ correlates positively with treatment temperature, while proportion smaller than 10^5 µm³ correlates negatively.  
   **Conditions**: Temperature range 25–800 °C; CT scanning with 0.2 mm slice thickness. **Evidence**: CT‑based pore‑size distributions across five temperature groups [Li 2023, pp. 1-2, 4-5]. **Limitations**: Specific pore‑connectivity or permeability implications not drawn from fragments.

3. **Claim**: AE peak ringing counts of water‑cooled granite under uniaxial compression first increase relative to untreated rock (200–400 °C) but then markedly drop at 600–800 °C, signaling that pre‑existing thermal cracks absorb energy and reduce violent main‑fracture energy release, while accumulated AE counts increase overall, indicating more extensive crack development.  
   **Conditions**: 4 MHz sampling, 40 dB gain, resonant AE sensors. **Evidence**: Peak counts +290.29% (200 °C), −64.60% (600 °C); accumulated counts increase with temperature [Li 2023, pp. 8-9]. **Limitations**: Only uniaxial compression tested; effect of cracking scale not separately quantified.

4. **Claim**: The failure mode of water‑cooled granite shifts with increasing treatment temperature from shear‑dominant (25 °C) to tensile (200 °C), back to shear (400 °C and 800 °C), and to mixed tensile‑shear at 600 °C, influenced by quartz α→β transition and trans‑granular crack generation.  
   **Conditions**: Heating to target temperature then water cooling; uniaxial loading. **Evidence**: AE RA‑AF density distributions and post‑failure morphology [Li 2023, pp. 1-2, 13-16]. **Limitations**: Anisotropy and thermal hardening‑damage competition cited as reasons for irregularity but not separated numerically.

## Connections To Other Work
- **Quartz phase‑change mechanism (Hartlieb et al. 2016; Ohno 1995)**： The α→β quartz transition at ~573 °C is linked to volume expansion, crystal‑energy drop, and critical changes in energy evolution and failure characteristics at 600 °C [Li 2023, pp. 11-13].
- **Energy evolution theory (Xie et al. 2005)**： The framework of releasable strain energy U_e and dissipated energy U_d is used as the basis for defining the failure intensity index η [Li 2023, pp. 9-11].
- **Crack classification method (Ohno and Ohtsu 2010)**： RA‑AF methodology is directly adopted to classify tensile and shear micro‑cracks in AE data [Li 2023, pp. 11-13].
- **Water‑cooling mechanical property trends (Liu and Xu 2015; Kang et al. 2021; Wu et al. 2022a, b)**： Prior studies found declining compressive strength, elastic modulus and ultrasonic velocity with temperature, and effects of cooling cycles and mineral heterogeneity are referenced to situate the current results [Li 2023, pp. 2-4].
