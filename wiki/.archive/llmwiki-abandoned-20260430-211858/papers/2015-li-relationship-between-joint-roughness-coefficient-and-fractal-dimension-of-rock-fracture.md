# Relationship between Joint Roughness Coefficient and Fractal Dimension of Rock Fracture Surfaces.

## One-line Summary
This study reviews the inconsistency among existing empirical equations linking Joint Roughness Coefficient (JRC) to fractal dimension (D) and, based on an enlarged dataset of 112 rock joint profiles, proposes new empirical equations showing that D from compass-walking and an updated h–L method closely relates to JRC, whereas box-counting D does not relate as closely. [Li 2015, pp. 1-1]

## Research Question
How can the empirical relationship between the Joint Roughness Coefficient (JRC) and the fractal dimension (D) of rock fracture surfaces be reliably established, given the inconsistency introduced by different D calculation methods (compass‑walking, box‑counting, h–L) and the limited number of standard profiles (Barton’s 10) used in previous equations? [Li 2015, pp. 1-1, 6-8]

## Study Area / Data
The study uses a total of **112 rock joint profiles** assembled from digitised images in the published literature. This dataset includes Barton’s 10 standard JRC profiles plus 102 additional profiles originally presented in previous publications by other authors. The digitised profile data and back‑calculated JRC values are provided as electronic supplementary material. The original rock joints span fresh, slightly weathered, and moderately weathered tension fractures ranging from well‑interlocked planar cleavage fractures to poorly interlocked film‑covered walls. [Li 2015, pp. 5-6]

## Methods
- **Digitisation and sampling:** Original PDF images of profiles were imported into AutoCAD; coordinates were sampled with a fixed horizontal interval of **0.4 mm** by tracing vertical lines placed across the profiles. [Li 2015, pp. 5-6, 8-8]
- **Fractal dimension determination:** Three methods were applied:
  - **Compass‑walking (divider) method** → produces **D<sub>c</sub>**;
  - **Box‑counting method** → produces **D<sub>b</sub>**;
  - **Updated h–L method** → produces **D<sub>h–L</sub>**; the update is intended to avoid subjectivity in identifying high‑order asperities. [Li 2015, pp. 1-1, 1-2, 6-8]
- **Trend removal and validation:** A computer program written by the authors removed the non‑zero overall slope of the digitised profiles and performed data validation. [Li 2015, pp. 6-8]
- **Correlation analysis:** New empirical linear and power‑law regressions between JRC and D (or D−1) were fitted to the 112‑profile dataset and compared with equations from the literature review. [Li 2015, pp. 6-8]

## Key Findings
- **Literature inconsistency:** Previously published empirical equations exhibit large scatter; this is partly due to the limited number of data points (often only the 10 standard profiles), partially due to inconsistent methods for determining D, and also because JRC values for the standard profiles have been assigned inconsistently in the literature. [Li 2015, pp. 1-1, 4-5]
- **Dataset expansion:** Using a larger population of **112 profiles** yields better‑constrained empirical relationships. [Li 2015, pp. 1-1, 6-8]
- **Method‑dependent correlation strength:**
  - **D<sub>c</sub>** (compass‑walking) and **D<sub>h–L</sub>** (updated h–L method) each show a close linear relationship with JRC; data points cluster within a narrow band. [Li 2015, pp. 1-1, 6-8]
  - **D<sub>b</sub>** (box‑counting) shows much lower correlation (R ≈ 0.673) and a widely dispersed point set; equations based on D<sub>b</sub> are not recommended. [Li 2015, pp. 6-8]
- **New equations proposed:** Table 3 in the paper lists the newly derived equations. For example:
  - **E1**: JRC = 1331.07 D<sub>c</sub> − 1328.44, with R = 0.8242 and JRC<sub>0</sub> = 2.63. [Li 2015, pp. 8-8]
  - Power‑law equations (E4–E6) can give a reasonable JRC value for planar or sub‑planar joints. [Li 2015, pp. 6-8]
  - Equation E6 (based on D<sub>h–L</sub>) is suggested to have the highest priority in usability because of the “easy and…” (claim incomplete in the indexed fragment). [Li 2015, pp. 6-8]
- **Sampling interval recommendation:** For applying the correlation, a sampling interval of about **0.4 mm** is recommended, as it is considered easily and conveniently achievable during scanning or digitising. [Li 2015, pp. 8-8]

## Limitations
- **Limited profile population:** Although enlarged relative to previous studies, the dataset of 112 profiles is still considered a sample; the authors state that “a bigger sample population would help justify or improve the empirical equations”. [Li 2015, pp. 8-8]
- **Digitisation error:** Re‑sampling from published PDF images via AutoCAD introduces some error; this process is essentially a re‑sampling of the original joint profile and may cause quality degradation. [Li 2015, pp. 5-6]
- **Method‑dependency of the relationship:** The recommended equations are only valid when the corresponding D‑determination method (compass‑walking, h‑L) and the recommended 0.4 mm sampling interval are used. [Li 2015, pp. 8-8]
- **Box‑counting not recommended:** The relationship for D<sub>b</sub> has low correlation (R ~ 0.673) and is explicitly not recommended. [Li 2015, pp. 6-8]
- **Source coverage limitations of this wiki:** The indexed fragments do not contain the full Table 1, Table 2, or all details on the h–L update; a complete error analysis and the full set of new equations (beyond E1, E2) cannot be verified from the fragments alone.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Sampling interval condition:** The recommended equations are derived for and should preferably be used with a sampling interval of about 0.4 mm. [Li 2015, pp. 8-8]
- **Profile preparation condition:** Profiles must be horizontally aligned (trend removal), as was performed by the authors’ program. [Li 2015, pp. 6-8]
- **Applicability to planar/sub‑planar joints:** Equations that use D as the independent variable (e.g., D not transformed to D−1) result in a non‑zero JRC for a perfectly smooth plane; such equations are not applicable for planar or sub‑planar joints. [Li 2015, pp. 2-4]
- **Data source representativeness:** The derived relationships are based on tension fractures of various weathering grades (fresh to moderately weathered) from the published literature; extrapolation to very different joint types or scales is not validated in the indexed fragments.
- **Model assumption:** Both linear and power‑law regressions were fitted; the final recommended equations do not assume a single functional form across all D ranges. [Li 2015, pp. 6-8]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **JRC** (Joint Roughness Coefficient): dimensionless index of rock joint roughness.
- **D<sub>c</sub>**, **D<sub>b</sub>**, **D<sub>h–L</sub>**: Fractal dimension estimated by compass‑walking, box‑counting, and h–L method, respectively.
- **D−1**: Independent variable used in equations (T6–T19) and in some new equations to handle the smooth‑joint condition.
- **Sampling interval**: 0.4 mm; a key control on the D value and the JRC–D correlation.
- **Correlation coefficient (R)**: Quality metric of the regression (e.g., 0.824 for E1, 0.673 for E2). [Li 2015, pp. 6-8, 8-8]
- **JRC<sub>0</sub>**: JRC value of a perfectly smooth profile (ideally zero) given by an equation; used to evaluate applicability to planar profiles. [Li 2015, pp. 2-4]
- **Z<sub>2</sub>** (root mean square of the first deviation of the profile): Mentioned as a parameter used in literature to derive JRC and potentially introduce transformation errors. [Li 2015, pp. 4-5]

## Reusable Claims
*   **Claim 1:** When deriving an empirical JRC–D equation, one must use the same fractal dimension determination method (compass‑walking, h–L, etc.) and the same sampling interval that were used to originally develop the equation; otherwise significant uncertainty is introduced. [Li 2015, pp. 1-1, 8-8] *Evidence:* The scatter among literature equations and the method‑specific correlation coefficients observed in this study. *Conditions:* Implicitly assumes that D is to be used as a predictor of JRC in rock engineering. *Limitations:* The claim is valid within the ranges of the 112 studied profiles and the 0.4 mm recommended interval.
*   **Claim 2:** The box‑counting method for determining the fractal dimension of rock joint profiles yields a D value that is poorly correlated with JRC (R ≈ 0.673) based on a dataset of 112 profiles, and empirical equations based on D<sub>b</sub> are not recommended for JRC estimation. [Li 2015, pp. 1-1, 6-8] *Evidence:* Direct quantitative comparison from this study. *Conditions:* 0.4 mm sampling interval, digitisation procedure as described. *Limitations:* The result may be specific to 2D profiles and the box‑counting procedure (geometric sequence of box sizes down to 0.2 mm) used herein.
*   **Claim 3:** A sampling interval of approximately 0.4 mm is suggested as a practical standard when digitising rock joint profiles for JRC estimation via fractal dimension, aiming for a balance between achievable resolution and a stable JRC–D correlation. [Li 2015, pp. 8-8] *Evidence:* Statement by the authors based on convenience in scanning/digitising and as a condition for the proposed equations. *Conditions:* For 2D profiles approximately 10 cm in length; re‑sampling or scanning setup must respect this interval.
*   **Claim 4:** Barton’s set of 10 standard JRC profiles is insufficient to derive a reliable JRC–fractal dimension empirical equation; an enlarged dataset is required to capture the true variability and constrain the correlation. [Li 2015, pp. 1-1, 6-8] *Evidence:* Agreement in trend between literature and this study, but persistent inconsistency among equations when only 10 profiles are used. *Conditions:* Applies to regression‑based empirical equations linking JRC to D.
*   **Claim 5:** Published empirical equations that use D as the independent variable (without the D−1 transformation) are not applicable to planar or sub‑planar joints because they yield a physically implausible non‑zero JRC for a perfectly smooth plane. [Li 2015, pp. 2-4] *Evidence:* Mathematical property of the equation form described in the literature review. *Conditions:* For models where JRC = f(D) and f(1)=a×1+b = a+b. *Limitations:* This does not comment on the accuracy of such equations for rough profiles within their calibration range.
