# Comparative Analysis of Direct (Core) and Indirect (Borehole Imaging Tools) Collection of Fracture Data in the Hot Dry Rock Soultz Reservoir (France).

## One-line Summary
A systematic comparison of fracture attributes (density, orientation, spacing) derived from continuous core versus multiple borehole imaging tools in the Soultz Hot Dry Rock reservoir, establishing that imaging techniques underestimate discrete fractures but reliably capture larger-scale altered fracture clusters and strike orientations [Genter 1997, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question
How do fracture data collected directly from cores compare with those derived from indirect borehole imaging techniques (BHTV, UBI, FMS, FMI, ARI) in terms of completeness and reliability for characterizing the fracture network in a granitic Hot Dry Rock reservoir [Genter 1997, pp. 1-1]?

## Study Area / Data
**Location:** Soultz-sous-Forêts Hot Dry Rock test site, France. The geological setting consists of a granite basement covered by approximately 1400 m of Triassic to Tertiary sediments [Genter 1997, pp. 1-3].

**Wells:**
- **EPS1 (Exploratory Slim Hole):** Drilled to 2230 m depth. Provides continuous cores with nearly 100% recovery (considered the reference dataset) and BHTV acoustic imagery. Data collected from 1000–1420 m (sandstone) and 1420–2230 m (granite) [Genter 1997, pp. 1-3; Genter 1997, pp. 3-4].
- **GPK1:** Drilled to 3600 m depth. Imaging data include FMS (1420–2000 m), FMI (2000–3600 m), ARI (2780–3500 m), BHTV (1376–3600 m), and UBI (2850–3505 m) [Genter 1997, pp. 3-3; Genter 1997, pp. 3-4].
- **GPK2:** Drilled to 3880 m depth. UBI data collected from 1420–3800 m [Genter 1997, pp. 1-3; Genter 1997, pp. 3-4].

**Temperature:** Approximately 140°C at 2000 m and 160°C at 3590 m depth in GPK1 [Genter 1997, pp. 1-3].

## Methods
- **Direct Data Collection:** Fracture attributes (orientation, density, spacing) were measured manually on continuous cores from well EPS1. Fracture strikes were determined by digitizing the sinusoidal trace on a flat core copy (±5° orientation precision), with an additional ±5° uncertainty from core orientation procedures [Genter 1997, pp. 3-4].
- **Indirect Imaging Techniques:** Multiple borehole imaging tools were deployed across three wells:
    - **BHTV (Borehole Televiewer):** Acoustic facsimile tool; fractures interpreted manually by superimposing overlays on images [Genter 1997, pp. 3-3].
    - **UBI (Ultrasonic Borehole Imager):** High-resolution focused transducer at 500 kHz (horizontal resolution: 5 mm; vertical sampling: 10 mm). Logging speed: 244 m/h [Genter 1997, pp. 3-3].
    - **FMS (Formation Microscanner):** Four-pad electrical imaging; maps microresistivity on ~50% of the borehole wall. Vertical resolution: ~3 mm; horizontal resolution per pad: 7.5 mm, but can detect thinner features if resistivity contrast is high [Genter 1997, pp. 3-3].
    - **FMI (Formation Microimager):** Higher resolution successor to FMS; full borehole coverage (specific resolution not detailed in fragments, but noted as higher than FMS) [Genter 1997, pp. 3-4; Genter 1997, pp. 8-10].
    - **ARI (Azimuthal Resistivity Imaging):** Lower resolution than the above electrical tools [Genter 1997, pp. 8-10].
- **Comparative Analysis:** Fracture density (cumulative number, fracture/m), orientation (stereographic plots), and spacing distributions (log/log cumulative frequency) were compared between the core reference set and each imaging technique. Cumulative fracture spacing distributions were tested against power-law (fractal) and exponential models [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8].
- **Error Assessment:** Estimated dip-azimuth error (±15°) and dip-magnitude error (±5°) for imaging tool interpretations, incorporating reproducibility and interpreter uncertainties [Genter 1997, pp. 3-4].

## Key Findings
- **Underestimation of Fractures:** Borehole imaging techniques are not as exhaustive as core data and cannot fully characterize the fracture network. Discrete fractures thinner than 1 mm are not properly detected, and discrete fractures closer than 5 mm may appear as a single trace [Genter 1997, pp. 1-1].
- **Detection Efficiency in Granite:** In the granite interval of well EPS1, BHTV detected 517 fractures compared to 3055 from cores. The overall BHTV fracture density was 0.64 fractures/m, versus 3.77 fractures/m for cores. In hydrothermally altered granite, less than 8% of discrete fractures were detected by BHTV [Genter 1997, pp. 4-5; Genter 1997, pp. 7-8].
- **Lithology-Dependent Bias:** The disparity between core and BHTV data is greater in granite than in Triassic sandstone. In the sandstone, detection rates were better due to more regular fracture properties and larger apertures [Genter 1997, pp. 4-5; Genter 1997, pp. 7-8].
- **Orientation Sampling Reliable:** Despite density underestimation, borehole imaging tools correctly sampled fracture strike orientations in both sandstone and granite, showing good agreement with core-derived orientation sets (e.g., N-S to NNW-SSE sets in sandstone; N-S and NE-SW sets in granite) [Genter 1997, pp. 5-7].
- **Fracture Spacing Distributions:** In sandstone, both core and BHTV data followed a negative exponential law for spacings > 0.8 m, but spacings < 0.8 m were strongly underestimated by BHTV. In granite, core-derived spacing followed a power-law (fractal) distribution with a breakdown in self-similarity above 3 m, whereas BHTV spacing followed an exponential law. The minimum spacing detected by BHTV was 5 mm [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8].
- **Altered Zone Detection:** Although imaging tools fail to resolve individual discrete fractures within intensely fractured zones, they are effective at detecting the entire fracture cluster as a single altered zone [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8].
- **Tool Resolution Variation:** In GPK1, higher fracture density was recorded by UBI (around 0.76 fractures/m) compared to FMI (0.37 fractures/m) and ARI (0.33 fractures/m) in overlapping intervals, consistent with tool resolution differences [Genter 1997, pp. 8-10].

## Limitations
- **Scope of Comparison:** The direct comparison was restricted to the BHTV and core data in well EPS1. Conclusions for FMS, UBI, FMI, and ARI draw on indirect comparisons of fracture density profiles across GPK1/GPK2, with no core reference available for these deeper sections [Genter 1997, pp. 8-10].
- **Borehole Wall Condition Bias:** BHTV detection is influenced by borehole wall roughness; altered and fractured zones with high roughness degrade the acoustic signal, leading to further underestimation [Genter 1997, pp. 5-7].
- **Interpretation Error:** The reproducibility error for dip picking from electrical images introduces uncertainty (dip magnitude: ±2–3°, dip azimuth: ±6–8°), in addition to core orientation uncertainty (±5°) [Genter 1997, pp. 3-4].
- **Data Completeness:** The study notes that continuous coring is becoming less common operationally, limiting the possibilities for such calibration studies elsewhere [Genter 1997, pp. 1-1].
- **Limited Depth Interval:** Spot coring comparison for FMS was only done on a 50-m interval, providing limited calibration [Genter 1997, pp. 8-10].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The study assumes that the EPS1 core dataset, with its ~100% recovery, represents the true, complete fracture population for comparison (“reference dataset”) [Genter 1997, pp. 1-3].
- The comparison assumes that differences in fracture detection between cores and imaging tools primarily arise from tool resolution limits and altered rock physical properties, not from the interpretative process [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8].
- The fractal analysis of spacing assumes that cumulative log/log plots can distinguish between power-law and exponential distributions [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Fracture Density:** Unit: fractures per meter (fractures/m). Determined cumulatively over depth intervals [Genter 1997, pp. 3-4].
- **Fracture Spacing:** Minimum spacing detectable by BHTV: 5 mm. Important metric for characterizing cluster vs. single trace effects [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8].
- **Vertical Resolution/Sampling Rate:** UBI = 10 mm; FMS = ~3 mm [Genter 1997, pp. 3-3].
- **Horizontal Resolution:** UBI = 5 mm; FMS = 7.5 mm [Genter 1997, pp. 3-3].
- **Fracture Thickness (Aperture):** Detection threshold for imaging tools: ~1 mm for discrete fracture identification [Genter 1997, pp. 1-1].
- **Discrimination Distance for Fractures:** Two discrete fractures closer than 5 mm appear as one single trace on images [Genter 1997, pp. 1-1].
- **Orientation Error:** Dip azimuth: ±15° (interpretation and tool combined); Dip magnitude: ±5° [Genter 1997, pp. 3-4].
- **Granite Fracture Classification:** Distinctions made between 'massive granite,' 'altered granite,' and 'altered fractured zones' for comparing detection rates [Genter 1997, 4-5].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Claim:** Borehole imaging tools systematically underestimate fracture density compared to continuous core, with the bias controlled by fracture aperture and spacing. Discrete fractures <1 mm thick are not properly detected, and those closer than 5 mm are registered as a single trace. [Genter 1997, pp. 1-1]
2.  **Claim:** In granitic reservoirs, the underestimation of fractures by acoustic (BHTV) and electrical (FMS/FMI/ARI) imaging logs is most severe in hydrothermally altered, clay-rich fractured zones due to low physical property contrast between filled fractures and the formation. Less than 8% of core-logged discrete fractures are detected in such zones. [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8; Genter 1997, pp. 8-10]
3.  **Claim:** Despite massive underestimation of fracture counts, borehole imaging tools provide a reliable sample of fracture strike orientations that matches core data, making them suitable for determining *in situ* stress-sensitive fracture set orientations. [Genter 1997, pp. 5-7]
4.  **Claim:** The use of uncorrected imaging-log fracture data for spatial analysis alters the modeled statistical distribution. In this study, granite fractures exhibited a power-law (fractal) distribution on core but appeared as an exponential distribution on BHTV log, demonstrating a loss of the small-spacing tail of the distribution. [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8]
5.  **Claim:** Imaging tools are effective for mapping the boundaries of large-scale altered fracture clusters, even when unable to resolve the individual constituent fractures, enabling a zonal rather than discrete characterization of the damage zone. [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8]
6.  **Claim:** Lithology controls the magnitude of image-log fracture bias; this study found significantly better fracture detection by BHTV in Triassic sandstone compared to the underlying granite basement. [Genter 1997, pp. 4-5]

## Open Questions
- What specific correcting factors are derived to filter core data for direct comparison with image logs, and how generalizable are they to other lithologies or stress regimes? (The abstract mentions this but fragments lack the derived factors) [Genter 1997, pp. 1-1; Genter 1997, pp. 7-8].
- How does the non-detection of sub-millimeter and closely spaced fractures affect the estimation of reservoir-scale permeability anisotropy or flow models, beyond spatial statistical changes?
- Is the shift from a power-law spacing distribution to an exponential one a universal filtering effect of borehole images in fractured hard rocks, or specific to the alteration and infill conditions at Soultz [Genter 1997, pp. 7-8]?
