# Timing, Scale and Mechanism of the Destruction of the North China Craton.

## One-line Summary

The North China Craton (NCC) was destroyed primarily in its eastern part during the Mesozoic, peaking at ~125 Ma, driven by unsteady mantle flow triggered by the subduction of the Pacific Plate under a globally hot Cretaceous mantle [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2, 6-7].

## Research Question

What are the timing, geographical scale, and deep dynamic mechanisms controlling the destruction of the North China Craton, and how does this destruction compare to lithospheric thinning observed in other global cratons? [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data

**Study Area**: The North China Craton, with a specific focus on the contrast between the eastern NCC, the central and western NCC, and the boundary zone near the Taihang Mountains and Tanlu Fault Zone [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4, 4-5].

**Data Sources**:
- Sedimentary records, including shallow-sea clastic and carbonate rocks deposited from the Early Cambrian onwards [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2].
- Magmatic activities, such as Paleozoic diamondiferous kimberlites (Mengyin and Fuxian) and Mesozoic-Cenozoic basalts with entrained mantle xenoliths [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2, 2-4].
- Structural geology, including analyses of metamorphic core complexes (MCCs) and extensional structures [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- Large-scale temporary seismic array data enabling the construction of crustal and lithospheric structural images, including the mantle transition zone discontinuity structure [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4, 4-5].
- Paleomagnetic data indicating regional block rotation, e.g., east Liaoning-Korean Peninsula rotation [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- Global comparative data on other cratons (e.g., India, South Africa, Siberia) [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].

## Methods

This paper is a review or progress report synthesizing results from a major interdisciplinary research project. Methods mentioned include:
- **Interdisciplinary Research**: Combining approaches from earth sciences, mathematics, physics, and information sciences [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2].
- **Seismic Imaging**: Using data from dense seismic arrays to image crustal thickness, lithospheric thickness, and mantle transition zone discontinuities (e.g., the 660-km discontinuity) [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4, 4-5].
- **Geochemical and Petrological Analysis**: Studies of mantle xenoliths and igneous rocks to infer past lithospheric mantle thickness and properties [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- **Tectonic Analysis**: Studying metamorphic core complexes (MCCs) and deformation patterns to understand extensional tectonics [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- **Paleomagnetic Analysis**: Determining regional block rotation history [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- **Global Comparison**: Comparing the NCC’s evolution with other cratons to distinguish common processes like lithospheric thinning from unique destruction [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].

## Key Findings

- **Overall Pattern**: The destruction of the NCC occurred mainly in the eastern part, whereas the western NCC was only locally modified and retained its stability. The central NCC represents a transitional zone [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
- **Timing of Destruction**: The destruction took place in the Mesozoic, with a peak age of approximately 125 Ma (Early Cretaceous). This followed a long period of stability after cratonization at ~1.8 Ga [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2]. Evidence for an earlier, thick lithosphere (~200 km) exists from early Paleozoic diamondiferous kimberlites [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2].
- **Geographical Scale of Destruction**: The eastern NCC is characterized by a thin crust (<35 km) and thin lithosphere (60–100 km), with lithospheric thickness increasing from southeast to northwest. In contrast, the central and western NCC have a thicker crust (40–60 km) [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- **Mantle Structure Contrast**: The mantle transition zone is thicker (>250 km) beneath the eastern NCC and normal-to-thin (≤250 km) beneath the central and western NCC. This contrast coincides with the boundary of craton destruction and is consistent with the presence of a cold, stagnant Pacific slab beneath the eastern NCC [Zhu 2011, pp. 4-5].
- **Primary Mechanism**: The destruction of the eastern NCC was caused by unsteady mantle flow, destabilization of mantle convection, and subsequent delamination and/or thermal-mechanical-chemical erosion, all triggered by the subduction of the Pacific Plate [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2, 6-7].
- **Global Context**: Lithospheric thinning is common among global cratons and is often linked to mantle plumes (e.g., India Craton and the Reunion plume), but cratonic *destruction* is rare. Mantle plumes are found to trigger thinning, but not the wholesale destruction of a craton’s stability [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].
- **Role of Global Tectonics**: The NCC destruction was coincident with abnormally high global mantle temperatures and active plate tectonics during the Cretaceous, which included the uprise of a mid-Pacific superplume and the breakup of Gondwana. These global conditions likely accelerated and changed the direction of Pacific subduction, intensifying its destructive impact [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7].

## Limitations

- **Spatial Data Gaps**: The deduction of lithospheric nature is limited in basins and vegetation-covered areas due to the spatially limited distribution of mantle xenoliths and igneous rocks [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- **Review Scope**: This is a “PROGRESS” report, not a primary research article. It synthesizes existing data rather than presenting new, individual experiments. Specific methodological details of source studies are not included.
- **Incomplete Mechanism**: The text notes that while unsteady mantle flow from Pacific subduction is identified as the main cause, further understanding of the specific processes and the quantification of their relative roles is needed. "However, what process..." is an incomplete statement in the provided fragment [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7].
- **Global Comparison Data**: The knowledge of the Antarctica Craton is explicitly stated to be limited due to the scarcity of data [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].

## Assumptions / Conditions

- **Craton Stability Baseline**: The craton is assumed to have been stable from cratonization at ~1.8 Ga until the Mesozoic, with the exception of localized, non-destructive modification [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2]. Conditions for this interpretation rest on accepting that events like the formation of Mesoproterozoic troughs and minor anorogenic magmatism did not destroy the overall cratonic stability.
- **Lithospheric Thickness Proxy**: The thickness of the lithospheric mantle at the time of kimberlite emplacement (ca. 480 Ma) is directly inferred from petrological studies of the kimberlites and their xenoliths, assuming they sample a representative portion of the mantle [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2].
- **Seismic Interpretation**: The interpretation that a thick mantle transition zone represents a cold, stagnant slab relies on combining seismic imaging results with mineral physics data [Zhu 2011, pp. 4-5].
- **Global Tectonic Coupling**: The model assumes a direct causal link between global tectonic events (mid-Pacific superplume uprise, Gondwana breakup, high mantle temperatures) and the intensified subduction of the Pacific Plate that drove the NCC destruction [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Crustal Thickness (km)**: Used as a primary indicator of destruction state. Eastern NCC: <35 km; Central/Western NCC: 40-60 km [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- **Lithospheric Thickness (km)**: Another key indicator. Eastern NCC: 60-100 km; Pre-destruction Paleozoic value: ~200 km [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2, 2-4].
- **Mantle Transition Zone Thickness (km)**: A proxy for thermal state and the presence of subducted slabs. Eastern NCC: >250 km; Central/Western NCC: ≤250 km [Zhu 2011, pp. 4-5].

- **Cratonization Age**: ~1.8 Ga [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2].

## Reusable Claims

- **[Claim with Condition]**: If a craton’s eastern part shows a thin crust (<35 km), thin lithosphere (60–100 km), and a thickened mantle transition zone (>250 km) containing a stagnant oceanic slab, then craton destruction, as opposed to mere thinning, is likely to have occurred [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4, 4-5]. Evidence comes from seismic imaging in the NCC. The limitation is that this is a correlative, not necessarily universal, signature.
- **[Claim with Condition]**: Under conditions of global high mantle temperature and active plate tectonics (e.g., the Cretaceous), the subduction of an oceanic plate (like the Pacific Plate) can trigger unsteady mantle flow, leading to thermo-mechanical-chemical erosion and/or delamination of the overlying continental lithosphere [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7]. This claim is based on the NCC case study and global comparisons. The mechanism’s predominance over other drivers (e.g., other subduction events) is attributed to the escalated subduction dynamics tied to the global tectonic context.
- **[General Claim]**: Lithospheric thinning, indicated by a decrease in lithospheric thickness from ~200 km to <100 km, does not necessarily imply cratonic destruction. This is evidenced by the India Craton, where thinning was triggered by a mantle plume, but cratonic stability, like the absence of strong intraplate deformation, was preserved [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].
- **[Claim with Condition]**: The spatial extent of craton destruction is constrained by pre-existing tectonic boundaries. The western NCC’s stability was maintained because the cratonic nucleus was efficiently protected by surrounding tectonic belts, despite experiencing localized lithospheric thinning and modification from nearby subduction and collision events [Zhu 2011, pp. 7-8].

## Candidate Concepts

- [[Craton Destruction]]: A process distinct from lithospheric thinning, involving the loss of cratonic stability, extensive magmatism, and intra-crustal deformation [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2].
- [[Lithospheric Thinning]]: A common global process that reduces lithospheric mantle thickness, often triggered by mantle plumes, but does not necessarily lead to the destruction of the craton [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].
- [[Mantle Transition Zone]]: A deep discontinuity zone whose thickness variations can indicate the presence of subducted slabs and mantle thermal anomalies [Zhu 2011, pp. 4-5].
- [[Delamination]]: A mechanism proposed for craton destruction involving the foundering of dense lithospheric material into the mantle [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2].
- [[Thermo-Mechanical-Chemical Erosion]]: A mechanism for destruction involving the erosion of the lithospheric base by hot, chemically reactive mantle flows [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2].
