# Mechanical Test of Granite with Multiple Water–Thermal Cycles.

## One-line Summary
This paper experimentally investigates how an increasing number of water-cooling thermal shock cycles (at 300 °C) degrades the physical and mechanical properties of granite, establishing empirical relationships among strength, wave velocity, and water absorption [Yu 2021, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
How do multiple high-temperature water-cooling cycles (thermal shocks) influence the physical properties (volume, density, water absorption, P‑wave velocity), static mechanical properties (compressive and tensile strength, deformation characteristics), and the interrelationships among these properties in granite? [Yu 2021, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
The rock specimens were machined from granite whose main components are quartz, feldspar, and black mica. The initial average density was 2.63 g/cm³, and specimens with an initial P‑wave velocity of 4.8 ± 0.1 km/s were selected [Yu 2021, pp. 3-4]. The number of samples was 30 (five groups differing in the number of thermal shock cycles; each group contained three compression and three tensile specimens) [Yu 2021, pp. 4-5].

## Methods
- **Experimental Design:** Granite samples were divided into five groups based on the number of high-temperature water-cooling treatment cycles: 1, 4, 8, 12, and 15 cycles [Yu 2021, pp. 4-5].
- **Thermal Treatment:** Specimens were heated in a muffle furnace to 300 °C at ~5 °C/min, held for 3 h, then quenched in 25 °C water with a 6‑h cooling period. After the final cycle, samples were air-dried for >48 h [Yu 2021, pp. 4-5].
- **Physical Property Measurement:** Mass was measured with a 0.01 g balance, volume with a Vernier caliper (0.1 mm accuracy), P‑wave velocity with a nonmetallic ultrasonic detector, and water absorption was calculated as the mass gain after the last cooling [Yu 2021, pp. 4, 6].
- **Mechanical Testing:** Static uniaxial compression tests (specimens φ = 50 mm, H = 100 mm) and static Brazilian splitting tests (φ = 50 mm, H = 50 mm) were conducted under displacement control using a WAW-3300 universal testing machine [Yu 2021, pp. 3-5].
- **Microstructural Observation:** Micrograph observations were performed using a scanning electron microscope (SEM) on samples after different numbers of thermal shocks [Yu 2021, pp. 8-9].

## Key Findings
- **Physical Properties:** With increasing thermal shock cycles, granite exhibits increased volume and water absorption, decreased density, and decreased P‑wave velocity. The water absorption rose from 0.28 % to 0.74 % (compression group) and from 0.47 % to 0.62 % (tensile group) after 15 cycles [Yu 2021, pp. 5-8].
- **Strength Degradation:** Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) decreased from 80.35 MPa (1 cycle) to approximately 42.67 MPa (15 cycles), a reduction of ~46.9 %, following a linear relationship `σc = −2.74N + 83.16` (R² = 0.974). Tensile strength decreased from 6.03 MPa to 3.09 MPa after 15 cycles [Yu 2021, pp. 11-16].
- **Deformability:** The elastic modulus and deformation modulus decreased with more thermal shocks, while plasticity increased. The compaction stage lengthened and the elastic deformation stage shortened with increasing cycle number [Yu 2021, pp. 12-13].
- **Microstructural Damage:** Thermal shock generates transgranular cracks due to differential mineral contraction and increases pore/crack density, loosening the texture [Yu 2021, pp. 5-6, 10-12].
- **Correlations:** UCS and P‑wave velocity show a linear relationship. UCS and tensile strength are linearly correlated (`σc = 12.41σt + 4.62`, R² = 0.989). Water absorption exhibits an exponential relationship with UCS and P‑wave velocity [Yu 2021, pp. 16-18].

## Limitations
- The study uses only one heating temperature (300 °C) and cooling water temperature (25 °C); effects of other temperature regimes are not explored [Yu 2021, pp. 1-5].
- The maximum number of cycles tested is 15; long‑term thermal shock behavior beyond this is unknown [Yu 2021, pp. 2-4].
- The sample size per group is small (n=3 for compression, n=3 for tensile) [Yu 2021, pp. 4-5].
- Natural cooling or other cooling methods were not compared; the influence of cooling rate was not isolated [Yu 2021, pp. 1-3].
- Microstructural analysis (SEM) is qualitative; quantitative mineralogy and pore‑network characterization from indexing fragment is limited [Yu 2021, pp. 8-9].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Thermal Shock Cycle:** Defined as heating to 300 °C at ~5 °C/min, holding for 3 h, and quenching in 25 °C water with a 6 h cooling period [Yu 2021, pp. 4-5].
- **Material Homogeneity:** Specimens were pre‑screened for consistent initial P‑wave velocity (4.8 ± 0.1 km/s), implying relative homogeneity before treatment [Yu 2021, pp. 3-4].
- **Damage Characterization:** Physical and mechanical degradation is attributed to combined effects of high‑temperature vapor‑escape porosity increase and thermal‑stress‑induced `transgranular cracking` during rapid water cooling [Yu 2021, pp. 5-6, 11-12].
- **Empirical Fits:** Linear, polynomial, and exponential regressions (e.g., relations among UCS, tensile strength, P‑wave velocity, water absorption) are statistical fits valid for the tested 1–15 cycle range; extrapolation validity is not established [Yu 2021, pp. 11-18].
- **Hoek–Brown Criterion Application:** The empirical constants `m` and `s` are assumed to capture the effect of thermal‑shock damage on rock strength; calculation methods for `m` and `s` are non‑complete in indexing fragment [Yu 2021, pp. 18-19].

## Reusable Claims
1.  For granite subjected to 300 °C heating and 25 °C water quenching, the unconfined compressive strength decreases linearly with the number of thermal shock cycles (N) following `σc = −2.74N + 83.16` (R² = 0.974), based on data for 1 ≤ N ≤ 15. The calibration is specific to this rock type and temperature condition [Yu 2021, pp. 11-12].
2.  The tensile strength of granite after thermal shock is linearly correlated with its compressive strength via `σc = 12.41σt + 4.62` (R² = 0.989). This relation holds for 1 to 15 thermal shock cycles at 300 °C and can be used to estimate one strength from the other for similarly damaged granite [Yu 2021, pp. 17-18].
3.  P‑wave velocity shows a strong linear correlation with UCS (`σc = 36.55VP − 73.94`, R² = 0.975) and can serve as a non‑destructive proxy for mechanical damage after water‑thermal cycling in this granite [Yu 2021, pp. 16-17].
4.  Water absorption (ωa) of granite increases with cycles of thermal shock; its relationship with UCS and P‑wave velocity can be expressed as exponential decay functions (`σc = 119.36e⁻¹²⁸·²⁶ωa`, `VP = 4.33e⁻⁴⁶ωa`, R² > 0.92), providing a method to estimate strength loss from easily measured water intake [Yu 2021, pp. 17-18].
5.  After sufficient thermal shock cycles (≥8 cycles in this study), granite exhibits a distinct progressive rupture stage in uniaxial compression, characterized by the appearance of closed, parallel microcracks (20–100 μm wide) that stem from axial tensile stress during loading, rather than from pre‑existing thermal shrinkage cracks [Yu 2021, pp. 9-11].
