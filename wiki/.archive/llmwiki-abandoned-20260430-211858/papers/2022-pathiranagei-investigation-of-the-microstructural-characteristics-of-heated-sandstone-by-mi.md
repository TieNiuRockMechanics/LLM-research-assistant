# Investigation of the Microstructural Characteristics of Heated Sandstone by Micro‑Computed Tomography Technique.

## One-line Summary
This study characterizes the microstructural and mechanical changes in a sandstone from Queensland, Australia, after heating from room temperature to 800 °C, finding that significant porosity increase and mineral alterations occur predominantly above 400 °C [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
What are the microstructural (pore structure, micro-crack propagation, mineral composition) and associated mechanical property changes (strength) of sandstone when subjected to high-temperature treatments up to 800 °C, and how do these changes relate to specific thermal events such as mineral phase transitions and decomposition [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 2-4]?

## Study Area / Data
The tested sandstone sample was obtained from the Nerang area of the Gold Coast, Queensland, Australia, and belongs to the Neranleigh-Fernvale Beds, formed in the Carboniferous period [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 4-5].

## Methods
- **µXCT (Micro X-ray Computed Tomography):** Used to quantify and visualize pore structure (including total, open, and closed porosity), crack distribution, and 3D imaging of sandstone at different heating stages non-destructively [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-5]. Scanning was performed with a SkyScan 1272 scanner at 100 kV and 100 µA [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 5-6].
- **XRD (X-ray Diffraction):** Used to identify mineralogical changes of powdered samples after each heat treatment using a Philips X’Pert multipurpose diffractometer [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-5].
- **SEM (Scanning Electron Microscopy):** Used to identify damage and fracture properties at high magnification [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 2-4].
- **TG and DTG (Thermogravimetry and Derivative Thermogravimetry):** Performed on powdered samples to recognize mass loss and chemical processes with temperature using a Netzsch STA 449F3 Jupiter analyzer at a 5 °C/min heating rate in an air atmosphere [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 4-5].
- **PLI (Point Load Index):** Used to study mechanical alteration (strength variation) with temperature. Tests were performed perpendicular to the bedding plane [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-6].

## Key Findings
- The main microstructural changes occurred when the sandstone’s temperature was greater than 400 °C [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-2].
- Total porosity measured by µXCT increased by more than 70% at 800 °C compared to room temperature [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-2].
- As temperature increases, open porosity increases while closed porosity decreases [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-2]. Specifically, for one sample, open porosity increased from 11.8% at 25 °C to 17.7% at 800 °C, while closed porosity decreased from 1.2% to 0.6% over the same range [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 9-11].
- Noticeable changes in rock mineralogy were identified at temperatures exceeding 400 °C, including the disappearance of kaolinite peaks due to dehydroxylation, the phase transition of quartz from trigonal α-phase to hexagonal β-phase at ~573 °C, and the decomposition of feldspar [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-6].
- TG and DTG analyses and point load index were in good agreement concerning these changes. The DTG curve shows an endothermic peak at 486 °C indicative of kaolinite dehydroxylation [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-6].
- The average point load index decreases with increasing temperature, with a slight decrease up to 400 °C, an almost 10% reduction up to 600 °C, and a relatively higher reduction of 37% up to 800 °C [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 9-11].
- A linear correlation was identified between the point load index (PLI) and porosity (n) described by the formula: `PLI = -0.0664n + 1.9379 (R² = 0.9973)` [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 11-13].

## Limitations
- The study presents porosity data for one sample (Sample 1) as an example of detailed open/closed pore changes, and the result for Sample 2 at 800 °C was unavailable [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 9-11].
- The influence of clay content on strength enhancement with temperature has not been comprehensively studied and remains a knowledge gap [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 2-4].
- The tested material is a single sandstone type from a specific geological formation (Neranleigh-Fernvale Beds); generalization to other sandstones with different compositions requires further validation [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 4-5].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- All rock specimens were tested under dry conditions [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 4-5].
- The heating rate for TG/DTG was 5 °C/min in an air atmosphere; thermal damage effects observed in the core experiments are assumed to relate to the specific heating profile used for sample preparation, which is not fully detailed in the provided fragments but implies steady-state conditions [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 4-5].
- The point load tests were assumed to be performed perpendicular to the bedding plane [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 5-6].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Temperature:** Range from room temperature (~25 °C) to 800 °C, with key observation thresholds at 400 °C, 600 °C, and 800 °C [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-2].
- **Porosity (Total, Open, Closed):** Measured by µXCT; key values include total porosity >70% increase at 800 °C, and for Sample 1, open porosity rose from 11.8% to 17.7% from 25 °C to 800 °C [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-11].
- **Point Load Index (PLI):** Indicator of rock strength; final value at 800 °C showed a 37% reduction [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 9-11].
- **Mineral Composition:** Quantified as 51% quartz, 40% feldspar, 7% kaolinite, 2% illite [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 11-13].
- **Thermal Events:** Kaolinite dehydroxylation (endothermic peak at 486 °C), quartz α-β phase transition (~573 °C), feldspar decomposition (500 °C-760 °C) [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 5-9].

## Reusable Claims
- For the studied sandstone, total porosity measured by µXCT increases by more than 70% after heating to 800 °C relative to its room temperature state. This is conditional on heating the rock under experimental conditions and may vary by rock type and heating method. [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-2]
- Heating sandstone to temperatures above 400 °C induces substantial microstructural changes, including a conversion of closed pores to open pores and the formation of new intergranular and transgranular cracks, driven by mineral decomposition and phase transitions. This is evidenced by µXCT, SEM, and XRD analyses. [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 1-11]
- A strong linear relationship exists between the point load index (PLI) and total porosity (n) in this thermally damaged sandstone, defined by `PLI = -0.0664n + 1.9379` (R² = 0.9973). This empirical correlation is derived from tests on a specific sandstone type (51% quartz, 40% feldspar) and is conditional on the point load test being performed perpendicular to bedding in dry conditions. [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 11-13]

## Connections To Other Work
- The study is built upon previous work showing that rock engineering properties are governed by microstructural properties like porosity and micro-cracks [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 2-2].
- The findings on microstructural responses of thermally treated sandstones are compared to similar work by Mahanta et al. (2020b), who found that these responses depend on the availability of clay minerals [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 2-2].
- The observed reduction in PLI with temperature, indicating a softening pattern, aligns with findings by other researchers such as Kong et al. (2016), Wu et al. (2005), Ersoy et al. (2007), and Tang et al. (2019) [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 9-11].
- The influence of clay content on strength enhancement with temperature is a topic of ongoing study, with contrasting observations reported by Ersoy et al. (2021) and Pathiranagei et al. (2021) [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 2-4].

## Open Questions
- What is the comprehensive influence of varying clay content on rock strength enhancement with temperature? [Pathiranagei 2022, pp. 2-4]
- How do these findings on a single sandstone type (Neranleigh-Fernvale Beds) translate to sandstones with significantly different mineralogical compositions?
- What is the quantitative relationship between the formation of specific crack types (intergranular vs. transgranular) and the measured decrease in strength?
