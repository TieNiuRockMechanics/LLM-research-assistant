# Acoustic Emission and Damage Characteristics of Granite Subjected to High Temperature.

## One-line Summary
Xu & Zhang [2018] conducted uniaxial compression tests with simultaneous acoustic emission (AE) monitoring on granite at temperatures from 25°C to 1000°C, establishing a thermomechanical total damage model by integrating thermal damage (from elastic modulus) and mechanical damage (from cumulative AE ring counts).

## Research Question
How do high-temperature treatment and mechanical loading jointly govern the acoustic emission characteristics, failure mode transitions, and coupled thermomechanical damage evolution of granite? [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
- **Rock type:** Granite sourced from Shandong Province, China, mainly composed of feldspar, quartz, and mica (based on X-ray diffraction analysis). [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Sample geometry:** Cylindrical specimens, 25 mm diameter, height-to-diameter ratio of 2, prepared following ISRM standards due to furnace volume limitations. [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Average density at room temperature:** 2.612 g/cm³. [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]

## Methods
- **Experimental Design:** Uniaxial compression tests were performed on granite specimens pre-heated to five target temperature levels (200°C, 400°C, 600°C, 800°C, 1000°C) and a room-temperature baseline (25°C), with three samples per temperature point. [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Heating protocol:** Samples heated at 50°C/min in an MTS652.02 high-temperature furnace, with the target temperature held for 20 minutes to ensure uniform heating. [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Loading protocol:** Uniaxial compression using an MTS810 material testing system under displacement control at a loading rate of 0.005 mm/s at the target high temperatures. [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **AE monitoring:** AE signals were recorded with a PCI-2 all-digital AE system (Physical Acoustics Corp.) using a NANO-30 sensor (operating frequency 100–400 kHz). AE threshold was set to 45 dB, preamplifier gain to 40 dB, and acquisition rate to 1 MHz (1024 samples per event). [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Damage modeling:** 
  - Thermal damage \\(D_T\\) defined by elastic modulus degradation: \\(D_T = 1 - E_T/E_0\\). [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]
  - Mechanical damage \\(D_M\\) derived from cumulative AE ring counts using a Weibull statistical distribution. [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]
  - Total damage \\(D\\) calculated under thermomechanical coupling using Lemaitre's strain equivalence principle: \\(D = D_T + D_M - D_T D_M\\). [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]

## Key Findings
1.  **Stress–strain behavior:** The stress–strain curve of granite at high temperatures can be divided into four stages: compaction, elasticity, yield, and failure. Increasing temperature weakens rock brittleness and enhances plasticity. [Xu 2018, pp. 10-11]
2.  **Peak strength and strain:** The average peak stress decreases with increasing temperature, with a marked drop after 400°C. The peak strain increases with temperature, rising sharply above 800°C. At 1000°C, peak stress is 24.82 MPa (21.51% of the 800°C value), and peak strain reaches 1.99% (an increase of 54.11% compared to 800°C). Quartz phase transition from β- to β-squamous quartz at 870°C is cited as a influencing factor. [Xu 2018, pp. 3-4]
3.  **AE ring count rate pattern:** The AE ring count rate exhibits the law of “interval burst” and “relatively calm.” This phenomenon occurs throughout the compression test and can be explained by energy accumulation and release cycles. [Xu 2018, pp. 10-11]
4.  **Failure mode evolution:** With temperature increase from 25°C to 1000°C, failure modes change sequentially: single splitting failure → multi-splitting failure → incomplete shear failure → ideal shear failure (at 600°C) → double shear failure (at 800°C) → complete integral failure (at 1000°C). [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2; pp. 4-6]
5.  **Thermal damage progression:** Thermal damage defined by elastic modulus \\(D_T\\) shows a logistic increase with temperature. [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2]
6.  **Mechanical damage stages:** Mechanical damage \\(D_M\\) derived from AE ring count rate can be divided into four stages: initial stage (microcrack closure), stable development stage (elastic phase), accelerated development stage (crack coalescence), and destructive stage (post-peak). [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2; pp. 6-8]
7.  **Total damage evolution:** Total damage \\(D\\) increases with strain and is consistent with the stress–strain curve at various temperatures; its initial value at zero strain equals the thermal damage \\(D_T\\). [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]

## Limitations
- The number of samples was limited to three per temperature point, which may constrain the statistical robustness of failure mode generalization. [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]
- The AE threshold was set to 45 dB to reduce ambient noise, which may filter out very low-energy AE events and affect the completeness of the AE dataset used to derive damage variables. [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]
- The study uses a single rock type (granite from Shandong). The generalizability of the thermomechanical damage model to other lithologies (e.g., sedimentary, metamorphic rocks) is unconfirmed from the provided fragments. [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3]
- Long-term high-temperature creep or cyclic thermal loading effects were not investigated; tests were performed under monotonic uniaxial compression at sustained high temperature.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The Weibull statistical distribution is assumed appropriate to describe the probability of rock microscopic element damage and the statistical nature of AE activity during loading. [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]
- Thermal damage \\(D_T\\) is zero at the reference temperature of 25°C. [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]
- The total damage model rests on Lemaitre's strain equivalence principle, which assumes that the strain behavior of a damaged material can be represented by the constitutive law of the undamaged material with effective stress. [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]
- The accumulated AE ring count is assumed to correspond to the slip of dislocations, the initiation of microscopic cracks, and the expansion of macroscopic cracks in rock materials. [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **T:** Temperature (°C), ranging from 25 to 1000.
- **D_T:** Thermal damage, defined as \\(D_T = 1 - E_T/E_0\\). [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]
- **D_M:** Mechanical damage, derived from accumulated AE ring counts (\\(\Omega, \Omega_m\\)) and Weibull distribution parameters (\\(m, \alpha\\)). [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]
- **D:** Total thermomechanical coupling damage, \\(D = D_T + D_M - D_T D_M\\). [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]
- **\\(\Omega_m\\):** Accumulated AE ring count at complete rock specimen damage. [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]
- **E_0, E_T:** Elastic modulus of granite at 25°C and at temperature T, respectively. [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]
- **AE ring count rate:** Number of AE ring counts per unit time. [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2]
- **Peak stress (\\(\sigma_c\\))** and **Peak strain (\\(\varepsilon_c\\))**: Key mechanical parameters derived from stress–strain curves. [Xu 2018, pp. 3-4]
- **Weibull parameters (m, α):** Shape and scale parameters for the statistical distribution of microintensity. [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Claim:** In granite under uniaxial loading across 25°C to 1000°C, AE ring count rate is non-monotonic and exhibits an “interval burst” and “relatively calm” pattern, driven by cyclic energy accumulation and pressure-relief states. [Xu 2018, pp. 10-11] *Conditions:* Continuous AE monitoring during monotonic uniaxial compression. *Limitation:* Pattern observed for granite; the relative calm is not an absolute zero-activity state.
2.  **Claim:** Total thermomechanical damage \\(D\\) in rock can be defined by coupling thermal damage (from elastic modulus degradation) and mechanical damage (from Weibull-distributed AE counts) via \\(D = D_T + D_M - D_T D_M\\). [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10] *Conditions:* Requires baseline elastic modulus at room temperature and continuous AE data during loading; application assumes validity of Lemaitre's strain equivalence principle and Weibull microcrack distribution. *Limitation:* Verified only for granite under uniaxial compression up to 1000°C.
3.  **Claim:** The failure mode of granite transitions from splitting-dominated to shear-dominated and finally to a completely integral failure type as temperature increases from 25°C to 1000°C. Ideal shear failure is observed around 600°C. [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2; pp. 4-6] *Conditions:* Quasi-static uniaxial compression (displacement control at 0.005 mm/s) on 25 mm diameter granite specimens after 20 min of uniform heating.
4.  **Claim:** Mechanical damage evolution derived from AE data can be segmented into four stages—initial, stable, accelerated, and destructive—that correspond respectively to the compaction, elastic, yield, and post-peak phases of the macroscopic stress–strain curve. [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2; pp. 6-8] *Conditions:* Uniaxial compressive loading. *Evidence:* D_M increases slowly in the initial stage, steadily in the stable stage, dramatically in the accelerated stage, and gently in the destructive stage until reaching 1.
