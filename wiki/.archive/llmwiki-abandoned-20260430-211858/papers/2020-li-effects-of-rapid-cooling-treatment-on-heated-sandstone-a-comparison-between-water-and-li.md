# Effects of Rapid Cooling Treatment on Heated Sandstone: A Comparison between Water and Liquid Nitrogen Cooling.

## One-line Summary

This study compares the physical and mechanical degradation of heated sandstone subjected to water versus liquid nitrogen (LN2) rapid cooling, finding LN2 causes significantly greater damage, as evidenced by reductions in P-wave velocity, strength, and fracture toughness [Li 2020, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question

What are the differential effects of water cooling versus liquid nitrogen (LN2) cooling on the physical properties, mechanical properties, and microscopic structure of heated sandstone? [Li 2020, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data

The rock material is fine-grained, homogeneous sandstone sourced from the floor strata of a coal seam at 479 m depth in the Xuzhou area, Jiangsu, China [Li 2020, pp. 2-3]. The sample's natural density was 2.39 g/cm³, and moisture content was 0.63% [Li 2020, pp. 2-3]. Mineral composition was 23.1% quartz, 63.5% feldspar, 8.1% kaolinite, and 4.9% microcline [Li 2020, pp. 2-3].

## Methods

The study employed a suite of laboratory tests on sandstone specimens after heating to target temperatures and rapid cooling by either water or LN2. Specimens were cored (diameter 50 mm) and cut to lengths of 100 mm for uniaxial compression tests and 30 mm for Brazilian tests. Notched semicircular bending (NSCB) specimens were prepared for fracture toughness measurement [Li 2020, pp. 3-5]. Testing methods included P-wave velocity measurement using a metallic ultrasonic detector (10 kHz), uniaxial compression tests, Brazilian tests for tensile strength, and NSCB tests for fracture toughness [Li 2020, pp. 3-5, 7-9]. Microscopic morphology was examined using a Quanta 250 scanning electron microscope (SEM) [Li 2020, pp. 3-5].

## Key Findings

*   **P-wave velocity**: P-wave velocity decreased continuously with increasing heating temperature for both cooling methods. LN2 cooling caused a larger reduction than water cooling; at 600 °C, the reduction was 75.6% for LN2 versus 63.8% for water cooling [Li 2020, pp. 3-5].
*   **Mechanical Properties**: Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS), Young's modulus, tensile strength, and fracture toughness all showed a continuous decrease with increasing temperature. The degradation was more severe under LN2 cooling than water cooling [Li 2020, pp. 1-2].
*   **Ductility and Failure Mode**: The ductility of thermally treated sandstone increased with heating temperature, as evidenced by stress–strain curves. A transition from shear-dominant fracturing to "complex fracturing" failure modes occurred with increasing temperature, indicating increased thermal damage [Li 2020, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
*   **Microscopic Damage**: SEM observations revealed that increased heating temperature and cooling rate weakened particle cementation and promoted micro-crack generation and propagation. Inter-granular cracks caused a reduction in tensile strength and fracture toughness [Li 2020, pp. 1-2].
*   **Damage Mechanism**: Thermal shock-induced tensile stress during rapid cooling was identified as the primary cause of crack growth. The exterior of the sample experiences tensile stress while the interior experiences compressive stress [Li 2020, pp. 9-11, 11-13].
*   **Correlations with P-wave velocity**: UCS and Young's modulus showed a linear relationship with P-wave velocity, while tensile strength and fracture toughness showed a quadratic relationship. Fitting equations with high R² values (0.94-0.95) were established [Li 2020, pp. 9-11].

## Limitations

*   The study was conducted on a single rock type (fine-grained sandstone from Xuzhou). The generalizability of the findings to other lithologies is not confirmed from the index fragments.
*   Only the LN2 and water cooling methods were compared; other cooling fluids or rates were not systematically evaluated [Li 2020, pp. 2-2].
*   The quantitative analysis of thermal stress distribution during cooling relies on a conceptual model (Fig. 11) rather than direct measurement of internal stress [Li 2020, pp. 11-13].

## Assumptions / Conditions

*   Specimen preparation followed ISRM recommendations to minimize initial moisture loss and ensure uniformity [Li 2020, pp. 2-3].
*   The P-wave velocity is a reliable nondestructive proxy for micro-crack density and damage, operating on the principle that sound travels faster in solids than in liquids or gases [Li 2020, pp. 3-5].
*   The thermal shock model assumes a temperature gradient where the sample's exterior is in tension and the interior is in compression upon rapid cooling [Li 2020, pp. 11-13].
*   All mechanical tests (UCS, Brazilian, NSCB) are assumed to be performed under quasi-static loading conditions.

## Reusable Claims

*   Claim: Rapid cooling of heated sandstone with liquid nitrogen (LN2) leads to a significantly greater reduction in physical and mechanical properties (P-wave velocity, UCS, Young's modulus, tensile strength, fracture toughness) than cooling with water, particularly at temperatures above 400 °C. This is attributed to a larger thermal shock and higher cooling rate [Li 2020, pp. 1-2, 3-5].
*   Claim: The uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and Young's modulus of thermal-damaged sandstone exhibit a linear correlation with P-wave velocity, while tensile strength and fracture toughness exhibit a quadratic correlation. These relationships allow for nondestructive estimation of mechanical properties based on P-wave velocity measurements [Li 2020, pp. 9-11].
*   Claim: The mechanism of thermal damage is driven by inter-granular crack formation due to thermal shock-induced tensile stress on the sample's exterior, which weakens particle cementation and reduces the surface energy of the rock [Li 2020, pp. 1-2, 11-13].
*   Claim: The failure mode of sandstone transitions from brittle shear-dominant fracturing at low temperatures (≤200 °C) to complex fracturing patterns at higher temperatures (≥400 °C), indicating a transition towards increased ductility and more distributed damage [Li 2020, pp. 5-7].

## Connections To Other Work

*   The paper builds on studies of thermal degradation of rock properties by methods like water cooling and air cooling, referencing work on granite (Shao et al. 2014; Zhang et al. 2014; Siratovich et al. 2015; Yin et al. 2018b) and sandstone (Rathnaweera et al. 2018; Wang et al. 2017; Liu and Xu 2015; Peng et al. 2015) [Li 2020, pp. 2-2].

*   The study confirms established correlations between P-wave velocity and mechanical properties seen in other rock types, citing works by Jamshidi et al. (2018), Sharma and Singh (2008), Pappalardo (2015), and Lü et al. (2017) [Li 2020, pp. 9-11].

## Open Questions

*   The quantitative relationship between the cooling rate, induced thermal stress, and resulting crack density was not fully parametrized. [Li 2020, pp. 11-13] describes the mechanism but does not offer a predictive model.
*   The long-term durability or subcritical crack growth in sandstone subjected to one-time LN2 cooling shock was not investigated.
*   The effect of cyclic heating and LN2 cooling on fatigue and accumulated damage in sandstone is not addressed.
*   The influence of initial pore structure and fluid saturation (beyond the stated 0.63% moisture content) on LN2 cooling damage is an open area.
