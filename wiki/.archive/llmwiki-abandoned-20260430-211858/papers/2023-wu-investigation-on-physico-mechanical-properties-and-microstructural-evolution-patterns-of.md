# Investigation on Physico-Mechanical Properties and Microstructural Evolution Patterns of Heated Granite after Liquid Nitrogen Cooling.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how LN₂ cooling after heating up to 600°C degrades granite’s physical (density, porosity, thermal conductivity) and mechanical (compressive strength, Brazilian tensile strength, elastic modulus) properties, linking macroscopic changes to CT/NMR-observed microstructural damage and mineral alterations [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
What are the physico-mechanical property changes and microscale pore-fracture evolution patterns in granite subjected to sequential heating (up to 600°C) and liquid nitrogen (LN₂) cooling, and what are the underlying thermal damage mechanisms? [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
- **Rock type:** Granite from a Hot Dry Rock (HDR) reservoir, primarily composed of Oligoclase (39.7%), Microcline (27.0%), K-feldspar (26.6%), Quartz (5.4%), Biotite (0.89%), and Clay (0.41%), tested via XRD [Wu 2023, pp. 4-5].
- **Specimen geometry:** Cylindrical specimens (for triaxial compression and permeability) and disc specimens (for Brazilian tensile strength). Each temperature group contained one cylindrical and one disc specimen, with three tests per property [Wu 2023, pp. 4-5].
- **Temperature groups:** Ambient (25°C), 150, 200, 300, 400, 500, and 600°C [Wu 2023, pp. 4-5].
- The rock has a compact, low-permeability structure typical of HDR reservoirs [Wu 2023, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Thermal treatment:** Heating in a muffle furnace at 5°C/min to target temperatures (150–600°C); holding for 2 hours at target; immersion in LN₂ (−196°C) for 1 hour; slow return to room temperature in an insulated container; drying at 85°C for 24 hours [Wu 2023, pp. 4-5].
- **Physical property tests:**
    - Density measurement [Wu 2023, pp. 6-9].
    - Porosity measurement by saturation method [Wu 2023, pp. 6-9].
    - Thermal conductivity: Measured via transient hot-wire method using an infrared temperature sensor [Wu 2023, pp. 5-6].
- **Mechanical property tests:**
    - Triaxial compression: Conducted on a GCTS servo-controlled system; confining pressure = 20 MPa (applied at 1 MPa/min); axial strain control at 0.001%/min until failure. Axial and lateral strains measured by LVDT (accuracy 0.0001 mm) [Wu 2023, pp. 5-6].
    - Brazilian Tensile Strength (BTS): Disk specimens loaded at 0.005 mm/min until failure; BTS calculated as σt = 2P/πdt [Wu 2023, pp. 5-6].
- **Permeability test:** Transient pulse decay method using helium gas under confining pressures of 2, 4, 5, 10, and 15 MPa with deviatoric stress maintained at 1 MPa [Wu 2023, pp. 5-6].
- **Microscopic characterization:**
    - CT scanning: To reconstruct spatial distribution of micro-defects and calculate volumetric porosity damage ratio and fractal dimensions [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].
    - Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR): To analyze pore size distribution and connectivity evolution [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].
    - Mineral stability analysis: To assess dehydration, phase transitions, decomposition, and bond fractures at high temperatures [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].

## Key Findings
**Physical Properties:**
- **Density:** Decrease follows three temperature phases: almost no effect up to 200°C; gradual reduction from 200–500°C (0.82% reduction at 500°C); rapid decline from 500–600°C (1.56% reduction at 600°C) [Wu 2023, pp. 6-9].
- **Porosity:** Post-treatment, all samples showed porosity increase. The increase rate is positively exponential with temperature: 10.73% increase at 200°C; sharp increase to 81.07% at 500°C; further rapid increase at 600°C [Wu 2023, pp. 6-9].
- **Thermal conductivity:** Decrease rate is slow up to 200°C; accelerates from 200–500°C (16.60% reduction at 500°C); fastest decline at 500–600°C (31.25% reduction at 600°C) [Wu 2023, pp. 9-11].

**Mechanical Properties:**
- **Stress-strain behavior:** Concavity in initial crack closure stage becomes more pronounced with increasing temperature, indicating more thermally-induced microcracks. Volumetric compression increases with treatment temperature [Wu 2023, pp. 9-11].
- **Compressive strength (σ1c) and BTS:** Both deteriorate with treatment temperature. BTS is more sensitive, dropping by 67.5% at 600°C, because maximum thermal stress from rapid cooling is vertical and tensile properties are more sensitive to microstructural damage [Wu 2023, pp. 11-12].
- **Elastic modulus:** Slight decrease (0.99%) below 200°C; further deterioration above 200°C; maximum reduction of 23.32% at 600°C [Wu 2023, pp. 11-12].
- **Poisson’s ratio:** Initially increases with temperature (attributed to brittle-to-ductile transition and softening), then decreases sharply at 600°C. Modeled by an equation [Wu 2023, pp. 11-12] (exact equation not provided in fragments).

**Microstructural Evolution:**
- CT analysis: As temperature rises, the area of micro-defects and volumetric porosity damage ratio increase progressively. Fractal dimensions were introduced to characterize thermal fracture geometry and distribution [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].
- NMR analysis: With temperature increase, micropores develop and interconnect into mesopores and macropores, enhancing pore connectivity. Macropores significantly impact physico-mechanical and permeability properties [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].
- Mineral stability: At high temperatures, minerals undergo dehydration, phase transitions, decomposition, and chemical bond fractures, altering mineralogical composition and strength, thereby affecting macroscopic properties [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].

## Limitations
- Triaxial tests were conducted only at a single confining pressure of 20 MPa [Wu 2023, pp. 5-6].
- The focus is on the rapid cooling effect; the influence of rapid thawing was deliberately excluded by allowing slow return to room temperature [Wu 2023, pp. 4-5].
- The majority of currently exploited geothermal reservoirs are below 200°C; the high-temperature ranges (up to 600°C) studied target future deep HDR resources, which limits immediate field applicability [Wu 2023, pp. 3-4].
- Exact equation for Poisson's ratio variation and quantitative mineral alteration data are not detailed in the provided index fragments.
- The study does not investigate cyclic thermal shock effects.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- A heating rate of 5°C/min is considered slow enough to avoid thermal shock during the heating phase [Wu 2023, pp. 4-5].
- The primary thermal shock damage is attributed to the rapid cooling process from LN₂ immersion, not the slow natural thawing that followed [Wu 2023, pp. 4-5].
- Granite is treated as a continuous, brittle material whose tensile failure is dominated by vertical thermal stress caused by rapid surface cooling [Wu 2023, pp. 11-12].
- Porosity increase is entirely attributed to thermally-induced microcrack initiation, growth, and interconnection [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent variables:** Heating temperature (25, 150, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600°C); cooling medium (LN₂ at −196°C).
- **Dependent physical variables:** Density, porosity, thermal conductivity coefficient.
- **Dependent mechanical variables:** Deviatoric stress, axial/lateral/volumetric strain, compressive strength (σ1c), Brazilian Tensile Strength (BTS), elastic modulus, Poisson’s ratio.
- **Microstructural parameters:** Volumetric porosity damage ratio, fractal dimensions of thermal fractures, pore size distribution (micropore, mesopore, macropore), pore connectivity.
- **Control parameters (triaxial test):** Confining pressure (20 MPa), axial strain loading rate (0.001%/min).
- **Control parameters (BTS):** Loading rate (0.005 mm/min).
- **Control parameters (permeability test):** Confining pressures (2–15 MPa), deviatoric stress (1 MPa).

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim 1:** For granite subjected to rapid LN₂ cooling, the tensile strength (BTS) is more severely degraded than the compressive strength. This is because the maximum transient thermal stress from rapid surface cooling is vertical tensile stress, and tensile rock properties are inherently more sensitive to microstructural damage. Applicable for thermal shock damage assessment in dry brittle rocks [Wu 2023, pp. 11-12].
- **Claim 2:** The degradation of physico-mechanical properties of LN₂-cooled granite follows three temperature phases: an imperceptible change phase up to 200°C; a gradual deterioration phase from 200–500°C; and a pronounced degradation phase at 600°C. This staging is applicable to predicting property changes in similar crystalline rocks under thermal shock, provided water/steam content is negligible [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].
- **Claim 3:** In NMR-based analysis of thermally shocked granite, increased connectivity is driven by micropore development merging into mesopores and macropores. Macropores primarily control the resultant permeability and mechanical weakening. This claim is limited to LN₂ thermal shock conditions and the specific HDR granite mineralogy tested [Wu 2023, pp. 1-2].
- **Claim 4:** The concavity in the initial stress-strain curve of triaxially compressed granite increases with the severity of prior thermal treatment (heating + LN₂ cooling), directly correlating with the closure of a greater density of thermally-induced microcracks. This can serve as an indirect indicator of thermal damage intensity in post-failure analysis [Wu 2023, pp. 9-11].

## Connections To Other Work
- **Context of LN₂ fracturing:** The work builds on McDaniel et al. (1997)’s initial proposal of LN₂ for coalbed methane, and extends it to HDR granite, citing disadvantages of hydraulic fracturing (water depletion, environmental contamination, clay swelling) [Wu 2023, pp. 2-3].
- **Prior thermal damage studies:** References Sha et al. (2020) for identifying 400°C as a threshold for conspicuous physico-mechanical changes in LN₂-cooled granite, and Wu et al. (2018) who observed mesh-like crack patterns from LN₂ jet rock-breaking [Wu 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Triaxial and seepage gap:** The study addresses the “dearth of studies” on triaxial compression characteristics and seepage evolution of LN₂-cooled granite under combined temperature-confining pressure, positioning its contribution in these specific areas [Wu 2023, pp. 3-4].
- **Crack closure interpretation:** Connects the observed concavity in stress-strain curves to the closure of thermally-induced microcracks, aligning with interpretations by Yang et al. (2017) for nonlinear deformation under loading [Wu 2023, pp. 9-11].
- **Thermal stress dominance:** Cites Pai et al. (2021) to support the finding that rapid cooling primarily causes vertical thermal stress on the surface, explaining the greater sensitivity of tensile properties [Wu 2023, pp. 11-12].
