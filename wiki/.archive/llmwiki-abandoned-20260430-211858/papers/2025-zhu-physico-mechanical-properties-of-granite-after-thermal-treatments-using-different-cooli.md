# Physico-mechanical Properties of Granite after Thermal Treatments Using Different Cooling Media.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how cooling heated granite (400 °C) with air, water, or liquid nitrogen alters its physical and mechanical properties, finding that liquid nitrogen cooling most significantly enhances permeability (5.24 times) and micro-fracture complexity [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question
How do different cooling media (air, water, liquid nitrogen) affect the physico‑mechanical properties, thermal damage mechanisms, and fracture evolution in granite from the Gonghe Basin after thermal treatment at 400 °C, a temperature representative of the upper range in enhanced geothermal systems? [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1, 2-3, 3-4]

## Study Area / Data
The granite samples were obtained from the Gonghe Basin, Qinghai, China, a region classified as a fracture‑type geothermal reservoir [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1, 3-4]. The average density of the prepared samples was 2800 kg/m³, and the average P‑wave velocity was 2434 m/s. Two sample geometries were used: cylindrical (diameter 25 mm, height 50 mm) and square (side 10 mm, height 4 mm) [Zhu 2025, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
*   **Thermal Treatment:** Samples were heated to a target temperature of 400 °C in an electric oven at a rate of 5 °C/min to minimize abrupt thermal damage [Zhu 2025, pp. 3-4].
*   **Cooling Methods:** Heated samples were rapidly cooled using three media: air, water, and liquid nitrogen (LN₂) to induce thermal shock [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1, 4-4].
*   **Microstructural Analysis:** Fractures were characterized using Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) on square samples and medical Computed Tomography (CT) on cylindrical samples. 3D fracture models were reconstructed from CT images using Mimics software, and fracture complexity was quantified using the 3D box‑counting fractal dimension [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1, 4-4].
*   **Physical & Mechanical Testing:** Permeability tests were conducted on cylindrical samples across different cooling treatments. Uniaxial compression tests were performed to obtain stress‑strain curves, from which elastic parameters (Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio) and characteristic stresses (crack initiation stress σ₍ci₎, crack damage stress σ₍cd₎, peak stress σ₍p₎) were derived. Acoustic emission (AE) monitoring was used during mechanical testing to assess damage evolution [Zhu 2025, pp. 4-5, 1-1, 6-8].

## Key Findings
*   **Permeability:** All thermal treatments increased permeability. Liquid nitrogen cooling increased permeability by a factor of 5.24 compared to the untreated sample, water‑cooling by 3.24 times, and air‑cooling by 44% [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1, 5-6].
*   **Mechanical Properties:** Liquid nitrogen cooling reduced compressive strength by 13.6% compared to untreated samples. Cooling with water and liquid nitrogen also reduced P‑wave velocity, strength, and Young's modulus while enhancing brittleness [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1].
*   **Failure Mode:** Thermal treatment caused a shift in failure mode from axial splitting to a combined shear and tension failure [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1].
*   **Microstructure and Fracture Complexity:** SEM and CT revealed that heating caused mineral expansion, partially closing natural fractures. Subsequent thermal shock from cooling reopened and expanded fractures. Liquid nitrogen‑cooled samples exhibited radially distributed fractures and the greatest fracture complexity, with the highest 3D fractal dimension (2.48) compared to untreated (2.43), water‑cooled (2.37), and air‑cooled (2.24) samples [Zhu 2025, pp. 4-5, 6-8].
*   **Acoustic Emission:** AE monitoring showed that liquid nitrogen cooling led to higher cumulative AE energy and a lower maximum AE energy rate, indicating more distributed damage evolution [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1].

## Limitations
*   The uniaxial loading rate (0.1 mm/min) is noted as a potential limitation; the effect of different loading rates was not investigated [Zhu 2025, pp. 4-5].
*   The study focuses on a single heating temperature (400 °C) and granite from one specific location (Gonghe Basin). The generalizability of results to other temperatures and rock types is not confirmed from the provided fragments [Zhu 2025, pp. 3-4, 3-4].
*   The thermal treatment used a heating rate of 5 °C/min, which differs from natural geothermal gradient conditions [Zhu 2025, pp. 3-4].
*   The impact of in‑situ stress conditions present in deep HDR formations was not replicated during cooling or mechanical testing based on the provided fragments [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   **Temperature‑Damage Relationship:** The target temperature of 400 °C is based on the optimal temperature range (160‑400 °C) for heat extraction in commercial EGS/HDR projects [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1, 3-4].
*   **Thermal Shock Dominance:** Rapid cooling from 400 °C is assumed to generate a thermal shock significant enough to represent the effects of different cooling media and cause measurable damage [Zhu 2025, pp. 3-4].
*   **Fractal Dimension Assumption:** The 3D box‑counting fractal dimension is assumed to be an effective quantitative measure of fracture network complexity [Zhu 2025, pp. 4-4].
*   **Characteristic Stress Determination:** Crack initiation stress (σ₍ci₎) and crack damage stress (σ₍cd₎) are identifiable from the stress‑strain curve transitions, assuming a specific sequence of crack‑closure, elastic, stable‑crack‑growth, and unstable‑crack‑growth stages [Zhu 2025, pp. 6-8].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Claim:** Rapid cooling with a cryogenic medium like liquid nitrogen significantly enhances the permeability of thermally treated granite compared to air or water cooling, achieving a factor of >5 increase over untreated rock. **Conditions:** Granite heated to 400 °C. **Evidence:** Permeability increased by a factor of 5.24 for LN₂‑cooled vs. untreated samples [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1, 5-6]. **Limitations:** Measured at effective stress conditions not fully specified in the fragments.
2.  **Claim:** Thermal treatment and subsequent rapid cooling (thermal shock) change the failure mode of granite under uniaxial compression from axial splitting to a combined shear‑tension mode. **Conditions:** Gonghe Basin granite, heated to 400 °C. **Evidence:** Direct statement of failure mode shift [Zhu 2025, pp. 1-1]. **Limitations:** Quantitative metrics for failure mode classification (e.g., crack angle) were not found in the fragments.
3.  **Claim:** Cooling‑induced fracture complexity in thermally treated granite, as measured by the 3D box‑counting fractal dimension, is highest for liquid nitrogen cooling (2.48), followed by water (2.37) and untreated (2.43), while air cooling (2.24) can result in a lower complexity than the initial state due to partial closure of natural fractures during heating. **Conditions:** Gonghe Basin granite, target temperature 400 °C. **Evidence:** Fractal dimension values from CT‑based analysis [Zhu 2025, pp. 5-6]. **Limitations:** Fractal dimension does not discriminate between connected and unconnected fracture porosity; air‑cooled samples showed increased permeability despite lower complexity.
4.  **Claim:** The mechanical distinction between cooling media is evident in the crack closure stage during uniaxial compression; LN₂‑cooled samples exhibit the longest stage, indicating severe thermal damage and extensive micro‑cracking. Air‑cooled samples show a shorter stage due to partial mineral expansion and crack closure from heating. **Conditions:** Loading conditions of uniaxial compression tests. **Evidence:** Stress‑strain curve characteristics described for each cooling medium [Zhu 2025, pp. 6-8]. **Limitations:** Quantitative durations for crack closure stages are not provided in the fragments.
