# Experimental Investigations on the Tensile Behaviour of Granite after Heating and Water-Cooling Treatment.

## One-line Summary
This paper experimentally investigates the changes in tensile strength, elastic modulus, and deformation behaviour of granite subjected to heating and rapid water-cooling thermal shock, identifying a critical temperature threshold (300 °C) where tensile properties degrade significantly compared to compressive properties [Zhang 2021, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
How does heating and water-cooling treatment (HWCT) affect the tensile mechanical behaviour (strength, elastic modulus, stress-strain response) of granite, and how does the thermal shock sensitivity of tensile properties compare to that of compressive properties? [Zhang 2021, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
- **Material:** Granite samples collected from Shandong Province, China [Zhang 2021, pp. 3-5].
- **Sample geometry:** Cylindrical disc samples of 50 mm diameter and 25 mm thickness, prepared following ISRM suggested methods for Brazilian split tests [Zhang 2021, pp. 3-5].
- **Temperature targets:** 25 °C (room temperature), 200 °C, 300 °C, 400 °C, 500 °C, and 600 °C [Zhang 2021, pp. 3-5].
- **Physical properties measured:** Dry density, saturated density, porosity, and P-wave velocity after HWCT [Zhang 2021, pp. 5-8].

## Methods
- **Thermal treatment:** Granite samples were heated at a slow rate of 5 °C/min in a furnace to avoid generating a thermal gradient during the heating phase. The pre-designed temperature was held for 2 hours to achieve thermal stabilization throughout the sample. Rapid cooling was performed by immersing the heated samples in a 25 L water container at 25 °C for 1 hour. Finally, samples were dried in a desiccator at 105 °C for 24 hours [Zhang 2021, pp. 3-5].
- **Mechanical test:** Brazilian split tests were conducted using a high-precision closed-loop servo-controlled system with a 1500 KN axial force capacity. An arc fixture was used to avoid stress concentration at the loading point. Tests were performed under a strain-rate controlled mode at a rate of 0.01 mm/min. Two strain gauges were attached to the centre of each end face of the sample to measure radial tensile strain (ε<sub>3</sub>) and axial compressive strain (ε<sub>1</sub>) [Zhang 2021, pp. 3-5].
- **Calculation formula:** Tensile strength (σ<sub>t</sub>) was calculated using the ISRM (1978) standard formula based on peak force (P), diameter (D), and thickness (B) of the specimen [Zhang 2021, pp. 3-5].
- **Comparative tests:** Uniaxial compression tests were also performed on the same granite subjected to identical HWCT for comparison [Zhang 2021, pp. 8-10].

## Key Findings
- **Porosity and density:** Porosity increases slightly from 25 to 500 °C due to opening of initial microcracks, then shows a marked increase beyond 500 °C, especially near the α- to β-quartz transition at 573 °C [Zhang 2021, pp. 5-8].
- **P-wave velocity:** P-wave velocity decreases continuously with increasing heating temperature, from an average of 5357 m/s at 25 °C to 1000 m/s at 600 °C [Zhang 2021, pp. 5-8].
- **Stress-strain behaviour:** Tensile stress-strain curves are nearly linear for heating temperatures between 25 and 400 °C. A marked nonlinear phase appears when the heating temperature exceeds 500 °C [Zhang 2021, pp. 5-8].
- **Failure strain:** The axial tensile strain at failure increases as the heating temperature increases [Zhang 2021, pp. 1-2].
- **Tensile strength and modulus:** Both tensile strength and tensile elastic modulus exhibit a transition from a slight increase to a significant decrease as heating temperature increases. The tensile strength at 600 °C (2.38 MPa) was about one-fifth of that at room temperature (10.2 MPa) [Zhang 2021, pp. 8-10].
- **Thermal hardening mechanism:** The slight increase in tensile elastic modulus at relatively low temperatures is attributed to thermal hardening caused by the closure of initial cracks due to the thermal expansion of mineral particles [Zhang 2021, pp. 8-10].
- **Thermal shock damage mechanism:** At high temperatures, a strong temperature gradient during rapid cooling generates microcracks, leading to a decrease in tensile elastic modulus and strength [Zhang 2021, pp. 8-10].
- **Comparative tensile vs. compressive properties:** Below 300 °C, temperature has a slight effect on both tensile and compressive strengths. Above 300 °C, tensile properties decrease significantly after a heating temperature threshold of 300 °C, while the threshold for compressive properties is 500 °C. **Thermal shock has a greater effect on tensile strength than on compressive strength** [Zhang 2021, pp. 1-2].
- **Micro-mechanism of differential sensitivity:** During the Brazilian split test, radial tensile stress causes thermal-shock-induced microcracks to rapidly expand and coalesce. Conversely, during uniaxial compression, these microcracks are closed under compressive stress, making compressive properties less sensitive to HWCT [Zhang 2021, pp. 10-10].

## Limitations
- **Post-peak data missing:** The strain gauges were split into two halves at failure, so the post-peak phase of tensile stress-strain curves could not be obtained [Zhang 2021, pp. 5-8].
- **Single rock type:** Results are limited to one granite collected from Shandong Province; the specific influence of grain size and mineral composition is noted as a factor but not quantitatively varied across multiple rock types [Zhang 2021, pp. 1-2].
- **Single cooling protocol:** Only one water-cooling protocol (immersion in 25 °C water for 1 hour) was used; the effects of different cooling rates were not investigated in this work, though cited literature suggests it is important [Zhang 2021, pp. 2-3].
- **Specific test conditions:** The conclusions are valid under the specific heating rate (5 °C/min) and strain rate (0.01 mm/min) used; strain-rate sensitivity is not explored [Zhang 2021, pp. 3-5].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Slow heating assumption:** The heating rate of 5 °C/min was chosen to avoid generating a thermal gradient during the heating phase, ensuring that cracking during heating was induced merely by the temperature effect, not by thermal shock [Zhang 2021, pp. 3-5].
- **Thermal stabilization:** A 2-hour holding time at the target temperature was assumed sufficient to achieve uniform temperature throughout the samples based on sample size and thermal conductivity [Zhang 2021, pp. 3-5].
- **Brazilian test validity:** The validity of the Brazilian split test relies on crack initiation at the centre of the disc under tensile stress; the assumption holds but the presence of thermal-shock-induced microcracks in the centre facilitates rapid fracture propagation [Zhang 2021, pp. 10-10].
- **Thermal hardening mechanism:** The observed slight increase in strength/modulus at low temperatures is attributed to the closure of initial microcracks due to thermal expansion of mineral particles [Zhang 2021, pp. 8-10].
- **Thermal shock cracking mechanism:** The decrease in properties at high temperatures is attributed to microcrack generation from a strong temperature gradient during rapid water cooling [Zhang 2021, pp. 8-10].
- **Micro-mechanical controls:** The tensile behaviour is mainly governed by the cohesion and fracture state of mineral particles, while compressive behaviour is mainly governed by the properties of the interface between mineral particles [Zhang 2021, pp. 10-10].

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim 1:** In granite subjected to HWCT, tensile strength and tensile elastic modulus show a slight increase at lower temperatures (below ~300 °C) followed by a significant decrease at higher temperatures. This is explained by two competing mechanisms: thermal hardening from microcrack closure due to mineral expansion at low temperatures, and thermal-shock-induced microcracking due to large temperature gradients during water cooling at high temperatures. [Zhang 2021, pp. 8-10].
- **Claim 2:** For the granite studied, HWCT causes a marked increase in nonlinearity of the pre-peak tensile stress-strain curve only when the heating temperature exceeds 500 °C; below 400 °C, the curves remain nearly linear [Zhang 2021, pp. 5-8].
- **Claim 3:** The heating temperature threshold at which mechanical properties (strength, modulus) degrade significantly is lower for tensile loading (~300 °C) than for compressive loading (~500 °C) under the same HWCT conditions. This differential sensitivity is caused by tensile stress opening and propagating thermal microcracks, whereas compressive stress closes them. [Zhang 2021, pp. 1-2].
- **Claim 4:** P-wave velocity in granite decreases continuously with increasing HWCT temperature, dropping from ~5357 m/s at 25 °C to ~1000 m/s at 600 °C for this granite. [Zhang 2021, pp. 5-8].
- **Claim 5:** Porosity increases only slightly up to 500 °C, then increases markedly at higher temperatures, partly due to the α- to β-quartz phase transition at 573 °C. [Zhang 2021, pp. 5-8].
- **Claim 6:** The tensile strength at 600 °C HWCT can be reduced to about one-fifth of the room-temperature value (from 10.2 MPa to 2.38 MPa in this study) [Zhang 2021, pp. 8-10].

## Open Questions

- How do variations in cooling rate (e.g., different water temperatures, LN<sub>2</sub> cooling) quantitatively influence the tensile threshold and degradation rate? [Zhang 2021, pp. 2-3] notes the importance of cooling rate from literature but does not test it here.
