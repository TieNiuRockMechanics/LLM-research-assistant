# Study on Fracture Surface Damage and Fluid Flow Characteristics of Hot Dry Rock with Different Failure Modes under Repeated Thermal Shock

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how repeated thermal shock (up to 500 °C) damages the surface morphology and alters the fluid flow characteristics of tension-induced versus shear-induced fractures in granite, finding that tension fractures exhibit greater surface damage and more significant changes in flow behavior after thermal treatment.

## Research Question
How do repeated thermal shocks influence fracture surface damage and subsequent fluid flow characteristics in hot dry rock (HDR) for fractures created under different failure modes (tension vs. shear)?

## Study Area / Data
- **Rock type:** Cylindrical granite specimens (diameter 25 mm, height 50 mm) with mineral composition 47.8% quartz, 40.6% albite, and 11.6% strontium feldspar. [Deng 2023, p. 2; Deng 2023, p. 2-3]
- **Sample preparation:** Single tension fractures were produced via Brazilian splitting tests; single shear fractures were produced via shear tests along the specimen diameter. Loading was performed using a DSZ‑1000 servo‑controlled tri‑axial testing system (Pmax = 1000 kN, shear force max = 200 kN). [Deng 2023, p. 2-3; Deng 2023, p. 3-5]
- **Thermal shock procedure:** Samples heated at 5 °C/min to target temperatures (200, 300, 400, 500 °C), held for 4 h, then cooled immediately in 20 °C water; the cycle was repeated 5 times. [Deng 2023, p. 3-5]
- **Measurement sequence:** 3D laser scanning of fracture surfaces, then fluid flow tests, both before and after the thermal shock protocol. [Deng 2023, p. 3-5]

## Methods
- **Mechanical fracture generation:** [[Brazilian splitting test]] for tension fractures; [[Shear test]] with opposing loads on opposite ends of the cylindrical specimen for shear fractures. [Deng 2023, p. 2-3]
- **3D laser scanning:** A JR‑AF type 3D topography scanner (accuracy ± 0.015 mm, mean point distance 0.06 mm) was used to digitize fracture surfaces. [Deng 2023, p. 3-5]
- **Roughness characterization:** Average roughness \(\text{Ra}\) and root‑mean‑square roughness \(\text{Rrms}\) were calculated from point‑cloud data. [Deng 2023, p. 5-7]
- **Fractal analysis of fracture surface:** The cube‑covering method was applied to compute the fractal dimension \(D\) of the fracture surface from the 3D point cloud at multiple sampling intervals (0.05–1.25 mm). [Deng 2023, p. 7-8]
- **Fractal analysis of fracture void:** The ultra‑thin square plate (UTSP) covering method was used to calculate the fractal dimension of the void space between the upper and lower fracture surfaces. [Deng 2023, p. 7-8]
- **Fluid flow tests:** Permeability was measured using a low‑field [[Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)]] instrument under confining pressures of 5–30 MPa; hydraulic aperture \(e\) was derived from the cubic law, and Reynolds number \(Re\) was computed to discern flow regime (laminar if \(Re < 4\) or \(Re < 10\) depending on the cited criterion). [Deng 2023, p. 3-5; Deng 2023, p. 9-12]

## Key Findings
1. Repeated thermal shock at 500 °C causes the greatest change in fracture morphology; the variation of average roughness (\(\Delta\) Rrms) reaches 0.0772 for tension fractures. [Deng 2023, p. 1-2]
2. Thermal shock above 300 °C produces obvious damage (mineral particle shedding) on fracture surfaces. For shear samples, the maximum average roughness change (\(\Delta\) Rrms = 0.0485) occurs after 300 °C treatment, whereas tension samples show the largest change after 500 °C. [Deng 2023, p. 8-9; Deng 2023, p. 1-2]
3. Before thermal shock, the slope of flow (\(Q\) vs. water pressure) is smaller in tension fractures than in shear fractures, indicating that smooth shear‑fracture surfaces facilitate fluid flow. [Deng 2023, p. 19-19]
4. After thermal shock, Reynolds numbers and hydraulic apertures of tension fractures increase markedly, while shear fractures show little change in flow characteristics. [Deng 2023, p. 9-12; Deng 2023, p. 12-13]
5. The fractal dimension of the fracture surface increases with rising treatment temperature; its change (\(\Delta D\)) correlates with the change in roughness, and the surface fractal dimension of tension fractures after shock remains higher than that of shear fractures. [Deng 2023, p. 15-17]
6. A linear relationship exists between the fractal dimension of the fracture void (UTSP method) and fluid flow rate; tension fractures exhibit a larger flow increase after thermal shock due to mineral‑particle shedding near fluid channels. [Deng 2023, p. 17-19]

## Limitations
- Study uses a single granite lithology; results may not be directly transferable to other rock types. [Deng 2023, p. 2]
- Thermal shock was limited to five cycles; longer‑term cycling effects are not evaluated. [Deng 2023, p. 3-5]
- Only one diameter‑to‑height ratio (25 mm × 50 mm) was tested, which may be subject to size effects. [Deng 2023, p. 2-3]
- The research focused on a single fracture; interactions with multiple fractures or natural fracture networks are not addressed. [Deng 2023, p. 1-2]
- Fluid flow tests were conducted at room temperature, not at elevated in‑situ geothermal temperatures. [Deng 2023, p. 3-5]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Fluid flow through the fracture before thermal shock is assumed to obey Darcy’s law and the cubic law, based on the observed linear relationship between \(Q\) and water pressure. [Deng 2023, p. 12-13]
- The threshold for laminar flow is taken as \(Re < 4\) (Ranjith and Viete 2011) or \(Re < 10\) (Hassanizadeh and Gray 1987), and the study assumes laminar conditions when \(Re\) is below these cut‑offs. [Deng 2023, p. 9-12]
- Thermal shock damage is attributed primarily to the shedding of mineral particles (especially on tension fractures) due to differential thermal expansion/contraction. [Deng 2023, p. 8-9; Deng 2023, p. 13-15]
- The 3D laser scanning was performed on dry fracture surfaces at room temperature, assuming the surface morphology does not change during scanning. [Deng 2023, p. 3-5]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent variable 1:** Fracture mode (tension vs. shear). [Deng 2023, p. 2-3]
- **Independent variable 2:** Thermal shock temperature (200, 300, 400, 500 °C). [Deng 2023, p. 3-5]
- **Key dependent variables (surface):** \(\text{Ra}\), \(\text{Rrms}\), fractal dimension of fracture surface (cube method), fractal dimension of fracture void (UTSP method). [Deng 2023, p. 5-7; Deng 2023, p. 7-8]
- **Key dependent variables (flow):** Hydraulic aperture (\(e\)), volumetric flow rate (\(Q\)), Reynolds number (\(Re\)). [Deng 2023, p. 5-7; Deng 2023, p. 9-12]
- **Test conditions:** Confining pressure (5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30 MPa), heating rate (5 °C/min), holding time (4 h), cooling water temperature (20 °C). [Deng 2023, p. 3-5; Deng 2023, p. 12-13]

## Reusable Claims
- Under repeated thermal shock (up to 500 °C), the surface roughness of tension‑induced granite fractures increases more than that of shear‑induced fractures. Supported by [Deng 2023, pp. 1-2; Deng 2023, pp. 8-9]
- The change in fractal dimension of the fracture surface after thermal shock can serve as a measure of roughness evolution; a greater fractal‑dimension change corresponds to a larger increase in \(\Delta\) Rrms. Supported by [Deng 2023, pp. 15-17; Deng 2023, pp. 19-19]
- The fractal dimension of the fracture void (obtained by the UTSP covering method) exhibits a linear relationship with fluid flow rate, which can be used to assess the impact of thermal shock on permeability. Supported by [Deng 2023, pp. 17-19]
- Thermal shock temperatures exceeding 300 °C cause noticeable mineral‑particle loss from fracture surfaces, significantly altering flow channels in tension fractures but having less effect on smooth shear‑fracture surfaces. Supported by [Deng 2023, pp. 8-9; Deng 2023, pp. 13-15]
- After repeated thermal shock, the influence on fluid flow is more significant in tension fractures than in shear fractures, demonstrating that flow change depends on both thermal damage and the inherent geometry of the fluid channel. Supported by [Deng 2023, pp. 1-2; Deng 2023, pp. 12-13]

## Open Questions
