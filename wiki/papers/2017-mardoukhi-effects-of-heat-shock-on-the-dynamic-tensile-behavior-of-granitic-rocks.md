# Effects of Heat Shock on the Dynamic Tensile Behavior of Granitic Rocks.

## One-line Summary
A new experimental method characterizes surface damage from heat shock on granite Brazilian disk samples using fractal dimension analysis, showing that increased surface crack complexity correlates with decreased dynamic tensile strength.

## Research Question
How does a localized heat shock affect the surface crack patterns and dynamic tensile strength of granitic rocks?

## Study Area / Data
The study used two types of granite: Balmoral Red and Kuru Grey. Their mineral compositions are provided [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 2-3]. The mean open porosity was 0.38% for Balmoral Red and 0.44% for Kuru Grey. The quasi-static tensile strength was 8 ± 2 MPa for Balmoral Red and 11 ± 2 MPa for Kuru Grey [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 2-3]. Brazilian disk (BD) samples were prepared with specific diameters and thicknesses for each rock type [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-3].

## Methods
- **Heat Shock Application:** Samples were subjected to heat shock using an acetylene and oxygen torch at a fixed distance for durations of 10, 30, and 60 seconds, then cooled in air [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-3].
- **Surface Characterization:** Before and after heat shock, surfaces were analyzed using liquid penetrant under UV light with a stereomicroscope and an Alicona Infinite Focus G5 optical profilometer [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-4]. The fractal dimension of surface crack patterns was calculated using a box counting method in MATLAB [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-4].
- **Mechanical Testing:** Dynamic tensile tests were performed using a Split Hopkinson Pressure Bar (SHPB) device with flat-ended bars and a copper pulse shaper [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-4]. Each test was repeated five times at an impact speed of 10 m/s [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-4].
- **Deformation Analysis:** Two high-speed cameras recorded deformation at 160 kfps, and Digital Image Correlation (DIC) was used to analyze fracture and measure surface strain prior to fracture [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-4].

## Key Findings
- Heat shock increased the normalized fractal dimension of surface crack patterns for both granites, indicating more complex and interconnected cracks [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 6-7].
- Dynamic tensile strength decreased significantly with increasing heat shock duration. For Balmoral Red, average strengths were 29, 23, 16, and 12 MPa for 0, 10, 30, and 60 s shocks. For Kuru Grey, strengths were 27, 21, 16, and 15 MPa [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 6-7].
- The surface strain prior to fracture decreased with increasing heat shock duration for both rocks [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 9-10].
- For Kuru Grey, both tensile strength and failure strain showed a saturation effect at higher normalized fractal dimensions, possibly due to pores acting as crack propagation barriers [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 6-7].

## Limitations
- The method characterizes only surface damage; subsurface cracks caused by heat shock are not quantified [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 1-2].
- The fractal dimension prior to heat shock shows significant scatter, complicating the use of absolute values [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 6-7].
- The Brazilian disk test assumes the sample remains elastic and does not measure tensile strength directly [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-3].
- The speckle pattern for DIC prevented investigation of crack propagation mechanisms (transgranular vs. intergranular) [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 9-10].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The rocks are mechanically isotropic, and anisotropy has a small effect under dynamic loading [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-3].
- No mineral phase transformations occur during the heat shock, as temperatures are below the transformation points for quartz and feldspar [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 3-3].
- The surface crack pattern analysis provides a reasonable estimate of the drop in tensile strength [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 1-2].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Heat shock duration (10, 30, 60 seconds)
- Normalized fractal dimension (fractal dimension after shock / before shock)
- Dynamic tensile strength (MPa)
- Surface strain prior to fracture (%)
- Areal surface roughness (µm)

## Reusable Claims
- For granitic rocks, a localized heat shock of 10 to 60 seconds duration significantly reduces dynamic tensile strength, with the reduction correlating to an increase in the normalized fractal dimension of surface crack patterns [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 6-7].
- The surface damage from heat shock can be quantified using fractal dimension analysis of UV-fluorescent penetrant images, providing a fast estimate of mechanical property degradation [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 1-2, 6-7].
- In granite with higher initial porosity (Kuru Grey), pores may mitigate further strength loss at high levels of thermal damage by impeding crack propagation [Mardoukhi 2017, pp. 6-7].

## Open Questions
- How accurate is the surface fractal dimension in predicting the full 3D volumetric damage and strength loss?
- How do different heat shock methods (e.g., plasma gun vs. flame torch) affect the 3D crack pattern and mechanical response?
- What is the exact relationship between the microstructural features (grain size, porosity) and the saturation effect observed in Kuru Grey?
