# Experimental Investigation of Thermal Cycling Effect on Fracture Characteristics of Granite in a Geothermal-Energy Reservoir.

## One-line Summary
Thermal cycling between 100°C and 300°C reduces the fracture toughness and increases the permeability of fine-grained granite by promoting intergranular crack development, which is critical for assessing geothermal reservoir stability.

## Research Question
How does repeated thermal cycling affect the fracture characteristics, meso-crack evolution, and physical properties of fine-grained granite relevant to the stability and stimulation of a geothermal-energy reservoir?

## Study Area / Data
The study used fine-grained granite collected from Suizhou, China, a potential site for geothermal exploitation [Feng 2020, pp. 5-6]. The granite primarily contains plagioclase, quartzite, and potash feldspar, with particle sizes mostly between 0.2-0.5 mm [Feng 2020, pp. 5-6].

## Methods
- **Thermal Cycling:** Granite samples were subjected to 1, 5, 10, 15, and 20 thermal cycles in a muffle furnace. Three temperature ranges were used: 20~100°C, 20~200°C, and 20~300°C. Samples were heated at 5°C/min, held at the target temperature for 2 hours, and then naturally cooled to room temperature [Feng 2020, pp. 5-6].
- **Fracture Testing:** Semi-circular bend (SCB) tests were performed on an MTS servohydraulic system to determine fracture toughness under mode-I, mode-II, and mixed-mode (I+II) loading conditions. The loading rate was 0.002 mm/s [Feng 2020, pp. 6-8].
- **Physical Property Tests:** P-wave velocity and permeability were measured on the thermally cycled samples [Feng 2020, pp. 10-13].
- **Meso-crack Analysis:** Thin sections were analyzed under a polarizing microscope to quantitatively study the number, length, and type (intergranular vs. intragranular) of cracks [Feng 2020, pp. 12-15].
- **Fracture Criterion Analysis:** The Modified Maximum Tangential Stress (MMTS) criterion was applied, and the critical crack propagation radius (rc) was calculated and compared to experimental values [Feng 2020, pp. 8-10].

## Key Findings
1.  Thermal cycling decreases the effective fracture toughness (Keff), absorbed energy (U), and P-wave velocity (Pv) of granite, while increasing its permeability (K) [Feng 2020, pp. 2, 6-8, 10-13].
2.  The most significant reduction in fracture toughness and P-wave velocity occurs within the first five thermal cycles [Feng 2020, pp. 6-8, 10-12].
3.  Thermal cycling promotes the formation of intergranular cracks more than intragranular cracks. After 20 cycles at 20~300°C, the number of intergranular cracks increased by 95% compared to one cycle [Feng 2020, pp. 12-15].
4.  Crack density increases with the number of thermal cycles, and fracture toughness is negatively correlated with crack density, permeability, and damage rate [Feng 2020, pp. 12-15].
5.  The theoretical critical crack propagation radius (rc) calculated using standard formulas is approximately three times larger than the experimentally derived value (rce), indicating a need for modification under thermal cycling conditions [Feng 2020, pp. 8-10].

## Limitations
- The study tested specific temperature ranges (max 300°C) and a maximum of 20 cycles, which may not represent all geothermal reservoir conditions or longer-term effects [Feng 2020, pp. 5-6].
- The granite samples were from a single location (Suizhou, China), and results may vary for other granite types or rock lithologies [Feng 2020, pp. 5-6].
- The cooling method was natural cooling in the furnace, which may differ from in-situ reservoir cooling rates [Feng 2020, pp. 5-6].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The granite is assumed to be a homogeneous, fine-grained, dense material [Feng 2020, pp. 2, 5-6].
- The MMTS fracture criterion is applicable to describe the mixed-mode fracture behavior of granite after thermal cycling [Feng 2020, pp. 8-10].
- The damage rate (δ) calculated from P-wave velocity is a valid index for thermal damage accumulation [Feng 2020, pp. 10-12].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables:** Number of thermal cycles (1, 5, 10, 15, 20); Temperature range (20~100°C, 20~200°C, 20~300°C); Fracture mode (Mode-I, Mode-II, Mixed-mode I+II).
- **Dependent Variables:** Fracture toughness (Keff, KI, KII); Absorbed energy (U); P-wave velocity (Pv); Permeability (K); Crack density (Pl); Number and length of intergranular and intragranular cracks; Damage rate (δ).
- **Derived Parameters:** Critical crack propagation radius (rc, rce); Mixed-mode parameter (Me).

## Reusable Claims
- Thermal cycling between 100°C and 300°C significantly reduces the fracture toughness of fine-grained granite, with the most severe degradation occurring in the initial cycles [Feng 2020, pp. 6-8].
- The interconnection of intergranular and intragranular cracks induced by thermal cycling fragments the granite structure, increasing permeability and reducing mechanical stability [Feng 2020, pp. 12-15].
- The standard formula for calculating the critical crack propagation radius (rc) overestimates its value for thermally cycled granite by a factor of approximately three [Feng 2020, pp. 8-10].
- Fracture toughness of granite after thermal cycling can be empirically correlated with non-destructive measurements like P-wave velocity and permeability [Feng 2020, pp. 13-15].

## Connections To Other Work
- The study builds on prior research showing that thermal cycling reduces Young's modulus and P-wave velocity in various rocks [Feng 2020, pp. 3-5, citing Rong et al. 2018, Zhou et al. 2015].
- It applies and evaluates the MMTS fracture criterion, which considers T-stress, for thermally damaged rock [Feng 2020, pp. 8-10, citing Yang et al. 2018].
- The findings on intergranular crack dominance align with observations that thermal cycling causes intergranular destruction in rocks [Feng 2020, pp. 15-16, citing Mahmutoglu 1998].
- The work addresses a noted gap in quantitative research on meso-crack evolution due to thermal cycling in fine-grained granite [Feng 2020, pp. 3-5].
