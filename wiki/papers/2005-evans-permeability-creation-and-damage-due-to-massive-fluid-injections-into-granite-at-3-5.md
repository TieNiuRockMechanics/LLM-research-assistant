# Permeability Creation and Damage Due to Massive Fluid Injections into Granite at 3.5 km at Soultz: 1. Borehole Observations.

## One-line Summary
Massive fluid injections into a deep granite borehole created and enhanced permeability primarily through shearing of pre-existing fractures within hydrothermally altered cataclastic shear zones, leading to a 200-fold transmissivity increase and structural damage.

## Research Question
How do massive fluid injections create and enhance permeability in deep, low-porosity crystalline rock, and what are the associated hydraulic and mechanical changes and their relation to geological structures?

## Study Area / Data
The study is located at the European Union's Hot Dry Rock (HDR) test site near Soultz-sous-Forêts in the Rhine Graben, France [Evans 2005, pp. 2-2]. Data are from borehole GPK1, which was extended to 3600 m depth, with 750 m of open hole below 2850 m that intersects a relatively transmissive fault near 3490 m [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2]. The dataset includes pre- and post-stimulation Ultrasonic Borehole Imager (UBI) logs, Formation Micro-Imager (FMI) logs, Azimuthal Resistivity Imager (ARI) logs, spinner logs, temperature logs, hydraulic test data, and microseismic event locations [Evans 2005, pp. 2-4].

## Methods
The analysis relies on comparing pre-stimulation and post-stimulation borehole logs to identify changes in fracture permeability and geometry. Key methods include:
-   Analysis of UBI sonic reflectivity and travel time logs to identify natural fractures and detect post-injection enhancement or dislocation [Evans 2005, pp. 4-4, 11-12].
-   Use of spinner logs to quantify flow distribution during injections and temperature logs (run during venting/production) to identify permeable fractures based on temperature perturbations [Evans 2005, pp. 8-9].
-   Interpretation of hydraulic test data (Q-P plots) to determine transmissivity evolution and pressure-limiting behavior [Evans 2005, pp. 4-6].
-   Correlation of permeable fracture locations with hydrothermal alteration profiles derived from cuttings and core samples [Evans 2005, pp. 14-15].
-   Measurement of fracture dislocations by comparing UBI travel time scans across fractures before and after injections [Evans 2005, pp. 12-12].

## Key Findings
-   The stimulations produced a fifteenfold increase in the net transmissivity of the entire well; for the 550 m section open during the 93SEP01 injection, the increase was approximately 200-fold [Evans 2005, pp. 7-8].
-   Prior to injections, 17 fractures were identified as permeable. Following injections, at least 93 fractures (a "high-confidence" set) supported flow, with the number of permeable fractures increasing to approximately 100 [Evans 2005, pp. 8-9, 18-18].
-   During injections, approximately 95% of the flow entered the rock mass at just 10 "major flowing fractures," most of which were naturally permeable [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].
-   The distribution of newly permeable fractures was organized, with major flowing fractures each surrounded by clusters of weakly-flowing, newly permeable fractures [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2, 9-11].
-   Permeability creation/enhancement occurred primarily within hydrothermally altered, cataclastic shear structures intersecting the borehole. The major flowing fractures represent the core of these structures, and the clusters of newly permeable fractures denote the damage zone [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2, 14-15].
-   Comparison of UBI logs showed that all permeable fractures had suffered damage, and major flowing fractures had suffered dislocations of millimeters to centimeters, mostly in shear [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2, 12-12].
-   Drilling-induced tension fractures did not accept flow, implying wellbore pressures remained below the minimum principal stress below 2900 m, implicating shearing as the primary permeability enhancement mechanism [Evans 2005, pp. 18-18].
-   Stimulation was ineffective below 3350 m, even in zones with optimally oriented, highly altered fractures that showed evidence of slip [Evans 2005, pp. 15-16].

## Limitations
-   The study is based on borehole observations, which primarily reflect changes near the wellbore and may not fully characterize far-field permeability changes [Evans 2005, pp. 2-2].
-   Identification of permeable fractures relies on temperature log perturbations, which may miss fractures with very small flow rates or where fluid temperature matches borehole fluid temperature [Evans 2005, pp. 8-9].
-   The "high-confidence" flowing fracture dataset (93 fractures) is considered a lower bound to the true number [Evans 2005, pp. 8-9].
-   Analysis of UBI logs for trace enhancement and dislocation involves some subjectivity in interpretation [Evans 2005, pp. 11-12].
-   The depth of origin of cuttings used for alteration profiling has inherent uncertainty due to mixing in the drilling mud [Evans 2005, pp. 14-15].

## Assumptions / Conditions
-   The stress regime at the site is consistent with an active graben setting: minimum principal stress is horizontal (N80°E±15°), with magnitude ~50% of vertical stress; maximum horizontal stress magnitude is approximately equal to vertical stress [Evans 2005, pp. 4-4].
-   Enhancement of a fracture's trace on the post-stimulation UBI reflectivity log indicates damage, likely due to shear dislocation [Evans 2005, pp. 11-12].
-   Pressure-limiting behavior observed during injections (at ~9.0 MPa differential pressure) reflects jacking, likely limited to the uppermost 50-100 m of the open hole [Evans 2005, pp. 4-6, 6-7].
-   The hydrothermally altered cataclastic shear structures are the primary geological features controlling permeability creation [Evans 2005, pp. 14-15].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   Injection flow rate (e.g., up to 50 L/s)
-   Differential pressure (excess of downhole wellbore pressure above formation pressure)
-   Transmissivity / Equivalent Porous Medium (EPM) permeability
-   Fracture orientation (strike and dip)
-   Hydrothermal alteration grade (low, moderate, high)
-   Fracture dislocation magnitude and orientation (Burgers vector)
-   Fracture flow ranking (F, F-, P, P?)

## Reusable Claims
-   Permeability creation in granite under massive fluid injections is primarily controlled by shearing of pre-existing fractures within hydrothermally altered cataclastic shear zones, not by extensive mode I hydrofracturing [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2, 18-18].
-   Major flowing fractures that accept most of the injected fluid are typically naturally permeable fractures located in the core zones of cataclastic shear structures and suffer millimeter- to centimeter-scale shear dislocations during stimulation [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2, 12-12].
-   The damage zone of a stimulated shear structure is characterized by clusters of newly permeable, minor-flowing fractures surrounding the major flowing fracture [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2, 9-11].
-   Drilling-induced tension fractures do not support flow during stimulation, indicating that wellbore pressure remained below the minimum principal stress in those sections, favoring shear over jacking as the damage mechanism [Evans 2005, pp. 9-11, 18-18].
-   Highly altered, optimally oriented fractures may not experience significant transmissivity enhancement despite evidence of shear slip, possibly due to smoothing of roughness or crushing of weak minerals like illite suppressing dilation [Evans 2005, pp. 15-16, 18-18].

## Connections To Other Work
-   This paper (Part 1) focuses on borehole observations; the companion paper (Evans, 2005, Part 2) evaluates the changes within the context of the prevailing stress and fracture strength [Evans 2005, pp. 2-2].
-   The results provide key background for interpreting the microseismicity that accompanied the injections, which is presented elsewhere (Evans et al., 2005) [Evans 2005, pp. 2-2].
-   The study builds on earlier geological characterization of the Soultz granite from the EPS1 core (e.g., Genter and Traineau, 1992, 1996) [Evans 2005, pp. 4-4].
-   The findings relate to broader debates on stimulation mechanisms in HDR/EGS systems, contrasting shear stimulation with hydrofracturing models [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].

## Open Questions
-   Why was stimulation so ineffective below 3350 m, even for highly altered, optimally oriented fractures that showed evidence of slip? [Evans 2005, pp. 15-16].
-   What is the exact mechanism by which strong hydrothermal alteration suppresses transmissivity enhancement despite shear slip (e.g., smoothing of roughness vs. gouge formation)? [Evans 2005, pp. 15-16, 18-18].
-   What is the relative contribution of aseismic slip versus seismic slip to the observed permeability enhancement? [Evans 2005, pp. 6-7].
-   How do the near-borehole observations of permeability creation relate to the larger-scale, far-field permeability network and long-term reservoir performance? [Evans 2005, pp. 2-2, 17-18].
