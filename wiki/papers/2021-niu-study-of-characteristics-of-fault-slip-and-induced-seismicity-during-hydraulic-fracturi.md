# Study of Characteristics of Fault Slip and Induced Seismicity during Hydraulic Fracturing in HDR Geothermal Exploitation in the Yishu Fault Zone in China.

## One-line Summary
This study uses 2D numerical simulation to investigate how fluid injection during hydraulic fracturing for HDR geothermal energy in China's Yishu Fault Zone can induce slip and seismicity on faults located outside the directly pressurized region.

## Research Question
What are the characteristics of fault slip and induced seismicity caused by fluid injection during hydraulic fracturing for HDR geothermal exploitation, specifically for faults not directly connected to the fluid-pressurized region, and how do engineering parameters affect them?

## Study Area / Data
The study is based on a hydraulic fracturing project in the geothermal field of the Yishu fault zone in Shandong Province, China [Niu 2021, pp. 1-2]. The east of the Yishu fault zone is identified as one of the best geothermal areas in Shandong Province [Niu 2021, pp. 2-3]. The numerical model was simplified from a field test in this zone, focusing on two major adjacent faults (F1 and F2) located beyond the hydraulic fracturing region [Niu 2021, pp. 4-5]. Material properties for the model were obtained from field logs, well data, and laboratory experiments [Niu 2021, pp. 5-7].

## Methods
The study employed a 2D plane strain numerical model using the finite-element software ABAQUS to simulate the EGS-Fault system [Niu 2021, pp. 4-5]. The interaction between faults and surrounding rock was modeled using contact analysis with a master-slave contact method and a Coulomb friction model [Niu 2021, pp. 3-4]. The hydraulic fracturing region was treated as a porous elastic medium, with fluid flow described by Darcy's law and a mass conservation equation [Niu 2021, pp. 4-5]. Induced seismic moment magnitude (Mw) was calculated using the seismic moment (M0) and the moment magnitude scale [Niu 2021, pp. 4-5]. A series of numerical experiments were designed to investigate the effects of: (1) the location of the fracturing region, (2) its permeability, (3) injection pressure, and (4) its area on fault slip and seismicity [Niu 2021, pp. 5-9].

## Key Findings
1.  Fluid injection during hydraulic fracturing can cause faults that exist beyond the fluid-pressurized region to slip and can even induce large seismic events (Mw ≥ 3) [Niu 2021, pp. 1-2, 17-18].
2.  The influence of the fracturing region on fault stability is not simply dependent on their relative spatial location, but on the displacement and stress disturbance induced by fluid injection [Niu 2021, pp. 9-11, 17-18].
3.  If the fracturing region is too close to a fault (e.g., region P1), injecting fluid may induce a felt seismic event (Mw ≥ 3) [Niu 2021, pp. 12-13, 17-18].
4.  It is easier to cause felt earthquakes when hydraulic fracturing is carried out simultaneously in different layers [Niu 2021, pp. 12-13, 17-18].
5.  When injection pressure and time are constant, the permeability of the fracturing region is a key factor affecting pressure build-up and fault stability. Too high a permeability threatens fault stability and reduces heat transfer efficiency [Niu 2021, pp. 15-16, 17-18].
6.  The induced fault slippage and moment magnitude increase evenly with injection pressure [Niu 2021, pp. 16-17, 17-18].
7.  The induced slippage and moment magnitude of faults increase with the area of the fracturing region [Niu 2021, pp. 16-17, 17-18].

## Limitations
The study used a simplified 2D plane strain model [Niu 2021, pp. 4-5]. The width of the fault plane in the model was assumed to be 200 m based on relevant observations and geological data [Niu 2021, pp. 9-11]. The caprock thickness in the model was reduced to 1000 m (from ~2000 m) for simulation efficiency, with the remainder replaced by a uniform pressure [Niu 2021, pp. 5-7]. The study focused only on the additional influence of pore pressure changes on fault slip, not temperature stresses [Niu 2021, pp. 5-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The fault and surrounding rock mass are treated as two separate parts interacting via contact analysis [Niu 2021, pp. 3-4].
- The hydraulic fracturing region is modeled as a porous elastic medium [Niu 2021, pp. 4-5].
- The initial stress field is geostatic, and water pressure is initially hydrostatic within the fracturing region [Niu 2021, pp. 5-7].
- The injected fluid only flows within the fracturing region due to low granite permeability; stress perturbation is transmitted to faults [Niu 2021, pp. 7-8].
- The shear modulus (G) for seismic moment calculation was derived from the fault's Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio [Niu 2021, pp. 9-11].

## Reusable Claims
- **Condition:** During hydraulic fracturing for HDR geothermal energy. **Limitation:** For faults not directly hydraulically connected to the pressurized region. **Claim:** Fluid injection can induce fault slip and seismicity through stress perturbation, potentially causing felt earthquakes (Mw ≥ 3) if the fracturing region is sufficiently close to the fault [Niu 2021, pp. 1-2, 12-13, 17-18].
- **Condition:** When injection pressure and time are held constant. **Limitation:** Within the tested permeability range (10⁻¹⁰ to 10⁻⁶ m/s). **Claim:** The permeability of the engineered fracturing region is a key control on pore pressure build-up and induced fault stability; permeabilities above ~10⁻⁷ m/s result in uniformly high pore pressure [Niu 2021, pp. 15-16, 17-18].
- **Condition:** For a given fracturing region geometry and location. **Limitation:** Based on 2D numerical simulation. **Claim:** Induced fault slippage and seismic magnitude increase linearly with injection pressure [Niu 2021, pp. 16-17, 17-18].
- **Condition:** During simultaneous hydraulic fracturing in multiple layers. **Limitation:** Compared to single-layer fracturing. **Claim:** Multilayer fracturing increases the likelihood of inducing felt seismic events [Niu 2021, pp. 12-13, 17-18].

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on the principle of effective stress for fluid-induced seismicity [Niu 2021, pp. 2-3]. It addresses the finding that fluid injection can induce fault slip outpacing pore-fluid migration, a concept supported by recent research [Niu 2021, pp. 2-3]. The work is contextualized within the global challenge of induced seismicity in geothermal projects, referencing the 2017 Pohang earthquake [Niu 2021, pp. 2-3]. It uses ABAQUS, a tool validated for nonlinear geotechnical problems [Niu 2021, pp. 3-4].
