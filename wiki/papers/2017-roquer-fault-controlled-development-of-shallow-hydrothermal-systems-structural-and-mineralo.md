# Fault-Controlled Development of Shallow Hydrothermal Systems: Structural and Mineralogical Insights from the Southern Andes.

## One-line Summary
This study uses structural and mineralogical analysis of exhumed fault zones in the Southern Andes to propose a conceptual model where the interaction between arc-parallel (LOFS) and transverse (ATF) faults controls fluid flow and storage in shallow hydrothermal systems.

## Research Question
How do the structural characteristics and failure modes of the Liquiñe-Ofqui Fault System (LOFS) and Andean Transverse Faults (ATF) control the development, fluid flow, and storage potential of shallow hydrothermal systems in the Southern Andes?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on two exhumed exposures located approximately 15 km SW of Lonquimay (ca. 38°43′S) in the Southern Volcanic Zone of the Andes. The outcrops represent a local splay fault of the LOFS (Outcrop 1) and a specific segment of the ATF (Outcrop 2). The host rocks are Miocene andesites (K-Ar ages 20–11 Ma) intruded by Miocene granitoids (K-Ar ages 15.2–7.2 Ma) [Roquer 2017, pp. 2-3]. Data includes meso- and microstructural mapping of 107 fractures, X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis of 60 samples, and scanning electron microscopy (SEM-EDX) [Roquer 2017, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
1.  **Field and Analytical Methods:** Meso- (1:50) and microstructural mapping of fracture textures and cross-cutting relationships. Microstructural analysis involved creating photomosaics and maps from oriented thin sections. Mineral identification used XRD and SEM-EDX. Linear fracture density was calculated [Roquer 2017, pp. 3-4].
2.  **Failure Conditions Modeling:** A new method was developed to construct failure envelopes in pore fluid factor (λ) – differential stress (σ) space for both Andersonian and non-Andersonian stress regimes. This involved projecting a stress tensor onto a pre-existing fault plane to calculate normal and shear stresses, then applying Griffith (extensional/shear) and Mohr-Coulomb (shear) failure criteria [Roquer 2017, pp. 4-6].

## Key Findings
1.  **Fault Architecture:** Both fault zones have simple cores and asymmetric damage zones with higher fracture density in the hanging wall. The LOFS splay fault damage zone is dominated by hydrothermal breccias, while the ATF segment is dominated by fault-localized hydrothermal breccias [Roquer 2017, pp. 7-9].
2.  **Mineralogy:** Hydrothermal infill is dominated by Ca and Ca(-Na) zeolites, primarily laumontite. Key assemblages indicate temperatures of ~110°C (laumontite + stilbite in LOFS), ~120°C (laumontite + scolecite in ATF), and ~210°C (laumontite + wairakite in ATF) [Roquer 2017, pp. 9-10, 13-15].
3.  **Failure Modes & Stress Regimes:** The LOFS splay fault cyclically fails in extension (mode I) or hybrid extension+shear (modes I+II/III) under an Andersonian strike-slip regime. The ATF segment records switches between shear (mode II/III) failure under an Andersonian regime and hybrid extension+shear failure under a non-Andersonian transtensional regime [Roquer 2017, pp. 11-13].
4.  **Hydromechanical Conditions:** Modeled failure envelopes show that for depths of 0.5–3.0 km, the ATF segment fails in shear at differential stresses >28 MPa and fluid pressures <40–80% lithostatic (Andersonian). Hybrid failure occurs at differential stresses <20 MPa and fluid pressures >85–98% lithostatic (non-Andersonian). The LOFS fails in extension/hybrid failure at differential stresses <28 MPa and fluid pressures >40–80% lithostatic (Andersonian) [Roquer 2017, pp. 15-17].
5.  **Conceptual Model:** Long-term Andersonian stress conditions favor continuous fluid flow through EW-striking LOFS faults. Quasi-instantaneous relaxation (non-Andersonian regime) allows sporadic failure in ATF faults, which may act as compartments that store overpressured fluids. Intersections between ATF and LOFS are proposed as highly probable sites for concentrated hydrothermal activity [Roquer 2017, pp. 15-17].

## Limitations
1.  Study is based on two exhumed, extinct hydrothermal system exposures, which may not fully represent active systems.
2.  Mechanical modeling uses assumed rock properties (T=5 MPa, μ=0.75) and a single representative fracture orientation for the non-Andersonian case.
3.  Paleodepth estimates rely on regional chronostratigraphy and a single fission track study.
4.  The conceptual model extrapolates meso-microscale observations to a regional fault-controlled system.

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Andersonian stress regime assumption (one principal stress vertical) is valid for the long-term LOFS behavior.
2.  Rock mechanical properties (tensile strength T=5 MPa, coefficient of internal friction μ=0.75) are representative of the andesitic host rock at depth.
3.  Zeolite stability temperatures from experimental data are a good approximation for paleotemperatures.
4.  The observed meso- and microstructural features can be extrapolated to infer regional fault behavior.

## Key Variables / Parameters
1.  **Pore fluid factor (λ):** Ratio of pore fluid pressure to lithostatic pressure (Pf/σV).
2.  **Differential stress (σ):** σ1 - σ3.
3.  **Tensile strength (T):** Rock property set to 5 MPa in models.
4.  **Stress tensor shape ratio (Φ):** (σ2 - σ3)/(σ1 - σ3).
5.  **Failure mode:** Extension (I), Shear (II/III), Hybrid Extension+Shear (I+II/III).
6.  **Paleodepth (z):** Estimated at <3 km.
7.  **Paleogeothermal gradient:** Estimated at 30–70 °C/km.

## Reusable Claims
1.  In the Southern Andes, intersections between the arc-parallel Liquiñe-Ofqui Fault System (LOFS) and the Andean Transverse Faults (ATF) are structurally favorable sites for concentrated hydrothermal activity due to the complementary roles of fluid storage (ATF) and transport (LOFS) [Roquer 2017, pp. 15-17].
2.  The ATF can act as a fluid compartment that stores overpressured fluids under long-term Andersonian stress conditions, with sporadic release during transient non-Andersonian regimes associated with co/postseismic relaxation [Roquer 2017, pp. 15-17].
3.  Zeolite assemblages (e.g., laumontite+wairakite) in fault damage zones can indicate paleofluid temperatures up to ~210°C and minimum paleogeothermal gradients of 30–70 °C/km in Andean-type settings [Roquer 2017, pp. 13-15].
4.  Cyclical failure in fault zones can be inferred from superimposed textures of tectonic comminution breccias (shear failure) and fluid-assisted hydrothermal breccias (extensional/hybrid failure) [Roquer 2017, pp. 11-13].
5.  For shallow depths (0.5–3.0 km), hybrid extensional+shear failure in misoriented faults (like ATF segments) requires anomalously high fluid pressures (>85–98% lithostatic) under a non-Andersonian stress regime [Roquer 2017, pp. 15-17].

## Connections To Other Work
1.  Builds on the theoretical framework for failure mode diagrams in λ-σ space by Cox (2010) and extends it to non-Andersonian regimes [Roquer 2017, pp. 2-2, 4-6].
2.  Uses stress tensor solutions from Pérez-Flores et al. (2016) for the same study area as input for mechanical models [Roquer 2017, pp. 4-5].
3.  Compares inferred fluid overpressure conditions with typical values from active geothermal areas like the Taupo Volcanic Zone (Rowland and Simmons, 2012) [Roquer 2017, pp. 2-2, 15-17].
4.  Relates fault-controlled compartmentalization to concepts proposed by other authors for the ATF (e.g., Cembrano and Lara, 2009; Sánchez et al., 2013) [Roquer 2017, pp. 15-17].
5.  The proposed rupture cycle for the ATF is comparable to observations in zeolite-bearing normal faults in other tectonic contexts (e.g., Dempsey et al., 2014) [Roquer 2017, pp. 12-13].

## Open Questions
1.  How do the specific hydromechanical conditions at ATF-LOFS intersections quantitatively control the efficiency of fluid focusing and geothermal reservoir formation?
2.  What is the temporal frequency and duration of the stress regime switches (Andersonian vs. non-Andersonian) and how does this affect the cumulative fluid flux and heat transport?
3.  Can the zeolite geothermometry and paleodepth estimates be refined with additional isotopic or fluid inclusion studies?
4.  How does the proposed conceptual model apply to other fault intersections in the Andes or similar volcanic arcs?
