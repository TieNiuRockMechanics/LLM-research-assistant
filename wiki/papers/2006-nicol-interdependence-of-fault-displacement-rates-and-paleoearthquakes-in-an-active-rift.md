# Interdependence of Fault Displacement Rates and Paleoearthquakes in an Active Rift.

## One-line Summary
Displacement rates on individual faults in the Taupo Rift are highly variable over short timescales (~18 k.y.) but stabilize when aggregated across larger fault zones or the entire rift, indicating that faults are components of an interacting, kinematically coherent system.

## Research Question
What are the nature and origin of fluctuations in fault displacement rates over short time intervals, and to what extent do they result from systematic, predictable earthquake processes?

## Study Area / Data
The study is located in the Taupo Rift, New Zealand, a backarc basin extending at rates up to ~10 mm/yr. Data were derived from 28 excavations (trenches) and offset geomorphic surfaces distributed across the 15 km width of the rift. The data record displacement of up to 11 volcanic tephra layers and two fluvial surfaces on 25 normal fault traces over the last 60 k.y. [Nicol 2006, pp. 1-1, 1-2].

## Methods
Displacement histories were charted using a combination of scarp heights and displaced horizons in trenches. Volcanic tephra layers were distinguished by chemistry and mineralogy and radiometrically dated. Displacement of trenched layers was augmented by offsets of the 60 ± 4 ka Earthquake Flat Pyroclastics. Displacement profiles were analyzed for individual fault traces, aggregated for fault zones, and aggregated for the entire rift [Nicol 2006, pp. 1-1, 1-2, 2-3].

## Key Findings
1.  Displacement histories on individual fault traces are highly variable, often showing "stepped" profiles with periods of slow (~0.1 mm/yr) and rapid (>0.7 mm/yr) displacement accumulation, consistent with temporal earthquake clustering [Nicol 2006, pp. 1-2].
2.  Displacement rates become more stable with increasing spatial scale: they are less variable when aggregated for fault zones (~1 km wide) and become near-constant when aggregated for the entire rift [Nicol 2006, pp. 2-3, 3-4].
3.  There is a positive correlation between fault length and displacement rate, and a larger spread in displacement rate ratios for smaller faults compared to larger ones [Nicol 2006, pp. 2-3].
4.  Rift-wide displacement rates are uniform over timescales of 2 to 60 k.y., indicating stable boundary conditions driven by constant plate motion rates [Nicol 2006, pp. 3-4].
5.  The increased stability at larger scales suggests each fault is part of a kinematically coherent, interacting system where earthquake histories are interdependent [Nicol 2006, pp. 1-1, 4-4].

## Limitations
-   Uncertainties on tephra ages are ±0.1 to ±0.3 ka for units younger than 20 ka, and ±2 ka for older horizons [Nicol 2006, pp. 1-2].
-   The timing of intervals of slow and rapid displacement accumulation cannot be correlated between faults across the entire rift [Nicol 2006, pp. 1-2].
-   It is unclear which specific factors control the activity of individual fault traces or splays within a fault zone [Nicol 2006, pp. 2-3].

## Assumptions / Conditions
-   Extension in the Taupo Rift is driven by constant rates of plate motion, providing stable boundary conditions [Nicol 2006, pp. 3-4].
-   Surface displacements recorded in trenches provide a record of the largest magnitude earthquakes in the rift [Nicol 2006, pp. 1-2].
-   The 18 k.y. time interval over which displacement rates stabilize may be applicable to other fault systems with comparable regional strain rates, numbers of active faults, and fault sizes [Nicol 2006, pp. 3-4].

## Reusable Claims
-   **Condition:** In an active rift with multiple interacting faults. **Claim:** Displacement rates on individual faults are highly variable over short timescales (~18 k.y.) but become increasingly stable when aggregated across larger spatial scales (fault zones, entire rift). **Limitation:** Stability emerges over timescales of ~18 k.y. or less for faults in the Taupo Rift [Nicol 2006, pp. 2-3, 3-4].
-   **Condition:** For normal faults in the Taupo Rift. **Claim:** There is a positive correlation between fault length and displacement rate, with larger faults generally having higher rates. **Limitation:** Smaller faults show a larger spread in displacement rate ratios, attributed to a stronger dependence on larger faults [Nicol 2006, pp. 2-3].
-   **Condition:** Under uniform regional extension rates. **Claim:** Temporal clustering of earthquakes on individual faults must be accompanied by migration of seismic activity between faults in the system to maintain overall uniform strain rates. **Limitation:** The degree of migration is related to the variability in displacement rates on individual faults [Nicol 2006, pp. 3-4].
