# Experimental Research on Seepage Laws of Granite under Thermal Cracking Action with 3D Stress.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally demonstrates that intact granite under 3D hydrostatic stress undergoes thermal cracking, causing its permeability to increase exponentially with temperature, with a maximum observed increase of 93 times the initial value.

## Research Question
How does thermal cracking action under three-dimensional stress influence the seepage laws (permeability evolution) of intact granite, particularly at temperatures and stresses corresponding to depths of 1000-3000 meters? [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
The experimental data comes from laboratory tests on four intact granite samples (φ200 mm × 400 mm) of Luhui granite from Pingyi, Shandong, China. The samples were tested under constant hydrostatic stresses of 25 MPa and 75 MPa, simulating in-situ conditions at depths of approximately 1000 m and 3000 m, respectively. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]

## Methods
The study used the "20 MN servo-controlled triaxial rock testing system with high temperature and high pressure" developed at China University of Mining and Technology. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3] The method involved:
1.  Applying a constant hydrostatic stress (25 or 75 MPa) to the granite sample.
2.  Heating the sample at a controlled rate using an electric heating alloy.
3.  Simultaneously measuring real-time permeability using the Darcy stable flow method with nitrogen (N₂) gas as the seepage medium. [Zhang 2010, pp. 3-5]
4.  Recording temperature with thermocouples and gas flow with a soap bubble flow meter. [Zhang 2010, pp. 3-5]

## Key Findings
1.  Under 3D stress, all granite samples experienced thermal cracking. The permeability (K) showed a positive exponential growth relationship with temperature (T) during the process. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]
2.  In the initial stage of thermal cracking, permeability increased slowly with temperature. In the later stage, it increased sharply until reaching a peak value. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]
3.  Throughout the heating process, both the permeability and its rate of change (dK/dT) continuously accelerated with increasing temperature. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]
4.  The ratio of peak permeability to its initial value (at the thermal cracking threshold) reached up to 93, and the maximum permeability change rate was 3.5×10⁻⁴ mD/°C, indicating that thermal cracking greatly enhances granite's permeability. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]

## Limitations
1.  The study acknowledges that previous research often tested permeability after cooling from high temperature, which may not accurately represent in-situ conditions due to structural changes during cooling. [Zhang 2010, pp. 5-6]
2.  The experimental setup and difficulty of high-temperature, high-pressure seepage testing limit the number of samples and the range of conditions that can be practically explored. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]
3.  The research focuses on intact granite; the behavior of pre-fractured rock masses is not addressed. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The granite samples are initially intact and homogeneous. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]
2.  The applied stress state is purely hydrostatic (no deviatoric stress) to isolate the effect of thermal cracking. [Zhang 2010, pp. 3-5]
3.  The seepage of nitrogen gas follows Darcy's law under the test conditions. [Zhang 2010, pp. 3-5]
4.  The heating rate is slow enough to avoid thermal shock. [Zhang 2010, pp. 3-5]

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Temperature (T):** The primary independent variable, ranging from room temperature up to 450°C.
-   **Hydrostatic Stress (σ):** Applied confining pressure, set at 25 MPa or 75 MPa.
-   **Permeability (K):** The primary dependent variable, measured in millidarcies (mD).
-   **Thermal Cracking Threshold Temperature:** The temperature at which permeability begins to increase significantly.
-   **Permeability Change Rate (dK/dT):** The rate of permeability increase per degree Celsius.

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For intact Luhui granite under hydrostatic stresses of 25-75 MPa. **Claim:** The permeability exhibits a positive exponential increase with temperature during thermal cracking, following a relationship of the form K = a * exp(T/b) + c. [Zhang 2010, pp. 3-5]
2.  **Condition:** Under the tested 3D stress and thermal cracking conditions. **Claim:** The peak permeability can be up to 93 times greater than the initial permeability measured at the thermal cracking threshold. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]
3.  **Condition:** During the thermal cracking process of granite. **Claim:** The rate of permeability increase (dK/dT) itself accelerates with rising temperature. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]

## Connections To Other Work
The study contrasts its real-time testing method with prior work that measured permeability after cooling, citing studies on basalt [Jones et al., 1997], sandstone [Sageev et al., 1980; Aruna, 1978], and carbonate rocks [Chen et al., 1999; Liu et al., 2001, 2008]. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3, 5-6] It positions itself as the first real-time experimental study on granite seepage under simultaneous high temperature and 3D stress in China. [Zhang 2010, pp. 1-3]

## Open Questions
1.  How does the permeability evolution change under non-hydrostatic (deviatoric) stress states?
2.  What is the detailed microstructural evolution of the crack network that leads to the observed exponential permeability increase?
3.  How do these laboratory findings on intact rock blocks scale up to predict the permeability of a fractured reservoir (人工储留层) in the field?
