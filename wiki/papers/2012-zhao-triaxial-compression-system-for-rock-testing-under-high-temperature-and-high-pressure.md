# Triaxial Compression System for Rock Testing under High Temperature and High Pressure.

## One-line Summary
A self-developed 20 MN servo-controlled triaxial rock testing system (XPS-20 MN) is presented, capable of testing large rock specimens (φ200 mm × 400 mm) under high temperature (up to 600 °C) and high pressure (axial and lateral up to 318 MPa and 250 MPa, respectively) to study mechanical, thermal, and coupled properties for deep engineering applications [Zhao 2012, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
How to develop a triaxial testing system that can overcome the limitations of existing high-temperature and high-pressure rock testing machines, which typically use small samples (e.g., 20 mm × 40 mm) and cannot adequately provide data on rock engineering behavior under deep Earth conditions [Zhao 2012, pp. 1-2]?

## Study Area / Data
The experimental data were obtained from tests on two types of rock:
1.  **Coal specimens:** Gas coal samples from Xinglongzhuang Coal Mine in Yanzhou, China, taken from a depth of 500 m. Samples were prepared to dimensions of φ200 mm × 400 mm [Zhao 2012, pp. 3-4].
2.  **Granite specimens:** Luhui Granite from Pingyi, Shandong Province, China. The processed specimen was a dense, crack-free cylinder with a diameter of 200.3 mm and height of 414.5 mm [Zhao 2012, pp. 4-5].

## Methods
The study describes the design, components, and operation of the XPS-20 MN testing system and presents example test results.
*   **System Design:** The system consists of a host loading system (max axial/lateral load 10,000 kN), a high-temperature triaxial pressure chamber (H13 steel, max 250 MPa, 600 °C), an auxiliary installing system, and a testing system for measuring deformation, temperature, permeability, and acoustic emission [Zhao 2012, pp. 2-3].
*   **Heating Method:** The specimen is heated via direct current passed through heat-resistant alloy films (Ni80Cr20) wrapped around it, controlled by a temperature-controlled cabinet [Zhao 2012, pp. 2-3].
*   **Testing Procedures:**
    *   **Coal:** Triaxial compression tests were conducted at temperatures up to 600 °C with a constant confining pressure (σₐ = 15 MPa). Stress-strain relationships and Young's modulus were measured [Zhao 2012, pp. 3-4].
    *   **Granite:** Thermal expansion was measured under a hydrostatic pressure of 25 MPa. Subsequently, triaxial compression tests were performed at temperatures from 200 °C to 500 °C with a constant confining pressure (σₐ = 25 MPa) to study deformation and failure [Zhao 2012, pp. 4-5].

## Key Findings
1.  **Coal Behavior at High T/P:** Gas coal at 600 °C exhibited high plasticity, with axial strain reaching ~30% and continuous volume contraction. Young's modulus decreased drastically with temperature, following an exponential decay: E_c = 19.6 exp(-T/199.2) - 1.55 GPa [Zhao 2012, pp. 3-4].
2.  **Granite Thermal Expansion:** Under 25 MPa confining pressure, granite's thermal expansion coefficient increased with temperature up to a peak at 460 °C, then decreased. The coefficient was smaller than values reported under low/no confining pressure due to restricted thermal cracking [Zhao 2012, pp. 4-5].
3.  **Granite Mechanical Behavior:** Granite transitioned from linear elastic to plastic behavior as temperature increased. Young's modulus decreased exponentially with temperature: E = 60 exp(-0.0006T) GPa. Failure at high temperature (300 °C) showed an "X"-shaped shear fracture [Zhao 2012, pp. 5-7].
4.  **System Capability:** The XPS-20 MN system can test specimens with volume 80–170 times larger than ordinary machines, improving sample representativeness. It enables servo-controlled loading and the study of permeability, deformation during thermal decomposition, and high-temperature drilling/creep [Zhao 2012, pp. 7-7].

## Limitations
*   The paper presents example results for coal and granite but does not provide a comprehensive validation across a wide range of rock types.
*   The long-term rheological (creep) behavior under high T/P is mentioned as a capability but not demonstrated in the provided results [Zhao 2012, pp. 7-7].
*   The system's maximum temperature is 600 °C, which may not cover all deep geothermal or magma-related conditions.

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   The deformation of the H13 steel pressers at high temperature is small and was accounted for in the coal sample deformation calculations [Zhao 2012, pp. 3-4].
*   The pyrophyllite ring and salt ring surrounding the specimen provide adequate pressure medium and insulation.
*   The measured temperature near the specimen surface is representative of the specimen's internal temperature.

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Independent Variables:** Axial stress (σ₁), Confining pressure (σ₂ = σ₃), Temperature (T), Time.
*   **Dependent Variables:** Axial strain (ε₁), Lateral strain (ε₂), Volumetric strain, Young's modulus (E), Poisson's ratio (ν), Linear thermal expansion coefficient, Permeability, Acoustic emission parameters.
*   **System Parameters:** Maximum axial/lateral load (10,000 kN), Maximum specimen pressure (318/250 MPa), Maximum temperature (600 °C), Specimen dimensions (φ200 mm × 400 mm), Machine stiffness (>9×10¹⁰ N/m) [Zhao 2012, pp. 1-2].

## Reusable Claims
*   Under high confining pressure (25 MPa), the linear thermal expansion coefficient of granite increases with temperature up to a critical point (~460 °C for Luhui Granite), beyond which it decreases due to mineral phase changes [Zhao 2012, pp. 4-5].
*   Young's modulus of both coal and granite decreases exponentially with increasing temperature under triaxial stress conditions [Zhao 2012, pp. 3-4, 5-7].
*   The application of high confining pressure suppresses thermal cracking in granite, resulting in a smaller thermal expansion coefficient compared to conditions with little or no confining pressure [Zhao 2012, pp. 4-5].
*   Gas coal exhibits extreme plasticity and continuous volume contraction under high temperature (600 °C) and pressure, a behavior attributed to void closure following thermal decomposition [Zhao 2012, pp. 3-4].
