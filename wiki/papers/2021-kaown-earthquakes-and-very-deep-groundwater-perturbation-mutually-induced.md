# Earthquakes and Very Deep Groundwater Perturbation Mutually Induced.

## One-line Summary
This study documents mutual induction between induced earthquakes and deep groundwater perturbations at approximately 4.3 km depth at the Pohang Enhanced Geothermal System site in South Korea, revealing hydraulic, chemical, and microbial responses.

## Research Question
The study aimed to (1) report unique observations of high-pressure water injection, flowback, injection-induced earthquakes, a huge water level drop, and subsequent earthquake-initiated deep pore water perturbations near the EGS wells, and (2) explore co-seismic and post-seismic interactions between deep pore water and earthquakes using hydraulic, chemical, and microbial signatures [Kaown 2021, pp. 2-3].

## Study Area / Data
The study was conducted at the Pohang Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) site in Pohang, South Korea. Two EGS wells, PX-1 and PX-2, were drilled to depths of approximately 4.3 km (measured depths of 4,362 m and 4,348 m, respectively) [Kaown 2021, pp. 10-11]. Data sources included drilling logs, hydraulic stimulation data, seismic event catalogs, acoustic image logs, water level and temperature monitoring data, and water samples for hydrochemical, isotopic (δ¹⁸O, δD, ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr, δ¹³C, ¹⁴C), and microbial community analysis [Kaown 2021, pp. 1-2, 10-11].

## Methods
Methods included surveying drilling logs and hydraulic stimulation data, simulating pore pressure diffusion around the fault, conducting acoustic image logging through the EGS wells, monitoring water levels and temperatures, and obtaining hydrochemical, isotopic, and microbial community data from water samples [Kaown 2021, pp. 1-2]. Specific analyses included wavelet analysis of water level and Earth tide data and calculation of mixing ratios using strontium isotopes and chloride [Kaown 2021, pp. 7-8, 11-12].

## Key Findings
1.  Significant mud loss (>650 m³) during drilling of PX-2 at ~3.8 km depth, indicating a fault zone, was followed by a swarm of microseismicity in November 2015 [Kaown 2021, pp. 2-3].
2.  The MW 3.2 earthquake on April 15, 2017, during hydraulic stimulation in PX-2, caused the discharge of deep formation water from PX-1, characterized by brown color, milky bubbles, higher temperature (31.7°C vs. 26.8°C pre-event), and isotopic signatures (δ¹⁸O, δD) deviating from the local meteoric water line, indicating mixing with deeper, warmer water [Kaown 2021, pp. 3-4].
3.  The MW 5.5 main shock on November 15, 2017, caused an extreme water level drop of >740 m in well PX-2, while the water level in PX-1 was 113 m below the surface [Kaown 2021, pp. 4-5].
4.  Acoustic image logging revealed the well casing of PX-2 was damaged or broken at a depth of ~3,800 m, corresponding to the fault intersection point [Kaown 2021, pp. 4-5].
5.  After the MW 5.5 earthquake, hydrochemical properties (e.g., Na, K, Cl, SO₄, Ca concentrations) and isotopic values (δ¹⁸O, δD, ¹⁴C_DIC, δ¹³C) of water from PX-1 and PX-2 became distinctly different, reflecting different aquifer conditions and mixing ratios [Kaown 2021, pp. 5-7].
6.  Microbial community structures in PX-1 and PX-2 were similar after the MW 3.2 earthquake but diverged after the MW 5.5 earthquake [Kaown 2021, pp. 5-7].
7.  Wavelet analysis showed the water level in PX-1 exhibited a distinct semi-diurnal cyclic fluctuation correlated with Earth tides, while PX-2 showed no such response, indicating a loss of hydraulic connection to deep groundwater [Kaown 2021, pp. 7-8].

## Limitations
Quantitative and thorough analysis of the more than 740 m water level drop in PX-2 after the Pohang Earthquake still requires further research [Kaown 2021, pp. 10-11]. The modeling results considering only stress changes around the mainshock rupture were insufficient to explain the observed significant water level drop [Kaown 2021, pp. 8-10].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The projected fault plane that best fit the hypocenters of the induced earthquakes corresponded with fault plane solutions of the MW 3.2 and 5.5 earthquakes [Kaown 2021, pp. 2-3]. The study assumes that the relative location between the wells and the slip model may have uncertainties [Kaown 2021, pp. 8-10]. The analysis of mixing ratios assumes binary mixing between injected surface reservoir water and deep formation water [Kaown 2021, pp. 7-8].

## Reusable Claims
-   A few hundred cubic meters of fluid injection under excessive pore pressure (~20 MPa) directly into a near-critically stressed fault zone can induce a swarm of earthquakes [Kaown 2021, pp. 10-11].
-   Fault movement during an induced earthquake can disturb surrounding formation water reservoirs, leading to mixing of deep groundwater and changes in temperature-pressure conditions, as evidenced by hydrochemical changes in discharged water [Kaown 2021, pp. 10-11].
-   Rupture of a well casing by fault slip can lead to extreme water level drops (>740 m) in the well due to discharge into the ruptured fault zone, followed by very slow recovery if the rupture point becomes clogged with fault gouge or mud [Kaown 2021, pp. 8-10].
-   The hydraulic connection of a deep well to the surrounding aquifer can be assessed by analyzing the well's water level response to Earth tides; a lack of response indicates disconnection [Kaown 2021, pp. 7-8].
-   Real-time monitoring and analysis of water chemistry, in addition to seismic monitoring, is valuable during Enhanced Geothermal System operation to understand subsurface changes and potentially prevent earthquakes [Kaown 2021, pp. 10-11].

## Connections To Other Work
The study references global examples of induced seismicity from fluid injection, including at The Geysers (California), Basel (Switzerland), Paradox Valley (Colorado), and Oklahoma (USA) [Kaown 2021, pp. 1-2]. It builds on prior hydrologic and hydrochemical studies of earthquake-induced changes at shallower depths (e.g., <100 m) [Kaown 2021, pp. 2-3]. The work is contextualized within the investigation of the 2017 Pohang earthquake's link to the EGS project [Kaown 2021, pp. 1-2, 12-13].

## Open Questions
What is the precise mechanism responsible for the extreme (>740 m) water level drop in PX-2 after the MW 5.5 earthquake, given that co-seismic stress changes alone are insufficient to explain it? [Kaown 2021, pp. 8-10]. How do the complex fault geometry and heterogeneity of the stress field influence the distribution of aftershocks relative to the mainshock slip patches? [Kaown 2021, pp. 4-5]. What are the long-term recovery trajectories and stability of the deep groundwater system following such large perturbations?
