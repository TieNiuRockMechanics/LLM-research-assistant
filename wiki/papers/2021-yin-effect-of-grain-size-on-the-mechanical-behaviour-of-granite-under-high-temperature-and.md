# Effect of Grain Size on the Mechanical Behaviour of Granite Under High Temperature and Triaxial Stresses.

## One-line Summary
Comparing coarse‑grained Luya Mountain granite with fine‑grained Luhui granite under high‑temperature (100–400 ℃) triaxial stresses reveals that coarser, more heterogeneous deep granite exhibits larger thermal expansion, lower elastic‑modulus thresholds, greater mechanical deterioration and higher permeability, which benefits HDR reservoir construction.

## Research Question
How do grain size, mineral composition, and micro‑structure affect the thermal expansion, elastic modulus, and failure behavior of granite under high‑temperature triaxial stress, and what implications do these differences have for hot dry rock (HDR) geothermal development? [Yin 2021, pp. 1-2; 2-3]

## Study Area / Data
- **Coarse‑grained granite**: Luya Mountain, Shanxi Province, China. Dark‑grey, coarse grains; intrusion age ≈1.9 Ga; crystallized at deep‑crustal conditions and later uplifted; outcrop area 300–400 km². Main minerals: hornblende (21%, avg. size ~8 mm, thermal expansion coeff. 8 × 10⁻⁶ ℃⁻¹), plagioclase (44%, ~6.3 mm, 4), mica (10%, ~1.9 mm, 3), quartz (23%, ~0.7 mm, 11), chlorite (2%). Room‑temperature micro‑observation shows abundant pre‑existing fractures. [Yin 2021, pp. 2-3; 3-5; 8-10]
- **Fine‑grained granite**: Pingyi, Shandong Province, China (“Luhui granite”). Grey‑white, compact; shallow, rapid‑cooling diagenesis. Main minerals: feldspar (43%, avg. size 1.5 mm, thermal expansion coeff. 6.5 × 10⁻⁶ ℃⁻¹), quartz (28%, 0.7 mm, 11), illite (25%), calcite, siderite, others. No visible fractures at room temperature. [Yin 2021, pp. 2-3; 3-5]
- Specimens: cylindrical, Φ 50 mm × 100 mm, prepared per ISRM standards; at least three specimens of each type from one rock block. [Yin 2021, pp. 2-3; 3-5]

## Methods
1. **Equipment**: 600 ℃–60 MPa HTHP rock mechanics triaxial testing machine (Taiyuan University of Technology). Axial deformation measured by two external grating displacement sensors cooled by water circulation to avoid thermal effects on measurements. [Yin 2021, pp. 3-5]
2. **Thermal expansion and elastic modulus tests**: Apply axial (σₐ) and confining (σ_c) pressures to 4 MPa; heat at 20 ℃/h to target temperatures (100, 150, 200, 250, 300, 350, 400 ℃); hold ≥2 h. At each temperature, keep σ_c constant, increase axial pressure to 30 MPa in 2 MPa steps; calculate elastic modulus from elastic section of stress–strain curve. Axial displacement recorded continuously during heating to obtain thermal strain and thermal expansion coefficient. [Yin 2021, pp. 3-5; 5-7]
3. **Complete stress–strain failure test**: Applied at σ_c＝25 MPa, 400 ℃. Hydrostatic pressure of 25 MPa first; heat to 400 ℃, hold 2 h; then increase axial pressure under constant σ_c until failure. [Yin 2021, pp. 7-8]
4. **Micro‑structural observation**: Thin sections observed under Leica polarizing microscope. [Yin 2021, pp. 8-10]
5. **Comparative analysis**: Coarse‑ vs. fine‑grained granite; results compared with literature data for the same fine‑grained granite at σ_c＝25 MPa. [Yin 2021, pp. 5-7; 7-8]

## Key Findings
1. **Thermal expansion**: Coarse‑grained granite shows a linear increase of thermal expansion coefficient from 1.09 × 10⁻⁵ ℃⁻¹ (100 ℃) to 9.44 × 10⁻⁵ ℃⁻¹ (400 ℃) at σ_c＝4 MPa—a nearly eightfold increase. The coefficient is on average 1.52 times that of fine‑grained granite, with the maximum difference at 400 ℃. [Yin 2021, pp. 5-7]
2. **Confining pressure effect**: Increasing confining pressure reduces the thermal expansion coefficient, but the effect is markedly smaller for coarse‑grained granite than for fine‑grained granite. [Yin 2021, pp. 5-7]
3. **Elastic modulus threshold**: The elastic modulus of coarse‑grained granite increases slowly with temperature up to ≈300 ℃, then decreases sharply above that; the fine‑grained threshold is ≈350 ℃. [Yin 2021, pp. 7-8]
4. **Modulus ratios**: Fine‑grained modulus is 1.4–2.6 times that of coarse‑grained granite; the difference grows with temperature and confining pressure. [Yin 2021, pp. 7-8]
5. **Failure behavior (400 ℃, σ_c＝25 MPa)**: Coarse‑grained granite exhibits lower peak strength (180 MPa vs. 230 MPa), lower elastic modulus (10.7 GPa vs. 46 GPa), larger peak strain (0.014 vs. 0.012), and more pronounced elasto‑plastic transition than fine‑grained granite. [Yin 2021, pp. 7-8]
6. **Micro‑structural control**: Larger crystal particles, more complex mineralogy, abundant pre‑existing fractures, and extreme heterogeneity cause more intense, large‑scale thermal cracking in coarse granite, leading to greater mechanical deterioration and higher permeability under HTHP conditions. [Yin 2021, pp. 8-10; 10-11]
7. **Implications for HDR**: The low Young’s modulus, large thermal expansion, and readiness to form thermal fracture networks make coarse‑grained granite bodies a favorable geological foundation for efficient, low‑cost, large‑scale artificial geothermal reservoirs. [Yin 2021, pp. 10-11; 11-12]

## Limitations
- Tests conducted up to 400 ℃ and 25 MPa confining pressure; actual deep HDR conditions (≥3000 m, 400–500 ℃, higher σ_c) may differ. [Yin 2021, pp. 1-2]
- Only two granite types from specific locations compared; generalizability to all deep granites is not confirmed. [Yin 2021, pp. 2-3; 11-12]
- The thermal cracking mechanical model assumes one‑dimensional stress and ignores multi‑axial interactions. [Yin 2021, pp. 10-11; 12-13]
- Plasticity effects were considered limited under the test differential stress; higher stresses may introduce additional mechanisms. [Yin 2021, pp. 10-10]
- Permeability enhancement is inferred from thermal cracking and mechanical degradation; direct permeability measurements under identical HTHP conditions are not reported in this paper. [Yin 2021, pp. 10-11; 11-12]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The coarse‑grained granite from Luya Mountain is representative of deep HDR masses because of its deep diagenetic history (slow cooling, hypersthene metamorphic product, large grain size). [Yin 2021, pp. 2-3]
- Grain size and mineral heterogeneity are the primary micro‑structural controls on thermal and mechanical behavior, while cement type and pre‑existing fractures modulate the response. [Yin 2021, pp. 8-10; 10-11]
- Thermal expansion is driven by lattice potential energy; within 400 ℃, the lattice remains stable (atoms within potential walls). [Yin 2021, pp. 10-10]
- In the elastic‑modulus tests, the axial stress increment up to 30 MPa is small enough to avoid significant plastic or failure effects, so the measured slope represents the elastic modulus. [Yin 2021, pp. 5-7]
- The one‑dimensional thermal stress model provides a first‑order approximation of interface stresses during hydraulic fracturing. [Yin 2021, pp. 10-11; 12-13]
- The comparison with previous fine‑grained data (Zhao et al. 2012) is valid because the same equipment and similar protocols were used. [Yin 2021, pp. 5-7]

## Reusable Claims
1. **For coarse‑grained granite (e.g., Luya Mountain type) under σ_c＝4 MPa, the thermal expansion coefficient increases linearly with temperature, rising from ~1.1 × 10⁻⁵ ℃⁻¹ at 100 ℃ to ~9.4 × 10⁻⁵ ℃⁻¹ at 400 ℃.** *Condition*: heating rate 20 ℃/h, 2‑h hold; sample cylinder Φ50 × 100 mm. [Yin 2021, pp. 5-7]
2. **The thermal expansion coefficient of coarse‑grained granite averages 1.52 times that of fine‑grained granite across 100–400 ℃, with the maximum difference at the highest tested temperature.** *Condition*: σ_c＝4 MPa; mineralogies as given in Table 1. [Yin 2021, pp. 5-7]
3. **Increasing confining pressure reduces the thermal expansion coefficient, but the suppression is weaker in coarse‑grained granite than in fine‑grained granite.** *Condition*: comparison σ_c＝4 MPa vs. 25 MPa; 100–400 ℃. [Yin 2021, pp. 5-7]
4. **The elastic modulus threshold temperature (T_c), above which modulus decreases with increasing temperature, is approximately 300 ℃ for the tested coarse‑grained granite and 350 ℃ for the fine‑grained granite.** *Condition*: σ_c＝4 MPa, axial loading up to 30 MPa. [Yin 2021, pp. 7-8]
5. **At 400 ℃ and σ_c＝25 MPa, the coarse‑grained granite shows 28% lower peak strength, 330% lower elastic modulus, and 17% larger peak strain than the fine‑grained granite, indicating more severe thermal‑mechanical degradation.** *Condition*: same heating protocol, direct failure test. [Yin 2021, pp. 7-8]
6. **Pre‑existing fractures in coarse‑grained granite appear to close by ~150 ℃, beyond which the thermal expansion behavior is dominated by lattice expansion of the constituent minerals.** *Condition*: based on convergence/divergence of thermal expansion coefficients between coarse and fine types. [Yin 2021, pp. 10-10]
7. **Coarse‑grained granite bodies, owing to low Young’s modulus, large thermal expansion, extreme micro‑structural heterogeneity, and readiness to develop large‑scale thermal fracture networks, provide favorable geological conditions for efficient artificial HDR reservoir construction.** *Condition*: applicable to HDR geothermal development where sustainable fluid circulation and low‑cost hydraulic stimulation are required. [Yin 2021, pp. 10-11; 11-12]

## Connections To Other Work
- **Literature on granite thermal‑mechanical properties**: Previous studies on Westerly, climax, charcoal, Luhui, Qinling, and Jalore granites show similar trends (decreasing elastic modulus, compressive strength, tensile strength with temperature; increasing specific heat and thermal expansion). This work extends those findings by explicitly comparing grain‑size effects and by testing under in‑situ triaxial conditions. [Yin 2021, pp. 1-2]
- **Yang et al. (2017)**: Used CT and AE for the same Luhui fine‑grained granite; their micro‑structural characterization is used here as the fine‑grained reference. [Yin 2021, pp. 2-3]
- **Zhao et al. (2012, 2015, 2017)**: Measured thermal expansion, mechanical properties, and permeability of the same fine‑grained granite under high temperature and triaxial stress; the present study uses their 25 MPa thermal expansion data as a comparison and their conceptual framework for thermal cracking. [Yin 2021, pp. 1-2; 5-7; 10-11]
- **Gautam et al. (2018)**: Identified 300 ℃ as a damage threshold for Jalore granite; the present 300 ℃ threshold for coarse granite is consistent with this general temperature range. [Yin 2021, pp. 1-2; 7-8]
- **Wan et al. (2008), Zhao et al. (2012)**: Provided evidence that confining pressure restricts thermally induced fracture aperture, reducing thermal expansion—confirmed here for both granite types. [Yin 2021, pp. 5-7]
