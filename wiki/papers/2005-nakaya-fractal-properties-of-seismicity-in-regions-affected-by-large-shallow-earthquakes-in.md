# Fractal properties of seismicity in regions affected by large, shallow earthquakes in western Japan Implications for fault formation processes based on a binary fractal fracture network model

## One-line Summary
Anomalous decreases in multifractal dimension Dq and increases in b-value are identified as common seismic responses prior to large shallow earthquakes in western Japan, indicating criticality around asperities and a transition in fault formation processes.

## Research Question
The study investigates whether common anomalous responses in the fractal properties of seismicity exist prior to large, shallow earthquakes and how these properties relate to fault formation processes, specifically examining the 1995 Southern Hyogo Earthquake (SHE) and the 2000 Western Tottori Earthquake (WTE) in western Japan [Nakaya 2005, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The analysis focuses on five target regions in western Japan affected by the SHE and WTE events, which occurred under a common regional tectonic stress field with an east-west maximum principal stress direction [Nakaya 2005, pp. 2-2]. The data are from the Japan Meteorological Agency (JMA) earthquake catalog for the period 1980–2001, including occurrence time, epicenter location, hypocenter depth (d < 30 km), and magnitude (M > Mc, where the cutoff magnitude Mc is 2–2.2) [Nakaya 2005, pp. 2-2]. The five regions are the SHE region, the region around the Yamazaki fault, and three WTE regions (A, B, and C) [Nakaya 2005, pp. 2-4].

## Methods
The study employs transient and cumulative analyses using moving time windows. The multifractal dimension Dq is calculated using the correlation integral method [Nakaya 2005, pp. 2-4]. The b-value of the Gutenberg-Richter frequency–magnitude relationship is determined using the maximum likelihood method [Nakaya 2005, pp. 2-4]. Seismic energy release Er is estimated from the empirical relationship log E = 1.5M + 4.8 [Nakaya 2005, pp. 4-6]. The analysis uses a constant number of events nw per moving window for transient analysis and a constant increment nd for cumulative analysis, with parameters specified for each region (e.g., nw = 100–300, ns = 5–10, nd = 5–10) [Nakaya 2005, pp. 4-6]. The scale range r for Dq is set to 1.0–3.98 km to avoid boundary effects and location errors [Nakaya 2005, pp. 4-6].

## Key Findings
A common anomalous seismic response prior to both the SHE and WTE main shocks is a temporal decrease in Dq for a wide range of q and an increase in b-value, associated with decreasing seismic energy release [Nakaya 2005, pp. 6-9]. This pattern reflects a transition from a dispersive rupture process to an anomalous rupture process, where the growth of the fault network through fault linkage is abruptly restrained in the fault formation process leading up to the main shock [Nakaya 2005, pp. 13-14]. An anomalous response prior to the SHE was also identified in the WTE region, suggesting a common tectonic stress influence [Nakaya 2005, pp. 9-9]. The cumulative analysis using a binary fractal fracture network model shows that fault network growth follows different paths (modes M1–M4) before and after main shocks, with changes corresponding to critical states of fault nucleation [Nakaya 2005, pp. 11-13].

## Limitations
The scale range for Dq is limited to 1.0–3.98 km due to boundary effects and location errors in hypocenters [Nakaya 2005, pp. 4-6]. The study assumes that the statistics of earthquake spatial distribution and magnitude are equivalent to those of fault locations and size distributions, which may not fully account for fault linkages decreasing the number of faults [Nakaya 2005, pp. 11-13]. The analysis relies on earthquake catalog data with standard errors for epicenter locations (~0.5 min) and depths (~2 km) [Nakaya 2005, pp. 2-2].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The study assumes that the spatial distribution of hypocenters reflects fault locations and that earthquake magnitude relates to fault size via the empirical relationship log S = M − 4.07 [Nakaya 2005, pp. 2-2, 11-13]. It is hypothesized that a minimal block size damaged by fractures exists, and that the binary fractal fracture network model can describe fault network geometry and connectivity [Nakaya 2005, pp. 11-13]. The seismicity in the study regions is governed by a common regional tectonic stress field [Nakaya 2005, pp. 2-2].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Dq**: Multifractal dimension of spatial distribution of hypocenters, calculated via correlation integral.
- **b-value**: Slope of the Gutenberg-Richter frequency–magnitude relationship.
- **Er**: Seismic energy release, estimated from magnitude.
- **nw**: Constant number of events per moving time window (e.g., 100–300).
- **ns**: Number of events for shifting consecutive time windows (e.g., 5–10).
- **nd**: Constant increment of events for cumulative analysis (e.g., 5–10).
- **Mc**: Cutoff magnitude (2–2.2).
- **r**: Scaling radius for Dq calculation (1.0–3.98 km).
- **a**: Fractal dimension of fault size distribution, equivalent to 2b.

## Reusable Claims
- An anomalous decrease in multifractal dimension Dq and an increase in b-value are common seismic responses prior to large, shallow earthquakes in regions under common tectonic stress, indicating criticality around asperities [Nakaya 2005, pp. 6-9].
- The transition in fractal dimensions of faults inferred from sequential seismic events reflects a change from a dispersive rupture process to an anomalous rupture process, with fault network growth through linkage abruptly restrained before main shocks [Nakaya 2005, pp. 13-14].
- The binary fractal fracture network model can describe fault network growth using four fundamental modes (M1–M4) based on the relationship between D2 and a, with changes corresponding to critical states of fault nucleation [Nakaya 2005, pp. 11-13].

## Connections To Other Work
The study references the asperity model for earthquake sources [Kanamori, 1981; Lay and Kanamori, 1981] and prior research on fractal properties of seismicity and fracture networks [e.g., Kagan and Knopoff, 1980; Hirata and Imoto, 1991; Nakaya and Hashimoto, 2002]. It connects to rock fracture experiments showing decreases in D2 and increases in b-value during prenucleation stages [Lockner, 1993; Hirata et al., 1987a, 1987b; Zang et al., 1996]. The binary fractal fracture network model is based on work by Darcel et al. [2003] and Nakaya et al. [2003].
