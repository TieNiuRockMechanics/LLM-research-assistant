# Paleoproterozoic Tectonic Evolution of the North China Craton.

## One-line Summary
A synthesis model proposing that the Eastern and Western Blocks of the North China Craton collided initially at 2.5 Ga during an arc/continent collision, not at 1.8 Ga as previously suggested, and that subsequent orogenic and extensional events shaped its evolution until 1.4 Ga. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2]

## Research Question
When did the amalgamation of the North China Craton occur—at 1.85 Ga or 2.5 Ga—and how can the geological, geochronological, and petrological records of the craton be reconciled into a coherent Neoarchean–Mesoproterozoic evolutionary model? [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2, 13-14]

## Study Area / Data
The North China Craton (NCC), covering ~1.7 million km² in northeastern China, Inner Mongolia, the Yellow Sea, and Korea. It comprises the Eastern Block, Western Block (Ordos Block), and the intervening Central Orogenic Belt (COB). Data sources include regional geology, geochronology (U–Pb, SHRIMP, Sm–Nd, Ar–Ar, Pb–Pb), structural geology, petrology of high-pressure granulites, stratigraphy of foreland basins and cover sequences, and igneous rock geochemistry. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2, 2-5, 5-7, 12-13]

## Methods
- Regional synthesis of published geology, geochronology, and tectonic data. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2]  
- Interpretation of upper- and lower-intercept U–Pb zircon ages to distinguish igneous crystallization (2.70–2.50 Ga) from later metamorphic lead-loss events (2.00–1.80 Ga). [Kusky 2003, pp. 2-2]  
- Structural, petrological, and stratigraphic correlations among the Central Orogenic Belt, northern granulite belts, and foreland basin sequences. [Kusky 2003, pp. 5-7]  
- Palinspastic reconstruction of tectonic stages from 2.75 Ga to 1.4 Ga. [Kusky 2003, pp. 7-8]  
- Integration of 40Ar/39Ar, Sm–Nd, and SHRIMP ages to constrain metamorphic and cooling histories. [Kusky 2003, pp. 2-2, 15-15]  

## Key Findings
1. **Collision at 2.5 Ga**: The Eastern and Western Blocks amalgamated through arc-continent collision, forming a foreland basin (Qinglong) on the Eastern Block and a granulite-facies belt on the Western Block. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2, 5-7]  
2. **Post-collisional rifting (2.5–2.4 Ga)**: Rapid extension led to mafic dyke swarms, flood basalts, and rift basins (e.g., Hutuo) across the central NCC, with an ocean opening along the northern margin. [Kusky 2003, pp. 8-9]  
3. **Arc accretion at c. 2.3 Ga**: An arc terrane (Inner Mongolia–Northern Hebei Orogen, IMNHO) collided with the north margin, forming a 1600-km-long, 200-km-wide granulite-facies plateau (Hengshan Plateau). [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2, 9-9]  
4. **Andean-style margin (2.20–1.85 Ga)**: The northern margin evolved into an accretionary arc with back-arc basin, accompanied by plutonism and metamorphic reworking. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2, 9-10]  
5. **1.9–1.85 Ga deformation**: A pulse of convergent deformation linked to an outboard collision closed the back-arc basin, producing foreland basin deposits (basal Changcheng Series) and reworking the northern granulite belt. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2, 10-12]  
6. **1.85–1.4 Ga extension**: Widespread rifting, emplacement of anorogenic rapakivi granites, mafic dykes, and aulacogens; final uplift and retrograde metamorphism of the northern granulites; opening of an ocean along the SW margin until 1.5 Ga. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2, 13-14]  
7. **Dual exhumation of high-pressure granulites**: High-pressure granulites are restricted to regions that experienced uplift at both 2.5 Ga and 1.8 Ga, exposing rocks from deeper crustal levels. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2, 13-14]

## Limitations
- Geochronological data are unevenly distributed, making regional correlation tentative. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2]  
- Single vs. multi-grain zircon population interpretations remain contested, particularly for lower-intercept ages as primary metamorphism vs. lead-loss overprints. [Kusky 2003, pp. 2-2]  
- High-pressure granulite petrogenesis is restricted to areas exhumed twice, potentially biasing the metamorphic record. [Kusky 2003, pp. 13-14]  
- Precise ages and stratigraphic correlation within the Changcheng Series are sparse. [Kusky 2003, pp. 12-13]  
- The model does not explain all petrological observations for a 1.85 Ga collisional event in the COB. [Kusky 2003, pp. 2-2]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Arc and ophiolitic rocks cannot remain undeformed for 700 Ma before accretion; deformation soon after formation is more plausible. [Kusky 2003, pp. 7-8]  
- Upper-intercept U–Pb ages represent rock formation, lower-intercept ages represent metamorphic lead-loss events, not necessarily a single collision. [Kusky 2003, pp. 2-2]  
- The N–S striking Central Orogenic Belt structures are late Archean; the 1.9–1.8 Ga metamorphic imprints are related to the E–W trending Inner Mongolia–Hebei orogen. [Kusky 2003, pp. 13-14]  
- High-pressure granulite belts require exhumation from lower crustal depths via multiple episodes. [Kusky 2003, pp. 13-14]  
- Tectonic processes in the Precambrian operated on timescales similar to Phanerozoic orogens. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Zircon age populations: upper intercepts 2.70–2.50 Ga; lower intercepts 2.00–1.80 Ga. [Kusky 2003, pp. 2-2]  
- High-pressure granulite P-T conditions: 1.2–1.0 GPa, 700–800 °C. [Kusky 2003, pp. 5-7]  
- Hengshan Plateau dimensions: ~1600 km long, 200 km wide. [Kusky 2003, pp. 1-2]  
- Timing of mafic dyke swarms: 2.50–2.40 Ga and 1.90–1.80 Ga. [Kusky 2003, pp. 5-7]  
- Changcheng Series basal age: 1848 ± 39 Ma (Pb–Pb). [Kusky 2003, pp. 12-13]  
- Ar–Ar ages on metamorphic garnets: 2510 ± 50 Ma. [Kusky 2003, pp. 7-8]

## Reusable Claims
1. **Claim**: The Eastern and Western Blocks of the NCC amalgamated at **2.5 Ga** via arc-continent collision. *Conditions*: Based on regional syntheses; valid for interpreting Archean orogens where long quiescence is unlikely. *Limitations*: Requires acceptance that 1.8 Ga granulite overprints are not the primary collision signature. [Kusky 2003, pp. 7-8, 13-14]  
2. **Claim**: High-pressure granulite belts can result from **two discrete exhumation episodes** at different times. *Conditions*: Applicable in polyphase orogens; here observed where 2.5 Ga and 1.8 Ga uplifts overlap. *Limitations*: Not a universal mechanism; requires geochronological confirmation. [Kusky 2003, pp. 13-14]  
3. **Claim**: Post-orogenic extension within 10–50 Myr of collision can produce **mafic dyke swarms, flood basalts, and rift basins** overprinting orogenic structures. *Conditions*: Recorded in the NCC between 2.5–2.4 Ga; may apply to other Precambrian cratons. *Limitations*: Requires precise geochronology to separate extension from late collisional processes. [Kusky 2003, pp. 8-9]  
4. **Claim**: **Foreland basin deposits** (Changcheng Series) record collision-related subsidence at 1.85 Ga, transitioning to a cratonic cover sequence. *Conditions*: Clast provenance, lateral facies changes, and basal unconformities support this interpretation. *Limitations*: Poor age control on some formations. [Kusky 2003, pp. 12-13]  
5. **Claim**: The **loss of a thick lithospheric root** in the Eastern Block occurred after the Ordovician, as shown by kimberlite xenoliths and Tertiary basalts. *Conditions*: Not part of the Paleoproterozoic model but a cratonic characteristic. *Limitations*: Exact timing and mechanism remain enigmatic. [Kusky 2003, pp. 2-5]

## Open Questions
- *Not confirmed*: How did the 1.9–1.85 Ga deformation relate to far-field convergence outboard of the IMNHO?  
- *Not confirmed*: What caused the 700-km-long high-pressure granulite belt to be oriented ENE, parallel to the younger orogen rather than the older COB?  
- *Not confirmed*: Are there intermediate-age igneous rocks (between 2.70 and 2.50 Ga) that could modify the accretionary model?  
- *Not confirmed*: What is the precise mechanism and timing of Phanerozoic lithospheric thinning in the Eastern Block?  
- *Not confirmed*: How do the Paleoproterozoic events correlate with the Sino-Korea boundary orogen and the Liaohe Group rifting?
