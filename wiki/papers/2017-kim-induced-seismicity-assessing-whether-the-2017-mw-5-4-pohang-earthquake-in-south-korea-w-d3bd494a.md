# Induced Seismicity: Assessing Whether the 2017 Mw 5.4 Pohang Earthquake in South Korea Was an Induced Event.

## One-line Summary
The 2017 Mw 5.4 Pohang earthquake in South Korea was likely induced by fluid injection at the Pohang Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) site, making it the largest known induced earthquake at an EGS site globally.

## Research Question
Was the 2017 Mw 5.4 Pohang earthquake an induced event caused by fluid injection at the Pohang Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) site?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Pohang basin in southeastern Korea, which lies within the Eurasian Plate and contains NNE-striking strike-slip and normal faults reactivated under the current compressional regime [Kim 2017, pp. 1-1]. The Pohang EGS site is located within this basin, with geology comprising Quaternary alluvia, Miocene mudstone, Cretaceous to Eocene sedimentary and igneous rocks, and Permian granodiorite with gabbroic dykes [Kim 2017, pp. 1-1]. Data include continuous seismic waveforms from a permanent station (PHA2) and eight temporary stations, fluid injection records from the Ministry of Trade, Industry, and Energy (MTIE), and the Korea Meteorological Administration earthquake catalog [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2].

## Methods
Methods included applying a matched-filter analysis to continuous waveforms to detect microearthquakes, precisely relocating the earthquake sequence using the hypoDD software package, determining focal mechanism solutions, and statistical plane-fitting of hypocenters to determine rupture plane orientations [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2, 2-3]. The temporal and spatial relationships between seismicity and fluid injection were analyzed [Kim 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
The Pohang earthquake was induced by fluid injection from the EGS site, which was injected directly into a near-critically stressed subsurface fault zone [Kim 2017, pp. 1-1]. It is the largest known induced earthquake at an EGS site worldwide [Kim 2017, pp. 1-1]. The timing of earthquakes coincides with fluid injection phases, and seismic activity decreased rapidly after injection stopped [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2]. The hypocenters of foreshocks and the mainshock were located immediately adjacent to the bottom of the production well (PX1) [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2]. The injected fluid volume (12,800 m³) was much smaller than the volume predicted by theory (~4.7 × 10⁶ m³) to induce an Mw 5.4 earthquake, indicating that direct injection into a fault can trigger larger events than current models predict [Kim 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Limitations
The study notes that detailed investigation of the geological, geochemical, and geophysical properties of the Pohang EGS site is needed to improve understanding of earthquake-inducing processes [Kim 2017, pp. 2-3]. The permeability structure of fault zones is highly heterogeneous, which complicates predictions [Kim 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The assessment assumes the fault was near-critically stressed before injection [Kim 2017, pp. 1-1]. The analysis assumes that the matched-filter method and hypocenter relocation accurately capture the seismicity sequence [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Injected fluid volume**: 12,800 m³ total [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Injection rates**: 1.00 to 46.83 liter s⁻¹ [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Earthquake magnitudes**: Mainshock Mw 5.4 (ML 5.4), largest foreshock ML ≤ 2.6, largest prior induced event ML 3.1 [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Hypocentral depths**: Mainshock ~4.5 km, most sequence 4-6 km [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Fault orientations**: Main segment N36°E/65°NW, subsidiary segment N19°E/60°NW [Kim 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Reusable Claims
- Fluid injected directly into a near-critically stressed fault zone can induce an earthquake larger than predicted by empirical volume-magnitude relationships [Kim 2017, pp. 2-3].
- The absence of significant seismicity prior to drilling and the immediate response of seismicity to fluid injection are key indicators of an induced event [Kim 2017, pp. 2-3].
- The spatial relationship between hypocenters and the injection well bottom is critical evidence for direct fault-zone injection [Kim 2017, pp. 1-2].

## Connections To Other Work
The study references McGarr's (2014) equation relating maximum induced earthquake magnitude to injected fluid volume [Kim 2017, pp. 2-3]. It compares the Pohang event to the largest previously reported induced earthquake at an EGS site (ML 3.4 in Basel, Switzerland) [Kim 2017, pp. 1-1]. The assessment uses Frohlich et al.'s (2016) system for classifying induced earthquakes [Kim 2017, pp. 2-3].
