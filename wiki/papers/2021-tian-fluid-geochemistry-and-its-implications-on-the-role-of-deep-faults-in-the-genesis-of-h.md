# Fluid Geochemistry and Its Implications on the Role of Deep Faults in the Genesis of High Temperature Systems in the Eastern Edge of the Qinghai Tibet Plateau.

## One-line Summary
A comparative geochemical study reveals that deep extensional faults facilitate high-temperature geothermal systems through deep fluid circulation and mantle volatile ascent, while thrust faults host only low-temperature systems due to shallow, closed circulation.

## Research Question
What is the role of deep faults in the genesis of high-temperature geothermal systems in the eastern edge of the Qinghai Tibet Plateau, and why does the Longmenshan Fault lack high-temperature geothermal manifestations compared to other major faults like the Xianshuihe Fault?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the West Sichuan Plateau (WSP) in the eastern part of the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau, specifically comparing geothermal fluids from the Xianshuihe-Anninghe Fault system and the Longmenshan-Minjiang Fault system. Data were collected from 20 hot springs and geothermal wells, including nine from the Xianshuihe fault, five from the Anninghe fault, four from the Longmenshan fault, and two from the Minjiang fault [Tian 2021, pp. 3-3]. Some data from the Anninghe, Longmenshan, and Minjiang faults were sourced from previous studies [Tian 2021, pp. 3-3].

## Methods
The study employed a comprehensive chemical and isotopic analysis of geothermal water and gas. Methods included:
- **Hydrochemical analysis**: Major cations and anions were measured using ICP-AES, ICP-MS, and ion chromatography [Tian 2021, pp. 3-4].
- **Stable isotope analysis**: δ²H and δ¹⁸O of water were measured using a laser absorption spectrometer [Tian 2021, pp. 3-4].
- **Gas composition and isotope analysis**: Gas compositions were determined via mass spectrometry; δ¹³C of CO₂ and CH₄ were measured using GC-IRMS; ³He/⁴He and ⁴He/²⁰Ne ratios were analyzed using a noble gas mass spectrometer [Tian 2021, pp. 3-4].
- **Geochemical modeling**: The SOLVEQ program was used to determine mineral saturation states. The FixAl method was applied to correct for aluminum concentration and CO₂ degassing to estimate reservoir temperatures [Tian 2021, pp. 4-5].
- **Cation geothermometry**: Na-K, K-Mg, and Na-K-Ca geothermometers were used to estimate reservoir temperatures for samples without silica data [Tian 2021, pp. 9-9].

## Key Findings
1.  **Reservoir temperatures and circulation depths**: Geothermal systems along the Xianshuihe Fault have high reservoir temperatures (up to 260°C) and deep circulation (up to 8 km), while those along the Longmenshan Fault have low reservoir temperatures (<150°C) and shallow circulation (<4 km) [Tian 2021, pp. 1-2, 9-9].
2.  **Fluid geochemistry**: Waters from the Xianshuihe Fault are predominantly HCO₃-Na type, whereas those from the Longmenshan Fault are often SO₄-Ca·Mg type with higher sulfate concentrations [Tian 2021, pp. 4-5, 5-5].
3.  **Isotopic characteristics**: Geothermal waters from the Longmenshan Fault show minimal oxygen isotope shift and enriched isotopic compositions compared to the Xianshuihe Fault, indicating shallower circulation and less intense water-rock interaction [Tian 2021, pp. 5-5].
4.  **Gas geochemistry**: Gases from the Xianshuihe Fault are CO₂-dominated with metamorphic δ¹³C signatures and higher mantle helium contributions (R/Ra up to 2.38 in Kangding). Gases from the Longmenshan and Anninghe faults are N₂-dominated with low CO₂ and predominantly crustal helium (R/Ra < 1) [Tian 2021, pp. 6-7, 7-9].
5.  **Fault control mechanism**: Extensional fracture systems in strike-slip faults (Xianshuihe, Anninghe) act as conduits for deep fluid and volatiles, enhancing heat convection. In contrast, closed fracture systems in thrust faults (Longmenshan) limit circulation depth and heat transfer [Tian 2021, pp. 1-2, 9-11].

## Limitations
- Silica concentrations, critical for geothermometric modeling, were not reported for some hot springs, requiring the use of cation geothermometers as an alternative [Tian 2021, pp. 3-4].
- Some hydrochemical and gas data for the Anninghe, Longmenshan, and Minjiang faults were sourced from previous studies (Zhou, 2011; Chen et al., 2014), which may involve different sampling or analytical protocols [Tian 2021, pp. 3-3].
- The circulation depth calculation assumes a uniform regional geothermal gradient (32.69°C/km) measured in the Aba area, which may not be perfectly representative across the entire study area [Tian 2021, pp. 9-9].
- The study acknowledges that the heat source for the Longmenshan thrust faults "remains to be studied" [Tian 2021, pp. 9-11].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The geothermal waters are recharged by infiltrated meteoric water, as indicated by their stable isotope compositions falling along the Global Meteoric Water Line [Tian 2021, pp. 5-5].
- The FixAl modeling assumes geothermal waters are in equilibrium with selected Al-bearing minerals (e.g., albite) at reservoir conditions [Tian 2021, pp. 4-5].
- The calculation of mantle He contribution assumes mixing between air, crustal, and mantle end-members, using the model of Sano (1985) [Tian 2021, pp. 7-9].
- The presence of oxygen in gas samples is attributed solely to air contamination during sampling/analysis, with a fixed N₂/O₂ ratio of 3.71 used for correction [Tian 2021, pp. 7-9].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Reservoir Temperature**: Estimated via FixAl modeling and cation geothermometers (Na-K, K-Mg, Na-K-Ca).
- **Circulation Depth**: Calculated from reservoir temperature, surface temperature, and geothermal gradient.
- **Stable Isotopes (δ²H, δ¹⁸O)**: Indicators of water origin and water-rock interaction.
- **Gas Composition (CO₂, N₂, CH₄, He)**: Indicators of fluid origin and degassing processes.
- **Helium Isotopes (R/Ra, ⁴He/²⁰Ne)**: Used to distinguish mantle, crustal, and atmospheric helium sources.
- **Carbon Isotopes (δ¹³C_CO₂)**: Used to identify the source of CO₂ (e.g., metamorphic, organic, mantle).
- **Hydrochemical Type**: Determined from major ion concentrations (e.g., HCO₃-Na, SO₄-Ca·Mg).

## Reusable Claims
- In the non-volcanic West Sichuan Plateau, the formation of high-temperature (>150°C) geothermal systems is critically dependent on the presence of deep, extensional fracture systems within lithospheric-scale strike-slip faults, which facilitate deep fluid circulation and the ascent of mantle-derived volatiles [Tian 2021, pp. 1-2, 9-11].
- The Longmenshan Thrust Fault, characterized by closed fracture systems, supports only shallow geothermal fluid circulation (<4 km depth), resulting in low-temperature (<150°C) geothermal systems with minimal mantle volatile input [Tian 2021, pp. 1-2, 9-11].
- A high helium isotopic ratio (R/Ra > 1) in geothermal gases is a diagnostic indicator of deep, extended fracture systems that act as conduits for mantle volatiles and are associated with high-temperature geothermal reservoirs [Tian 2021, pp. 7-9].
- The absence of a significant oxygen isotope shift in geothermal waters from a fault zone suggests relatively shallow circulation and/or limited high-temperature water-rock interaction, correlating with lower reservoir temperatures [Tian 2021, pp. 5-5].

## Connections To Other Work
- The study builds on previous geothermal investigations in the region, citing reservoir temperature estimates for Ganzi (180-210°C) and Kangding (260°C) from Fan et al. (2019) and Guo et al. (2017) [Tian 2021, pp. 2-3].
- It references the work of Ai et al. (2021) on shear frictional heat along the Xianshuihe Fault as a controlling heat source for geothermal fields [Tian 2021, pp. 9-11].
- The interpretation of metamorphic CO₂ sources connects to the regional tectonic history involving the Paleo-Tethys and Neo-Tethys oceans and subsequent continental collision [Tian 2021, pp. 6-7].
- The use of the FixAl method follows the theoretical framework established by Pang and Reed (1998) [Tian 2021, pp. 4-5].

## Open Questions
- What is the specific heat source mechanism for the geothermal systems along the Longmenshan Thrust Fault, given the lack of evidence for significant shear frictional heat or underlying molten crust in this study?
- How does the heterogeneity of fracture systems within a single fault zone (e.g., the Xianshuihe Fault) lead to the observed spatial variation in reservoir temperatures?
- What is the quantitative contribution of shear frictional heat versus other potential heat sources (e.g., radiogenic heat, lower crustal flow) to the high-temperature systems in the study area?
