# Experimental Research of the Effect of Freezing-Thawing Cycles on Acoustic Emission Characteristics of Granite.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how freezing-thawing cycles alter the acoustic emission characteristics, frost resistance, and failure behavior of granite specimens under uniaxial compression.

## Research Question
How do freezing-thawing cycles affect the acoustic emission (AE) characteristics, frost resistance indices, and failure mechanisms of granite in cold regions?

## Study Area / Data
- **Rock Type:** Granite specimens collected from a railway tunnel in Jilin, China. The rock is slightly weathered, light brownish-gray, with a density of 2.62 g/cm³ and a natural water content of 0.20%. Mineral composition includes quartz (25%), potassium feldspar (50%), plagioclase (20%), and biotite (5%) [Su 2019, pp. 1-2].
- **Specimen Preparation:** Standard cylindrical specimens (height 100 mm, diameter 50 mm) were prepared from the same intact granite block, meeting the standards of "Standard for tests method of engineering rock masses" (GB/T50266-99) [Su 2019, pp. 2-5].
- **Experimental Groups:** 18 specimens were divided into 6 groups (A-F), each with 3 specimens, subjected to 0, 10, 30, 60, 90, and 120 freezing-thawing cycles, respectively [Su 2019, pp. 2-5].

## Methods
- **Freezing-Thawing Process:** Specimens were vacuum-saturated, then subjected to cycles of freezing at -20°C for 12 hours and thawing at 20°C for 12 hours (24-hour cycle). After cycles, specimens were oven-dried at 105-110°C for 24 hours [Su 2019, pp. 2-5].
- **Loading and AE Testing:** Uniaxial compression tests were performed using a YAW-2000 servo-controlled rock mechanics loading system (displacement control at 0.1 mm/min) coupled with a DS5-16B acoustic emission monitoring system. Eight AE sensors (RS-2A, center frequency 150 kHz) were symmetrically arranged on the specimen surfaces. AE sampling frequency was 3 MHz, with a threshold adjusted to 50 dB [Su 2019, pp. 2-5].
- **Data Analysis:** AE parameters analyzed include hit counts, ring-down counts, peak frequency (via Fast Fourier Transform), and energy probability density. Frost resistance coefficient (Rd) and mass loss rate (Km) were calculated [Su 2019, pp. 2-5, 5-8, 8-10].

## Key Findings
1.  **Frost Resistance and Mass Loss:** The frost resistance coefficient (Rd) of granite decreases with increasing freezing-thawing cycles, while the mass loss rate (Km) increases. The initial cycles (0-10) show a slower decrease in Rd but a significant increase in Km, indicating damage primarily on the specimen surface initially [Su 2019, pp. 2-5].
2.  **Failure Modes:** Failure modes change with cycles. Unfrozen specimens (0 cycles) exhibit shear failure. With increasing cycles, end effects become more pronounced, and specimens show more spalling and fragment ejection near failure, especially after 90 and 120 cycles [Su 2019, pp. 2-5, 5-8].
3.  **AE Hit Counts:** AE hit counts during loading can be divided into four stages: initial deceleration growth, uniform acceleration growth, acceleration growth, and deceleration stabilization. The duration of the initial stage lengthens, and the hit count at failure increases linearly with the number of cycles (N = 295 + 9.75Nx, R² = 0.9128) [Su 2019, pp. 5-8].
4.  **AE Ring-Down Counts:** Ring-down counts are divided into contact, quiet, and destructive periods. With more cycles, the contact period duration and counts increase, the quiet period shows more abrupt points, and the destructive period has larger counts and longer duration [Su 2019, pp. 5-8].
5.  **AE Peak Frequency:** The peak frequency points of AE signals shift from a scattered state to concentrated dominant frequency bands with increasing cycles. Near failure, the dominant frequency band concentrates in the mid-high range (110-135 kHz), and low-frequency signals decrease [Su 2019, pp. 5-8].
6.  **AE Energy Distribution:** The probability density of AE energy follows a power-law distribution for all cycle numbers. The critical exponent (r), reflecting scale invariance, first decreases and then slowly increases with more cycles [Su 2019, pp. 8-10].

## Limitations
- The study uses only one type of granite from a specific location, limiting generalizability to other rock types or geological settings.
- The freezing-thawing cycle protocol (temperature range, duration) is fixed and may not represent all natural or engineering conditions.
- Only uniaxial compression tests were performed; the effect of confining pressure or other stress paths was not investigated.
- The analysis focuses on AE parameters; direct correlation with internal micro-crack evolution is inferred but not directly imaged.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The granite specimens are homogeneous and representative of the in-situ rock mass.
- The vacuum saturation process fully saturates the specimens.
- The AE sensor coupling and arrangement provide accurate and representative signals of internal fracturing.
- The power-law distribution model is appropriate for describing the AE energy statistics.

## Key Variables / Parameters

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** The frost resistance coefficient of granite decreases with increasing freezing-thawing cycles, with a slower initial decrease followed by a more gradual decline, indicating a shift from surface to internal damage.
    - **Condition:** For granite specimens subjected to cyclic freezing (-20°C) and thawing (20°C) under laboratory conditions.
    - **Limitation:** Specific to the tested granite type and cycle protocol.
- **Claim:** The number of acoustic emission hits at failure increases linearly with the number of prior freezing-thawing cycles, providing a quantitative indicator of accumulated freeze-thaw damage.
    - **Condition:** For granite under uniaxial compression after vacuum saturation and freezing-thawing cycles.
    - **Limitation:** The linear relationship (N = 295 + 9.75Nx) is empirical and specific to the experimental setup.
- **Claim:** As freezing-thawing cycles increase, the dominant peak frequency of acoustic emission signals during rock failure concentrates in the mid-high frequency band (110-135 kHz), while low-frequency signals diminish.
    - **Condition:** For granite specimens under uniaxial compression, analyzed via FFT of AE waveforms.
    - **Limitation:** Frequency bands may vary with rock type, sensor characteristics, and loading conditions.
- **Claim:** The probability density of acoustic emission energy follows a power-law distribution across different freezing-thawing states, with the critical exponent exhibiting a non-monotonic trend (first decreasing, then slowly increasing) with cycle number.
    - **Condition:** For granite under uniaxial compression, with energy calculated from AE signal voltage.
    - **Limitation:** The power-law fit and exponent trend are specific to the analyzed energy range and specimen behavior.
