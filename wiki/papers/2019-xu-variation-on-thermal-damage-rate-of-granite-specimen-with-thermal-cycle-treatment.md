# Variation on Thermal Damage Rate of Granite Specimen with Thermal Cycle Treatment.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how repeated heating-cooling cycles and temperature affect the uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and thermal damage rate of granite, finding that water cooling and more cycles cause greater strength reduction, with a shift in the temperature threshold for rapid damage.

## Research Question
How do the number of thermal cycles and the maximum temperature affect the uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and thermal damage rate of granite specimens, and how do different cooling methods (air vs. water) influence these effects?

## Study Area / Data
The granite specimens were cored from a block collected from Zhangzhou, Fujian Province, China [Xu 2019, pp. 2-3]. The mineral composition is 35% feldspar, 40% quartz, 20% amphibole, and 5% mica, with a moisture content of 0.31% and density of 2.97 g/cm³ [Xu 2019, pp. 2-3]. A total of 93 specimens were prepared in standard size (diameter 50 mm, length 100 mm) [Xu 2019, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Thermal Cycle Treatment:** Specimens were heated in a furnace (type CYM-100) at a rate of 10°C/min to one of five designed temperatures (250°C, 350°C, 450°C, 550°C, 650°C) and then cooled. Five different cycle numbers (1, 5, 10, 15, 20) were used for water cooling. For comparison, air cooling was only tested with one thermal cycle [Xu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- **Cooling Conditions:** Discharge cycles were carried out either in water (quick cooling to 20°C) or in air (cooling to 25°C) [Xu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- **Mechanical Testing:** Uniaxial compression tests were conducted on an MTS816 servo-controlled system at a constant axial strain rate of 1.0×10⁻⁵/s [Xu 2019, pp. 3-5].
- **Damage Index:** A thermal damage index, Dσc, was defined as Dσc = 1 - (UCS_T / UCS_0), where UCS_T is the mean UCS after treatment and UCS_0 is the mean original UCS (125 MPa) [Xu 2019, pp. 3-5].
- **Rate Analysis:** Two rates of damage change were calculated: dDσc_T (related to temperature) and dlogDσc_N (related to thermal cycles) [Xu 2019, pp. 5-6].

## Key Findings
1.  UCS decreases and the thermal damage index (Dσc) increases as both temperature and the number of thermal cycles increase [Xu 2019, pp. 3-5].
2.  UCS of specimens cooled in water after treatment is generally lower than that of specimens cooled in air [Xu 2019, pp. 3-5].
3.  For specimens with one thermal cycle under air cooling, a rapid reduction in UCS occurs at 400°C. For specimens with multiple cycles under water cooling, this threshold shifts to 550°C [Xu 2019, pp. 3-5, 5-6].
4.  The evolution of the rate of thermal damage index change (dDσc_T) is "thoroughly antipodal" for specimens with multiple water-cooled cycles compared to those with a single air-cooled cycle [Xu 2019, pp. 5-6].

## Limitations
- The study used a single type of granite from one location (Zhangzhou, China) [Xu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- Experiments were conducted under controlled laboratory conditions, which may not fully replicate complex real-world thermal environments.
- The analysis focuses on macroscopic properties (UCS, damage index) and infers microcrack behavior without direct observation in this study.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Specimens were assumed to be homogeneous and representative of the granite block [Xu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- The heating rate (10°C/min) and cooling rates (rapid in water, slower in air) were constant for all treatments [Xu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- The thermal damage index (Dσc) is a valid measure of the degree of UCS degeneration [Xu 2019, pp. 3-5].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables:** Temperature (250, 350, 450, 550, 650°C), Number of thermal cycles (1, 5, 10, 15, 20), Cooling medium (air, water).
- **Dependent Variables:** Uniaxial Compressive Strength (UCS), Thermal damage index (Dσc), Rate of thermal damage index change (dDσc_T, dlogDσc_N).
- **Controlled Parameters:** Heating rate (10°C/min), specimen size (50 mm diameter, 100 mm length), strain rate (1.0×10⁻⁵/s).

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** For granite, water cooling after thermal treatment results in lower uniaxial compressive strength compared to air cooling at the same temperature and cycle number. **Condition:** Observed for temperatures from 350°C to 650°C with 1 thermal cycle. **Limitation:** Effect is minimal at 250°C [Xu 2019, pp. 3-5].
- **Claim:** The temperature threshold for a rapid reduction in granite's UCS shifts to a higher value when the specimen undergoes multiple thermal cycles with water cooling. **Condition:** Threshold shifts from ~400°C (1 cycle, air cooling) to ~550°C (multiple cycles, water cooling). **Limitation:** Specific to the granite type and cycle numbers tested [Xu 2019, pp. 3-5, 5-6].
- **Claim:** The rate of thermal damage accumulation (dDσc_T) with increasing temperature follows an opposite trend for specimens subjected to a single air-cooled cycle versus multiple water-cooled cycles. **Condition:** Air-cooled (1 cycle) shows an increase then decrease; multi-cycle water-cooled shows a decrease then increase. **Limitation:** Phenomenon observed but mechanism attributed to microcrack development timing [Xu 2019, pp. 5-6].
