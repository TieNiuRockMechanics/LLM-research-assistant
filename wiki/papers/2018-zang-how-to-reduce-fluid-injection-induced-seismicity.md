# How to Reduce Fluid-Injection-Induced Seismicity.

## One-line Summary
This study proposes and tests advanced fluid-injection schemes, specifically fatigue hydraulic fracturing, to mitigate larger induced seismic events by replacing them with a cloud of smaller events while enhancing reservoir permeability.

## Research Question
How can advanced fluid-injection schemes be used to manage and reduce the magnitude of fluid-injection-induced seismicity, particularly in short-term operations like enhanced geothermal systems (EGS) stimulations? [Zang 2018, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The research integrates data from multiple scales:
*   **Laboratory Scale:** Small-scale fluid-injection tests on cylindrical granite core samples (diameter 50 mm, height 100 mm) from Pocheon, Korea. [Zang 2018, pp. 8-10]
*   **Intermediate Scale:** Controlled underground experiments at the Äspö Hard Rock Laboratory (HRL) in Sweden at 410 m depth in granitic rock (Ävrö granodiorite and fine-grained granite). The in situ stress state (SV = SH > Sh) is well-characterized. [Zang 2018, pp. 8-10]
*   **Field Context:** The study differentiates between long-term injection operations (e.g., wastewater disposal, CCS) and short-term operations (e.g., hydraulic fracturing, EGS), focusing on the latter. [Zang 2018, pp. 3-3]

## Methods
The core method is **Fatigue Hydraulic Fracturing (FHF)**, an advanced injection scheme combining:
1.  **Cyclic Hydraulic Fracturing (CHF):** Injection with progressively increasing target pressure, separated by depressurization phases to relax crack tip stresses. [Zang 2018, pp. 4-5]
2.  **Pulse Hydraulic Fracturing (PHF):** During pressurization phases, a second pump system generates high-frequency (5-20 Hz) dynamic pressure pulses to disintegrate rock and remove weak material from fracture faces. [Zang 2018, pp. 5-7]
*   **Monitoring & Analysis:** Experiments used acoustic emission (AE) sensors and accelerometers for seismic monitoring, X-ray CT for fracture pattern analysis in cores, and impression packers for fracture orientation at mine scale. [Zang 2018, pp. 8-10, 10-12]
*   **Comparison:** Results from FHF/CHF were compared against conventional hydraulic fracturing (HF) with monotonic, continuous injection. [Zang 2018, pp. 10-12]

## Key Findings
1.  **Reduced Breakdown Pressure:** Cyclic injection lowers the fracture breakdown pressure (FBP) compared to monotonic injection. This reduction was ~10% in laboratory granite tests and ~15% in underground granodiorite tests. [Zang 2018, pp. 10-12, 13-14]
2.  **Reduced Seismic Magnitude:** Cyclic injection systematically reduces the magnitude of the largest induced seismic event. In lab tests, average maximum acoustic emission amplitude was reduced by 26 dB. [Zang 2018, pp. 10-12, 16-17]
3.  **Enhanced Permeability & Fracture Complexity:** Cyclic injection consistently increases hydraulic performance. The combination of cyclic progressive and pulse injection (FHF) yielded the best permeability enhancement. It promotes a broader fracture process zone (FPZ) with more complex, branching fractures (grain boundary cracks) rather than a single planar fracture. [Zang 2018, pp. 12-13, 13-14, 16-17]
4.  **Lower Seismic Efficiency:** The seismic injection efficiency (ratio of radiated seismic energy to injected hydraulic energy) decreases with an increasing number of fatigue cycles. In lab tests, efficiency decreased by five orders of magnitude when cycles increased from 1 to ~839. [Zang 2018, pp. 14-15, 15-16]

## Limitations
*   The concept is still in development and requires more fundamental research and in situ validation at various scales. [Zang 2018, pp. 15-16]
*   The total number of induced seismic events is not consistent and depends on rock type, stress heterogeneity, and injection scheme. [Zang 2018, pp. 16-17]
*   The longer duration of a stimulation campaign for fatigue testing may be economically disadvantageous in the short term. [Zang 2018, pp. 16-17]
*   The study primarily focuses on granitic rock; applicability to other rock types (sandstones, shales) needs more work. [Zang 2018, pp. 17-17]
*   The underground experiment had limitations, such as a fixed, low total injected volume (30 L) and a limited number of fatigue cycles (5). [Zang 2018, pp. 15-16]

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   The in situ stress state at the test sites is well-known and fixed. [Zang 2018, pp. 8-10]
*   The analysis excludes scenarios where the induced seismic cloud interacts with a major tectonic fault, which can drastically increase magnitude. [Zang 2018, pp. 3-4]
*   The maximum allowable induced seismic magnitude must be determined site-specifically through risk assessment. [Zang 2018, pp. 2-3]
*   The fracture breakdown pressure (FBP) equation assumes zero initial pore pressure in the rock mass. [Zang 2018, pp. 4-5]

## Reusable Claims
*   **Claim:** Cyclic fluid injection reduces the fracture breakdown pressure by 10-20% compared to monotonic injection in granitic rock, allowing the use of smaller, safer pumps.
    *   **Condition:** Observed in laboratory (unconfined) and underground (in situ stress) experiments on granite/granodiorite.
    *   **Limitation:** The magnitude of reduction may vary with rock type, confinement, and specific cyclic parameters. [Zang 2018, pp. 10-12, 13-14, 16-17]
*   **Claim:** Cyclic injection systematically reduces the magnitude of the largest induced seismic event compared to continuous injection.
    *   **Condition:** Demonstrated in controlled laboratory and underground experiments where direct comparison was possible.
    *   **Limitation:** The total number of events may not decrease; the effect is on the maximum magnitude. Optimization of injection parameters is needed. [Zang 2018, pp. 10-12, 16-17]
*   **Claim:** Fatigue hydraulic fracturing (cyclic progressive + pulse injection) enhances permeability more effectively than conventional or simple cyclic injection, promoting a broader fracture process zone.
    *   **Condition:** Tested in Ävrö granodiorite at the Äspö HRL.
    *   **Limitation:** Long-term stability of the enhanced permeability under cyclic loading is not confirmed. [Zang 2018, pp. 12-13, 13-14, 16-17]
*   **Claim:** Seismic injection efficiency decreases with an increasing number of hydraulic fatigue cycles.
    *   **Condition:** Based on unconfined laboratory tests on granite cores.
    *   **Limitation:** Absolute values and trends may differ under triaxial confinement and in different rock types. [Zang 2018, pp. 15-16]
