# Stylolites: A Review.

## One-line Summary
Stylolites are ubiquitous rough rock interfaces formed by localized pressure-solution that act as markers of strain and paleostress and influence crustal fluid flow.

## Research Question
How do stylolites form? How can they be used to measure strain and formation stress? How do they control fluid flow in the upper crust? [Toussaint 2018, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
The review synthesizes field observations from numerous global locations, including sedimentary rocks (carbonates, sandstones, cherts), deformation zones in folds, faults, and shear zones. Specific study areas mentioned include the Paris Basin (Bure-sur-Meuse), Cirque de Navacelle (France), Blanche cliff (Israel), Apennines (Italy), and various North Sea formations. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 5-7, 44-46, 67-68]

## Methods
The review compiles and analyzes methods including: field outcrop and core description; microstructural analysis (SEM, EBSD, cathodoluminescence); 3D imaging (X-ray micro-tomography); surface topography measurement (profilometers, stereophotogrammetry); spectral analysis (Fourier Power Spectral Density, Average Wavelet Coefficient); mass balance geochemistry; laboratory experiments (pressure-solution on quartz and calcite); and physics-based numerical modeling (Discrete Element Method, spring network models, linearized analytical theories). [Toussaint 2018, pp. 9-10, 14-16, 26-28, 38-40]

## Key Findings
1.  Stylolite geometry is self-affine with three characteristic scaling regimes: a Hurst exponent H≈1.1 at small scales (up to tens/hundreds of microns), H≈0.5-0.6 at intermediate scales (up to millimeters/centimeters), and a flattening (H≈0) at large scales for sedimentary stylolites. Tectonic stylolites show anisotropic scaling. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 14-16]
2.  The crossover length scale (L*) between the small-scale and intermediate-scale regimes is a function of the stress tensor and can be used to determine the magnitude of the paleostress perpendicular to the stylolite surface during its formation. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 42-44]
3.  Stylolites promote permeability increase parallel to their plane but can have negligible or reducing effects on transverse permeability, depending on their maturity and the accumulation of insoluble material. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 3-5, 46-48]
4.  Stylolite formation is driven by chemical potential differences, where the normal stress term is often dominant, but surface energy, elastic strain energy, and plastic strain energy can also be significant contributors depending on conditions. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 18-20]
5.  Experimental reproduction of macro-scale stylolites (spanning multiple grains) remains a major challenge due to the extremely long formation timescales predicted by models. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 38-40]

## Limitations
1.  The exact driving force for stylolite formation (stress difference vs. free energy gradients) is still debated. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 28-30]
2.  The role of clays and other insoluble materials in enhancing pressure solution and controlling localization is not fully understood. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 25-26]
3.  It is unclear whether stylolite formation is reaction-limited or transport-limited under various conditions. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 22-23]
4.  Macro-scale stylolites have not been reproduced in the laboratory, limiting experimental validation of models. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 38-40]
5.  The dynamic evolution of stylolite networks and their coupled effect on rock transport properties over geological time is poorly constrained. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 48-50]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The initial distribution of insoluble material (e.g., clays) in the host rock is often assumed to be homogeneous for mass balance calculations. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 40-42]
2.  Models often assume the fluid is saturated with respect to the solid under the average system stress. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 28-30]
3.  The linearized analytical models assume the stylolite interface is close to flat. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 28-30]
4.  For paleostress estimation from sedimentary stylolites, simple vertical compaction with no horizontal movement is often assumed. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 44-46]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Hurst exponent (H):** Quantifies the self-affine roughness of the stylolite surface.
*   **Crossover length (L*):** The scale separating the small-scale and intermediate-scale roughness regimes; a key paleostress indicator.
*   **Correlation length (X):** The large-scale cutoff for sedimentary stylolites, related to total dissolution.
*   **Normal stress (σ_n):** The stress component perpendicular to the stylolite surface.
*   **Shear stress (τ):** The difference between principal stresses in the stylolite plane.
*   **Dissolution rate (V_sty):** The speed of stylolite growth, e.g., ~10⁻⁴ to 10⁻³ m/Myr for quartz, ~0.001 to 0.1 m/Myr for calcite. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 25-26]
*   **Surface energy (γ):** A material property influencing small-scale roughness.
*   **Young's modulus (E):** A material property influencing elastic energy storage.

4.  **Condition:** In rocks with stylolites. **Claim:** The amount of dissolution accommodated by a stylolite can be estimated from the correlation length (X) of its large-scale roughness, even when the largest teeth have been dissolved. [Toussaint 2018, pp. 14-16, 40-42]
