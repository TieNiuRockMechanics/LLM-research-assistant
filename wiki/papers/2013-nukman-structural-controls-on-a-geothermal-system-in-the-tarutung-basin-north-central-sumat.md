# Structural Controls on a Geothermal System in the Tarutung Basin, North Central Sumatra.

## One-line Summary
This study uses field structural geology and geothermal manifestation mapping to demonstrate that the geothermal system in the Tarutung Basin is controlled by a complex fault pattern generated by compressional stress acting at a high angle to the right-lateral Sumatra Fault System, with specific fault orientations acting as fluid conduits or barriers.

## Research Question
The research aims to understand the structural controls on the geothermal system in the Tarutung Basin, specifically investigating how fault dynamics, fracture orientation, and the present-day stress field influence fluid pathways and the distribution of geothermal manifestations in a non-volcanic, fault-controlled setting [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Tarutung Basin in North Central Sumatra, an en-echelon pull-apart basin along the NW–SE striking dextral Sumatra Fault System (SFS) [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-2]. Data sources include:
*   Satellite imagery (SRTM 90 m resolution) for identifying major fault traces and geothermal manifestations [Nukman 2013, pp. 2-3].
*   Field structural geological mapping, including measurements of 95 fault plane/striation spots, 349 joints, and veins at 20 different sites [Nukman 2013, pp. 2-3].
*   Mapping of geothermal manifestations, including hot springs (temperatures 38–66°C, pH 1.26–8.3) and travertine terraces [Nukman 2013, pp. 4-5].
*   Published geological maps (Aldiss et al., 1983; Apsden et al., 1982; Clarke et al., 1982) [Nukman 2013, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
The methodological approach combined remote sensing and field-based structural analysis:
1.  **Satellite Image Analysis:** Analysis of SRTM data to identify major fault traces, outcrops, and geothermal manifestations [Nukman 2013, pp. 2-3].
2.  **Field Structural Geology Mapping:** Included joint pattern analysis after Hancock (1985) and fault plane analysis after Means (1987), with each fault plane/striation pair measured at least four times [Nukman 2013, pp. 2-3].
3.  **Statistical Analysis:** Structural data (faults, joints) were processed in lower hemisphere projection using TectonicsFP software (Ortner et al., 2001), which was also employed for stress inversion of fault slip data after Angelier (1979) [Nukman 2013, pp. 2-3].
4.  **Geothermal Manifestation Mapping:** Detailed mapping of hot springs and travertine outcrops, including their structural position and characteristics (e.g., temperature, pH, fracture control) [Nukman 2013, pp. 2-3, 4-5].

## Key Findings
*   The fault pattern in the Tarutung Basin is generated by a compressional stress direction acting at a high angle to the right-lateral Sumatra Fault System [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1].
*   **Fluid Pathways:** NW–SE striking normal faults (possibly related to negative flower structures) and NNW-SSE to NNE-SSW oriented dilative Riedel shears are preferential fluid pathways [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1, 8-10].
*   **Fluid Barriers:** ENE–WSW striking faults act as barriers in this system, as indicated by a sharp geochemical boundary between acidic and alkaline spring domains [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1, 8-8].
*   **Manifestation Distribution:** The dominant geothermal manifestations (hot springs, travertine) are concentrated at the eastern part of the basin, indicating local extension due to clockwise block rotation in the SFS [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1, 8-8].
*   **Travertine Types:** Two travertine types were identified: (1) dilational travertine forming along N–S extensional fractures parallel to the maximum principal stress, and (2) shear travertine forming along NW–SE shear fractures parallel to the SFS [Nukman 2013, pp. 5-6].
*   **Exploration Implication:** The geothermal system lacks a typical steam-brine interface and alteration clays, meaning conventional magnetotelluric surveys may not be sufficient; field structural geology is critical for delineating fluid pathways [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1, 10-10].

## Limitations
*   The dataset from nine outcropping faults with sufficient slickensides was too small to robustly employ stress inversion from fault slip data to definitively determine the stress field [Nukman 2013, pp. 5-6].
*   Tropical conditions with high weathering rates limit outcrop conditions suitable for structural geological analysis [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-2].
*   Chemical analysis of fluids and gas sampled at the hot springs was still ongoing at the time of publication, making some hydrochemical conclusions preliminary [Nukman 2013, pp. 8-8].
*   The influence of the Medial Sumatra Tectonic Zone (MSTZ) on the formation of the Tarutung Basin is not studied yet [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-2].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   The study assumes the Andersonian faulting concept, where faults and fractures have a specific orientation relative to the stress regime that caused faulting [Nukman 2013, pp. 5-6].
*   It is assumed that all measured fractures were generated in the same stress field [Nukman 2013, pp. 5-6].
*   The present-day stress field in the region is characterized by compression at a high angle to the principal deformation zone (the SFS), with the maximum principal stress direction variably reported as N–S to NE–SW [Nukman 2013, pp. 5-6, 6-8].
*   The geothermal system is assumed to be structurally controlled rather than volcanically controlled, as the Tarutung Basin is not associated with active volcanism [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Fault Orientation & Kinematics:** Strike, dip, and slip direction (normal, reverse, strike-slip) of faults and fractures.
*   **Stress Field Orientation:** Directions of maximum (σ1), intermediate (σ2), and minimum (σ3) principal stresses.
*   **Fluid Pathway Indicators:** Presence of hot springs, travertine dykes, and open fractures.
*   **Fluid Barrier Indicators:** Fault gouge, sharp changes in spring geochemistry (e.g., pH).
*   **Geothermal Manifestation Properties:** Temperature and pH of springs [Nukman 2013, pp. 4-5].

## Reusable Claims
*   In the Tarutung Basin, NW–SE striking normal faults and NNW-SSE to NNE-SSW oriented dilative Riedel shears serve as preferential fluid pathways for geothermal fluids [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1, 8-10].
*   ENE–WSW striking faults in the Tarutung Basin act as sealing barriers to fluid flow, compartmentalizing the geothermal reservoir [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1, 8-8].
*   The concentration of geothermal manifestations on the eastern margin of the Tarutung Basin is attributed to local extension caused by clockwise block rotation along the right-lateral Sumatra Fault System [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1, 8-8].
*   For fault-controlled geothermal systems lacking typical volcanic alteration signatures, field structural geological surveys are essential to delineate preferential fluid pathways from complex fracture patterns [Nukman 2013, pp. 10-10].
*   The morphology and orientation of travertine deposits can be used to infer the tectonic stress regime active during their formation, a concept termed "travitonics" [Nukman 2013, pp. 5-6].

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study compares the Tarutung Basin to other geothermal systems controlled by major strike-slip faults, such as the Alpine Fault (New Zealand), El Salvador Fault Zone, and San Andreas Fault/Walker Lane (USA) [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1].
*   It contrasts the structurally controlled Tarutung Basin with the nearby Sarulla Basin, which is a volcanically controlled geothermal system within the same SFS [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-1].
*   The work builds on the "travitonics" concept introduced by Hancock et al. (1999) to link travertine formation to tectonic stress [Nukman 2013, pp. 5-6].
*   It references regional tectonic studies on the Sumatra Fault System's evolution, slip rates, and rotation (e.g., Sieh and Natawidjaja, 2000; Cattin et al., 2009) [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-2, 5-6].

## Open Questions
*   What is the exact nature and influence of the Medial Sumatra Tectonic Zone (MSTZ) on the formation and structure of the Tarutung Basin? [Nukman 2013, pp. 1-2]
*   Does a buried caldera-like feature northeast of the basin represent a potential heat source for the geothermal system? [Nukman 2013, pp. 3-4, 8-8]
*   How does the detailed fluid and gas chemistry of the hot springs clarify the compartmentalization of the subsurface reservoir and the specific role of faults as conduits or barriers? [Nukman 2013, pp. 8-8]
*   Is the Carboniferous Alas Formation (potential limestone source for travertine) present beneath the basin, and if so, what is its distribution? [Nukman 2013, pp. 3-4]
