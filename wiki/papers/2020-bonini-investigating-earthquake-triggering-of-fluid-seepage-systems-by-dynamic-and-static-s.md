# Investigating Earthquake Triggering of Fluid Seepage Systems by Dynamic and Static Stresses.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how dynamic and static stress changes from earthquakes trigger eruptions and increased activity in fluid seepage systems like mud volcanoes, using case studies from the Northern Apennines, Italy, and Azerbaijan.

## Research Question
How do static and dynamic stress changes from earthquakes trigger eruptions of fluid expulsion features and modulate their activity? [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
The primary study area is the Northern Apennines, Italy, which offers a long historical seismic record coupled with information on co-seismic to post-seismic activity of seepage structures. Specific sites include mud volcanoes (e.g., Montegibbio, Regnano, Nirano, Torre, Rivalta, Casola-Querzola, Puianello), dry or aqueous methane vents (e.g., Portico di Romagna everlasting fires, Sasso Cardo), and gas seeps (e.g., Lake Vadimone). Additional data come from mud volcanoes in the Greater Caucasus, Azerbaijan (e.g., Shikzagirli, Keyreki, Lokbatan). [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-1, 3-3, 5-5, 6-7, 9-10, 10-12, 17-18]

## Methods
Dynamic stress changes were estimated using peak ground velocity (PGV). For instrumental earthquakes, PGV was interpolated from seismic station data. For historical earthquakes, PGV was estimated using empirical Ground Motion Prediction Equations (GMPEs) from Bindi et al. (2010). Peak dynamic stress (σr) was calculated using the equation σr = μr Vs PGV, where Vs is shear wave velocity and μ is shear modulus. Minimum and maximum bounds for rock density (2100–2400 kg m⁻³) and shear wave velocity (800–1800 m s⁻¹) were used to determine stress ranges. Static stress changes were computed using the Coulomb Failure Function (ΔCFF = Δτ + μ′ Δσn) in a homogeneous elastic half-space model (Okada, 1992) using Coulomb software. Normal stress changes (Δσn) were calculated on inferred vertical feeder dikes to evaluate clamping/unclamping. Standard material properties were assumed (Poisson's Ratio = 0.25, Young's modulus = 8 × 10⁴ MPa, apparent friction coefficient = 0.4). [Bonini 2020, pp. 3-5, 5-5]

## Key Findings
1.  Dynamic stress changes are the dominant triggering mechanism for fluid seepage systems, especially in the intermediate- to far-field of earthquake faults. [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-1, 17-17]
2.  Paroxysmal activity of seepage features was influenced by minimum dynamic stress thresholds ranging from approximately 15 kPa to 50 kPa. Mud volcano eruptions were generally triggered by peak dynamic stresses >45–50 kPa, while some methane vents and springs showed sensitivity to ~30–40 kPa. [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-1, 14-15, 17-17]
3.  Co-seismic static stress changes can be large enough to dilate fault-controlled fluid pathways in the near-field (e.g., the 1501 Pede-Apennine thrust earthquake), but in most studied cases, static stress changes were negligible or subordinate. [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-1, 12-13, 17-17]
4.  The recovery time between eruptions is irregular, suggesting a complex interplay of factors (e.g., gas production rate, plumbing system characteristics, earthquake frequency) governs the achievement of a critical, trigger-able state. [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-1, 15-16]
5.  Responses of mud volcanoes generally follow the empirical seismic energy density triggering criterion of Wang and Manga (2010), but can occasionally be overwhelmed when systems are critically stressed. [Bonini 2020, pp. 13-14]

## Limitations
1.  Uncertainty in rock density and shear wave velocity leads to uncertainty in computed peak dynamic stress. [Bonini 2020, pp. 3-5]
2.  PGV at the surface is assumed similar to that experienced by the subsurface reservoir. [Bonini 2020, pp. 3-5]
3.  For historical earthquakes, PGV is estimated from GMPEs, introducing model-dependent uncertainty. [Bonini 2020, pp. 3-5]
4.  The number of documented triggered responses is statistically limited. [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-2]
5.  Precise geometry and kinematics of some historical seismic sources are uncertain. [Bonini 2020, pp. 12-13]
6.  The exact mechanism by which dynamic stress changes generate eruptions is still unclear. [Bonini 2020, pp. 3-3]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The subsurface is modeled as a homogeneous, linear elastic half-space. [Bonini 2020, pp. 5-5]
2.  Standard, homogeneous material properties are assumed for static stress calculations (ν=0.25, E=8×10⁴ MPa, μ′=0.4). [Bonini 2020, pp. 5-5]
3.  PGV measured at the surface is representative of the subsurface reservoir. [Bonini 2020, pp. 3-5]
4.  Feeder systems are modeled as vertical planar fractures (dikes). [Bonini 2020, pp. 5-5]
5.  The triggering threshold is governed by a complex interplay of external (earthquake) and internal (system) factors. [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For mud volcanoes and seepage systems in the intermediate- to far-field of an earthquake fault. **Claim:** Dynamic stress changes are the dominant triggering mechanism, with paroxysmal activity often requiring peak dynamic stresses exceeding approximately 15–50 kPa. [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-1, 14-15, 17-17]
2.  **Condition:** When a seepage system is located in the near-field of a ruptured fault that also controls its plumbing. **Claim:** Static stress changes can be large enough (e.g., ~0.5 MPa) to dilate fault-controlled fluid pathways and significantly influence triggering. [Bonini 2020, pp. 12-13, 17-17]
3.  **Condition:** For the recovery time between eruptions of mud volcanoes. **Claim:** The repose time is generally irregular, indicating that the achievement of a critical state is governed by a complex interplay of factors including gas production rate, plumbing system characteristics, and the frequency of triggering earthquakes. [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-1, 15-16]
4.  **Condition:** When assessing the potential for earthquake triggering. **Claim:** The response of seepage features is influenced by a combination of epicentral distance and earthquake magnitude, generally following empirical seismic energy density thresholds, though systems critically close to eruption may be triggered by smaller stresses. [Bonini 2020, pp. 13-14]

## Open Questions
1.  What are the precise modalities through which dynamic and static stress changes influence eruptions separately or in combination? [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-2]
2.  What are the critical stress change thresholds for triggering specific features, given the complex interactions of governing factors? [Bonini 2020, pp. 1-2]
3.  What is the exact mechanism by which dynamic stress changes generate eruptions of mud volcanoes? [Bonini 2020, pp. 3-3]
4.  How can the assessment of both internal and external stresses at the passage of seismic waves be improved to better understand triggering thresholds and why some potentially responsive features remain inactive? [Bonini 2020, pp. 16-17]
5.  How do delayed far-field responses occur, given that static stresses are negligible and dynamic stresses are transitory? [Bonini 2020, pp. 17-17]
