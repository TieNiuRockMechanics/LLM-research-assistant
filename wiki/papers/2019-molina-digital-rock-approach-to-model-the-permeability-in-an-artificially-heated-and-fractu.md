# Digital Rock Approach to Model the Permeability in an Artificially Heated and Fractured Granodiorite from the Liquiñe Geothermal System (39°S).

## One-line Summary
This study uses digital rock analysis and fluid flow simulations to model how low-temperature thermal stress affects the permeability of granodiorite from the Liquiñe geothermal system, finding that thermal damage creates microcracks but permeability remains similar when accounting for fluid viscosity changes.

## Research Question
How do intrinsic and extrinsic factors, particularly low-to-medium temperature thermal stress, allow permeable pathways to form and be sustained in initially low-porosity crystalline host rocks of a shallow geothermal system? [Molina 2019, pp. 2-3]

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Liquiñe geothermal system in the Southern Volcanic Zone (SVZ) of the Andes, Chile (39°S). This area is characterized by the Liquiñe–Ofqui Fault System (LOFS) and Andean Transverse Faults (ATF), which control fluid circulation. Samples of Miocene granodiorite were collected from a quarry near Liquiñe town, assumed to be an analogue of the fractured rock at depth. [Molina 2019, pp. 3-5]

## Methods
The methodology combined petrophysical characterization, thermal treatment, mechanical testing, and digital rock analysis.
1.  **Sample Preparation:** Cylindrical (1 cm diameter, 2.5 cm length) and cubic (5 cm edge) samples were drilled from a granodiorite block. [Molina 2019, pp. 5-7]
2.  **Petrophysical Characterization:** Initial measurements included bulk and real density, effective porosity, capillary coefficient, and ultrasonic P- and S-wave velocities. [Molina 2019, pp. 5-7]
3.  **Thermal Stress:** Samples were heated to 150°C and 210°C with a ramp of 6°C/min, held for 4 hours, and slowly cooled. [Molina 2019, pp. 7-7]
4.  **Mechanical Testing:** Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) tests were performed on fresh and heated samples until failure. [Molina 2019, pp. 7-8]
5.  **Digital Rock Analysis (XR-μCT):** Samples were scanned with a Bruker Skyscan 1272 at 6 μm voxel resolution. Image processing used FIJI software with an Anisotropic Diffusion Filter for enhancement and the Multiscale Hessian Fracture Filter (MHFF) for fracture segmentation. Mineral phases were segmented using Otsu's threshold. [Molina 2019, pp. 7-8, 10-12]
6.  **Permeability Modeling:** Fracture permeability was estimated using the alternative cubic law and simulated via the Lattice-Boltzmann Method (LBM) using the Palabos software. [Molina 2019, pp. 8-10, 15-17]

## Key Findings
1.  Thermal stress at 150-210°C produced intercrystalline microcracks, primarily between K-feldspar and plagioclase crystals. [Molina 2019, pp. 8-10]
2.  Thermal damage resulted in: an increase in capillary absorption (23% at 150°C, 75% at 210°C); a decrease in ultrasonic wave velocities (Vp decreased 9.8-13.6%); and a decrease in uniaxial compressive strength (28-32%). [Molina 2019, pp. 10-12]
3.  Artificial fractures generated by UCS loading in heated samples were narrower (aperture decreased from 96 μm in unheated to 40 μm at 210°C) and more complex in morphology. [Molina 2019, pp. 12-13, 15-17]
4.  Fracture permeability calculated by the cubic law decreased with increasing treatment temperature (from 9.5×10⁻⁹ m² at 25°C to 1.5×10⁻⁹ m² at 210°C). However, when recalculated using water kinematic viscosity at the respective temperatures, permeability values became similar. LBM simulations showed a similar trend but with values about two orders of magnitude lower. [Molina 2019, pp. 15-17, 21-22]
5.  The combined effect of thermal stress and tectonic stresses is inferred to be an effective mechanism for sustaining secondary permeability at shallow depths (<500 m) in the Liquiñe system. [Molina 2019, pp. 21-22]

## Limitations
1.  The XR-μCT resolution (6 μm voxel size) limits the detection of fractures and textural changes smaller than this scale. [Molina 2019, pp. 20-21]
2.  Experiments were conducted under unconfined pressure, not simulating true reservoir conditions at depth. [Molina 2019, pp. 21-22]
3.  The cubic law for permeability estimation may overestimate values as it uses a single averaged aperture and does not account for fracture connectivity or roughness. [Molina 2019, pp. 21-22]
4.  The study focused only on fracture permeability (Kf), not the equivalent permeability which includes matrix flow (Km). [Molina 2019, pp. 20-21]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The hydraulic aperture (eh) and mechanical aperture (em) are considered identical for the artificially generated, completely open fractures. [Molina 2019, pp. 8-10]
2.  The granodiorite sample from the quarry is a valid analogue for the fractured host rock at depth in the Liquiñe system. [Molina 2019, pp. 5-7]
3.  The experimental conditions (temperatures up to 210°C, unconfined pressure) approximate shallow crustal conditions (<500 m depth). [Molina 2019, pp. 15-17]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Temperature:** 25°C, 150°C, 210°C (thermal treatment).
*   **Fracture Aperture (e):** Measured in micrometers (μm) from XR-μCT.
*   **Porosity (Pe):** Effective porosity measured by hydric tests and estimated from digital analysis.
*   **Ultrasonic Velocities (Vp, Vs):** P-wave and S-wave velocities in m/s.
*   **Permeability (Kf):** Fracture permeability in m², calculated via cubic law and LBM.
*   **Water Kinematic Viscosity (υ):** Temperature-dependent parameter used in cubic law calculations (e.g., 8.92×10⁻⁷ m²/s at 25°C). [Molina 2019, pp. 21-22]

## Reusable Claims
1.  Thermal stress at temperatures as low as 150-210°C is sufficient to induce intercrystalline microcracking in granodiorite, significantly altering its petrophysical properties. [Molina 2019, pp. 8-10, 17-18]
2.  In granodiorite, the average aperture of artificially generated fractures decreases as the pre-treatment temperature increases, leading to more complex, narrower fracture networks. [Molina 2019, pp. 12-13, 19-20]
3.  For shallow geothermal reservoirs in crystalline rock, the coupled thermo-mechanical effect can sustain secondary permeability by creating interconnected fracture networks, even with reduced individual fracture apertures. [Molina 2019, pp. 21-22]
4.  Permeability estimates from the cubic law can be highly sensitive to the assumed fluid viscosity; using temperature-appropriate viscosity values can reconcile permeability estimates from samples treated at different temperatures. [Molina 2019, pp. 21-22]

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on research regarding the tectonic control of geothermal systems in the SVZ [Cembrano and Lara 2009; Pérez-Flores et al. 2016] and the effects of thermal damage on rock properties [Siratovich et al. 2015; Griffiths et al. 2017]. It applies digital rock analysis techniques, which are increasingly used in geosciences [Bultreys et al. 2016; Saxena et al. 2017], to a geothermal context. The findings relate to broader work on fracture permeability in crystalline rocks [Mitchell and Faulkner 2008; Ranjram et al. 2015].

## Open Questions
1.  How would permeability evolve under cyclic thermal loading or different cooling rates?
2.  What is the effect of confining pressure and pore fluid pressure on the thermo-mechanical behavior and permeability of this granodiorite?
3.  How do the findings scale from the laboratory sample to the reservoir scale, considering natural fracture networks and longer-term fluid-rock interactions?
4.  Can the digital rock model be validated against direct laboratory permeability measurements?
