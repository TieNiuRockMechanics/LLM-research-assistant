# Experimental Study on Hydraulic Fracturing of Granite under Thermal Shock.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally demonstrates that the crack initiation pressure during hydraulic fracturing of granite decreases significantly above 200°C, primarily due to thermal shock from the fracturing fluid rather than changes in rock mechanical parameters.

## Research Question
How does temperature (20°C to 400°C) affect the hydraulic fracturing behavior, specifically the crack initiation pressure and fracture propagation pattern, in granite under triaxial stress, and what is the underlying mechanism?

## Study Area / Data
The experimental subject was "Shandong Grey" granite from Pingyi, Shandong Province, China. Samples were cylindrical (φ200 × 400 mm) with a central borehole (18 mm diameter, 250 mm depth) for water injection [Zhou 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Experimental Setup:** Used a "triaxial testing machine of servo control for rock mass at the high temperature of 600 °C and the high pressure of 20 MN" [Zhou 2017, pp. 1-2]. The system applied axial and confining pressure (25 MPa) and heated samples to target temperatures (20°C, 100°C, 200°C, 300°C, 400°C) at a rate of 10°C/h, followed by a 2-hour stabilization period [Zhou 2017, pp. 2-3].
- **Hydraulic Fracturing:** High-pressure water was injected via an automatic control mode at a steady rate. Fracturing was indicated by a sudden pressure drop (>1 MPa) [Zhou 2017, pp. 2-3].
- **Thermal Shock Modeling:** A strong transient thermal stress model was established using non-Fourier heat conduction equations to simulate the temperature and stress fields near the borehole during cold fluid injection into hot rock [Zhou 2017, pp. 5-6, 6-8].

## Key Findings
1.  **Fracturing Mode Change:** At 20°C, fracturing was instantaneous and brittle. At temperatures ≥100°C, fracturing mode changed to continuous development, where fracture growth rate matched fluid injection rate [Zhou 2017, pp. 3-4].
2.  **Initiation Pressure Drop:** Crack initiation pressure decreased with increasing temperature. The drop was gradual from 20°C to 200°C but became abrupt between 200°C and 300°C (from 43.2 MPa to 24.6 MPa, a 43.1% relative drop) [Zhou 2017, pp. 3-4, 4-5].
3.  **Primary Mechanism:** The main factor controlling the sharp drop in initiation pressure above 200°C is not the change in rock mechanical parameters (e.g., tensile strength), but the **thermal shock** generated by the cooling effect of the fracturing fluid on the high-temperature borehole [Zhou 2017, pp. 4-5, 9-10].
4.  **Thermal Shock Effect:** The model showed that cold fluid injection creates a strong transient thermal gradient near the borehole, generating tensile stress. The rock near the borehole experiences two impact shocks: an elastic wave followed by a thermal wave [Zhou 2017, pp. 8-8, 8-9].
5.  **Fracture Propagation:** With increasing temperature, fracture propagation shifted from primarily axial to more radial development. Transverse fractures were observed at 300°C and 400°C [Zhou 2017, pp. 3-4].

## Limitations
- The thermal shock model involved simplifications, such as assuming the borehole wall temperature drops instantaneously to water temperature and treating the rock mass as infinite [Zhou 2017, pp. 6-8].
- Experiments were conducted on a single type of granite ("Shandong Grey"). Results may vary for other rock types [Zhou 2017, pp. 2-3].
- The study focused on the initiation pressure and macroscopic fracture patterns; detailed analysis of micro-crack network development was not performed [Zhou 2017, pp. 5-6].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Before water injection, the rock sample was considered a stable isothermal body at the target temperature [Zhou 2017, pp. 6-8].
- The engineering rock mass was considered infinite relative to the fracturing point [Zhou 2017, pp. 6-8].
- The temperature of the rock contact surface was assumed to drop to the water temperature at the moment of contact [Zhou 2017, pp. 6-8].
- Axial and confining pressures were held constant at 25 MPa during heating and fracturing [Zhou 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Reusable Claims
- For Shandong Grey granite under 25 MPa confining pressure, the crack initiation pressure during hydraulic fracturing decreases sharply in the temperature range of 200°C to 300°C, with a relative drop rate exceeding 40% [Zhou 2017, pp. 3-4].
- The dominant mechanism for reduced hydraulic fracturing initiation pressure in high-temperature granite (>200°C) is the tensile stress induced by thermal shock from cold fracturing fluid, not the degradation of the rock's intrinsic tensile strength [Zhou 2017, pp. 9-10].
- In high-temperature hydraulic fracturing, the rock near the borehole experiences a sequential dynamic load: first an elastic wave shock, then a thermal wave shock, which together facilitate fracture initiation [Zhou 2017, pp. 8-9].

## Connections To Other Work
- The study addresses a gap in physical experiments on hydraulic fracturing at high temperatures (>200°C), which are relevant for EGS but were previously limited by equipment and mostly studied via numerical simulation [Zhou 2017, pp. 1-2].
- It builds on prior knowledge that temperature affects rock mechanical properties [Yin et al., 2015; Xu and Liu, 2000; Zhang et al., 2011] and causes thermal fracturing [Chaki et al., 2008; Fu et al., 2007], but identifies thermal shock as the key mechanism for fracturing pressure reduction [Zhou 2017, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
- The experimental setup uses a machine developed by Zhao et al. (2008a) [Zhou 2017, pp. 1-2].

## Open Questions
- How do the findings translate to other rock types (e.g., basalt, sandstone) or different granite varieties?
- What is the precise relationship between the magnitude of thermal shock stress and the resulting fracture network complexity?
- How do varying injection rates or fracturing fluid temperatures modulate the thermal shock effect?
