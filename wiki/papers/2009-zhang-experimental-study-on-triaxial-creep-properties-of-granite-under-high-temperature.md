# Experimental Study on Triaxial Creep Properties of Granite under High Temperature.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates the triaxial creep behavior of large granite specimens under high temperatures, identifying a temperature threshold between 300°C and 400°C that marks the transition from steady-state to non-steady-state creep.

## Research Question
How do high temperature and triaxial stress conditions affect the creep properties of granite, and is there a critical temperature threshold that governs the transition between steady-state and non-steady-state creep?

## Study Area / Data
The experiments were conducted on large cylindrical granite specimens (Φ200 mm × 400 mm) from China. The granite's mineral composition was analyzed via X-ray diffraction, and its density was 2.695 g/ml [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4]. The study utilized a 20 MN servo-controlled triaxial rock testing system with high temperature and high pressure capability, developed at the China University of Mining and Technology [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4].

## Methods
The research employed a 20 MN servo-controlled triaxial testing machine capable of applying axial force up to 10000 kN, confining pressure up to 250 MPa, and pore pressure up to 120 MPa [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4]. Temperature was controlled with an accuracy of 0.1°C, and deformation was measured with an accuracy of 0.001 mm [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4]. Granite specimens were subjected to creep tests under two primary conditions: 1) 300°C with an axial stress of 94 MPa and a lateral stress of 75 MPa, and 2) 400°C with an axial stress of 125 MPa and a lateral stress of 100 MPa [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4]. Axial, lateral, and bulk strains were recorded over time.

## Key Findings
1.  At 300°C (axial stress 94 MPa, lateral stress 75 MPa), granite exhibited the first and second phases of creep, showing clear characteristics of steady-state creep that gradually stopped [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4].
2.  At 400°C (axial stress 125 MPa, lateral stress 100 MPa), the deformation showed obvious characteristics of non-steady-state creep [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4].
3.  The creep properties of granite increase with the increase of temperature and stress [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4].
4.  There is a threshold of temperature, approximately between 300°C and 400°C, for the transition from steady-state creep to non-steady-state creep [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4].
5.  At 300°C, the steady-state creep rate was on the order of 10⁻⁵, while at 400°C, the creep rate was significantly higher and did not stabilize [Zhang 2009, pp. 1-4].

## Limitations
1.  The study used a specific type of granite and large specimen size; results may not directly apply to other rock types or smaller scales [Zhang 2009, pp. 4-5].
2.  The heating rate in the experiments (5°C/h) was different from some other studies (e.g., 1/12 of David and Dvivedi's rate), which may affect the comparison of results [Zhang 2009, pp. 4-5].
3.  The test duration was limited, and long-term creep behavior beyond the observed timeframe was not characterized.

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The granite specimens were dry during testing.
2.  Temperature and stress were maintained constant during each creep test.
3.  The large specimen size (Φ200 mm × 400 mm) is considered more representative of in-situ conditions than smaller specimens used in some prior studies [Zhang 2009, pp. 4-5].
