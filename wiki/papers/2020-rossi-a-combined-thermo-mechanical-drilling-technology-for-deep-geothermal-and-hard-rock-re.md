# A Combined Thermo-Mechanical Drilling Technology for Deep Geothermal and Hard Rock Reservoirs.

## One-line Summary
Proposes and field-demonstrates a combined thermo-mechanical drilling (CTMD) technology that integrates a flame-jet thermal assistance system with conventional rotary drilling, achieving up to a threefold increase in rock removal performance in hard granite compared to standalone mechanical drilling [Rossi 2020, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
Can a combined thermo-mechanical drilling (CTMD) method, using a flame jet for either thermal spallation or thermal weakening, enhance drilling performance in deep, hard rock formations under realistic field conditions, and what are the underlying mechanisms of material removal? [Rossi 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
- **Test Site**: International Geothermal Centre (GZB) drill-test site, Bochum, Germany [Rossi 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Rock Sample**: A 0.5 × 0.5 × 2 m block of Central Aare granite, placed in a metal housing and cemented in place [Rossi 2020, pp. 3-5].
- **Drilling Equipment**: The CTMD system was integrated on a mobile, research-purpose drilling rig (Bo.Rex) [Rossi 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Data Collected**: Measurement-while-drilling (MWD) data (penetration depth, rotational speed, weight-on-bit, torque); acoustic emission (AE) signals from 16 sensors on the rock; optical and photoelectric scans of the spallation crater; three-dimensional reconstructions of cutter wear [Rossi 2020, pp. 3-7].

## Methods
The combined thermo-mechanical drilling (CTMD) method has two primary modes investigated in the field using a full-scale drilling rig [Rossi 2020, pp. 1-2]:
1.  **Thermal Spallation Drilling**: Uses an air-shielded flame jet to induce thermal stresses leading to rock fracture and spall detachment without bit rotation. Acoustic emissions (AEs) are monitored to characterize spallation onset. The resulting crater was mapped with a photoelectric distance sensor [Rossi 2020, pp. 2-2, 6-6].
2.  **Flame-Assisted Rotary Drilling**: Combines the flame jet for thermal weakening with a rotating drill bit featuring tungsten carbide cutters. A standalone-mechanical drilling test with identical parameters served as a benchmark. Penetration performance, rock removal rate, and cutter wear rate were quantitatively compared [Rossi 2020, pp. 2-3, 5-6].

The thermal assistance system uses oxygen and methane combustion, with a compressed air shield to protect the flame jet in the water-filled borehole [Rossi 2020, pp. 3-3].

## Key Findings
- **Technical Feasibility**: CTMD, including both thermal spallation and flame-assisted rotary drilling modes, is feasible under realistic field conditions with a water-filled borehole when the flame jet is air-shielded [Rossi 2020, pp. 6-6, 7-8].
- **Drilling Performance Enhancement**: Flame-assisted rotary drilling increased the rock removal rate by up to a factor of three compared to standalone-mechanical drilling in granite [Rossi 2020, pp. 7-8].
- **Process Efficiency**: The overall drilling efficiency, measured as the ratio of removed rock volume to cutter wear rate, improved by a factor of approximately 2.5, indicating a more effective balance between penetration and tool wear [Rossi 2020, pp. 7-8].
- **Mechanism of Removal**: The enhanced performance is attributed to an effective thermal-cracking removal mechanism, evidenced by a reduction in mechanical specific energy from 2.29 to 1.19 kJ/cm³ and the production of larger rock cuttings [Rossi 2020, pp. 7-8].
- **Thermal Spallation Monitoring**: Acoustic emission analysis successfully monitored the temporal occurrence of thermal spallation, which was enhanced by increasing the air shield flow rate [Rossi 2020, pp. 6-6].

## Limitations
- **Research-Purpose Drill Bit**: The drill bit design enabled comparison but the absolute penetration values (on the order of centimeters) are typical for this specific research-purpose bit face design in hard granitic rocks [Rossi 2020, pp. 7-8].
- **Thermal Specific Energy Estimation**: The value for thermal spallation drilling (114.3 kJ/cm³) is a rough approximation due to inaccuracies in estimating heat power from the combustion chamber and heat dissipation to the circulation water [Rossi 2020, pp. 6-7].
- **Deep-Drilling Apparatus**: The current system uses gaseous fuels supplied from the surface; modifications using liquid reactants and concentric pipes are identified as necessary for deep borehole applications [Rossi 2020, pp. 8-9].
- **In-Situ Stress Effects**: Additional investigations are required to evaluate the effects of in-situ stresses under deep drilling conditions on the thermal-cracking-enhanced rock removal performance [Rossi 2020, pp. 8-9].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **CTMD Integration**: The technology is designed to integrate into existing conventional drilling rigs, allowing for standalone-mechanical drilling as a third operational mode [Rossi 2020, pp. 2-2].
- **Rock Requirements for Spallation**: Thermal spallation mode requires specific source heat flux and rock material properties; it is impeded in soft rocks or rocks with stress-relieving cracks [Rossi 2020, pp. 1-2].
- **Operating Conditions**: Critical operating parameters include combustion power (40 kW), an equivalence ratio of 1.1, water flow rate (40 l/min), and air-shield flow rates [Rossi 2020, pp. 5-6].
- **Benchmarking**: The standalone-mechanical drilling test was conducted under identical operating conditions (weight-on-bit, rotational speeds, test duration, identical cutter configuration) for a direct and fair comparison [Rossi 2020, pp. 5-6].

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim 1**: For hard crystalline rocks like granite, integrating a flame jet into a conventional rotary drilling system can increase the rock removal rate by a factor of up to three under field conditions, provided the flame is effectively air-shielded from the water-based drilling fluid. [Source: Rossi 2020, pp. 7-8] [Condition: Weight-on-bit, rotational speed, and bit design held constant between assisted and unassisted tests].
- **Claim 2**: Thermal pre-weakening by a flame jet changes the dominant rock removal mechanism from scraping to a thermal-cracking process, demonstrated by a decrease in mechanical specific energy and a larger average size of rock cuttings. [Source: Rossi 2020, pp. 7-8] [Condition: Observed in granite; the claim may extend to other hard crystalline rocks that undergo significant thermal cracking].
- **Claim 3**: In a combined thermo-mechanical drilling system, while flame-assistance increases the total removed rock volume and bit wear, the ratio of removal to wear (process efficiency) can still be substantially higher (by a factor of ~2.5) than for conventional drilling. [Source: Rossi 2020, pp. 7-8] [Condition: For the specific modular bit design and operating conditions tested on granite].
- **Claim 4**: Acoustic emission (AE) monitoring, particularly analyzing signal energy and spectral centroid, is a viable technique for detecting the onset and intensity of thermal spallation drilling under field conditions. [Source: Rossi 2020, pp. 6-6] [Condition: Requires attachment of broadband AE sensors to the target rock sample].
