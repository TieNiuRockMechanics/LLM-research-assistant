# Thermal Effect on Permeability in a Single Granite Fracture: Experiment and Theoretical Model.

## One-line Summary
This study isolates the mechanical thermal effect on fracture permeability by conducting permeability tests on granite fractures using argon gas, and develops a coupled thermal-mechanical model that incorporates asperity damage to predict the observed permeability changes.

## Research Question
How does temperature, in the absence of chemical effects, influence the permeability of a single granite fracture under constant confining pressure, and what are the underlying mechanical mechanisms? [Peng 2020, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
The study uses five cylindrical samples of Beishan granite (diameter 50 mm, length 100 mm) collected from the Beishan area, Gansu Province, China, a candidate site for a high-level radioactive waste repository. Each sample contains a single artificial fracture created via Brazilian disc tensile testing. The granite is composed of 30% quartz, 35% plagioclase, 25% alkali feldspar, 6% biotite, and 4% muscovite by volume, with grain sizes of 0.1–0.2 mm. Fracture surface roughness was characterized using a 3D blue light scanner and the dimensionless parameter Z₂. [Peng 2020, pp. 2-2]

## Methods
1.  **Sample Preparation:** Fractures were created by splitting cylindrical samples. Unwanted edge damage was filled with silica gel, and samples were inserted into heat-resistant rubber sleeves. [Peng 2020, pp. 2-2]
2.  **Experimental Setup:** A self-designed system with a confining cell (up to 50 MPa), a high-temperature furnace (up to 200°C), and a permeability measurement system using high-pressure argon gas was used. [Peng 2020, pp. 2-3]
3.  **Permeability Measurement:** A quasi-stationary flow test was performed. The pressure decay in a buffer reservoir was monitored, and the hydraulic aperture was calculated using the cubic law (Poiseuille equation) for parallel plates, assuming isothermal gas flow. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-3]
4.  **Experimental Scenarios:**
    *   **Scenario I (Creep):** Samples 1-3 were tested at a constant confining pressure of 5 MPa and constant temperatures of 22°C, 90°C, and 150°C for 22 days. Permeability was measured every 24 hours. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-5]
    *   **Scenario II (Cyclic Heating-Cooling):** Samples 4-5 were subjected to three heating-cooling cycles (30°C to 150°C and back) at a constant confining pressure of 5 MPa. Permeability was measured at 30°C intervals. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-5]
5.  **Theoretical Model:** A coupled thermal-mechanical model was developed. It models the fracture as two half-spaces separated by cylindrical asperities on a lattice. Asperities are treated as elastic-perfectly plastic materials. The model incorporates:
    *   Thermal expansion/contraction of asperity height.
    *   Temperature-dependent Young's modulus.
    *   Irreversible (residual) plastic deformation of asperities upon unloading due to stress redistribution from temperature changes. [Peng 2020, pp. 5-8]

## Key Findings
1.  **Creep Effect:** Under constant temperature and confining pressure, fracture permeability decreases with time due to creep-induced closure, following a negative exponential function. The reduction is greater at 90°C and 150°C than at 22°C. [Peng 2020, pp. 4-4, 10-10]
2.  **Cyclic Heating-Cooling Effect:** Fracture aperture decreases significantly during heating and shows only slight changes during cooling, leading to a net reduction after each cycle. The magnitude of permeability reduction decreases with increasing cycles. [Peng 2020, pp. 4-4, 10-10]
3.  **Model Validation:** The coupled thermal-mechanical model, which accounts for asperity damage and temperature-dependent Young's modulus, properly predicts the experimental results for normalized aperture evolution and local aperture distributions. [Peng 2020, pp. 9-9, 10-10]
4.  **Key Mechanisms:** The residual plastic deformation of asperities and the decline in Young's modulus with increasing temperature are identified as playing important roles in thermally-induced fracture closure. [Peng 2020, pp. 9-9, 10-10]

## Limitations
1.  The study assumes parallel plate flow (cubic law) to calculate hydraulic aperture, which is an approximation for rough fractures. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-3]
2.  It is difficult to separate creep-induced and heating-induced fracture closure during the experiments, as creep occurs concurrently with temperature changes. [Peng 2020, pp. 10-10]
3.  The model does not account for the effect of temperature on the creep rate. [Peng 2020, pp. 10-10]
4.  A pump malfunction during testing of sample 5 caused an unplanned increase in confining pressure to ~17 MPa, which was not included in the model simulation. [Peng 2020, pp. 9-9]
5.  Direct comparison between hydraulic apertures (from experiments) and mechanical apertures (from the model) is not possible; validation relies on similar evolutionary trends. [Peng 2020, pp. 9-9]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Chemical effects (e.g., pressure solution, water-rock reactions) on permeability are isolated by using argon gas instead of water for permeability tests. [Peng 2020, pp. 1-1, 2-2]
2.  Gas flow through the fracture is isothermal. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-3]
3.  Matrix permeability is negligible; gas flows only through fracture voids. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-3]
4.  The fracture can be approximated as two parallel plates for hydraulic aperture calculation. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-3]
5.  In the theoretical model, asperities behave as elastic-perfectly plastic materials. [Peng 2020, pp. 7-7]
6.  The change in asperity radius due to thermal expansion is neglected. [Peng 2020, pp. 8-8]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Hydraulic Aperture (e):** Calculated from pressure decay data using the cubic law. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-3]
*   **Confining Pressure:** Constant at 5 MPa for all tests. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-5]
*   **Temperature:** Varied between 22°C and 150°C. [Peng 2020, pp. 3-5]
*   **Surface Roughness (Z₂):** Dimensionless parameter characterizing fracture surface roughness. [Peng 2020, pp. 2-2]
*   **Young's Modulus (E):** Temperature-dependent in the model: E = (1 + βΔT)E₀. [Peng 2020, pp. 8-8]
*   **Coefficient of Thermal Expansion (α):** Used to calculate asperity height change. [Peng 2020, pp. 8-8]
*   **Yield Strain (ε₀):** Threshold for asperity plastic deformation in the model. [Peng 2020, pp. 7-7]
*   **Contact Ratio:** Proportion of contact area over the fracture surface area. [Peng 2020, pp. 9-9]

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Claim:** Under constant confining pressure, the permeability of a single granite fracture decreases over time due to creep, and this reduction is more pronounced at higher temperatures (up to 150°C).
    *   **Condition:** Isolated mechanical effect (no water), constant confining pressure (5 MPa), temperature range 22-150°C.
    *   **Limitation:** Specific to Beishan granite with artificial fractures; creep behavior may differ for natural fractures or other rock types.
    *   **Source:** [Peng 2020, pp. 4-4, 10-10]
2.  **Claim:** Cyclic thermal loading (30°C to 150°C) causes irreversible reduction in granite fracture permeability, with the largest reduction occurring in the first cycle.
    *   **Condition:** Constant confining pressure (5 MPa), heating-cooling cycles.
    *   **Limitation:** Magnitude of reduction may depend on initial aperture, roughness, and number of cycles.
    *   **Source:** [Peng 2020, pp. 4-4, 10-10]
3.  **Claim:** A coupled thermal-mechanical model that incorporates asperity damage (elastic-perfectly plastic behavior) and temperature-dependent Young's modulus can effectively predict thermally-induced changes in fracture aperture.
    *   **Condition:** Model validated against experimental data for normalized aperture evolution.
    *   **Limitation:** Model uses simplified asperity geometry (cylinders on a lattice) and does not account for temperature-dependent creep rate.
    *   **Source:** [Peng 2020, pp. 9-9, 10-10]

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study builds on the fracture deformation model by Hopkins (1991) and Pyrak-Nolte and Morris (2000). [Peng 2020, pp. 5-5]
*   It addresses a gap in previous work by isolating the mechanical thermal effect from chemical effects (e.g., pressure solution) that were coupled in earlier studies. [Peng 2020, pp. 1-1, 2-2]
*   The concept of "thermal over-closure" proposed by Barton (2007) is relevant to the observed irreversible closure. [Peng 2020, pp. 1-1]
*   The experimental approach is contrasted with long-term in-situ tests like the Yucca Mountain Drift Scale Test, which involved coupled thermal-hydrological-chemical-mechanical processes. [Peng 2020, pp. 1-1]
