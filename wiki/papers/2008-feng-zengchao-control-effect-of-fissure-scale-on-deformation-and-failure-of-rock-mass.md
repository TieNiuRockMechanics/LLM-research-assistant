# Control Effect of Fissure Scale on Deformation and Failure of Rock Mass.

## One-line Summary
Multi‑scale defect arrangement governs rock deformation and failure, with large‑scale fissures playing a dominant control role; heterogeneity amplifies this effect. [Feng 2008, pp. 1-6]

## Research Question
How does the scale of fissures (defect level) control the deformation and failure of rock masses, and what is the relative contribution of different fissure sizes to rock strength degradation? [Feng 2008, pp. 1-6]

## Study Area / Data
- Field fracture mapping of coal seams from multiple Chinese mining districts (e.g., ྎᇜါঀ, ೶ঀ, ဝಅ၂ঀ, ຦ሌঀ, ඣოঀ) – see Table 2. [Feng 2008, pp. 1-4]
- Laboratory‑scale fracture statistics on 17 coal samples (Tables 3 and 4) with observation scales from 100 mm down to 1 mm. [Feng 2008, pp. 1-4]
- Representative elementary volume (REV) for the studied coal rock is 10⁻² m. [Feng 2008, pp. 1-4]

## Methods
1. **Fractal characterisation** – Number‑scale relationship of fissures described by $N(\delta) = N_0 \, \delta^{-D}$, where $\delta$ is crack length, $N(\delta)$ the number of fissures, $N_0$ a reference count, and $D$ the fractal dimension (Equation 1). Applied to field, sample, and micro‑scale data. [Feng 2008, pp. 1-4]
2. **Numerical simulation** – 2 mm × 2 mm finite element mesh (500 × 500 cells, total 250 000 elements) of a 1 m rock block. Element failure follows a Weibull distribution with heterogeneity parameters $m$, $\sigma_c$, $\sigma_{0c}$ (Equation 2). Defects were distributed according to the measured fractal statistics, with 9 scale levels (L₀–L₈). [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
3. **Model parameters** – Three models were compared (Table 5):
   - Model 1: $m = 1.5$, $N_0 = 0.164$, $D = 1.6$
   - Model 2: $m = 3.0$, $N_0 = 0.164$, $D = 1.6$
   - Model 3: $m = 30.0$, $N_0 = 0.500$, $D = 1.5$ [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]

## Key Findings
1. Coal‑rock fissure systems exhibit a fractal dimension $D$ in the range $10^{-2}$ to $10^{0}$ across all scales examined (Tables 2–4). [Feng 2008, pp. 1-4]
2. **Strength reduction is scale‑dependent; the largest‑scale cracks dominate:**
   - Adding Level‑1 defects alone reduces compressive strength by **34.40 %–52.42 %** (Model 2) and **41.35 %** (Model 3). [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
   - Together, Level‑1 and Level‑2 defects account for **≥ 35 %** of the total strength decrease; higher levels (L₃–L₇) contribute less than **5 %** each. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
3. **Heterogeneity intensifies the control effect** – With the same defect number, Model 2 ($m = 3.0$) showed a strength reduction **21.30 %** greater than Model 1 ($m = 1.5$); Model 2 reduced strength by **75.66 %** compared to **40.82 %** for Model 1 under the same defect forcing. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
4. **Defect arrangement, not merely defect count, governs deformation and failure** – when fissures of different scales coexist, the spatial arrangement of the largest defects determines the overall load‑bearing capacity. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]

## Limitations
- The numerical simulations are two‑dimensional and use a fixed element size (2 mm), which may not capture true three‑dimensional crack propagation and interaction. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6, inferred from model description]
- The study focuses on coal rock; generality to other lithologies is not confirmed.
- No explicit discussion of in‑situ stress conditions or pore pressure effects is provided in the indexed fragments.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The rock matrix is treated as a heterogeneous continuum with Weibull‑distributed mesoscopic strength. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
- Fissure networks are assumed to follow a fractal scaling law $N(\delta) \propto \delta^{-D}$, with $D$ constant across the observed scales. [Feng 2008, pp. 1-4]
- The defect distribution is generated by populating the 2 mm elements with assigned weakness according to the fractal scheme. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
- All simulations are for uniaxial compression with incremental loading $\Delta \varepsilon = 0.000 33$. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Fissure scale hierarchy**: L₀ (reference crack length), L₁, …, L₈ (subdividing L₀ by powers of 2). [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
- **Fractal dimension $D$** of the fissure network. [Feng 2008, pp. 1-4]
- **Weibull heterogeneity parameter $m$**: controls the distribution breadth of element strength. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
- **Number of fissures at each scale**, $N(\delta)$. [Feng 2008, pp. 1-4]
- **Compressive strength** and **strength reduction rate** (%). [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]

## Reusable Claims
1. *Claim*: In fractured rock masses, the deformation and failure are controlled primarily by the largest‑scale defects (cracks, fissures, cavities). *Condition*: When multiple defect scales coexist. *Limitation*: Demonstrated for coal rock under uniaxial compression; 3D effects and confining pressure not tested. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
2. *Claim*: Strength reduction attributable to the largest one or two defect‑scale levels is never less than 35 % of the final strength loss. *Condition*: Fractal fissure network with $D \approx 1.5–1.6$. *Limitation*: Based on numerical models with specific ($m$, $N_0$) parameters. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
3. *Claim*: An increase in the heterogeneity parameter $m$ (more uniform meso‑strength) amplifies the control effect of large fissures on strength degradation. *Condition*: Same defect population. *Limitation*: Verified for $m = 1.5$, $3.0$, $30.0$. [Feng 2008, pp. 4-6]
4. *Claim*: Fissure systems in coal rock are self‑similar over a scale range of at least $10^{-2}$ m to $10^{0}$ m, with fractal dimension $D \approx 1.28–1.85$. *Condition*: Chinese coal seams examined. *Limitation*: May vary with tectonic history. [Feng 2008, pp. 1-4]
