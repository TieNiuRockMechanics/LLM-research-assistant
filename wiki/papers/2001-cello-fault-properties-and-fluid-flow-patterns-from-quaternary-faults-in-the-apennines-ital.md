# Fault Properties and Fluid Flow Patterns from Quaternary Faults in the Apennines, Italy.

## One-line Summary

This study investigates the permeability structure, fluid characteristics, and fluid flow patterns of Quaternary fault zones in the central and southern Apennines of Italy through integrated geo-structural and fluid inclusion analysis, revealing that active fault systems control fluid circulation from both super- and deep-crustal levels.

## Research Question

They also assess the potential of quantitative structural analysis in situ for unraveling fault history and for evaluating the spatial variations of fluid pre-surface along a given structure by referring to their results on two active systems of the Quaternary fault network of peninsular Italy [Cello 2001, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data

The study area is located in the High Agri Valley (southern Italy) and in the axial zones of the central Apennines, roughly NE-SW direction while it is still contracting parallel to the mountain chain (i.e. in a roughly NW-SE direction), possibly as a result of the convergence between Africa and Europe [Cello 2001, pp. 2-3]. The area is currently extending in a NE-SW direction while it is still contracting parallel to the mountain chain (i.e. in a roughly NW-SE direction), possibly as a result of the convergence between Africa and Europe [Cello 2001, pp. 1-2].

Two active fault systems were studied:
- **Val d'Agri Fault System (VAFS):** The Val d'Agri Fault System (VAFS; Fig. 3) dissects pre-existing structures belonging to the southern Apennines thrust belt, which developed from the Neogene deformation of different paleo-tectonic domains of the Afro-Adriatic continental margin [Cello 2001, pp. 2-3].
- **Central Apennines Fault System (CAFS):** The Central Apennines Fault System, or CAFS (Cello et al., 1997, 2000). The CAFS discusses the Neogene thrust structure of this sector of the Apennines, and extends from Camerino to L'Aquila (Fig. 2) [Cello 2001, pp. 1-2].

The CAFS consists of roughly N120 trending left-lateral strike-slip faults (Monaco et al., 1998) and associated features, and includes several fault-bounded intramontane basins infilled with Pleistocene-Holocene continental deposits that are often displaced by active fault segments [Cello 2001, pp. 2-3].

## Methods

The methodology involved several integrated approaches:

**Field mapping and data collection:** Detailed structural mapping (mostly at the 1:10,000 scale) in order to obtain detailed fault maps of the Quaternary fault network dissecting the fold and thrust belt structure and associated fabrics [Cello 2001, pp. 5-5]. Quantitative data on the spatial attributes of CAFS- and VAFS-related structures were obtained from fracture statistics (Mandelbrot, 1983) of the different fault patterns mapped out at various scales [Cello 2001, pp. 5-7].

**Scan line and scan area analysis:** For each discontinuity set, the following parameters were recorded: (i) spatial position, (ii) attitude (dip direction and angle of dip), (iii) typology (minor fault, fracture, cleavage plane, extension vein), (iv) width, (v) occurrence and type of filling, (vi) morphology of discontinuity surface (ranging from very rough to smooth) [Cello 2001, pp. 5-5]. For fault discontinuities, additional parameters included: (vi) occurrence and type of fault rocks, (vii) azimuth and angle of plunge of mechanical striae/shear breccia [Cello 2001, pp. 5-5].

**Fracture statistics:** The spatial position of rock discontinuities is recorded in histograms of the type shown in Figs. 4a, 5a and 5b, where the distribution of the different sets recognized on the basis of orientation data (Fig. 4b) allowed us to evaluate quantitatively the contribution of each set within the total fracture field and to identify fault sections that can be considered as 'regional' features related to thrust belt evolution [Cello 2001, pp. 5-5].

**Petrographic analysis:** A fluid inclusion study of the calcite crystals in-filled the extensional veins analyzed in the studied sectors of the Apennines [Cello 2001, pp. 7-9]. Petrographic analysis was performed by means of optical transmission microscopy, whereas microthermometric analysis was carried out by using the U.S.G.S. gas flow heating/freezing stage calibrated on the basis of synthetic fluid inclusions (Bodnar and Sterner, 1984) [Cello 2001, pp. 7-9].

**Fluid inclusion analysis:** Temperatures of phase changes at Tm,ice are accurate to ±0.1°C; homogenization temperatures (Th) are accurate to ±1°C [Cello 2001, pp. 9-9]. The analyzed calcite crystals are rich in primary fluid inclusions although most of them are too small to be investigated [Cello 2001, pp. 9-9].

**Box-counting analysis:** Population graphs of the cumulative length (Fig. 5d) and spacing (Fig. 5e) can also be computed, together with integrated fault zone thickness data (Fig. 6), with the aim of defining possible scaling relations among structures of different size [Cello 2001, pp. 5-7].

## Key Findings

1. **Fractal nature of fault patterns:** The box-counting curves derived from the maps obtained from scan area data as well as from map patterns of the Col-lorito fault array (Fig. 5c) and from the whole CAFS (Fig. 5d) display a linear relation-ship on a log-log plot, thus indicating that the analyzed discontinuity patterns are fractals [Cello 2001, pp. 7-7].

2. **Self-similarity across scales:** The value of the box-counting dimension (D) varies within a very limited range (from 1.25 to 1.30); the low-values are indicative of a poorly-developed fracture geometry that may result from a low degree of maturity of the fault pattern (Cello, 1995) [Cello 2001, pp. 7-7].

3. **Fault zone thickness scaling:** The fault zone thickness distribution in carbonate rocks [Cello 2001, pp. 10-15]. The box-counting curves derived from the maps obtained from scan area data as well as from map patterns of the Col-lorito fault array (Fig. 5c) and from the whole CAFS (Fig. 5d) display a linear relation-ship on a log-log plot, thus indicating that the analyzed discontinuity patterns are fractals [Cello 2001, pp. 7-7].

4. **Fluid inclusion evidence for deep circulation:** The composition of the analyzed fluid inclusions and related homogenization temperatures are associated with fault-driven fluid circulation from both super- and deep (4-6 km) levels [Cello 2001, pp. 1-1].

5. **Active evolving structures:** The CAFS and VAFS structures are still evolving within active fault systems, with their geometric and kinematic characteristics shaped by long-lived seismotectonic processes [Cello 2001, pp. 15-15].

6. **Fluid flow control by fault architecture:** Active fault zones within the CAFS and VAFS play an important role in controlling seismic energy release in both central and southern Apennines, and their geometric and kinematic properties can be adequately described by means of a fractal (box-counting) dimension [Cello 2001, pp. 15-15].

7. **Cataclasite characteristics:** The majority of rock samples from the fault cores belong to the cataclasite series, ranging from protocataclasite to cataclasite with little calcite cement, and grain size below 0.5 mm (Fig. 8a and b), where the embedding damage zone consists of intensely fractured rocks and fault breccias [Cello 2001, pp. 7-7].

8. **Calcite vein characteristics:** Calcite blocky crystals contained within the veins commonly show two (or rarely three) thin (<0.1 mm) straight lamellae [Cello 2001, pp. 15-15]. One sample from a fault zone in quartzy sandstone from the Val d'Agri area consists of a fine crush breccia made of quartz and subordinate feldspar grains [Cello 2001, pp. 7-7].

## Reusable Claims

- **Fault zone thickness follows power-law scaling:** In carbonate rocks of the Apennines, fault zone thickness distribution follows a power-law relationship (y = 17,627x^(-0.2827), R² = 0.9734), indicating self-similar scaling across different structural sizes [Cello 2001, pp. 10-15].

- **Active faults control fluid circulation at multiple depths:** The composition of fluid inclusions and homogenization temperatures demonstrate that active fault systems in the Apennines facilitate fluid circulation from both super-crustal and deep (4-6 km) levels, with fluids likely originating from Triassic evaporite-bearing sequences [Cello 2001, pp. 1-1; Cello 2001, pp. 16-16].

- **Fractal dimension indicates fault maturity:** The box-counting dimension D (1.25-1.30) of fault patterns is indicative of a poorly-developed fracture geometry resulting from low maturity of the fault pattern, suggesting these are relatively young, still-evolving structures [Cello 2001, pp. 7-7].

- **Fault rocks record progressive deformation:** The cataclasite series in fault cores ranges from protocataclasite to cataclasite, with calcite twin lamellae in veins recording deformation at temperatures below 150°C under critically resolved shear stress conditions [Cello 2001, pp. 7-7; Cello 2001, pp. 15-15].

- **Integrated analysis reveals permeability architecture:** Systematic geo-structural analysis combining field mapping, fracture statistics, and fluid inclusion studies provides a powerful tool for assessing the overall architecture and permeability structure of fault zones [Cello 2001, pp. 1-2].
