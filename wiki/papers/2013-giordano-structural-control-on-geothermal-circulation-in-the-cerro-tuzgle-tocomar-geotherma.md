# Structural Control on Geothermal Circulation in the Cerro Tuzgle–Tocomar Geothermal Volcanic Area (Puna Plateau, Argentina).

## One-line Summary
Geothermal fluid circulation in the Cerro Tuzgle–Tocomar area is structurally controlled by the NW–SE Calama–Olacapato–Toro (COT) fault system and intersecting fractures, which create secondary permeability in the basement reservoir and localize upwelling.

## Research Question
How do the structural framework and associated fracture patterns control the circulation of geothermal fluids in the Cerro Tuzgle–Tocomar volcanic area?

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Cerro Tuzgle–Tocomar geothermal volcanic area on the Puna plateau in NW Argentina, crossed by the active NW–SE Calama–Olacapato–Toro (COT) fault system [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-1]. Data include new field-based stratigraphic and hydrogeological surveys, analysis of morphostructural lineaments from remote-sensed images (SRTM and ASTER DEMs), reinterpretation of unpublished vertical electrical soundings (VES), and compilation of existing geochemical and geophysical data [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-3].

## Methods
Methods included: 1) Field-based stratigraphic and hydrogeological mapping to define rock units and spring distributions [Giordano 2013, pp. 3-4, 6-8]. 2) Semi-automatic detection and manual tracing of morphological lineaments from multiple hillshade renderings of DEMs to analyze fracture patterns [Giordano 2013, pp. 11-13]. 3) Reinterpretation of geoelectric (VES) profiles to model subsurface resistivity units and fluid pathways [Giordano 2013, pp. 8-11]. 4) Integration of field, remote sensing, and geophysical data to construct a conceptual model of geothermal circulation [Giordano 2013, pp. 16-17].

## Key Findings
1.  The main geothermal reservoir is located within or below the fractured Pre-Palaeozoic–Ordovician basement units, which have unevenly distributed secondary permeability [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-1, 16-16].
2.  Recharge occurs via infiltration in high-altitude ridges (>4500 m a.s.l.) where fractured basement rocks outcrop [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-1, 16-16].
3.  Below 4500 m a.s.l., the reservoir is covered by low-permeability Miocene–Quaternary units that host a shallow, poorly productive aquifer [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-1, 8-11].
4.  Geothermal fluids upwell where main NW–SE COT-parallel lineaments intersect with secondary structures (e.g., ENE–WSW faults), creating zones of high permeability, as seen at the Tocomar field [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-1, 16-16].
5.  The Tocomar field is more productive (higher temperature and discharge) than the Cerro Tuzgle field due to a more developed network of intersecting faults and fractures [Giordano 2013, pp. 16-16].
6.  Lineament analysis shows the most frequent and longest lineaments strike NW–SE, ENE–WSW, and N–S, corresponding to regional tectonic structures [Giordano 2013, pp. 13-16].
7.  Newly discovered phreatic craters (maars) near Tocomar indicate the presence of pressurized fluids at depth [Giordano 2013, pp. 4-6].

## Limitations
1.  The study area is remote and difficult to access, limiting detailed field surveys [Giordano 2013, pp. 3-4].
2.  Reliance on remote sensing for lineament analysis introduces potential operator bias, though tested via multiple illumination directions [Giordano 2013, pp. 11-13].
3.  Geoelectric data (VES) are unpublished and their reinterpretation is based on provided resistivity values and geometries [Giordano 2013, pp. 8-11].
4.  The conceptual model integrates data of varying resolution and vintage (e.g., older geochemical data) [Giordano 2013, pp. 3-4].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Secondary permeability from fracturing is the primary control on fluid flow in the basement reservoir [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-1, 16-16].
2.  The low-permeability Tertiary–Quaternary cover effectively seals the reservoir at lower elevations (<4500 m) [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-1, 8-11].
3.  Morphological lineaments detected from DEMs are reliable proxies for subsurface fracture zones [Giordano 2013, pp. 11-13].
4.  The geothermal system is recharged by meteoric water infiltrating at high elevations [Giordano 2013, pp. 6-8, 16-16].
5.  Active tectonics along the COT maintain open fracture permeability [Giordano 2013, pp. 16-17].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Fracture Density/Orientation:** Measured as lineament density (km/km²) and azimuthal frequency from DEM analysis [Giordano 2013, pp. 13-16].
*   **Rock Permeability:** Qualitatively assessed (primary vs. secondary) for different lithologies (e.g., Ordovician basement vs. Trinchera ignimbrite) [Giordano 2013, pp. 4-6].
*   **Elevation:** Critical for defining recharge zones (>4500 m) and cover distribution [Giordano 2013, pp. 6-8].
*   **Resistivity:** Used to distinguish geologic units and identify fluid pathways (<5 Ωm for upwelling zones) [Giordano 2013, pp. 8-11].
*   **Spring Temperature/Discharge:** Indicators of geothermal productivity, higher at Tocomar [Giordano 2013, pp. 3-4, 16-16].
*   **Geothermometer Temperatures:** Estimated reservoir temperatures (e.g., 134–143 °C from K-Mg and silica) [Giordano 2013, pp. 3-4].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** In volcanic areas with active strike-slip fault systems (like the COT). **Claim:** Geothermal reservoirs are likely hosted in fractured crystalline basement, with fluid upwelling focused at structural intersections (e.g., main fault with secondary extensional fractures). **Limitation:** Requires confirmation of basement fracturing and reservoir presence via geophysics.
2.  **Condition:** Where low-permeability volcanic/sedimentary covers overlie a fractured basement. **Claim:** Recharge is restricted to high-elevation outcrop areas of the permeable basement, while the cover acts as a seal at lower elevations, potentially hosting a shallow, cool aquifer that mixes with upwelling geothermal fluids. **Limitation:** Cover permeability must be field-verified.
3.  **Condition:** For remote geothermal prospecting. **Claim:** Integration of field stratigraphy, hydrogeological mapping, and DEM-based lineament analysis is effective for defining structural controls on circulation and identifying target zones for exploration. **Limitation:** Effectiveness depends on DEM resolution and field validation.

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study builds on and reinterprets earlier geophysical (magnetotelluric, gravimetric) and geochemical surveys of the area by Aquater (1980), Coira (1995), and Sainato and Pomposiello (1997) [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-3].
*   It relates the structural framework to the regional tectonic context of the Central Andes and the active COT fault system studied by Petrinovic et al. (2006) and Acocella et al. (2011) [Giordano 2013, pp. 1-1, 16-16].
*   The conceptual model for fluid circulation in fractured basement draws on general principles discussed by Cox et al. (2001) [Giordano 2013, pp. 16-17].
*   The identification of the Pirgua Subgroup as a potential reservoir connects to findings at other Andean geothermal sites like Rosario de la Frontera [Giordano 2013, pp. 4-4].

## Open Questions
1.  What is the precise geometry, connectivity, and permeability of the fracture network within the basement reservoir?
2.  What is the exact depth, extent, and temperature of the deeper magma chamber heat source?
3.  How does the shallow aquifer system interact dynamically with the deeper geothermal reservoir over time?
4.  What is the long-term sustainability and potential capacity of the geothermal resource for exploitation?
5.  Can the lineament analysis methodology be quantitatively calibrated to subsurface permeability measurements?
