# Experimental and Visual Analysis of Single-Phase Flow through Rough Fracture Replicas.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally analyzes single-phase water flow through seven rough fracture replicas, finding that surface roughness, quantified by fractal dimension, reduces transmissivity and increases flow channeling, with turbulence developing earlier than in smooth fractures.

## Research Question
How does surface roughness, quantified by variogram fractal dimension, affect single-phase flow through tightly-closed rough fracture replicas under varying normal loads and injection directions?

## Study Area / Data
Seven cubic rock blocks (20x20x20 cm³) from various natural stone quarries across Turkey were selected for lithological diversity, including limestone, marble, and granite [Develi 2015, pp. 3-4]. Single fractures were created via indirect tensile tests and reproduced as mirror-image models with a silicone rubber lower part and a transparent polyurethane upper part [Develi 2015, pp. 3-4, 4-4]. The models represent tightly closed, perfectly matching joints with no initial aperture [Develi 2015, pp. 4-4].

## Methods
Surface roughness was measured using a computer-controlled scanning device on the polyurethane halves, generating 2D elevation data sets (190x190 points, 1 mm interval) [Develi 2015, pp. 4-4, 4-6]. Fractal dimension (Dva) was calculated via variogram analysis on 1D profiles extracted from these data sets [Develi 2015, pp. 4-6]. Single-phase water flow experiments were conducted under non-loading and normal loading conditions (N=2, 4, 8, 16 kg) for two opposite injection directions (NS and SN) [Develi 2015, pp. 6-7]. Flow patterns were visualized using a fluorescent tracer under black light and analyzed with MATLAB image processing to calculate the percentage of water-invaded wet planar area (WPA) and dry planar area (DPA) [Develi 2015, pp. 7-8].

## Key Findings
- Fractal dimension (Dva) ranged from 1.263 to 1.390 and showed an increasing trend with average grain size (R²=0.70) [Develi 2015, pp. 4-6].
- Pressure vs. injection rate curves exhibited a short initial linear portion followed by nonlinear behavior. The inflection point ending the linear trend occurred at Q=5.0×10⁻⁷ m³/s (Re=2.80) for all rough fracture models, independent of roughness, loading, or direction [Develi 2015, pp. 8-9, 9-9].
- For a smooth parallel plate model, the linear trend ended at Re=3.73, indicating turbulence develops earlier for rough fractures [Develi 2015, pp. 9-9].
- Transmissivity (kA) decreased with increasing fractal dimension and normal loading. The relationship was strongest under moderate normal load (N=2 kg, R²=0.82) [Develi 2015, pp. 9-10, 10-12].
- Under high normal loads (e.g., N=16 kg), transmissivity values for different fractures converged [Develi 2015, pp. 10-12].
- Flow exhibited directional dependence (anisotropy), with different transmissivity values for opposite injection directions on the same fracture [Develi 2015, pp. 12-15].
- The percentage of wet planar area (WPA) decreased with increasing fractal dimension (Dva) and the ratio of fracture surface area to apparent area (Af/A). The Af/A ratio correlated better with WPA than Dva [Develi 2015, pp. 15-16].
- Dry planar areas (DPA) that did not involve flow could constitute up to 20% of the fracture area, controlled by tortuous flow channeling [Develi 2015, pp. 15-16].

## Limitations
- The fracture models are tightly closed with no initial aperture; flow occurs through forced mechanical aperture induced by injection pressure [Develi 2015, pp. 4-4].
- Micro-scale invisible voids may exist due to potential deformations during casting [Develi 2015, pp. 4-4].
- Normal loading was limited to a maximum of 16 kg due to material constraints of the silicone and polyurethane models [Develi 2015, pp. 6-7].
- Visualization was only performed under non-loading conditions [Develi 2015, pp. 7-8].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Fracture models represent perfectly matching, tightly closed joint-type fractures [Develi 2015, pp. 4-4].
- Flow is single-phase (water) and experiments are conducted horizontally, negating gravity effects [Develi 2015, pp. 6-7].
- Normal loading is applied via constant weights, providing homogeneous stress distribution [Develi 2015, pp. 6-7].
- Injection rates are constant and range from 1.667×10⁻⁷ to 4.667×10⁻⁶ m³/s [Develi 2015, pp. 6-7].

## Reusable Claims
- Under non-loading conditions, the initial linear flow regime ends at Re≈2.80 for rough fractures, compared to Re≈3.73 for smooth parallel plates, indicating earlier turbulence onset in rough fractures [Develi 2015, pp. 9-9].
- Transmissivity decreases with increasing fractal dimension, with the relationship strengthening under moderate normal loads (e.g., N=2 kg, R²=0.82) [Develi 2015, pp. 9-10].
- Dry planar areas can constitute up to 20% of the fracture area, controlled by tortuous flow channeling [Develi 2015, pp. 15-16].
- The ratio of fracture surface area to apparent area (Af/A) is a more successful indicator of wet planar area (WPA) than fractal dimension (Dva) [Develi 2015, pp. 15-16].

## Connections To Other Work
- The characteristic nonlinear pressure-flow curves align with in-situ water test curves for deformable rocks described by Louis and Maini (1970) [Develi 2015, pp. 8-9].
- Findings on turbulence developing earlier for rough fractures support results from Chen et al. (2009) [Develi 2015, pp. 9-9].
- The decrease in transmissivity with normal loading is consistent with experimental works by Hakami and Larsson (1996), Chen et al. (2000), and Watanabe et al. (2005) [Develi 2015, pp. 10-12].
- The study addresses the unresolved issue of surface roughness's effect on flow, as highlighted by Berkowitz (2002) [Develi 2015, pp. 2-3].

## Open Questions
- Further research is needed to clarify the turbulent nature of flow at high injection rates (Q≥4.0×10⁻⁷ m³/s, Re≥22.38) where curves bend upward [Develi 2015, pp. 15-16].
- How do micro-scale invisible voids in the models affect flow characteristics?
- Can the findings be generalized to other rock types and more complex fracture geometries?
