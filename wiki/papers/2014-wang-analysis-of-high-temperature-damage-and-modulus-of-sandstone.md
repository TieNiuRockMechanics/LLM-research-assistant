# Analysis of High Temperature Damage and Modulus of Sandstone.

## One-line Summary
This study analyzes the staged compression behavior, modulus characteristics, and thermal damage of sandstone after exposure to temperatures from 25°C to 1000°C, proposing a damage factor based on the modulus of the compact stage.

## Research Question
How do high temperatures (25°C to 1000°C) affect the mechanical properties, specifically the moduli of different compression stages and the damage factor, of sandstone?

## Study Area / Data
The study uses sandstone samples. The mineral composition is approximately 52% quartz, 27% feldspar, 8% mica, and 6% other minerals [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4]. Samples were prepared as cylinders with a diameter of 50 mm and a height of 100 mm according to ISRM standards [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4]. The natural water content was 1.41%, and the saturated water content was 2.91% [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4].

## Methods
- **Sample Preparation:** Cylindrical sandstone samples (φ50 mm × 100 mm) were prepared following ISRM suggested methods [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4].
- **Heating:** Samples were heated in a RX3-20-12 furnace to target temperatures (25, 100, 200, 400, 600, 800, 1000°C) at a rate of 10°C/min and held for 3 hours [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4].
- **Testing:** Uniaxial compression tests were conducted using a HYY servo-controlled testing machine at a loading rate of 0.5 MPa/s [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4]. Ultrasonic P-wave velocity (Vp) was measured using an RSM-SYS5N detector with a pulse width of 0.1 μs [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4].
- **Analysis:** Complete stress-strain curves were analyzed to define moduli for four stages: compact stage (Mc), linear elastic stage (Ml), yield stage (My), and failure stage (Mf) [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4]. A damage factor (D) was calculated using two methods: based on P-wave velocity (Vp) and based on the modulus of the compact stage (Mc) [Wang 2014, pp. 4-6].

## Key Findings
1.  The compression of sandstone after high temperature exhibits prominent staged characteristics [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4].
2.  Peak stress decreased significantly from 57.96 MPa at 25°C to 11.87 MPa at 1000°C, a reduction of 79.5% [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4].
3.  The moduli of all four stages generally decrease with increasing temperature, with a sharp decline observed above 800°C [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4].
4.  The modulus of the compact stage (Mc) has a strong linear correlation with longitudinal wave velocity (Vp), with an adjusted R-square of 0.97 [Wang 2014, pp. 4-6].
5.  The damage factor defined by the modulus of the compact stage (Mc) is considered more scientific than the Vp-based method, as the latter ignores density and Poisson's ratio [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4].
6.  Thermal damage is more sensitive to temperature within the range of 25°C to 200°C [Wang 2014, pp. 1-4].

## Limitations
- The study focuses on a single type of sandstone; results may vary for other rock types.
- The heating and cooling history (e.g., cooling rate) is not detailed beyond the 3-hour hold time.
- The analysis of damage factors relies on specific definitions (e.g., modulus of compact stage) which may not be universally applied.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The sandstone samples are homogeneous and isotropic.
- The modulus of the compact stage (Mc) is a valid and representative parameter for defining damage.
- The relationship between modulus and damage is linear, as per the formula D = 1 - (M/M0) [Wang 2014, pp. 4-6].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Temperature (T):** 25, 100, 200, 400, 600, 800, 1000 °C.
- **Moduli:** Compact stage modulus (Mc), Linear elastic modulus (Ml), Yield modulus (My), Failure modulus (Mf).
- **Ultrasonic Properties:** Longitudinal wave velocity (Vp).
- **Damage Factor (D):** Defined by Vp (D = 1 - (Vp/Vp0)²) and by Mc (D = 1 - (Mc/Mc0)).

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** The modulus of the compact stage (Mc) is strongly linearly correlated with P-wave velocity (Vp) for sandstone after high-temperature treatment (Adj. R-Square = 0.97). **Condition:** For the tested sandstone within 25-1000°C. **Limitation:** Specific to the material and experimental setup.
- **Claim:** Defining the damage factor using the modulus of the compact stage (Mc) is more scientifically sound than using P-wave velocity (Vp) because it accounts for material density and Poisson's ratio. **Condition:** For uniaxial compression analysis of thermally damaged rock. **Limitation:** Assumes the modulus of the compact stage is a fundamental property.
- **Claim:** Sandstone exhibits heightened sensitivity to thermal damage in the temperature range of 25°C to 200°C. **Condition:** Based on the observed trends in the damage factor. **Limitation:** May be specific to the tested sandstone's mineralogy.
