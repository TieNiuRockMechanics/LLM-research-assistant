# Thermal Conductivity and Porosity Maps for Different Materials: A Combined Case Study of Granite and Sandstone.

## One-line Summary
This study uses thermal conductivity and porosity maps derived from Optical Scanning to characterize millimeter-scale petrophysical heterogeneities in granite and sandstone samples from a geothermal reservoir.

## Research Question
How can high-resolution thermal conductivity and porosity maps be used to quantify and determine the size of petrophysical heterogeneities induced by rock variability in granite and sandstone geothermal reservoirs?

## Study Area / Data
The study analyzed two rock samples from the EPS1 borehole at the deep experimental geothermal site of Soultz-sous-Forêts in the Upper Rhine Graben, France [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-2]. The granite sample (K195-4777) was extracted from approximately 2162 m depth, within a siliciﬁed/cataclased hydrothermal alteration facies [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-2]. The sandstone sample (EPS1 6 1) was extracted from approximately 1214 m depth, from the Buntsandstein sandstones formation ("Grès Vosgiens" facies) [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-2].

## Methods
Thermal conductivity maps were obtained using an Optical Scanning apparatus (Thermal Conductivity Scanner, TCS) [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-2]. The TCS measures thermal conductivity along a scan line with a resolution of 1 mm [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-2]. Maps were constructed by scanning the sample surface under both air-saturated and water-saturated conditions [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-4]. Porosity maps were computed from the thermal conductivity maps using a geometric mean mixing law model [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-4]. The calculated porosity was validated against total porosity measured via a water saturation test [Haffen 2017, pp. 4-4].

## Key Findings
The granite sample exhibited a bimodal distribution of thermal conductivity, corresponding to distinct structural zones: a damaged zone with altered feldspar (lower thermal conductivity, higher porosity) and a protolith/fault core zone rich in quartz (higher thermal conductivity, lower porosity) [Haffen 2017, pp. 4-6]. The sandstone sample showed a unimodal thermal conductivity distribution, where a decrease in grain size was associated with an increase in thermal conductivity and a decrease in porosity [Haffen 2017, pp. 6-7]. The mean porosity values calculated from the thermal conductivity maps (granite: 2.87 ± 0.14%; sandstone: 9.27 ± 0.46%) were close to those obtained from water saturation tests (granite: 2.90 ± 0.09%; sandstone: 9.92 ± 0.30%) [Haffen 2017, pp. 4-6].

## Limitations
The volume investigated by thermal conductivity scanning is limited to a maximum thickness of 2.5 cm, whereas water porosity measurements analyze the entire sample volume [Haffen 2017, pp. 7-7]. Water saturation can modify the contact resistance between rock components, potentially altering thermal conductivity characteristics [Haffen 2017, pp. 7-7]. A significant gap between the thermal conductivity of the standards and the sample can induce more extensive measurement errors [Haffen 2017, pp. 7-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The porosity calculation assumes that mineralogy and other microstructural parameters remain constant between air- and water-saturated states [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-4]. The method uses a simplified geometric mean mixing law model without needing an empirical model [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-4]. Thermal conductivity variations induced by sample heating during measurement (limited to 3°C) were neglected as they were inferior to the measurement error [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-2].

- Structural zones (e.g., damaged zone, protolith, fault core)

## Reusable Claims
- Thermal conductivity and porosity maps allow the determination of property variations at millimeter resolution on a core scale, enabling precise quantification of heterogeneity size [Haffen 2017, pp. 1-2].
- In granite, structural heterogeneities (fault zones) control porosity distribution, with porosity mainly located near faults along grain borders in altered zones [Haffen 2017, pp. 7-7].
- In sandstone, sedimentological heterogeneities (layered organization) control porosity distribution, with porosity varying according to facies and grain size within each layer [Haffen 2017, pp. 7-7].
- The percentage increase in thermal conductivity induced by water saturation is higher for feldspar-rich zones (lower initial thermal conductivity) than for quartz-rich zones [Haffen 2017, pp. 7-7].

## Connections To Other Work
The study references the importance of understanding thermal and hydraulic properties for geothermal modeling and installation planning, citing works by Clauser and Huenges (1995) and Hartmann et al. (2008) [Haffen 2017, pp. 1-2]. The Optical Scanning method is based on work by Popov et al. (1999) [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-2]. The porosity calculation method follows approaches from Pribnow and Sass (1995) and Surma and Geraud (2003) [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-4]. The geological context of the Soultz-sous-Forêts site is detailed in Genter and Traineau (1992) and Rosener (2007) [Haffen 2017, pp. 2-2].

## Open Questions
How can these high-resolution maps be integrated into larger-scale geothermal reservoir models to improve predictions of heat and fluid flow? What are the long-term implications of the identified heterogeneities for the sustainability and efficiency of a geothermal heat exchanger?
