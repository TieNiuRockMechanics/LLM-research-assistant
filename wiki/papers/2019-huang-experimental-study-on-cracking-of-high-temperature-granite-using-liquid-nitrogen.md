# Experimental Study on Cracking of High Temperature Granite Using Liquid Nitrogen.

## One-line Summary
Liquid nitrogen cooling effectively damages high-temperature granite, reducing its wave velocity and mechanical strength while significantly increasing its permeability, with the degree of damage increasing with the rock's initial temperature.

## Research Question
How does rapid cooling by liquid nitrogen affect the physical and mechanical properties of high-temperature granite, and what are the resulting damage characteristics?

## Study Area / Data
The study used dry granite rock samples. The samples were cylindrical (diameter 25 mm, height 50 mm) and disc-shaped (diameter 25 mm, height 8 mm). The initial average wave velocity was 4515.32 m/s, and the average permeability was 0.0075×10⁻³ μm² [Huang 2019, pp. 1-2]. Samples were heated to predetermined temperatures of 25, 150, 260, 370, 480, and 600 °C [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5].

## Methods
1.  **Sample Preparation:** Granite samples were dried at 104 °C until mass change was less than 0.1% in 12 hours. Samples were grouped based on initial wave velocity to reduce heterogeneity [Huang 2019, pp. 1-2].
2.  **Heating:** Samples were heated in a muffle furnace at a rate of 5 °C/min to the target temperature and held for 12 hours [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5].
3.  **Cooling:** Two cooling methods were applied:
    *   **Natural Cooling:** Samples were removed and cooled in air to room temperature.
    *   **Liquid Nitrogen Cooling:** Samples were immediately immersed in liquid nitrogen for 12 hours, then removed to return to room temperature [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5].
4.  **Testing:** Post-cooling, the following were measured: sample dimensions, longitudinal wave velocity, permeability. Subsequently, uniaxial compressive strength and Brazilian tensile strength tests were performed using a deep-water pore pressure servo system (TAW-1000) [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5].

## Key Findings
1.  **Physical Properties:** For granite initially at 150–600 °C, liquid nitrogen cooling (compared to natural cooling) caused:
    *   Wave velocity to decrease by an additional 4.13%–10.04%.
    *   Permeability to increase by an additional 0.21–182.80 times [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5, 5-8].
2.  **Mechanical Properties:** For granite initially at 150–600 °C, liquid nitrogen cooling (compared to natural cooling) caused:
    *   Tensile strength to decrease by an additional 4.95%–34.54%.
    *   Uniaxial compressive strength to decrease by an additional 13.95%–29.30%.
    *   Elastic modulus to decrease by an additional 7.33%–45.74% [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5, 5-8].
3.  **Temperature Dependence:** The degree of damage from liquid nitrogen cooling increases with the rock's temperature before cooling. A significant change in properties (e.g., permeability increase by orders of magnitude) occurred between 480 °C and 600 °C, attributed to quartz phase transition at 573 °C causing structural damage [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5, 5-8].
4.  **Damage Mechanism:** Damage is caused by thermal stress generated during rapid cooling. This stress arises from two sources: 1) differential contraction of heterogeneous mineral grains, and 2) temperature gradients within the rock. SEM analysis showed intergranular cracks and branching microcracks [Huang 2019, pp. 5-8].

## Limitations
1.  The study focused exclusively on dry granite. The effect of liquid nitrogen on water-saturated rock was not investigated [Huang 2019, pp. 1-2].
2.  The experimental temperature range was 25–600 °C. Behavior at higher temperatures (e.g., >600 °C) is not confirmed.
3.  The study was laboratory-based. Field-scale application effects (e.g., in-situ stress, fluid interaction) are not confirmed.

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The heating process (5 °C/min to target temperature, held for 12 hours) caused consistent damage across samples, allowing the difference in cooling methods to be isolated [Huang 2019, pp. 5-8].
2.  The granite samples were homogeneous enough for grouped comparisons after initial wave velocity screening [Huang 2019, pp. 1-2].
3.  The thermal stress model assumes a linear elastic, isotropic material for derivation, though granite is acknowledged as heterogeneous and anisotropic [Huang 2019, pp. 5-8].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For dry granite initially heated to 150–600 °C. **Claim:** Rapid cooling with liquid nitrogen induces greater damage than natural air cooling, as evidenced by a 4.13%–10.04% greater reduction in wave velocity and a 0.21–182.80 times greater increase in permeability [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5, 5-8].
2.  **Condition:** For dry granite initially heated to 150–600 °C. **Claim:** The mechanical degradation caused by liquid nitrogen cooling is significant, reducing tensile strength by an additional 4.95%–34.54%, compressive strength by 13.95%–29.30%, and elastic modulus by 7.33%–45.74% compared to natural cooling [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5, 5-8].
3.  **Condition:** For granite undergoing thermal shock. **Claim:** The primary damage mechanism is the generation of thermal stress due to the temperature difference (ΔT) and the rock's heterogeneous mineral composition, leading to intergranular cracking and microcrack propagation [Huang 2019, pp. 5-8].
4.  **Condition:** For granite containing quartz. **Claim:** A critical temperature range exists between 480 °C and 600 °C where structural damage is greatly amplified due to the α-β quartz phase transition at ~573 °C, leading to orders-of-magnitude increases in permeability [Huang 2019, pp. 2-5].
