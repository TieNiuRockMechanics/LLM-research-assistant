# Thermal Evolution of Archean Basement Rocks from the Eastern Part of the North China Craton and Its Bearing on Tectonic Setting.

## One-line Summary
The basement rocks in the eastern North China Craton preserve counterclockwise **P-T paths** indicative of regional metamorphism driven by **mantle-derived magma underplating** and vertical crustal growth, best explained by a **mantle plume (hotspot) model** rather than Phanerozoic-style plate tectonics. [Zhao 1998, pp. 1-2, 13-14]

## Research Question
Was **Phanerozoic‑style plate tectonics** the main mechanism governing crustal formation and growth in the Archean, or did a different tectonic regime dominate? Specifically, what tectonic setting is responsible for the thermal evolution recorded by the eastern North China Craton basement? [Zhao 1998, pp. 2]

## Study Area / Data
The **eastern zone** of the North China Craton includes nine domains (eastern Hebei, Miyun-Chengde, western Shandong, eastern Shandong, Anshan-Benxi, western Liaoning, southern Liaoning, northern Liaoning, southern Jilin). The basement consists mainly of **pretectonic tonalitic-trondhjemitic-granodioritic (TTG) gneisses** and **syntectonic granitoids** (>80%), with minor supracrustal rocks (ultramafic to felsic volcanics and sediments) metamorphosed from greenschist to granulite facies. [Zhao 1998, pp. 3-5]  

Geochronological data indicate:
- remnants of **>3800 Ma** crust (eastern Hebei, Anshan);
- mid‑Archean TTG gneisses and volcanics (3500–2800 Ma);
- syntectonic granitoids and peak metamorphism at **2500–2600 Ma** (sm‑Nd, zircon U‑Pb, SHRIMP). [Zhao 1998, pp. 4-7, 8-12]

Metamorphic assemblages and textures are well preserved in mafic granulites, amphibolites, and some pelitic gneisses, enabling reconstruction of **prograde (M1), peak (M2), and post‑peak (M3) stages**. [Zhao 1998, pp. 1-2, 6-13]

## Methods
- **Microstructural analysis** of mineral inclusions, reaction coronas, and replacement textures to define relative timing. [Zhao 1998, pp. 1, 6-7]
- **Thermobarometry** using:  
  - conventional geothermobarometers (CTB): two‑pyroxene, garnet‑orthopyroxene, garnet‑clinopyroxene, hornblende‑plagioclase, garnet‑biotite, etc.;  
  - **TWQ multi‑equilibrium calculations** (Berman, 1991) for the eastern Hebei domain. [Zhao 1998, pp. 6-8]
- **Fluid inclusion** studies (Liu, 1995, 1996) and **mineral zoning** (hornblende, garnet) as complementary indicators. [Zhao 1998, pp. 7, 12-13]
- Integration of **geochronological, structural, and lithological** data to constrain tectonic interpretation. [Zhao 1998, pp. 2, 13-14]

## Key Findings
1. **Every domain** in the eastern zone exhibits a **counterclockwise P-T path** (near‑isobaric cooling), regardless of age, metamorphic grade, or composition. [Zhao 1998, pp. 12-13, Fig. 4]  
2. Prograde (M1) assemblages occur as inclusions within peak minerals; peak (M2) assemblages define the regional foliation; post‑peak (M3) textures are symplectic coronas or replacement rims. [Zhao 1998, pp. 1-2]  
3. Estimated P-T conditions (examples):  
   - Eastern Hebei: M1 650–770°C/5.5–6.5 kbar → M2 850–900°C/8.0–8.5 kbar → M3 700–750°C/7.5–8.0 kbar. [Zhao 1998, pp. 7-8]  
   - Miyun-Chengde: M1 650–700°C/6.5–7.5 kbar → M2 800–900°C/10.0–12.0 kbar → M3 670–770°C/9.0–10.0 kbar. [Zhao 1998, pp. 8-9]  
   - Western Liaoning: M1 750–800°C/5.5–6.7 kbar → M2 880–990°C/9.0–11.5 kbar → M3 750–850°C/9.5–11.5 kbar. [Zhao 1998, pp. 9]  
   (Full dataset in Table 1 of the paper).  
4. The counterclockwise paths **rule out** a continent‑continent collision setting and are most consistent with **magmatic underplating** – large volumes of mantle‑derived magma added heat and material to the crust. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13]  
5. A **continental‑rift** model is rejected because of the broad width of exposed basement, dominant oval domes, and absence of abundant mafic dikes. A **continental‑magmatic‑arc** model fails to explain the extreme width, lack of an adjacent continent, and the presence of komatiites. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13-14]  
6. The preferred model is a **mantle plume (hotspot)** scenario:  
   - Early Archean: primitive sialic crust (e.g., 3800 Ma remnants).  
   - Mid‑Archean: plume head uplift → lithospheric stretching → komatiite/basalt volcanism + large TTG magmas.  
   - Late Archean: second plume event → regional metamorphism (M1→M2→M3) and syntectonic granite generation, ending with isobaric cooling. [Zhao 1998, pp. 14-15]

## Limitations
- Errors may exist in P-T estimates because **internally inconsistent thermobarometers** were used for some assemblages. However, the inferred paths are **independently constrained by reaction textures**. [Zhao 1998, pp. 12-13]  
- The **low‑grade domains** (e.g., Anshan-Benxi, southern Liaoning) lack detailed P-T data; their paths are inferred mainly from textural analogies. [Zhao 1998, pp. 11]  
- The source and initial location of the primitive Early Archean crust remain unknown. [Zhao 1998, pp. 14]  
- The proposed mantle-plume scenario is a **preliminary model**; the geometry and dynamics of plumes at that time are not directly observed.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The mineral assemblages used for thermobarometry are assumed to represent **equilibrium** at each stage.  
- Prograde (M1) assemblages are **preserved as inclusions** inside peak minerals and reflect the conditions at the time of entrapment.  
- The counterclockwise P-T paths are **robust** because they are consistent across domains and supported by both **mineral reaction textures** and **geothermobarometric calculations**.  
- The tectonic interpretation assumes that **modern analogue environments** (arc, rift, plume) are applicable to the Archean, but plate‑tectonic rates and geometries may have differed.

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Temperature (°C)** and **pressure (kbar)** at prograde, peak, and post‑peak stages.  
- **P-T path shape**: clockwise vs. counterclockwise; presence of **isobaric cooling** segment.  
- **Metamorphic field gradient**: estimated at 30–35 °C/km (comparable to modern magmatic arcs). [Zhao 1998, pp. 13]  
- **Crustal thickness**: inferred to be “double” normal continental thickness during peak metamorphism. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13]  
- **Geochronological ages**: crustal formation events at >3800 Ma, 3500–2800 Ma, and peak metamorphism at 2500–2600 Ma.  
- **Plume thermal structure**: head temperatures ~350°C cooler than the tail, responsible for komatiites vs. basalts. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13-14]

## Reusable Claims
- In the eastern North China Craton, Archean basement rocks **uniformly record counterclockwise P-T trajectories** featuring near‑isobaric cooling, regardless of metamorphic grade. [Zhao 1998, pp. 12-13]  
- Such **CCW paths** are characteristic of metamorphism driven by **magmatic underplating** and crustal thickening through vertical addition of mantle‑derived melts. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13]  
- The **distribution** of high‑grade and low‑grade (granite‑greenstone) domains, dominant TTG gneisses, oval domal structures, komatiites, and minimal metasediments argues against a **continental‑arc or rift setting** and is best explained by a **mantle‑plume model**. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13-15]  
- **Textural sequences** (inclusions → peak minerals → symplectic coronas/replacements) are reliable indicators of prograde and post‑peak stages, even where accurate thermobarometry is challenging. [Zhao 1998, pp. 1-2, 6-12]  
- The age constraints show that major crustal growth and metamorphism occurred in the **mid‑ to Late Archean (3.5–2.5 Ga)**, with remnants of >3.8 Ga crust representing the earliest preserved material. [Zhao 1998, pp. 4-7]

## Connections To Other Work
- **Bohlen (1987, 1991)** argued that granulite terrains with CCW paths most commonly form in **continental‑magmatic‑arc** settings, based on width, crustal thickness, and geotherm comparisons. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13]  
- **Wells (1980)** modelled magmatic accretion as a cause of clockwise and counterclockwise metamorphic paths. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13]  
- **Sandiford and Powell (1986)** suggested that isobaric cooling paths can also develop in **continental‑rift** environments. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13]  
- **England and Thompson (1984)** linked clockwise P-T paths to **collisional orogenesis**. The absence of such paths in the eastern NCC further argues against collision. [Zhao 1998, pp. 2, 13]  
- **Campbell et al. (1989)** and **Hill et al. (1992)** provided the thermal‑mechanical plume model that is adopted here – komatiites from the hot plume tail, basalts from the cooler head, and extensive crustal melting during plume‑lithosphere interaction. [Zhao 1998, pp. 13-14]  
- **Liu et al. (1992)**, **Nutman et al. (1993)**, and **Song et al. (1996)** documented >3800 Ma crustal remnants, establishing the antiquity of the craton. [Zhao 1998, pp. 4-7]

## Open Questions
- **What were the source and initial location** of the primitive >3.8 Ga sialic crust? [Zhao 1998, pp. 14]  
- **How many plume events** occurred, and were they part of a global superplume episode? [Zhao 1998, pp. 14-15]  
- **Can the mantle‑plume model be tested** by isotopic or geochemical fingerprints (e.g., high‑Mg komatiites, specific mantle signatures)?  
- **Why are almost all domains characterized by CCW paths**, while other parts of the North China Craton (central and western zones) may have different metamorphic patterns? [Zhao 1998, pp. 3-6]  
- **What are the precise P-T-t paths** for the low‑grade granite‑greenstone belts that currently lack quantitative data? [Zhao 1998, pp. 11]  
- **How does the plume model reconcile** with the absence of Phanerozoic‑style ophiolites and arc sequences in the eastern NCC?
