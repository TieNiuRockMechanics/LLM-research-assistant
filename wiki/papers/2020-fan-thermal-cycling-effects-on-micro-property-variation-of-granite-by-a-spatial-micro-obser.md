# Thermal Cycling Effects on Micro-property Variation of Granite by a Spatial Micro-observation.

## One-line Summary
This study uses X-ray computed tomography (CT) to quantitatively analyze the spatial distribution of micro-defects in granite induced by thermal cycling, finding that the first thermal cycle at 500°C causes a significant increase in porosity, heterogeneity, and anisotropy, while subsequent cycles have a marginal effect.

## Research Question
How do thermal cycling treatments affect the three-dimensional spatial distribution and quantitative characteristics (porosity, heterogeneity, anisotropy) of micro-defects within granite? [Fan 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The rock material is granite sampled from Rizhao, Shandong Province, China. [Fan 2020, pp. 1-2] The average uniaxial compressive strength and Young's modulus of initial specimens (50 mm diameter, 100 mm height) were 180 MPa and 31.7 GPa, respectively. [Fan 2020, pp. 1-2] Mineral components were 36.2% plagioclase, 20.6% k-feldspar, 37.0% quartz, 2.7% biotite, and 3.5% other minerals. [Fan 2020, pp. 1-2] For CT testing, specimens were processed into dimensions of 10 mm × 10 mm × 20 mm. [Fan 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Methods
- **Micro-observation Instrument:** A Nano Voxel-2200 series High Resolution CT System with a minimum resolution of 10 μm. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
- **Experimental Procedure:**
    1. Initial CT scanning of specimens before thermal treatment. The specimen was divided into layers (16 μm thickness). An analysis region (approx. 8 mm × 8 mm × 8 mm cube) was selected to avoid edge hardening effects. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
    2. Thermal treatment: Specimens were heated to a prescribed temperature (500°C) at a rate of 2.5 °C/min, held for 3.0 hours, then slowly cooled to room temperature. This constituted one thermal cycle. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
    3. Various thermal cycles were considered: 0, 1, 2, 4, 8, and 16. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
    4. Post-treatment CT scanning was conducted. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
- **Quantitative Analysis:**
    - **Volumetric Porosity (Dv):** Percentage ratio of damage volume to total volume in the cubic analysis region. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
    - **Areal Porosity (Ds):** Percentage ratio of damage area to total area in a cross-section. [Fan 2020, pp. 4-5]
    - **Heterogeneity Coefficient (U):** Describes the distribution characteristics of micro-cracks in the same direction of specimens. [Fan 2020, pp. 5-6]
    - **Anisotropy Coefficient (A):** Describes the distribution characteristics of micro-cracks in different directions of specimens. [Fan 2020, pp. 5-6]

## Key Findings
1.  **Porosity:** The volumetric porosity (Dv) increased significantly after the first thermal cycle (from 0.011% to 0.599%). Subsequent cycles caused only marginal increases (e.g., 0.712% after 16 cycles). [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4] Areal porosity (Ds) in x, y, and z directions showed a similar trend, with the most significant increase occurring after the first cycle. [Fan 2020, pp. 4-5]
2.  **Micro-crack Development:** The first thermal cycle generated many new micro-cracks, primarily in regions of grains with lower densities. Beyond the first cycle, no marked changes in crack propagation or new crack generation were observed. [Fan 2020, pp. 4-5]
3.  **Heterogeneity:** The heterogeneity coefficient (U) in all three directions increased dramatically after the first thermal cycle (e.g., in x-direction from 10.109% to 38.188%). Changes in subsequent cycles were slight. [Fan 2020, pp. 5-6]
4.  **Anisotropy:** The anisotropy coefficients (Axy and Azy) increased significantly after the first thermal cycle (e.g., Axy from 5.714% to 57.609%). Subsequent cycles showed only marginal increases. [Fan 2020, pp. 5-6]
5.  **Interpretation:** The first thermal cycle induces significant thermal stress due to differential mineral expansion, causing major micro-cracking. These initial cracks then relieve thermal stress during subsequent cycles, limiting further damage. [Fan 2020, pp. 4-5, 5-6]

## Limitations
- The study focused on a single temperature (500°C) and a specific granite type. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
- The analysis region for CT was a subset (approx. 8 mm cube) of the specimen, not the entire volume. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
- The study primarily describes the spatial distribution of damage but does not directly correlate it with changes in macroscopic mechanical properties like strength or permeability. [Fan 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The heating rate of 2.5 °C/min was sufficient to avoid thermal shock. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
- A holding time of 3.0 hours at the target temperature ensured uniform temperature within the specimen. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
- The grayscale values in CT images could be segmented to distinguish solid phases from void spaces (pores/cracks). [Fan 2020, pp. 4-5]
- The micro-structural characteristics (grain size, shape, mineralogy) of the granite are the primary factors controlling the heterogeneity and anisotropy of induced micro-cracks. [Fan 2020, pp. 6-7]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variable:** Number of thermal cycles (n = 0, 1, 2, 4, 8, 16). [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
- **Controlled Variable:** Thermal cycle temperature (500°C). [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
- **Dependent Variables:**
    - Volumetric Porosity (Dv). [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4]
    - Areal Porosity (Ds) in x, y, z directions. [Fan 2020, pp. 4-5]
    - Heterogeneity Coefficient (U) in x, y, z directions. [Fan 2020, pp. 5-6]
    - Anisotropy Coefficient (A). [Fan 2020, pp. 5-6]

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** The first thermal cycle at 500°C induces the most significant micro-structural damage in granite, characterized by a large increase in porosity and the generation of new micro-cracks, primarily in lower-density mineral grains. **Condition:** For the tested granite from Rizhao, China, under the specific thermal treatment protocol (2.5°C/min heating, 3h hold at 500°C). **Limitation:** This dominant first-cycle effect may vary with different rock types, mineralogies, or temperature ranges. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4, 4-5]
- **Claim:** Subsequent thermal cycles (beyond the first) at 500°C cause only marginal increases in porosity and no significant propagation of existing cracks or generation of new cracks in granite. **Condition:** For the tested granite and thermal protocol. **Limitation:** The threshold for "marginal" increase and the long-term effect of many more cycles are not defined. [Fan 2020, pp. 2-4, 4-5]
- **Claim:** The spatial distribution of thermally induced micro-cracks in granite becomes more heterogeneous and anisotropic after the first thermal cycle at 500°C, with these characteristics stabilizing in subsequent cycles. **Condition:** For the tested granite and thermal protocol. **Limitation:** The degree of heterogeneity and anisotropy is strongly dependent on the rock's initial micro-structure. [Fan 2020, pp. 5-6, 6-7]

## Open Questions
- How do the observed micro-property changes (porosity, heterogeneity, anisotropy) quantitatively relate to changes in macroscopic mechanical properties (strength, elastic modulus, permeability) of the granite?
- Would the "first-cycle dominance" effect hold for different maximum temperatures (e.g., below or above 500°C)?
- How do the spatial distribution characteristics of micro-cracks evolve under a much larger number of thermal cycles (e.g., hundreds or thousands)?
- How do different mineral compositions and initial micro-structures in other rock types influence the heterogeneity and anisotropy of thermal cracking?
