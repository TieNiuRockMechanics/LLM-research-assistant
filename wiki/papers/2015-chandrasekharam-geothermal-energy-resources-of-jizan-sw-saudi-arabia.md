# Geothermal Energy Resources of Jizan, SW Saudi Arabia.

## One-line Summary
The Jizan geothermal province in SW Saudi Arabia, characterized by high heat flow and thermal springs, has a high-temperature reservoir in granites with the potential to generate significant electricity and reduce CO2 emissions.

## Research Question
What is the geothermal energy potential of the Jizan province, and what are its geological, geophysical, and geochemical characteristics?

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Jizan geothermal province in southwestern Saudi Arabia, located within the Asir terrane of the western Arabian shield [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-3]. The region's geology includes Precambrian formations (Sabya Formation, Baish and Halaban Groups), Quaternary igneous and sedimentary formations, and a 40 km wide coastal plain covered by Quaternary sediments overlying 5 km of Tertiary/Quaternary rocks [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-3]. Data include physical and chemical analyses of nine thermal springs (surface temperatures 44-76°C) and several warm springs (30-34°C), along with published data from Yemen for comparison [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 3-5]. Geophysical data from seismic reflection and 2D electrical resistivity surveys conducted near Al Khouba are also used [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 8-11].

## Methods
The study employed a multi-disciplinary approach:
*   **Geochemical Analysis:** Major ion, trace element, and isotopic (δD, δ18O, δ13C, 14C) analysis of thermal and warm spring waters [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 3-5].
*   **Geothermometry:** Application of silica and cation (Na-K, Na-K-Ca, K-Mg) geothermometers to estimate reservoir temperatures [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 7-8].
*   **Geophysical Surveys:** 2D seismic reflection surveys using a multi-channel Strata Visor equipment and 2D electrical resistivity surveys using Syscal-Pro equipment with Wenner and Wenner-Schlumberger configurations [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 8-11].
*   **Isotopic Dating:** Use of 14C (dissolved inorganic carbon) to estimate groundwater residence time, assuming an initial value of 85 pmc [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 6-7].

## Key Findings
*   The Jizan geothermal system is hosted in granitic rocks and is fault-controlled, with thermal fluids circulating along NW-SE and ENE-WSW trending faults [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 11-12].
*   Oxygen isotope shifts indicate high-temperature water-rock interaction, suggesting a reservoir temperature of around 220°C [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-1].
*   The residence time of the thermal fluids, estimated from 14C, is approximately 33,000 years [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-1].
*   Geophysical surveys identified a shallow reservoir indicated by a low-resistivity zone and a deeper reservoir connected via a fault/fracture system [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 11-12].
*   The province is estimated to be capable of generating 134 × 10^6 kWh of electricity, which could help meet future fresh water demand and save CO2 emissions by reducing domestic fossil fuel consumption [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-1].

## Limitations
*   The study relies on surface manifestations and geophysical surveys; no deep exploratory drilling has been conducted to directly confirm reservoir parameters [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 11-12].
*   Tritium values were uncertain, necessitating the use of 14C for dating with an assumed initial value [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 6-7].
*   A detailed Magnetotelluric (MT) survey is noted as being undertaken to better map the reservoir before drilling [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 11-12].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   The initial (primitive) 14C value for groundwater age calculation was assumed to be 85 pmc, following the recommendation of Geyh (2005) [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 6-7].
*   The geothermal system is considered to be of the Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) type, associated with radiogenic granites [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-1].
*   The high chloride content in the thermal waters is attributed to water-rock interaction with granitic minerals (e.g., mica, apatite) rather than mixing with Red Sea water or salt domes [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 11-12].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Reservoir Temperature:** ~220°C (from isotope shift) [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-1].
*   **Fluid Residence Time:** ~33 kilo years (from 14C) [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-1].
*   **Surface Spring Temperature:** 44-76°C [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 3-5].
*   **Heat Flow:** High values (111–206 mW/m²) reported for the region [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 11-12].
*   **Geothermal Gradient:** High, as indicated by the thermal springs [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-1].
*   **Electricity Generation Potential:** 134 × 10^6 kWh [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 1-1].

## Reusable Claims
*   **Condition:** In geothermal provinces overlying thinned continental crust underplated by oceanic ridge material. **Claim:** The sharp physical and thermal contrast between these crustal types can result in high heat flow values conducive to geothermal system development [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 11-12].
*   **Condition:** For thermal waters with high chloride content but Na/Cl ratios dissimilar to seawater. **Claim:** The chloride likely originates from water-rock interaction with chlorine-bearing minerals (e.g., mica, apatite) in granitic host rocks, rather than from marine sources [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 11-12].
*   **Condition:** When trace elements like Rb, Li, Sr, and B show strong positive correlations in thermal waters. **Claim:** This indicates a common granitic source, with elements contributed from minerals such as tourmaline, phlogopite, and biotite [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 6-7].

## Connections To Other Work
*   The Jizan province is considered the northward extension of the Yemen geothermal provinces, with similar geological and structural features [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 3-5].
*   The study compares its data with thermal and cold water samples from Yemen [Minissale et al., 2007] and the Al-Lith geothermal system in Saudi Arabia [Lashin et al., 2014] [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 3-5].
*   The tectonic framework and heat flow values are based on earlier geophysical studies [Gettings et al., 1986; Girdler and Evans, 1977] [Chandrasekharam 2015, pp. 11-12].
