# Development of a Self-Similar Strike-Slip Duplex System in the Atacama Fault System, Chile.

## One-line Summary
The study documents a self-similar strike-slip duplex system in the Atacama Fault System, where the geometric relationship between duplex width and master fault length is consistent across seven orders of magnitude, indicating scale-invariant mechanical processes.

## Research Question
How do strike-slip fault systems propagate and develop, and can their structural geometry be described by a scale-invariant model from regional to microscopic scales? [Jensen 2011, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Atacama Fault System (AFS), a ~1000 km-long trench-parallel fault system in the Andean Forearc of Northern Chile. Specific analysis centers on the Bolfín Fault, a first-order structure within the Caleta Coloso Duplex (CCD), and its associated smaller faults (e.g., Parulo and Palmera Faults) around its tip point. Data comprises detailed structural maps at scales from 1:250,000 to 1:5, ortho-rectified photomosaics, and oriented thin sections for microstructural analysis. The host rock is primarily meta-diorite. [Jensen 2011, pp. 1-2, 2-4, 4-5]

## Methods
Multi-scale structural mapping and measurement were employed. Regional-scale (1-10 km) faults were mapped using existing maps. Meter- to hundred-meter-scale faults were mapped at 1:10,000 scale at the Bolfín Fault tip. Outcrop-scale (mm to m) fractures were mapped at 1:20 and 1:5 scales using field measurements and ortho-rectified photomosaics. Microscopic-scale (µm to mm) fractures were characterized via oriented thin sections, with analysis conducted on the M-plane (parallel to striation, perpendicular to fault plane). Fracture orientations were analyzed using equal-area projections and weighted mean orientations. [Jensen 2011, pp. 2-4, 4-5, 5-7]

## Key Findings
1.  The most characteristic feature of the fault arrangements at all scales (from regional to microscopic) is a strike-slip duplex pattern. [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13]
2.  This duplex system is self-similar. The width (W) of the duplex and the length (L) of the Main Faults forming it show an almost linear relationship (L ≈ 10W) across a scale range of seven orders of magnitude (10⁻⁴ to 10⁴ m). [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13]
3.  Fractures within the duplexes can be separated into two types: longer, trend-parallel **Main Faults** with larger offsets, and shorter, sigmoid-shaped **Secondary Fractures** oriented 15° to 75° counterclockwise from the Main Faults. [Jensen 2011, pp. 1-2, 5-7]
4.  Based on spatial distribution and density gradients, Main Faults propagated earlier, and Secondary Fractures formed later as linking structures between them. [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13, 13-13]
5.  Fault propagation occurred under hydrothermal, low-greenschist facies conditions, with evidence of simultaneous brittle and plastic deformation (e.g., deformation twins in plagioclase) and fracture healing/sealing by epidote and chlorite. [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13, 13-15]

## Limitations
1.  The exact mechanism of fault nucleation in the crystalline rock is not fully clear from field data alone. [Jensen 2011, pp. 13-15]
2.  Intense hydrothermal fluid activity and associated fracture healing may have erased evidence of early crack distributions around fault tips, complicating the observation of initial propagation processes. [Jensen 2011, pp. 13-15]
3.  The relative timing between structures is inferred from spatial relationships and damage zone models, not from direct cross-cutting relationships in all cases. [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The structural system is self-similar, meaning the same fundamental mechanical processes operate at all scales. [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13]
2.  All analyzed faults (Bolfín, Jorgillo, Parulo, Palmera) are contemporaneous at a geological time scale, as they are cemented by the same epidote-chlorite mineral assemblage. [Jensen 2011, pp. 13-13]
3.  The damage area around a fault increases with total fault displacement, allowing proximal areas to be interpreted as more deformationally mature. [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13]
4.  Deformation occurred under upper-crustal, low-greenschist facies hydrothermal conditions. [Jensen 2011, pp. 2-4, 13-15]

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Duplex Width (W):** The distance between the Main Faults forming a duplex. [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13]
-   **Main Fault Length (L):** The length of the primary, trend-parallel faults within the duplex. [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13]
-   **Fracture Orientation:** Secondary Fractures are oriented 15° to 75° counterclockwise from the Main Faults (measured in the P-diedra sense). [Jensen 2011, pp. 1-2]
-   **Displacement:** Horizontal sinistral displacement measured on faults (e.g., offsets of mafic dikes). [Jensen 2011, pp. 5-7, 7-8]
-   **Scale Range:** Observations span 7 orders of magnitude (10⁻⁴ m to 10⁴ m). [Jensen 2011, pp. 1-2, 11-13]

## Reusable Claims
1.  In a self-similar strike-slip duplex system, the length of the master faults is approximately ten times the width of the overlapping duplex area, a relationship that holds across at least seven orders of magnitude in scale. [Jensen 2011, pp. 11-13]
2.  Secondary linking fractures in strike-slip duplexes typically initiate as extensional or hybrid fractures oriented at a high angle (15°-75°) to the main fault trend and may be sheared during continued deformation. [Jensen 2011, pp. 1-2, 13-15]
3.  The progressive development of a fault core with a banded cataclastic fabric can result from the heterogeneous, ongoing generation of fractures oriented between 15° and 75° to the main shear plane. [Jensen 2011, pp. 13-15]
4.  Hydrothermal fluid circulation in fault zones facilitates fracture healing and sealing, which can alter the observable damage zone structure and influence fault rock development. [Jensen 2011, pp. 13-15]

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on and compares its findings with previous work on the AFS and CCD [Cembrano et al., 2005; González, 1996]. It references similar duplex patterns in other fault systems (e.g., Thailand, California, Italian Alps) [Morley, 2004; Flodin and Aydin, 2004; Di Toro and Pennacchioni, 2005]. The discussion connects the self-similar geometry to experimental fault growth models [Lockner et al., 1991; Otsuki and Dilov, 2005] and concepts of fault propagation by crack coalescence [Cox and Scholz, 1988]. The role of fluids links to seismic pumping models [Sibson, 1987].

## Open Questions
1.  What is the precise nucleation mechanism for the initial Main Faults in crystalline rock under the observed brittle-plastic conditions? [Jensen 2011, pp. 13-15]
2.  How does the interplay between ongoing hydrothermal healing and new fracture generation quantitatively control the evolution of fault zone width and internal structure? [Jensen 2011, pp. 13-15]
3.  Can the self-similar scaling relationship (L ≈ 10W) be generalized to other strike-slip fault systems in different tectonic settings and rock types?
