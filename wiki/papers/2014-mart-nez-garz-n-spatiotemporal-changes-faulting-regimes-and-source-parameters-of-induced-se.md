# Spatiotemporal Changes, Faulting Regimes, and Source Parameters of Induced Seismicity: A Case Study from The Geysers Geothermal Field.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how changes in fluid injection rates at The Geysers geothermal field correlate with spatiotemporal patterns, faulting mechanisms, and source parameters of induced seismicity, concluding that thermal fracturing is the primary mechanism, with pore pressure effects becoming significant during peak injections.

## Research Question
The research aims to determine the predominant physical mechanisms responsible for induced seismicity at the northwestern part of The Geysers geothermal field by analyzing how variations in fluid injection rates affect the spatiotemporal distribution, faulting regimes, and source parameters of the seismicity [Mart 2014, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on a spatially isolated cluster of induced seismicity in the northwestern part of The Geysers geothermal field in Northern California, USA [Mart 2014, pp. 3-4]. Data analyzed include a relocated hypocenter catalog of 770 events, 973 focal mechanisms from the NCEDC catalog, and daily injection rates from wells Prati-9 and Prati-29 provided by Calpine Corporation [Mart 2014, pp. 4-5]. The seismicity occurred between September 2007 and July 2012, with moment magnitudes (Mw) ranging from 1.0 to 3.3 [Mart 2014, pp. 4-5].

## Methods
Methods included applying the double-difference earthquake relocation method to improve hypocenter precision [Mart 2014, pp. 4-5]. Focal mechanisms were recalculated using FPFIT software and categorized into strike-slip, normal, and thrust events [Mart 2014, pp. 4-5]. Temporal analyses involved calculating Gutenberg-Richter b-values using moving windows, performing stress inversion with the MSATSI package to obtain relative stress magnitude (R), and estimating static stress drops via a spectral fitting method [Mart 2014, pp. 4-5]. Reservoir permeability and diffusivities were estimated using injectivity test data and theoretical models [Mart 2014, pp. 13-14].

## Key Findings
During peak injection intervals, the relative contribution of strike-slip faulting mechanisms increased, and b-values decreased [Mart 2014, pp. 1-1]. Seismicity during peak injections tended to occur at greater distances from the injection well, preferentially trending parallel to the maximum horizontal stress (SHMax) direction [Mart 2014, pp. 1-1]. At lower injection rates, seismicity aligned in a different direction, suggesting the presence of a local fault [Mart 2014, pp. 1-1]. The characteristic diffusion length for pore pressure was found to be much greater than for temperature, more consistent with the observed spatial extent of seismicity [Mart 2014, pp. 1-1]. The study suggests that regardless of injection stage, most induced seismicity results from thermal fracturing, but during peak injections, increased pore pressure may also be responsible [Mart 2014, pp. 1-1].

## Limitations
The number of relocated events in some stages (e.g., Cycle 1) was small, which might weaken statistical analyses [Mart 2014, pp. 8-9]. The relative stress magnitude (R) from stress inversion has the largest uncertainty irrespective of confidence interval assessment [Mart 2014, pp. 4-5]. Estimating pore pressure during Cycle 2 was more difficult due to injection from two wells [Mart 2014, pp. 13-14]. The study does not consider the influence of reservoir depletion as no production occurred during the analyzed times in the area [Mart 2014, pp. 14-15].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The reservoir is assumed to be a laterally infinite, isotropic, porous, and linearly elastic medium that deforms uniaxially in the vertical direction [Mart 2014, pp. 11-12]. A normal faulting stress regime is assumed for stress magnitude estimations [Mart 2014, pp. 15-16]. Physical properties such as porosity (2%), rock density (2700 kg/m³), and thermal conductivity (3.2 W/m/°C) are taken from literature and operator data [Mart 2014, pp. 13-14]. The friction coefficient is assumed to be 0.85 following Byerlee's law [Mart 2014, pp. 15-16].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Injection Rate**: Volume of water injected per month from wells Prati-9 and Prati-29 [Mart 2014, pp. 3-4].
- **Pore Pressure**: Estimated bottomhole pressure at Prati-9, ranging from ~6.20 MPa to ~7.23 MPa during injectivity test [Mart 2014, pp. 13-14].
- **Stress Magnitudes**: Vertical stress (Sv) estimated as 78 MPa at 2.4 km depth; horizontal stresses estimated from stress inversion and frictional equilibrium [Mart 2014, pp. 15-16].
- **b-value**: Gutenberg-Richter frequency-magnitude distribution parameter, calculated for moving windows of 55-75 events [Mart 2014, pp. 4-5].
- **Static Stress Drop**: Calculated from spectral fitting of P and S waveforms, using a circular source model [Mart 2014, pp. 5-7].
- **Reservoir Permeability**: Estimated as ~1.3 × 10⁻¹⁴ m² from injectivity index [Mart 2014, pp. 13-14].
- **Thermal Diffusivity (K_thermal)**: ~10⁻⁶ m²/s [Mart 2014, pp. 13-14].
- **Hydraulic Diffusivity (K_hydraulic)**: ~10¹ m²/s [Mart 2014, pp. 13-14].

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim**: Increased fluid injection rates correlate with a decrease in Gutenberg-Richter b-values and an increased relative contribution of strike-slip faulting mechanisms in induced seismicity.
  - **Condition**: Observed at The Geysers geothermal field during peak injection intervals.
  - **Limitation**: Based on a specific seismicity cluster; may not apply universally to all injection-induced seismicity.
- **Claim**: The spatial extent of induced seismicity during peak injections is more consistent with pore pressure diffusion than thermal diffusion, as the hydraulic diffusivity is orders of magnitude larger than thermal diffusivity.
  - **Condition**: Assumes heat transport is primarily by conduction; advection could alter this.
  - **Limitation**: Derived from estimated reservoir properties and theoretical models.
- **Claim**: Thermal fracturing due to cooling of reservoir rock by injected water is the primary mechanism for induced seismicity at The Geysers, regardless of injection stage.
  - **Condition**: Based on field observations and modeling studies at The Geysers.
  - **Limitation**: Poroelastic effects become significant during peak injections.
- **Claim**: Alignment of strike-slip seismic events can indicate the presence of previously unmapped local faults.
  - **Condition**: Observed in the NW Geysers cluster where strike-slip events aligned NNW-SSE.
  - **Limitation**: Requires sufficient number of well-constrained focal mechanisms.

## Connections To Other Work
This study builds on previous work by Martínez-Garzón et al. [2013] that observed stress tensor rotations correlated with injection rates at The Geysers [Mart 2014, pp. 1-2]. It compares findings to other geothermal sites like Soultz-sous-Forêts, France, where seismicity aligns with SHMax [Mart 2014, pp. 14-15], and the Basel Deep Heat Mining Project, where b-values decreased post-injection [Mart 2014, pp. 15-16]. The discussion references modeling by Rutqvist et al. [2013] that suggested thermal effects dominate EGS-induced seismicity at The Geysers [Mart 2014, pp. 14-15].

## Open Questions
The exact role of heat transport by advection versus conduction in shaping the seismicity cloud remains unclear [Mart 2014, pp. 11-12]. The study notes that further analysis is needed to determine the exact physical mechanism behind the observed stress rotations [Mart 2014, pp. 1-2]. The implications of the identified local fault for seismic hazard depend on its size, which is not determined [Mart 2014, pp. 16-17].
