# Destruction of the North China Craton in the Mesozoic.

## One-line Summary
The North China Craton (NCC), stable since cratonization at ∼1.9 Ga, was destroyed in the Mesozoic primarily through flat-slab subduction of the paleo-Pacific plate, leading to extensive felsic magmatism, large-scale ductile deformation, gold mineralization, and removal of >100 km of Archean lithospheric mantle replaced by juvenile mantle [abstract, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
How can a long-lived stable craton like the NCC be destroyed, and is decratonization a unique event or a common process in Earth’s evolution? [pp. 2-3]

## Study Area / Data
The North China Craton (area ∼1.5 million km²), bounded by the Central Asian Orogenic Belt, Qilianshan‑Qinling‑Dabie Orogen, and Sulu‑Rimjingang Orogen. It comprises two Archean‑Paleoproterozoic blocks separated by a Paleoproterozoic orogen [pp. 3-5]. Data include:
- Mantle and crustal xenoliths from Paleozoic kimberlites (Shandong, Liaoning), Mesozoic diorites/lamprophyres, and Cenozoic basalts [pp. 5, 8–10].
- Zircon U‑Pb ages and whole‑rock geochemistry (major, trace, Sr, Nd, Os isotopes) of Mesozoic igneous rocks [pp. 5-7, 11–12].
- Structural‑basin records of the Yanshan Orogeny [pp. 8].
- Geophysical estimates of crustal and lithospheric thickness [pp. 8-9].

## Methods
- Zircon U‑Pb geochronology of magmatic rocks [pp. 5-7].
- Whole‑rock geochemistry (major/trace elements, Sr/Y systematics) [pp. 7].
- Sr‑Nd‑Os isotopic analyses on mantle xenoliths and mafic rocks [pp. 11-13].
- Structural mapping of deformation and basin sedimentation [pp. 8].
- Geophysical (seismic, thermal) lithospheric thickness estimates [pp. 8-9].
- Global comparative synthesis of craton stability [pp. 10-12].

## Key Findings
- The NCC lost its cratonic stability during the Mesozoic, shown by intense crust‑derived felsic magmatism, widespread gold mineralization, pull‑apart basins, metamorphic core complexes, and exhumation of deep crust [abstract, pp. 1-2].
- Lithospheric keel replacement: a Paleozoic refractory mantle root >200 km thick (heat flow ∼40 mW/m²) was replaced by a thin (<80 km), hot, fertile mantle in the Cenozoic; >100 km of lithosphere was removed [pp. 3, 8–9].
- Two‑stage magmatic evolution with a spatial age reversal:  
  *Jurassic (200–145 Ma)*: adakitic felsic rocks, low zircon saturation temperatures (peak 730 °C), westward‑younging trend, derived from water‑rich thickened lower crust in garnet stability field [pp. 5-7].  
  *Early Cretaceous (145–110 Ma, peak ∼125 Ma)*: non‑adakitic rocks, high temperatures (peak 770 °C, up to 1100 °C), eastward‑younging trend, multiple sources (depleted mantle, enriched lithospheric mantle, ancient lower crust, juvenile crust) indicating intense crust‑mantle interaction [pp. 5-7, 15].
- Mantle source transition from enriched (low εNd) to depleted (high εNd) at ca. 115–125 Ma, recording replacement of ancient lithospheric mantle [pp. 7, 10].
- Deformation history: Jurassic contraction (thrust faults, folds, no sedimentation in eastern NCC); Early Cretaceous extension (metamorphic core complexes, detachment faults, pull‑apart basins) [pp. 8].
- Destruction driven by flat‑slab subduction of the paleo‑Pacific plate (possibly carrying an oceanic plateau) that initiated crustal thickening, followed by slab rollback and delamination [pp. 15-17].
- Global context: Oceanic subduction is the primary trigger for craton destruction (also seen in the Wyoming Craton); mantle plumes can cause lithospheric thinning without destruction (e.g., Indian Craton) [pp. 10-12].  
- Petrological and structural indicators of craton destruction: widespread crust‑derived felsic magmatism and large‑scale ductile deformation (metamorphic core complexes) [pp. 17, abstract].

## Limitations
- Timing and spatial extent of lithospheric thinning in the central NCC remain debated; some Os isotope data suggest both ancient relics and juvenile addition [pp. 12-13].
- The geometry and composition of the proposed subducted oceanic plateau are unknown; alternative ridge‑subduction models exist [p. 15].
- The relative roles of delamination vs. thermal erosion are still unresolved; refertilization models require ∼30% partial melting not documented in surface geology [p. 14].
- Isolating the sole contribution of paleo‑Pacific subduction is complicated by simultaneous subductions to the north (paleo‑Asian) and south (paleo‑Tethys) [p. 14].
- Direct samples of the delaminated lower crust are lacking; all inferences are based on surface magmas and mantle xenoliths [pp. 12-14].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Zircon U‑Pb ages date magmatic crystallization without significant inheritance or resetting.
- Mantle xenoliths are representative of the ambient lithosphere at the time of entrainment.
- Sr‑Nd‑Os isotopic compositions of xenoliths are not significantly altered by crustal contamination.
- Westward‑younging magmatism reflects east‑to‑west migration of subduction‑related melting.
- Flat‑slab subduction requires a buoyant oceanic plateau to maintain a near‑horizontal trajectory [p. 15].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Lithospheric thickness (derived from xenolith thermobarometry and geophysics).
- Ancient geotherm (∼40 mW/m²) vs. Cenozoic elevated heat flow.
- Zircon saturation temperature (Tzr) and SiO₂ content.
- Sr/Y and Y in granitoids (adakite discriminant).
- εNd(t) and ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratios in mafic rocks and xenoliths.
- ¹⁸⁷Os/¹⁸⁸Os and Re‑depletion age (tRD) of peridotite xenoliths.
- Ages of magmatic events (zircon U‑Pb).
- Deformation style (thrust faults, metamorphic core complexes).

## Reusable Claims
1. Craton destruction (loss of long‑term stability) is not equivalent to lithospheric thinning; many cratons underwent significant thinning while retaining a stable sedimentary cover (e.g., Indian Craton) [pp. 2-3, 10–11]. *Condition: stability assessed by absence of strong deformation, felsic magmatism, and disruption of sedimentary cover.*
2. The North China Craton was destroyed in the Mesozoic, as evidenced by widespread crust‑derived felsic magmatism, large‑scale extensional deformation, gold mineralization, and removal of >100 km of refractory Archean lithospheric mantle [abstract; pp. 1-2, 8–10]. *Condition: integration of petrological, geochronological, geochemical, and geophysical data.*
3. Oceanic subduction, particularly flat‑slab subduction, is the dominant tectonic trigger for craton destruction via lithospheric hydration and weakening; mantle plumes can thin the lithosphere but do not destroy cratons [pp. 14-17, 10–12]. *Condition: supported by NCC and Wyoming Craton examples; other subductions (e.g., Tethys) produced only local effects.*
4. A reversal in magmatic age migration (westward in Jurassic → eastward in Early Cretaceous) records a transition from flat‑slab subduction to slab rollback and subsequent delamination [pp. 5-7, 15]. *Condition: ages are compiled U‑Pb zircon data; alternative ridge‑subduction models exist.*
5. Crust‑derived felsic (granitic/rhyolitic) magmatism and large‑scale ductile deformation (metamorphic core complexes) are petrological and structural indicators of craton destruction [pp. 17, abstract]. *Condition: when widespread and accompanied by lithospheric thinning.*
6. Cenozoic mantle xenoliths from eastern NCC and surrounding blocks share identical juvenile Os isotopic ages, indicating large‑scale delamination and replacement of ancient lithospheric mantle [pp. 11-13]. *Condition: Re‑depletion ages of peridotites; some metasomatic interpretations exist.*
7. Low water content in the Cenozoic lithospheric mantle and lack of hydration by metasomatism suggest transient hydration by subduction is key to craton weakening [p. 14]. *Condition: water measurements in xenoliths; hydration by ancient subduction may have been erased.*

## Connections To Other Work
- The Wyoming Craton (North America) underwent destruction through Farallon plate flat‑slab subduction, paralleling the NCC [pp. 11-12, 17].
- The Indian Craton experienced >100 km lithospheric thinning during Deccan Trap emplacement but remained undestroyed [pp. 10-11].
- The Siberian, Brazilian, Yilgarn, Tanzanian, and North Atlantic cratons show lithospheric thinning without destruction [pp. 10-11].
- Possible decratonization of the Yangtze Craton is mentioned but not elaborated here [p. 14].
- The destruction model aligns with global synthesis that oceanic subduction is the chief mechanism for decratonization [pp. 17-18].
