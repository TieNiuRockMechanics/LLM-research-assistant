# Hydraulic Fracturing Induced Seismicity.

## One-line Summary
This review synthesizes the global state of knowledge on earthquakes induced by hydraulic fracturing (HF), documenting cases, identifying common themes, and discussing interpretations, research questions, and management implications.

## Research Question
What is the current understanding of hydraulic fracturing-induced seismicity, including its documented cases, common characteristics, underlying physical mechanisms, geological susceptibility, operational controls, and effective regulatory management?

## Study Area / Data
The review synthesizes documented cases of HF-induced seismicity from multiple basins worldwide, including the Horn River Basin (Canada), Bowland Shale (UK), Wabamun/Exshaw/Banff Formation (Canada), Duvernay Formation (Canada), Montney Formation (Canada), Utica & Marcellus Shales (Appalachian Basin, USA), SCOOP & STACK plays (Oklahoma, USA), Fayetteville Formation (Arkansas, USA), Eagle Ford Shale (Texas, USA), Wolfcamp & Bone Spring Formations (Delaware Basin, Texas, USA), and the Wufeng-Longmaxi Formation (Sichuan Basin, China) [Schultz 2020, pp. 6-28].

## Methods
The review synthesizes findings from numerous seismological and operational studies. Methods used in the cited research include: analysis of regional and local seismograph networks, waveform cross-correlation and template matching to detect similar events and enrich catalogs [Schultz 2020, pp. 29-31, 35-38], double-difference relocation for precise hypocenter determination [Schultz 2020, pp. 18-20], focal mechanism analysis [Schultz 2020, pp. 10-12, 12-14], statistical analysis of spatiotemporal associations between HF operations and seismicity [Schultz 2020, pp. 38-42], and machine learning for detection and susceptibility modeling [Schultz 2020, pp. 38-40, 50-52].

## Key Findings
1.  **Common Themes:** HF-induced earthquakes consistently exhibit: (a) waveform similarity and swarm-like clustering, (b) spatial proximity to the causal wellbore (often within a few km), (c) a strong temporal correlation with HF stimulation (most events occur during or shortly after operations), and (d) a paucity of reported cases (less than ~1% of HF wells induce detectable earthquakes) [Schultz 2020, pp. 29-34].
2.  **Documented Cases:** The largest HF-induced earthquake documented is a 5.7 M_L event in the Sichuan Basin, China [Schultz 2020, pp. 27-29]. Cases have been reported in Canada, the United States, the United Kingdom, and China [Schultz 2020, pp. 6-28].
3.  **Triggering Mechanisms:** Proposed mechanisms include direct pore pressure diffusion along hydraulically conductive fault zones, poroelastic stress transmission, and a hybrid model involving aseismic slip preceding seismic rupture [Schultz 2020, pp. 46-50].
4.  **Geological Susceptibility:** HF-induced seismicity is spatially localized and associated with geological factors such as proximity to crystalline basement, target formation overpressure, proximity to fossil reef margins, and inferred paleo-fluid flow along faults [Schultz 2020, pp. 50-52].
5.  **Operational Controls:** Injected fluid volume is a primary operational control on earthquake rate, but does not solely control maximum magnitude, which is constrained by tectonic factors like fault size [Schultz 2020, pp. 52-54].
6.  **Regulatory Response:** Traffic light protocols (TLPs) are the de facto regulatory tool, with varying magnitude thresholds (e.g., 0.5 M_L red-light in the UK, 4.0 M_L in parts of Canada) implemented in several jurisdictions [Schultz 2020, pp. 54-56].

## Limitations
1.  **Detection and Reporting:** Sparse seismic instrumentation, small induced magnitudes (M<3), and restricted access to detailed HF stimulation records have historically led to under-detection and under-reporting of cases [Schultz 2020, pp. 35-38, 40-42].
2.  **Association Challenges:** It is difficult to confidently associate seismicity with HF when it occurs contemporaneously with other sources (e.g., wastewater disposal) or when earthquake catalogs have poor resolution [Schultz 2020, pp. 38-42].
3.  **Incomplete Data:** Public reporting of HF operational details (stage timing, volumes, pressures) is limited and often incomplete, hindering scientific analysis [Schultz 2020, pp. 40-42].
4.  **Mechanistic Uncertainty:** The relative contributions of pore pressure diffusion, poroelastic stressing, and aseismic slip in triggering earthquakes remain debated and are difficult to disentangle [Schultz 2020, pp. 46-50].
5.  **Fault Characterization:** Reliably imaging subtle, often strike-slip, fault structures prior to stimulation is a major technical challenge [Schultz 2020, pp. 50-52].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  HF-induced earthquakes preferentially occur on faults that are critically stressed in the pre-existing tectonic stress field [Schultz 2020, pp. 46-48].
2.  The target formations for HF are highly impermeable and often overpressured [Schultz 2020, pp. 29-31].
3.  Spatiotemporal association (e.g., within 5 km and during/shortly after stimulation) is a primary criterion for classifying earthquakes as HF-induced [Schultz 2020, pp. 33-35, 38-42].
4.  The volume of injected fluid is a key control on the rate of induced seismicity in geologically susceptible areas [Schultz 2020, pp. 33-35, 52-54].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Injected Fluid Volume:** Total volume pumped per stage, well, or pad [Schultz 2020, pp. 12-14, 33-35].
-   **Injection Rate and Pressure:** Operational parameters during stimulation [Schultz 2020, pp. 4-6].
-   **Magnitude (M_L, M_w):** Size of induced earthquakes, used for regulatory thresholds [Schultz 2020, pp. 54-56].
-   **b-value:** Slope of the frequency-magnitude distribution, which can vary and provide insights into fault properties [Schultz 2020, pp. 42-46].
-   **Geological Factors:** Depth to basement, formation overpressure, proximity to reef margins, fault architecture [Schultz 2020, pp. 50-52].
-   **Spatiotemporal Windows:** Distance (e.g., 5 km) and time (e.g., during stimulation + 7 days) used to associate earthquakes with HF [Schultz 2020, pp. 38-42].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** In geologically susceptible areas (e.g., near basement, overpressured formations). **Claim:** The productivity of HF-induced earthquakes scales linearly with stimulation volume [Schultz 2020, pp. 12-14, 33-35].
2.  **Condition:** For HF operations in the Duvernay play. **Claim:** More than 96% of the variability in earthquake rate is controlled by the completion volume on a single pad [Schultz 2020, pp. 33-35].
3.  **Condition:** Globally, across documented cases. **Claim:** Less than one percent of HF wells are associated with detectable earthquakes [Schultz 2020, pp. 33-35].
4.  **Condition:** When using template matching on HF-induced seismicity sequences. **Claim:** The technique commonly increases the number of detected earthquakes by roughly an order of magnitude [Schultz 2020, pp. 35-38].
5.  **Condition:** In the Eagle Ford Shale play. **Claim:** Cases where multiple laterals were stimulated concurrently tripled the probability of seismicity relative to a single lateral stimulated in isolation [Schultz 2020, pp. 25-27].

## Connections To Other Work
The review connects HF-induced seismicity to:
-   **Wastewater Disposal-Induced Seismicity:** Often occurs in the same basins, and distinguishing the two drivers is a key challenge [Schultz 2020, pp. 4-6, 23-25].
-   **Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS):** Both involve high-pressure fluid injection; forecasting and mitigation approaches from EGS may be portable to HF [Schultz 2020, pp. 52-54].
-   **General Induced Seismicity:** HF is one category of anthropogenic seismicity, alongside mining, reservoir impoundment, and hydrocarbon extraction [Schultz 2020, pp. 4-6].
-   **Fault Mechanics and Hydrogeology:** Understanding triggering requires integrating knowledge of fault zone architecture, fluid flow, and rock friction [Schultz 2020, pp. 46-50].
