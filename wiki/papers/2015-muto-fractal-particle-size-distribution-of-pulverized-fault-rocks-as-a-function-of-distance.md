# Fractal Particle Size Distribution of Pulverized Fault Rocks as a Function of Distance from the Fault Core.

## One-line Summary
Pulverized fault rocks from the San Andreas and Arima-Takatsuki faults exhibit fractal particle size distributions that decrease in dimension with distance from the fault core, indicating formation by a dynamic stress pulse similar to impact loading.

## Research Question
The study investigates the microstructural and fractal characteristics of pulverized fault rocks, focusing on quantitative differences between minerals and spatial variations in fractal dimensions as a function of distance from the fault core to discuss the evolution of off-fault damage by dynamic loading [Muto 2015, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
Samples were collected from the Arima-Takatsuki Tectonic Line (ATTL) in southwest Japan and the San Andreas fault (SAF) in California. Along the ATTL, samples were taken at Hakusui-kyo (distances of 1, 2, 200, and 400 m) and Horai-kyo (distances of 10, 80, 100, and 800 m) from the fault core. Along the SAF, samples were collected at Juniper Hills (10 m from core) and Lake Hughes (500 m from core) [Muto 2015, pp. 1-3]. The ATTL samples are granitic rocks, while the SAF samples are gneissic diorite and granite [Muto 2015, pp. 1-3].

## Methods
Microstructural analysis was conducted using optical microscopy. Photomicrographs of fractured particles were taken at different magnifications, and the diameters of several hundred particles were measured using image analysis software (Win ROOF version 5.7). A particle was defined as the portion surrounded by fractures. Cumulative probability was calculated, and distributions were fitted by the power law equation P(r) ∝ r^(-Ds), where r is particle size and Ds is the fractal dimension [Muto 2015, pp. 3-4].

## Key Findings
- Particle size distributions of all 18 samples follow a fractal (power law) distribution down to the micron scale (17.5 to 480 μm) with R² > 0.99 [Muto 2015, pp. 3-4].
- Fractal dimensions (Ds) range from 1.86 to 2.92, dependent on mineral type, distance from the fault core, and rock type (pulverized rock vs. gouge) [Muto 2015, pp. 3-4].
- Fractal dimensions decrease with increasing distance normal to the fault core for the same mineral species (e.g., quartz) [Muto 2015, pp. 4-6].
- Amphibole particles have higher fractal dimensions (up to 2.92) than quartz and K-feldspar particles [Muto 2015, pp. 3-4].
- Pulverized rocks near the fault core have higher fractal dimensions (Ds ~2.7-2.9) than natural fault gouges/cataclasites and experimentally created gouges, and exceed the theoretical upper limit of 2.58 for confined comminution [Muto 2015, pp. 4-6, 7-8].
- Microstructural characteristics show a lack of shear strain, angular particles, and opening-mode fractures, indicating volumetric deformation [Muto 2015, pp. 1-3, 7-8].

## Limitations
- The scatter in the Ds vs. distance plot (Figure 5) likely results from heterogeneity in physical properties (rock types, fault structures) and repeated damage events [Muto 2015, pp. 6-7].
- The exact distance from the SAF core is approximate for the Lake Hughes locality, and distances for samples SAF-A1 and SAF-A2 are not available [Muto 2015, pp. 3-4].
- The number of samples from the SAF is limited [Muto 2015, pp. 4-6].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The particle size distribution follows a power law (fractal) relationship within the measured size range [Muto 2015, pp. 3-4].
- The relationship between fractal dimension and fracture energy is based on the Griffith energy balance concept [Muto 2015, pp. 6-7].
- The linear decrease of Ds with distance is derived from a model combining energy-size relationships and elastic energy decay from a fault core [Muto 2015, pp. 6-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Fractal Dimension (Ds)**: Slope of the power law distribution in log-log space, ranging from 1.86 to 2.92.
- **Distance from Fault Core (d)**: Measured normal to the fault core, ranging from 1 to 800 m.
- **Mineral Type**: Quartz, K-feldspar, amphibole.
- **Particle Size (r)**: Diameter in micrometers (μm).

## Reusable Claims
- Pulverized fault rocks exhibit fractal particle size distributions down to the micron scale [Muto 2015, pp. 1-1].
- The fractal dimension of pulverized rocks decreases linearly with increasing distance from the fault core [Muto 2015, pp. 4-6, 6-7].
- Fractal dimensions of pulverized rocks near the fault core are higher than those of natural fault gouges and cataclasites, and exceed the theoretical upper limit for confined comminution [Muto 2015, pp. 4-6, 7-8].
- Amphibole minerals exhibit higher fractal dimensions than quartz and feldspar under the same pulverization conditions [Muto 2015, pp. 3-4].
- The high fractal dimensions and microstructural lack of shear indicate pulverization was caused by a dynamic stress pulse with instantaneous volumetric expansion, likely during seismic rupture propagation [Muto 2015, pp. 7-8].
