# Spatial Relationship between Earthquakes and Volcanic Vents in the Central-Northern Main Ethiopian Rift.

## One-line Summary
Fractal analysis of earthquake and volcanic vent distributions in the Main Ethiopian Rift reveals genetically linked, self-similar clustering patterns, indicating that faulting is induced above a narrower central zone of diking.

## Research Question
How are extension by faulting (earthquakes) and by intrusion (volcanic vents) spatially distributed and related in three dimensions within the Main Ethiopian Rift?

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the central-northern Main Ethiopian Rift (MER) in East Africa, a seismically and volcanically active continental rift [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 1-2]. The datasets consist of 1439 earthquakes recorded from October 2001 to February 2003 by the EAGLE seismic network and 702 mapped monogenetic volcanic vents [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 2-3, 6-7]. Earthquake locations are constrained to ±600 m horizontally and ±2000 m in depth [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 2-3]. Vent locations were acquired from high-resolution satellite imagery, with a sample that does not include vents smaller than ~10 m or those buried by younger deposits [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
The spatial distribution was analyzed using the Coefficient of Variation (CV) of nearest-neighbor distances, 2D symmetric Gaussian kernel density estimation, and fractal analysis via a two-point correlation function [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 3-5]. The fractal exponent (D) and size range (lower and upper cut-offs, Lco and Uco) were determined from log-log plots of the correlation integral C(l) versus distance l [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 5-6]. The analysis was applied to earthquake epicentres (2D), hypocentres (3D), and vent centres [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 5-6].

## Key Findings
1.  Both earthquakes and vents show clustered distributions (CV > 1) [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 6-7].
2.  Vents exhibit self-similar clustering with a fractal exponent D = 1.21 ± 0.01 in the 0.6–10.6 km size range [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 6-7].
3.  Earthquake epicentres (2D) have D = 0.15 ± 0.02 in the 5.5–18 km size range, and hypocentres (3D) have D = 0.22 ± 0.02 in the 8.5–18 km size range [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 6-7].
4.  The upper cut-off for vents (~11 km) is interpreted to relate to the depth of solidified magma reservoirs, while the upper cut-off for earthquakes (~18 km) corresponds to the thickness of the seismogenic crust [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 6-7].
5.  The zone of seismicity is generally 20–30 km wide, while the zone of vents is narrower and centered within it, suggesting faults are induced above a central zone of diking [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 1-1, 9-9].
6.  Along-rift, regions with higher vent density (greater magmatism) show reduced seismicity and faulting, indicating strain is accommodated more by intrusion than faulting in those segments [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 9-9].

## Limitations
1.  The earthquake catalogue covers a limited time interval (2001-2003) and may not be fully representative of long-term rift behavior [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 2-3].
2.  The vent dataset is a sample of the true population, excluding small (<10 m) and buried vents [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 2-3].
3.  Uncertainties in earthquake locations (especially depth) may lead to truncation effects and overestimation of clustering, potentially producing smaller fractal exponents [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 7-9].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The spatial distribution of volcanic vents reflects the properties of the connected (backbone) part of the subsurface fracture network [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 3-5].
2.  The upper cut-off of the vent fractal distribution approximates the thickness of the fractured crust between magma source and surface [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 3-5].
3.  The 2D fractal analysis of epicentres can reliably characterize the 3D properties of the fault network, as supported by the similar upper cut-offs from 2D and 3D earthquake data [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 7-9].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Fractal Exponent (D)**: Measures the degree of clustering; a lower D indicates higher clustering.
-   **Size Range**: The distance interval (between lower cut-off Lco and upper cut-off Uco) over which power-law scaling holds.
-   **Coefficient of Variation (CV)**: Ratio of standard deviation to mean of nearest-neighbor distances; CV > 1 indicates clustering.
-   **Kernel Density Estimate (λ(v))**: Used to compute the spatial intensity of earthquakes and vents.

[Mazzarini 2013, pp. 5-6]
3.  **Condition**: In continental rift zones with bimodal volcanism. **Claim**: Regions with a higher degree of diking (inferred from higher vent density) are characterized by a reduced degree of faulting, suggesting magmatic intrusion accommodates a significant proportion of crustal strain. **Limitation**: Observed along-rift variation; may not apply uniformly to all rift segments. [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 9-9]

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on previous work linking vent distributions to fracture networks in various tectonic settings [Mazzarini 2013, pp. 3-5]. It uses and validates the fractal analysis methodology developed for volcanic fields [Mazzarini 2004; Mazzarini and Isola 2010]. The findings support models where diking induces faulting in rift zones [Rubin and Pollard 1988; Behn et al. 2006] and are consistent with geodetic and geological observations of strain localization in the MER [Keir et al. 2006; Kogan et al. 2012].

## Open Questions
1.  How do the spatial relationships between earthquakes and vents evolve over longer time scales than the 2001-2003 seismic catalogue?
2.  What are the precise mechanisms controlling the along-rift variations in the balance between magmatic and tectonic strain accommodation?
3.  Can the inferred 3D fracture network properties from 2D vent analysis be directly validated with deeper geophysical imaging?
