# Timing, Scale and Mechanism of the Destruction of the North China Craton.

## One-line Summary
The North China Craton (NCC) was destroyed mainly in the eastern part during the Mesozoic, peaking at ca 125 Ma, driven by unsteady mantle flow triggered by the subduction of the Pacific Plate; the western NCC experienced only localized lithospheric modification and thinning.

## Research Question
What were the timing, geographical extent, and mechanism of the destruction (decratonization) of the North China Craton, and how do plate‑margin processes interact with deep mantle dynamics to destabilize an ancient craton?

## Study Area / Data
- The North China Craton (NCC), a classical destroyed craton [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2].
- Dense temporary seismic arrays (NCISP, DNCC) covering the NCC (Figure 1) [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- Petrological and geochemical data from Paleozoic kimberlites, Mesozoic–Cenozoic basalts, and mantle xenoliths [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- Paleomagnetic data from Cretaceous volcanic rocks indicating intra‑plate rotation [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2].
- Structural data: metamorphic core complexes (MCCs), detachment faults, and fault‑basins [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2].
- Mantle transition zone thickness from receiver function migration [Zhu 2011, pp. 4-5].
- Global comparison of cratons (South Africa, India, North America, etc.) [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].

## Methods
- Lithospheric‑ and crustal‑thickness mapping using large‑scale temporary seismic arrays [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4]
- Receiver function migration to image mantle transition zone discontinuities [Zhu 2011, pp. 4-5]
- Paleomagnetic measurement of block rotations [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2]
- Structural analysis of extensional tectonics (MCCs, basins, fault kinematics) [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2]
- Integrated petrology‑geochemistry‑geophysics approach [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4]
- Comparative global craton analysis [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6]

## Key Findings
- **Timing**: After cratonization at ~1.8 Ga the NCC remained stable until the early Mesozoic [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2]; peak destruction occurred at ca 125 Ma (Early Cretaceous), coeval with extensive magmatism, regional extension, and large‑scale mineralization [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2, 2‑2].
- **Scale**: Destruction is largely confined to the eastern NCC; the western NCC was only locally modified [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2]. Eastern NCC shows thin crust (<35 km) and thin lithosphere (60–100 km); central/western NCC has thicker crust (40–60 km) and highly variable lithosphere (80 km beneath Cenozoic rifts to >200 km beneath the Ordos Basin) [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4]. A rapid thickness change coincides with the North‑South Gravity Lineament [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- **Manifestations**: Lithospheric thinning, mantle transformation, widespread intra‑crustal ductile deformation and magmatism, metamorphic core complexes (exhumed 135–115 Ma with consistent NWW‑SEE movement), extensional fault‑basins, and regional ~20°–35° rotation of the east Liaoning‑Korean Peninsula relative to Eurasia since the Early Cretaceous [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2].
- **Deep dynamics**: The mantle transition zone beneath the eastern NCC is thicker (>250 km) and hosts a stagnant Pacific slab; beneath the central/western NCC the transition zone is normal to thin (≤250 km) and lacks a slab [Zhu 2011, pp. 4-5]. This difference correlates with whole‑scale destruction in the east vs. localized thinning in the west [Zhu 2011, pp. 4-5].
- **Mechanism**: Unsteady mantle flow induced by the westward subduction of the Pacific Plate destabilized the eastern NCC lithosphere, leading to delamination and/or thermal‑mechanical‑chemical erosion [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7]. Globally, most cratons have undergone thinning without destruction; destruction only occurs when a craton is disturbed by oceanic subduction [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2, 5‑6]. Mantle plumes alone can thin lithosphere but are insufficient to destroy a craton [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6]. The Early Cretaceous peak was accompanied by globally high mantle temperatures and active plate tectonics, which intensified subduction‑related weakening and magmatism [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7].
- **Western NCC**: Localized thinning in the Yinchuan‑Hetao and Fenwei rifts is attributed to pre‑existing weak Paleoproterozoic tectonic belts that focused strain and heating, while the cratonic nucleus (Ordos Block) remained stable and protected [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7].

## Limitations
- Mantle xenoliths and surface igneous rocks have spatially limited distribution, hindering lithospheric interpretation in basins and vegetated areas [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4].
- The precise mechanism of destruction remains debated—whether dominated by delamination or thermo‑chemical erosion [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-5].
- Seismic imaging provides smoothed, large‑scale structures; sharp boundaries may be artificial.
- Global comparisons rely on literature that may not capture all local complexities.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Craton destruction is defined as loss of tectonic stability and is distinct from simple lithospheric thinning [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2].
- The unsteady mantle flow driven by Pacific subduction was the primary driver; the slab stagnation and thickness anomalies are taken as evidence [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6, 6‑7].
- Lithospheric thickness estimates from receiver functions and tomography are assumed representative of the average lithosphere.
- The age of peak destruction (125 Ma) is based on the culmination of spatially widespread magmatic and extensional events and may average asynchronous local events.

## Key Variables / Parameters

- Crustal thickness (<35 km eastern, 40–60 km central/western) [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-4]
- Mantle transition zone thickness (>>250 km eastern, ≤250 km central/western) [Zhu 2011, pp. 4-5]
- Peak magmatic/tectonic age: ca 125 Ma [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2]
- Rotation angle of east Liaoning‑Korean Peninsula: 20°–35° [Zhu 2011, pp. 2-2]

- Early Cretaceous Pacific Plate subduction acceleration and direction change [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6]

## Reusable Claims
- “The destruction of the NCC occurred mainly in the eastern NCC, with a peak age of ca 125 Ma, while the western NCC was only locally modified.” [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2] (Supported by integrated seismic, magmatic, structural, and geochemical data; condition: based on 2011 synthesis.)
- “Cratonic destruction is distinct from lithospheric thinning; most global cratons have experienced thinning but not destruction, suggesting that oceanic subduction disturbance is required for complete loss of cratonic stability.” [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6] (Based on global comparison; condition: requires craton to be in a subduction setting.)
- “The stagnant Pacific slab in the mantle transition zone beneath eastern NCC induced unsteady mantle flow, leading to delamination and/or thermal‑mechanical‑chemical erosion of the lithospheric root.” [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7] (Inferred from seismic structure and temporal coupling; condition: depends on subduction history and slab morphology.)
- “Localized lithospheric thinning in the central and western NCC is controlled by pre‑existing weak Paleoproterozoic tectonic belts, which focus strain and heating during later tectonic events, while the cratonic nucleus (Ordos) remains protected.” [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7] (Based on thickness contrasts and geological history; condition: applicable to regions with ancient mobile belts.)
- “Mantle plumes alone can cause lithospheric thinning but are insufficient to destroy a craton; thermal erosion of a thick Archean keel would require the craton to reside above a plume for at least 200 Ma.” [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6] (Based on global examples and modelling; condition: unless combined with plate‑margin forces.)
- “The peak of NCC destruction coincided with globally high mantle temperatures and active plate tectonics in the Early Cretaceous, which amplified subduction‑driven weakening and magmatism.” [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7] (Global data from refs [65–69]; condition: correlation, not proven causation.)

## Connections To Other Work
- The concept builds on early ideas: Wong (1927) “Yanshanian Movement” and Chen (1956) “Platform Reactivation” [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2].
- Lithospheric thinning beneath the NCC was documented by Menzies et al. (1993) and Griffin et al. (1998) [Zhu 2011, pp. 1-2, 8‑9].
- Destruction mechanism parallels the western North America Craton, where Pacific subduction triggered unsteady flow (Sonder & Jones, 1999; Zandt et al., 2004) [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].
- India Craton thinning without destruction is linked to the Reunion plume and Deccan flood basalts (Lehmann et al., 2010) [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].
- Numerical models of craton longevity and stability (Lenardic et al., 2003) support the protective role of weak belts [Zhu 2011, pp. 6-7].
- The Kaapvaal Craton (South Africa) shows preservation despite thinning, consistent with plume‑only modification [Zhu 2011, pp. 5-6].

## Open Questions
- Was the eastern NCC destruction a unique event or part of a broader circum‑Pacific decratonization process?
- What exact physical mechanism (delamination, thermal erosion, or a combination) dominated lithospheric removal in the eastern NCC?
- How did pre‑existing lithospheric heterogeneities influence the lateral extent of destruction?
- Could other late Mesozoic tectonic events (Mongolian‑Okhotsk closure, Yangtze collision) have contributed synergistically to the destruction?
- What explains the preservation of the Ordos block despite proximity to subduction‑driven flow?
- How does the timing of NCC destruction correlate with global mantle avalanche and superplume events?
