# Structural Control on the Salmas Geothermal Region, Northwest Iran, from Fractal Analysis and Paleostress Data.

## One-line Summary
The Salmas geothermal field in NW Iran is structurally controlled, with high fracture density and specific paleostress orientations facilitating geothermal fluid circulation, as quantified by fractal analysis and stress inversion.

## Research Question
How do fractures and faults control the formation and fluid circulation pathways of the Salmas geothermal reservoir, and how can fractal analysis and paleostress data characterize this structural control?

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Salmas geothermal region in northwestern Iran, located between the Sanandaj-Sirjan metamorphic zone and the Urumieh-Dokhtar magmatic arc [Behyari 2018, pp. 1-2]. Data sources include ASTER satellite images for lineament extraction, field observations of faults and fractures, and structural measurements (fault planes with slickensides, conjugate faults, foliation) for paleostress analysis [Behyari 2018, pp. 3-5]. The region contains six warm springs (23–29°C) and travertine outcrops [Behyari 2018, pp. 3-5].

## Methods
1.  **Lineament Extraction:** Automatic extraction from ASTER satellite images using an RGB combination (R:2 G:1 B:1) and a Sobel filter for edge enhancement [Behyari 2018, pp. 3-5].
2.  **Fractal Analysis:** The square counter method was applied to a lineament and fault map. The study area was divided into four zones (A, B, C, D), and fractal dimension was calculated for each by counting the number of squares containing fractures at different scales [Behyari 2018, pp. 5-5].
3.  **Paleostress Analysis:** Principal stress axes (σ1, σ2, σ3) were determined using fault slip data, conjugate fault geometry, and foliation orientation. Data were processed using Win-Tensor 4.0 software [Behyari 2018, pp. 7-8].

## Key Findings
1.  Thermal spring outlets are located in zones of high fracture density [Behyari 2018, pp. 3-5].
2.  The highest fractal dimension (1.96) was calculated in Zone D, which hosts thermal springs, indicating a strong correlation between fracture density and geothermal activity [Behyari 2018, pp. 5-5].
3.  Paleostress analysis revealed distinct stress regimes:
    *   In the central part (stations S2, S3, S4), the average σ1 orientation is S90°W/10°, favoring strike-slip faulting. E-W fractures are permeable pathways here [Behyari 2018, pp. 8-9].
    *   Near thermal springs (stations S1, S7, S8), the average σ1 orientation is N15°E/20°, favoring thrust faulting. N-S fractures, like the F1 fault, are critical fluid pathways [Behyari 2018, pp. 8-9].
4.  The N-S trending F1 fault, with a left-lateral strike-slip mechanism, acts as a major conduit for geothermal fluid flow [Behyari 2018, pp. 5-7].

## Limitations
The study relies on satellite image resolution for lineament extraction, which may miss smaller fractures. Paleostress analysis is dependent on the quality, quantity, and representativeness of the measured structural data at each station. The direct link between fractal dimension and permeability is inferred rather than directly measured.

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Fractures and faults are the primary controls on secondary permeability and fluid circulation in the geothermal reservoir [Behyari 2018, pp. 2-3].
2.  Fractal dimension is a reliable quantitative proxy for fracture density and intensity [Behyari 2018, pp. 5-5].
3.  The orientation of structures (faults, foliation, dikes) can be used to infer the paleostress field [Behyari 2018, pp. 7-8].
4.  The presence of thermal springs indicates an underlying geothermal reservoir [Behyari 2018, pp. 3-5].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Fractal Dimension (D):** Quantitative measure of fracture network complexity (values range from 1.90 to 1.96 in the study area).
*   **Paleostress Axes (σ1, σ2, σ3):** Orientation and plunge of the principal stress axes.
*   **Fault Orientation:** Strike and dip of major fault zones (e.g., F1, F2, F3).
*   **Fracture Density:** Qualitative and quantitative (via fractal dimension) measure of fracture concentration.

## Reusable Claims
*   In convergent tectonic settings like NW Iran, the intersection of major fault sets (e.g., N-S and NE-SW) creates zones of high fracture density that can localize geothermal reservoirs [Behyari 2018, pp. 5-5].
*   The permeability of a fracture set is controlled by its orientation relative to the contemporary stress field; fractures parallel to the maximum horizontal stress (σ1) tend to be open and conductive, while those perpendicular are squeezed shut [Behyari 2018, pp. 8-9].
*   A fractal dimension approaching 2.0 in a faulted terrain indicates a highly interconnected fracture network conducive to fluid flow [Behyari 2018, pp. 5-5].
*   Paleostress analysis using fault-slip data can identify local stress perturbations that create permeable fracture corridors critical for geothermal fluid ascent [Behyari 2018, pp. 7-8].

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on research linking geothermal systems to tectonic settings, particularly magmatic arcs like the Urumieh-Dokhtar arc in Iran [Behyari 2018, pp. 1-2]. It applies fractal geometry methods used in other geological contexts [Babadagli, 2001; Kruhl, 2013] and paleostress techniques established for structural analysis [Navabpour et al., 2007; Pérez-López et al., 2005]. The findings align with global studies showing structural controls on geothermal fields in extensional and convergent settings [Faulds et al., 2010; Pérez-Flores et al., 2017].

## Open Questions
1.  What is the precise depth and geometry of the geothermal reservoir inferred from the surface structural data?
2.  How do the identified paleostress regimes relate temporally to the different phases of tectonic activity (e.g., ophiolite obduction, collision)?
3.  What is the relative contribution of deep-seated, possibly hidden, faults versus the mapped shallow fracture network to the overall geothermal system?
