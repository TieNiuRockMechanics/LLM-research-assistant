# Effect of Thermal Treatment on the Dynamic Fracture Toughness of Laurentian Granite.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how thermal treatment up to 850°C affects the dynamic fracture toughness of Laurentian granite, finding that toughness generally decreases with temperature but shows distinct behavior below 250°C and above 450°C due to competing microscopic physical processes.

## Research Question
What is the effect of thermal treatment temperature on the dynamic fracture toughness of Laurentian granite, and what are the underlying microscopic mechanisms? [Yin 2012, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study used Laurentian granite (LG) obtained from the Laurentian region of the Grenville province, Precambrian Canadian Shield, north of St. Lawrence and northwest of Quebec City, Canada. The mineralogical composition is feldspar 60%, quartz 33%, biotite 3–5%. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3] Physical and strength properties are listed in Table 1 of the source. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3]

## Methods
- **Specimen Preparation:** Notched semi-circular bend (NSCB) specimens were prepared from LG cores (40 mm diameter, 18 mm thick). A notch with ~1 mm thickness and a tip diameter of 0.5 mm was machined. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3]
- **Thermal Treatment:** Thirty-seven samples were divided into six groups: untreated and treated at 100, 250, 450, 600, and 850°C. Heating/cooling rate was 2°C/min. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3]
- **Microscopic Characterization:** Scanning electron microscope (SEM) was used to examine micro-cracks in samples from each temperature group. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3]
- **P-wave Velocity Measurement:** Ultrasonic P-wave velocity was measured on three samples per treatment temperature. [Yin 2012, pp. 3-5]
- **Dynamic Fracture Testing:** A modified split Hopkinson pressure bar (SHPB) system was used to apply dynamic load to the NSCB specimens. A composite pulse shaper was used to achieve dynamic force balance. [Yin 2012, pp. 3-5]
- **Fracture Toughness Calculation:** Mode-I stress intensity factor (SIF) was calculated using an equation based on ASTM standard E399-06e2, adapted for the NSCB geometry. The maximum SIF was taken as the dynamic fracture toughness (K_IC^D). [Yin 2012, pp. 3-5]
- **Loading Rate Determination:** The loading rate (K̇_I) was determined from the slope of the linear range of the SIF history before peak load. [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]

## Key Findings
1.  **P-wave Velocity:** Decreased monotonically with treatment temperature when temperature was higher than 250°C. At 100°C, it was not sensitive to heating. [Yin 2012, pp. 3-5]
2.  **SEM Observations:** Thermally induced micro-cracks were hardly visible below 250°C but became very obvious at and above 450°C, with density and width increasing with temperature. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3]
3.  **Dynamic Fracture Toughness vs. Loading Rate:** Fracture toughness increased almost linearly with increasing loading rate for all treatment temperatures. [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]
4.  **Dynamic Fracture Toughness vs. Temperature:** Fracture toughness generally decreased with treatment temperature. However, samples treated at 250°C showed ~5% higher toughness than those at 100°C for the same loading rate. The decrease in toughness was less significant between 450°C and 850°C compared to between 250°C and 450°C. [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]
5.  **Loading Rate Sensitivity:** The slope (x₂) of the fracture toughness vs. loading rate relationship decreased with treatment temperature, featuring a sharp decrease between 250°C and 450°C. [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]
6.  **Microscopic Explanations:**
    - Below 250°C: Thermal expansion of grains leads to closure of original cracks, increasing toughness. [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]
    - Above 250°C: Thermally induced micro-cracking increases, decreasing toughness. [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]
    - Above 450°C: Sources of weakness (grain boundaries, α to β quartz phase transition) are depleted, limiting further significant decrease in toughness. [Yin 2012, pp. 6-8]

## Limitations
- The study is specific to Laurentian granite; results may vary for other rock types. [Yin 2012, pp. 1-2]
- The maximum treatment temperature was 850°C. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3]
- The NSCB method, while noted to suffer less friction than the SR method, may still have inherent limitations. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3]
- The inertial effect in dynamic measurements was not sufficiently addressed in some prior work cited for comparison. [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The NSCB specimen geometry and the static SIF formula (Eq. 1) are valid for dynamic loading when dynamic force balance is achieved. [Yin 2012, pp. 3-5]
- The heating/cooling rate of 2°C/min was sufficiently slow to avoid cracking due to thermal shock. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3]
- The sharpened notch tip (0.5 mm diameter) is small enough relative to the average grain size (0.59 mm) for accurate toughness measurement. [Yin 2012, pp. 2-3]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables:** Treatment temperature (T), Loading rate (K̇_I).
- **Dependent Variables:** Dynamic fracture toughness (K_IC^D), P-wave velocity (Vp).
- **Derived Parameters:** Fitting parameters x₁(T) and x₂(T) for the toughness-loading rate relationship. [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** For Laurentian granite, dynamic fracture toughness increases linearly with loading rate across all tested thermal treatment temperatures (25-850°C). **Condition:** Using NSCB specimens tested with a pulse-shaped SHPB system. **Limitation:** Specific to the tested rock and temperature range. [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]
- **Claim:** The sensitivity of dynamic fracture toughness to loading rate (slope x₂) decreases with increasing treatment temperature, with a sharp decrease occurring between 250°C and 450°C. **Condition:** Derived from the linear fit K_IC^D = x₁ + x₂K̇_I. **Limitation:** The functional form of x₂(T) is empirical (Eq. 4). [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]
- **Claim:** Thermal treatment below 250°C can increase the dynamic fracture toughness of Laurentian granite due to grain expansion closing pre-existing cracks. **Condition:** Compared to untreated or 100°C treated samples at similar loading rates. **Limitation:** The increase is modest (~5%). [Yin 2012, pp. 5-6]
- **Claim:** The decrease in dynamic fracture toughness with temperature is less pronounced above 450°C because sources of weakness (grain boundaries, quartz phase transition) are depleted. **Condition:** Observed in the trend of the x₂ parameter and toughness values. **Limitation:** This is a proposed mechanism based on SEM and literature. [Yin 2012, pp. 6-8]
