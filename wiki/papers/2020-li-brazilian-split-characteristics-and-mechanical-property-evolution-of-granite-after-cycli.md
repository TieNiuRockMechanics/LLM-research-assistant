# Brazilian Split Characteristics and Mechanical Property Evolution of Granite after Cyclic Cooling at Different Temperatures.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how cyclic cooling at different temperatures (100-700°C) with two methods (water and natural cooling) affects the tensile strength, failure mode, and physical properties of granite, relevant to geothermal engineering.

## Research Question
To reveal the mechanism of wellbore failure and increased fracturing in heat reservoirs during hot dry rock geothermal development, which involves high-temperature rock subjected to repeated cooling and heating cycles [Li 2020, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study used Shandong Wendeng granite, which is dense and crack-free. Samples were processed into Brazilian discs (ϕ50 mm × 25 mm) according to ISRM standards [Li 2020, pp. 2-6]. The main mineral composition is quartz (45.6%), potassium feldspar (19.0%), plagioclase (28%), mica (6.5%), and others (8.9%) [Li 2020, pp. 2-6].

## Methods
- **Sample Preparation:** Granite discs were subjected to cyclic heating and cooling. Temperatures ranged from 100°C to 700°C in 100°C increments. Cycle numbers were 5, 10, 15, 20, and 25. Two cooling methods were used: water cooling (rapid) and natural cooling (slow) [Li 2020, pp. 2-6].
- **Heating/Cooling Procedure:** Samples were heated in a muffle furnace at 4°C/min to the target temperature, held for 8 hours for uniform heating, then cooled either in room temperature water or naturally in the furnace [Li 2020, pp. 2-6].
- **Testing:** After cycling, Brazilian splitting tests were performed using a universal testing machine with a loading rate of 0.1 mm/s. An acoustic emission (PCI-2) system monitored energy release, and a camera recorded crack propagation [Li 2020, pp. 2-6].
- **Measurements:** Longitudinal wave velocity, peak load, tensile strength (calculated via σ_t = 2P/(πDL)), and failure patterns were recorded [Li 2020, pp. 2-6].

## Key Findings
1.  **Appearance & Damage:** With increasing temperature and cycles, sample color changed from gray-black to earthy yellow. Water cooling caused more severe damage, with some high-temperature, high-cycle samples (e.g., W600-25, W700-15) fragmenting and becoming untestable [Li 2020, pp. 2-6].
2.  **Wave Velocity:** Longitudinal wave velocity decreased with increasing temperature and cycle number. The decline was greater under water cooling than natural cooling. For example, at 100°C after 5 cycles, velocity dropped to 71% of the original for water cooling vs. 69% for natural cooling [Li 2020, pp. 2-6].
3.  **Tensile Strength:** Tensile strength decreased with increasing cycle number for both cooling methods. At the same temperature and cycle count, water-cooled samples had lower strength than naturally cooled ones. The degradation was more pronounced at high temperatures (>500°C) and high cycle numbers [Li 2020, pp. 6-8].
4.  **Failure Modes:** Failure patterns were categorized by temperature stage:
    - **Low Temperature (100-200°C):** Samples split along the central diameter ("linear type") [Li 2020, pp. 6-8].
    - **Medium Temperature (300-400°C):** Main cracks were arc-shaped, deviating from the center, indicating shear-tension mixed failure ("arc type") [Li 2020, pp. 6-8].
    - **High Temperature (500-700°C):** Failure became complex and fragmented. At 500°C, secondary cracks appeared. At 600-700°C, especially with water cooling and high cycles, samples showed severe fragmentation, granular debris, and non-rejoining fracture surfaces [Li 2020, pp. 6-8].
5.  **Brittle-Ductile Transition:** When temperature exceeded 500°C, the stress-time curve showed gradual failure, indicating a transition from brittle to ductile behavior [Li 2020, pp. 6-8].
6.  **Microscopic Analysis:** Thin sections of samples cycled at 600°C showed that water cooling induced more transgranular and intergranular cracks than natural cooling. Crack number, width, and length increased with cycle number [Li 2020, pp. 8-9].

## Limitations
- The study used only one type of granite (Shandong Wendeng). Results may vary for other rock types.
- Laboratory conditions (controlled heating/cooling rates, sample size) may not fully replicate complex field conditions in geothermal reservoirs.
- Some high-temperature, high-cycle water-cooled samples were lost, creating data gaps for those specific conditions [Li 2020, pp. 2-6].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The granite samples are representative and homogeneous.
- The cyclic heating/cooling procedure simulates the thermal stress cycles experienced by rock in geothermal operations.
- The Brazilian splitting test is a valid method for determining tensile strength under these conditions.

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables:** Temperature (100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 700°C), Number of cycles (5, 10, 15, 20, 25), Cooling method (water cooling, natural cooling).
- **Dependent Variables:** Longitudinal wave velocity, Tensile strength, Failure mode, Acoustic emission energy, Apparent color and integrity.

## Reusable Claims
- Cyclic cooling, particularly with water, significantly degrades the tensile strength of granite, with degradation amplifying at temperatures above 500°C and with higher cycle counts [Li 2020, pp. 6-8].
- The failure mode of granite under Brazilian splitting evolves from brittle, linear splitting at low temperatures (<200°C) to complex, ductile fragmentation at high temperatures (>500°C) after cyclic thermal treatment [Li 2020, pp. 6-8].
- Water cooling induces more severe internal damage (cracking) and greater reductions in mechanical properties compared to natural cooling under identical thermal cycling conditions [Li 2020, pp. 2-6, pp. 6-8].
