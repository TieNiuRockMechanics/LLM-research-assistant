# Comparison of Mechanical Properties in High Temperature and Thermal Treatment Granite.

## One-line Summary

Static uniaxial compression tests on granite after high temperature treatment and under high temperature (25–800 °C) reveal that granite tested under high temperature exhibits a lower brittle–ductile transition temperature, longer densification stage, smaller elastic modulus, larger damage, lower peak stress, greater peak strain, and a change in failure mode from splitting to shear/conjugate‑shear compared with granite tested after cooling.

## Research Question

How do the static mechanical properties (stress–strain behaviour, peak strength, peak strain, elastic modulus, failure modes) of granite differ when specimens are loaded at the same high temperature versus after being cooled from that temperature? The study addresses the practical gap: “the mechanical properties of rocks after high temperature treatment and under high temperature can certainly be different” \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\].

## Study Area / Data

- **Material:** A fine‑ to medium‑grained granite; composition (mass fraction): quartz 46.54 %, feldspar 37.95 %, mica 9.72 %, montmorillonite 5.76 %, others 0.03 % \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\].
- **Specimens:** Cylinders, d50 mm × 100 mm, prepared according to ISRM standards (parallelism ±0.05 mm, flatness ±0.02 mm) and selected by similar wave velocity \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\].
- **Temperature levels:** 25 °C (room temperature), 100, 200, 400, 600, 800 °C. At least five specimens per group \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\].
- **Test conditions:** Two series – “after high temperature treatment” (heated, held 2 h at target, cooled, then tested at room temperature) and “under high temperature” (heated, held 2 h, tested while still at target temperature) \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\].
- **Data collected:** Stress–strain curves, peak stress, peak strain, elastic modulus, failure mode photographs, density and longitudinal wave velocity before/after heating \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3, 5‑6, 9‑11\].

## Methods

- **Loading frame:** Electro‑hydraulic, servo‑controlled material testing machine with an auxiliary heating device (maximum design temperature 1000 °C) that includes insulation, resistance heaters, three thermocouples, and a water‑cooled pressure bar \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\].
- **Heating protocol:** Rate 2 °C/min; hold at target temperature for 2 h to achieve uniform heating \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\].
- **Mechanical testing:** Uniaxial compression at a constant displacement rate (not explicitly given, but controlled by the servo system) until failure; stress and strain recorded continuously \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\].
- **Parameter extraction:**
  - Peak stress $\sigma_{\text{peak}}$: maximum stress on the stress–strain curve.
  - Peak strain $\varepsilon_{\text{peak}}$: strain at $\sigma_{\text{peak}}$.
  - Elastic modulus $E$: secant slope between 40 % and 60 % of $\sigma_{\text{peak}}$ on the rising branch, $E = (\sigma_2 - \sigma_1)/(\varepsilon_2 - \varepsilon_1)$ \[Yin 2016, pp. 6-7\].
  - Thermal damage $D(T) = 1 - E_T/E_0$, where $E_T$ and $E_0$ are the elastic moduli at temperature $T$ and at room temperature, respectively \[Yin 2016, pp. 9-11\].
- **Physical property measurements:** Mass loss (density decrease rate $P_d$), longitudinal wave velocity decrease rate $P_v$ to capture macroscopic damage \[Yin 2016, pp. 9-11\].
- **Fitting:** Quadratic or linear regression for peak stress, peak strain, elastic modulus, wave velocity, and damage as functions of temperature \[Yin 2016, pp. 5-6, 6‑7, 9‑11\].

## Key Findings

1. **Stress–strain curves and brittle–ductile transition**  
   Under both conditions, rising temperature prolongs the densification stage, reduces the slope, shifts the curve to the right, and extends the yielding stage. However, the brittle–ductile transition critical temperature is lower for granite under high temperature (between 100 °C and 400 °C) than for granite after high temperature treatment (between 400 °C and 600 °C) \[Yin 2016, pp. 3-5\].

2. **Peak stress**  
   Peak stress decreases with temperature under both conditions, but the reduction is larger when tested under high temperature. At 800 °C, peak stress is 52.85 % lower than at room temperature for after‑treatment granite, versus 61.31 % lower for under‑temperature granite. The absolute difference increases with temperature: e.g., at 800 °C the under‑temperature peak stress is 13.17 MPa (17.95 %) lower \[Yin 2016, pp. 5-6\].

3. **Peak strain**  
   For after‑treatment granite, peak strain remains roughly constant up to 400 °C, then increases sharply (33.74 % from 400 °C to 600 °C, and 43.24 % further to 800 °C). For under‑temperature granite, peak strain rises already at 100 °C (20.25 % increase over room temperature), and above 400 °C the increase accelerates more dramatically: 71.69 % from 400 °C to 600 °C \[Yin 2016, pp. 6-7\].

4. **Elastic modulus**  
   Elastic modulus falls rapidly with temperature. From room temperature to 200 °C, the drop is 40.98 % for after‑treatment and 50.92 % for under‑temperature specimens. The under‑temperature modulus is consistently smaller at all temperatures above ambient \[Yin 2016, pp. 6-7\].

5. **Failure modes**  
   - Below 100 °C both conditions show **splitting failure** (two main blocks with a failure plane angled to the specimen axis).  
   - Above 100 °C, after‑treatment granite continues to fail by splitting, with the two failure planes joining at progressively deeper levels (at 800 °C they join at the bottom plane).  
   - Under‑temperature granite above 100 °C fails in **shear** (two main blocks with shear planes terminating in the middle of the specimen) and, at 800 °C, in **conjugate shear** (one main block plus abundant small blocks and powder) \[Yin 2016, pp. 7-9\].

6. **Damage magnitude**  
   Thermal damage $D(T)$ is consistently higher for granite under high temperature: at 100 °C, $D_1 = 0.162$ (after) vs. $D_2 = 0.382$ (under); at 800 °C, $D_1 = 0.724$ vs. $D_2 = 0.811$ \[Yin 2016, pp. 9-11\].

7. **Physical property changes**  
   With rising temperature, density decreases (3.67 % loss at 800 °C) and longitudinal wave velocity drops linearly ($v = 5221.42 - 5.17T$). The colour changes from gray to claybank, especially at 800 °C \[Yin 2016, pp. 9-11\].

## Limitations

- Only one rock type (this specific granite) was tested; generalisation to other lithologies is not addressed.
- All tests were uniaxial; no triaxial or true‑triaxial data are presented, so the role of confining pressure under high temperature is approximated only by the concept of thermal stress.
- The elastic modulus definition uses a fixed 40 %–60 % secant range, which may be sensitive to the densification phase shape.
- The heating rate (2 °C/min) and 2‑h hold time are fixed; effects of different heating/cooling rates or longer thermal soaking are not explored.
- The damage parameter $D(T)$ is derived solely from elastic modulus; other damage measures (e.g., crack density, ultrasonic attenuation) could refine the comparison.
- The paper does not report microstructural imaging, so the link between observed mechanical changes and specific grain‑scale mechanisms remains qualitative.

## Assumptions / Conditions

- Specimen preparation follows ISRM suggestions (flatness, parallelism, wave‑velocity homogeneity) \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\].
- For under‑high‑temperature tests, the thermal stress field is assumed to be uniform and isotropically equivalent to a pseudo‑confining pressure, though the paper acknowledges that thermal stress differs from an external mechanical confining pressure because it enhances crack propagation and mineral softening \[Yin 2016, pp. 7-9\].
- The brittle–ductile transition is identified from the shape of the stress–strain curve (prolonged yielding plateau), not from a quantitative brittleness index.
- The 2‑h holding time is considered sufficient for thermal equilibrium; any residual temperature gradient is neglected.
- The quadratic/linear regressions are empirical fits valid only within 25–800 °C and for this particular granite composition.

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Dependent mechanical variables:** Peak stress $\sigma_{\text{peak}}$, peak strain $\varepsilon_{\text{peak}}$, elastic modulus $E$, thermal damage $D(T)$.
- **Categorical variable:** Thermal condition – “after high temperature treatment” vs. “under high temperature”.
- **Physical indicators:** Density $\rho$, longitudinal wave velocity $v$, density decrease rate $P_d$, wave‑velocity decrease rate $P_v$.
- **Failure descriptors:** Failure plane geometry, fragment size, mode classification (splitting, shear, conjugate shear).

## Reusable Claims

1. For the tested granite, the brittle–ductile transition critical temperature under uniaxial compression occurs between 100 °C and 400 °C when the rock is loaded at high temperature, but between 400 °C and 600 °C after cooling from the same temperature \[Yin 2016, pp. 3-5\].
2. Under high temperature, peak stress is lower at all temperatures above ambient, e.g., at 800 °C the strength loss relative to 25 °C is 61.31 % (under) versus 52.85 % (after) \[Yin 2016, pp. 5-6\].
3. The peak strain of granite loaded under high temperature begins to rise immediately by 20.25 % at 100 °C, whereas after high temperature treatment peak strain remains essentially unchanged up to 400 °C \[Yin 2016, pp. 6-7\].
4. Elastic modulus declines more rapidly for granite under high temperature; from 25 °C to 200 °C the reduction is 50.92 % (under) vs. 40.98 % (after) \[Yin 2016, pp. 6-7\].
5. Above 100 °C, the failure mode transforms from splitting to shear (and ultimately conjugate shear) when granite is loaded at high temperature, while after thermal treatment the failure mode remains splitting (though with evolving failure‑plane geometry) up to 800 °C \[Yin 2016, pp. 7-9\].
6. Thermal damage $D(T) = 1 - E_T/E_0$ is systematically larger for under‑high‑temperature conditions: at 800 °C, $D = 0.811$ (under) versus $D = 0.724$ (after) \[Yin 2016, pp. 9-11\].
7. The quartz α→β transition at ~575 °C contributes to a marked acceleration of crack growth, volumetric expansion, and stiffness loss in both thermal scenarios, but the effect is more pronounced when loading occurs at high temperature \[Yin 2016, pp. 3-5, 5‑6\].
8. The difference in mechanical response is attributed not only to pre‑existing thermal cracks (after treatment) but also to concurrent thermal stress, mineral softening, and enhanced fissure propagation under high temperature \[Yin 2016, pp. 5-6, 7‑9\].

*These claims are conditioned on the specific granite composition, uniaxial loading, a heating rate of 2 °C/min, a 2‑h soak, and the temperature range 25–800 °C. Extrapolation beyond these boundaries is not supported.*

## Connections To Other Work

- The study explicitly builds on earlier investigations of temperature effects on rock, citing that many works focused on after‑treatment mechanical properties but “barely came down to static mechanical properties of rocks under high temperature” \[Yin 2016, pp. 2-3\]. It references CHEN et al. \[18\] (heated granite: decreasing peak stress and elastic modulus, increasing peak strain), LIU & XU \[21\] (thermal treatment biotite granite), and ZHAO et al. \[16\] (development of a high‑temperature triaxial system) as foundational work.
- The observed influence of the quartz α–β transition at 575 °C echoes findings in other high‑temperature rock mechanics studies (e.g., those on sandstones, marbles) where that phase change causes marked property degradation.
- The damage concept $D(T)=1-E_T/E_0$ is a common continuum‑damage‑mechanics approach; the paper’s damage values could be compared with those obtained for other granites or heating rates in the literature.
- The paper’s static results complement dynamic property studies on thermally treated rocks (e.g., LIU & XU \[5\], YIN et al. \[14,15\]), highlighting that static parameters also differ significantly between after‑treatment and under‑temperature conditions.
- The note that thermal stress under high temperature “can be simply considered as the confining pressure” albeit with different mechanical consequences \[Yin 2016, pp. 7-9\] connects to triaxial investigations where confining stress increases strength; here the opposite occurs, suggesting a need for a more nuanced model of thermal–mechanical coupling.

## Open Questions

- How exactly does the thermal stress field under high temperature alter the fracture path compared to an equivalent external confining pressure, given that the paper notes “the thermal stress … is different from the confining pressure” and both accelerates crack growth and softens minerals \[Yin 2016, pp. 7-9\]?  
- What are the microstructural mechanisms (crack density, crack orientation, grain‑boundary opening) that cause the difference in brittle‑ductile transition temperature between the two conditions?  
- Is the observed change in failure mode from splitting to shear at ~100 °C under high temperature a general feature for other granites or crystalline rocks, and does it depend on heating rate, fluid content, or confining stress?  
- How would the damage evolution quantified by elastic modulus compare with direct measurements of crack density (e.g., from X‑ray CT or acoustic emission) for the two thermal scenarios?  
- Can the empirical fitting formulas (quadratic for peak stress, peak strain, etc.) be extrapolated to temperatures >800 °C or to cyclic thermal loading?  

*These questions are directly motivated by the findings and limitations reported in the paper; the paper itself does not propose further experimental designs to resolve them.*
