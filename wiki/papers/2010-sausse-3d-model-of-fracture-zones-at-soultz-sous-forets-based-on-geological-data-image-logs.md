# 3D Model of Fracture Zones at Soultz-sous-Forets Based on Geological Data, Image Logs, Induced Microseismicity and Vertical Seismic Profiles.

## One-line Summary
This study presents a new deterministic 3D model of the main fracture zones in the Soultz-sous-Forêts granitic geothermal reservoir, constructed by integrating well-logging data, induced microseismicity, and vertical seismic profiles to characterize 53 structures for improved reservoir understanding.

## Research Question
The objective is to build a 3D network of the main permeable fractures within the target rock volume to connect the wells, achieve a better understanding and prediction of the hydraulic response of the granite, and improve the efficiency of the geothermal exchanger [Sausse 2010, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Soultz-sous-Forêts Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) site in the Upper Rhine Graben, France. Data were compiled from six wells: the geothermal wells GPK2, GPK3, GPK4 (extending to >5000 m depth), the observation wells GPK1 (3600 m deep) and EPS1 (fully cored to 2230 m), and the shallow well 4550 (1400 m deep) [Sausse 2010, pp. 1-2]. The integrated dataset includes geological data from cores and cuttings, well-logging data (especially Ultrasonic Borehole Imager - UBI), flow logs from hydraulic tests, induced microseismicity recordings from hydraulic stimulations of GPK2 (2000), GPK3 (2003), and GPK4 (2004-2005), and Vertical Seismic Profile (VSP) data from surveys in EPS1 and GPK1 [Sausse 2010, pp. 1-2, 2-3, 5-6].

## Methods
The 3D model was built using a deterministic Discrete Fracture Network (DFN) approach. Fracture zones identified from UBI logs were represented as elliptical discs in the gOcad modeling platform using the FractCar plug-in, characterized by orientation (dip direction and dip), location (well intersection depth), and extension (major and minor axes) [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6, 7-9]. Fracture extensions were initially weighted by the importance of fluid loss observed in flow logs, with maximum extensions fixed at 600 m to avoid artificial connections between wells unless supported by data [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6]. Microseismic event clouds were analyzed using 3D grid clustering and best-fit ellipsoid functions in gOcad to define anisotropic microseismic structures [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6, 7-9]. VSP data, interpreted through ray path modeling and polarity analysis, identified reflectors corresponding to dipping fault zones [Sausse 2010, pp. 4-5]. The final model integrates and correlates structures from all three data sources.

## Key Findings
The final 3D model comprises 53 main structures: 39 fracture zones from well-logging, 8 microseismic structures, and 6 structures derived from VSP [Sausse 2010, pp. 1-2, 14-15]. A major first-order discontinuity, fault zone GPK3-FZ4770, was identified, intersecting GPK3 at 4775 m depth with an orientation consistent with NNW-SSE Rhine Graben tectonics and a final adjusted dip of 71°W [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6, 9-10]. This structure was validated by its correlation with high-magnitude microseismic events from GPK3 and GPK4 stimulations and its intersection with GPK2 at a known casing collapse depth [Sausse 2010, pp. 9-10, 10-12]. The model illustrates the complexity of 3D fracture zone correlation between meter-scale borehole data and kilometer-scale geophysical imaging [Sausse 2010, pp. 1-2]. Tracer tests indicated two hydraulic circulation loops between the wells, which the model helps explain through the geometry of major faults like GPK3-FZ4770 [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6].

## Limitations
The model is based on the construction of smooth surfaces, which may not capture minor sinuosity, roughness, or complex geometries (steps, bends, splays) of real fault planes [Sausse 2010, pp. 12-13]. Scale-related heterogeneity is difficult to detect with sufficient confidence at the microseismic and VSP interpretation scales [Sausse 2010, pp. 12-13]. There are significant differences in resolution between methods (e.g., VSP depth resolution of tens of meters vs. UBI resolution of centimeters), making direct correlation tricky [Sausse 2010, pp. 12-13]. The quantification of fracture extensions is problematic as their full lateral extent is rarely observable; the model relies on respecting well intersections and matching with other data [Sausse 2010, pp. 12-13]. The real influence of fluid pressure on generating large microseismic events is still not fully understood [Sausse 2010, pp. 12-13].

## Assumptions / Conditions
Fracture zone extensions were initially assigned based on the relative importance of fluid loss observed in flow logs [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6]. A maximum extension of 600 m was assumed for most fractures to avoid creating connections between wells that are not observed in UBI data [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6]. The presence of first-order discontinuities (kilometric-scale faults) in the reservoir was assumed, exemplified by GPK3-FZ4770 [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6]. The orientation of GPK3-FZ4770 was adjusted from its initial UBI measurement to a final dip of 71°W to satisfy multiple criteria: fitting isolated high-magnitude microseismic events, matching technical observations (casing collapse at GPK2 intersection), and aligning with hydraulic and structural data [Sausse 2010, pp. 9-10]. VSP structures without precise azimuth were matched with UBI-derived fracture zones at similar depths [Sausse 2010, pp. 12-13].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Fracture orientation (dip direction and dip)
- Fracture extension (major axis `dv` and minor axis `dh`)
- Fracture location (Measured Depth - MD at well intersection)
- Fluid loss percentage from flow logs
- Microseismic event location (X, Y, Z coordinates) and magnitude
- VSP reflector depth and orientation
- Hydraulic connectivity (injectivity, tracer test loops)

## Reusable Claims
- In crystalline rock EGS reservoirs, integrating borehole-scale fracture data from UBI logs with reservoir-scale data from induced microseismicity and VSP is essential for constructing a realistic 3D structural model [Sausse 2010, pp. 1-2, 14-15].
- The orientation of major fault zones derived from borehole imagery may require adjustment (e.g., dip correction) to reconcile with microseismic event distributions and other geophysical data at the reservoir scale [Sausse 2010, pp. 9-10].
- High-magnitude induced microseismic events often correlate spatially with major pre-existing fault zones, suggesting these structures act as primary conduits for fluid pressure propagation during stimulation [Sausse 2010, pp. 9-10, 10-12].
- The geometry of large-scale fault zones (e.g., GPK3-FZ4770) can explain observed hydraulic behaviors, such as the existence of distinct fast and slow circulation loops between wells, based on their intersection or proximity to wellbores [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6].
- Deterministic DFN modeling using elliptical discs is a practical approach for representing major fracture zones in a 3D geothermal reservoir model when constrained by multiple data sources [Sausse 2010, pp. 5-6, 7-9].

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds upon and integrates extensive prior geological and geophysical investigations at Soultz-sous-Forêts, including core analysis [Genter 1989; Genter and Traineau 1992, 1996], well-logging studies [Dezayes et al. 2005, 2010; Sausse and Genter 2005], microseismicity analysis [Cuenot et al. 2007, 2008; Dorbath et al. 2009], and VSP interpretation [Place et al. 2007]. It references a regional 3D geological model of the Soultz horst in the sediments [Renard and Courrioux 1994; Caste´ra et al. 2008] but notes a lack of direct link, possibly due to resolution differences or decoupling of deformation between sediments and basement [Sausse 2010, pp. 13-14]. The work is part of the European EGS Pilot Plant project.

## Open Questions
- Is the absence of a direct link between the sedimentary regional model and the basement reservoir model due solely to resolution differences, or does it indicate a decoupling of stress and strain between the sedimentary cover and the granitic basement? [Sausse 2010, pp. 13-14].
- Can the 3D model be improved by integrating the results of the 2007 VSP survey in GPK2 and GPK3? [Sausse 2010, pp. 12-13].
- How can the complex geometry of real faults (steps, bends, splays) be better represented in deterministic 3D models? [Sausse 2010, pp. 12-13].
- What is the precise mechanism linking fluid pressure propagation to the generation of large microseismic events on fault planes? [Sausse 2010, pp. 12-13].
