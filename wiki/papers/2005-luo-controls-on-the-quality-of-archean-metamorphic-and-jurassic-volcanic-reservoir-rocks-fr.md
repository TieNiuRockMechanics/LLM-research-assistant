# Controls on the Quality of Archean Metamorphic and Jurassic Volcanic Reservoir Rocks from the Xinglongtai Buried Hill, Western Depression of Liaohe Basin, China.

## One-line Summary
This study demonstrates that reservoir quality in Archean metamorphic and Jurassic volcanic rocks of the Xinglongtai buried hill (Liaohe basin, China) is primarily controlled by burial-thermal diagenesis (including compaction, cementation, and dissolution), paleogeomorphology, and the nature of overlying strata, which together govern the development and preservation of fractures, dissolution voids, and weathering fissures [Luo 2005, pp. 1-2; 24-26].

## Research Question
What are the petrological, lithological, and diagenetic controls on the evolution and distribution of reservoir quality in Archean metamorphic and Jurassic volcanic rocks in the Xinglongtai buried hill? [Luo 2005, pp. 3-3].

## Study Area / Data
- **Location:** Xinglongtai buried hill, western depression of Liaohe basin, northeastern China [Luo 2005, pp. 1-2; 3-3].
- **Geological setting:** A Tertiary intracratonic rift basin with multiple Mesozoic–Cenozoic volcanic episodes; the buried hill is bounded by the Panshan and Chenjia sags, the Maquanzi buried hill, and the Lengdong fault [Luo 2005, pp. 3-3; 3-6].
- **Reservoir rocks:** Archean biotite plagiogneiss and migmatite; Jurassic volcanic and volcaniclastic rocks interbedded with fluvial clastics, unconformably overlain by Paleogene source rocks (Shahejie and Dongying formations) [Luo 2005, pp. 3-6; 6-8].
- **Data extent:** 30 wells drilled, 8 cored (total core length 150.04 m), commercial production from 8 volcanic and 8 metamorphic reservoirs; cumulative oil output ~1.97×10⁶ m³ and gas output 126×10⁸ m³ up to 1998 [Luo 2005, pp. 3-3].

## Methods
- **Well logging:** 30 wells logged at 1:200 scale for stratigraphic and lithological architecture [Luo 2005, pp. 26-27].
- **Core and thin-section petrography:** 150 samples vacuum‑impregnated and examined with a standard petrographic microscope [Luo 2005, pp. 26-27].
- **Petrophysical measurements:** 51 helium porosity and permeability analyses on core plugs from 7 wells [Luo 2005, pp. 26-27].
- **Scanning electron microscopy (SEM):** 24 samples coated with gold, examined with a JEOL JSM-T330 [Luo 2005, pp. 26-27].
- **Electron microprobe analysis:** 8 polished thin sections coated with carbon; Cameca Camebax BX50 instrument [Luo 2005, pp. 26-27].
- **Stable isotope geochemistry:** Carbon and oxygen isotope analyses of carbonate cements on 20 samples [Luo 2005, pp. 17-19; 26-27].
- **Whole-rock geochemistry:** Major, trace, and rare-earth elements analysed on 21 volcanic rock samples [Luo 2005, pp. 26-27].

## Key Findings
1. The Archean metamorphic reservoir is vertically zoned into a top weathered zone (porosity 3.0–9.2%, permeability 0.04–24.9 md), a middle fracture zone (porosity 3.1–10.8%, permeability 0.1–70.4 md), and a lower tight zone (porosity avg. 3.3%, permeability <0.1 md). Log responses sharply differentiate these zones [Luo 2005, pp. 3-6; 6-8].
2. Four Jurassic volcanic lithofacies – pyroclastics, lavas, volcaniclastics, and volcaniclastic-epiclastic rocks – exhibit distinct pore types (vesicles, fractures, dissolution voids) and variable reservoir quality. Pyroclastics and volcaniclastic-epiclastic rocks show the best permeability (avg. 25.6 md and 23.5 md, respectively) [Luo 2005, pp. 8-10; 10-12; 12-14; 14-15].
3. Burial-thermal diagenesis is the primary control on reservoir evolution. Eogenetic compaction (biotite alteration) destroyed primary porosity, but early carbonate and zeolite cements limited further compaction. Mesogenetic dissolution and meteoric leaching during uplift generated secondary porosity, the major porosity‑enhancing process [Luo 2005, pp. 1-2; 21-23; 23-24].
4. Paleogeomorphology strongly influences reservoir quality: rocks in paleohighs and adjacent transitional areas have enhanced properties due to more extensive weathering and vug/fissure development, while paleolows suffer from pore infilling and limited secondary porosity [Luo 2005, pp. 8-10; 14-15].
5. The nature of overlying strata determines preservation of open fractures in the Archean weathered zone: direct cover by Jurassic volcanics or Paleogene source rocks keeps fractures open; Mesozoic mudstone cover leads to fracture filling by mud and iron oxides [Luo 2005, pp. 6-8; 24-26].

## Limitations
- The study is based on data from only 8 cored wells (total core length 150.04 m) within a limited area (~30 km²); lateral heterogeneity beyond the sampled wells is not fully captured [Luo 2005, pp. 3-3; 26-27].
- Hydrocarbon production and reserve figures are only up to the end of 1998 [Luo 2005, pp. 3-3].
- Stable isotope interpretations rely on assumed formation water compositions (δ¹⁸O_SMOW of –6‰ and –4‰) for temperature calculations [Luo 2005, pp. 23-24].
- The diagenetic paragenetic sequence is partly inferred from petrographic relationships without absolute age dating of cements.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The burial‑thermal history model assumes two main subsidence phases and a rapid uplift/erosion event in the early Miocene (27–22 Ma) [Luo 2005, pp. 21-23].
- Carbonate precipitation temperatures were estimated using the Friedman and O’Neil (1977) fractionation equation and assumed evolved formation water δ¹⁸O values [Luo 2005, pp. 23-24].
- Organic acids and CO₂ derived from maturing source rocks in the Shahejie Formation were the primary solvents for mesogenetic dissolution [Luo 2005, pp. 21-23].
- The direct contact of source rocks with reservoirs via faults is assumed to have facilitated hydrocarbon migration [Luo 2005, pp. 3-6].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Lithofacies:** pyroclastics, lavas (basalt, basaltic andesite, andesite), volcaniclastics, volcaniclastic‑epiclastic rocks [Luo 2005, pp. 8-10].
- **Pore types:** vesicles, interbreccia pores, cooling fractures, intergranular/intragranular pores, dissolution voids, structural fractures, weathering fissures [Luo 2005, pp. 10-12].
- **Porosity** (helium, %): ranges for each lithofacies and zone as listed in Core Evidence Table.
- **Permeability** (Klinkenberg‑corrected air, md): ranges as above.
- **Log responses:** SP (mv), GR (API), AC (µs/m), RT (ohm·m), DEN (g/cm³) for differentiating reservoir zones and lithofacies [Luo 2005, pp. 12-14; Tables 1 and 5].
- **Carbonate cement composition:** mol% Ca, Mg, Fe, Mn; δ¹³C_PDB and δ¹⁸O_PDB values [Luo 2005, pp. 17-19; 21-23].
- **Burial depths:** eodiagenesis <1.3 km, mesodiagenesis 2.1–2.8 km [Luo 2005, pp. 21-23].
- **Timing of diagenetic events:** eodiagenesis (90–41.5 Ma), mesodiagenesis (41.5–3 Ma), uplift (27–22 Ma), hydrocarbon migration (35–27 Ma and 24–3 Ma) [Luo 2005, pp. 21-23].
- **Paleogeomorphology:** paleohigh vs. paleolow positions [Luo 2005, pp. 8-10; 14-15].

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** In volcanic and metamorphic reservoirs, burial‑thermal diagenesis, especially dissolution during mesodiagenesis and meteoric leaching during uplift, is the principal process enhancing reservoir quality by generating secondary porosity. [Luo 2005, pp. 1-2; 23-24; 24-26]
- **Claim:** The presence of early (eogenetic) carbonate and zeolite cements can limit mechanical compaction in volcanic rocks, thereby preserving a framework for later secondary porosity development. [Luo 2005, pp. 1-2; 23-24]
- **Claim:** Biotite alteration during shallow burial (eodiagenesis) leads to expansion and pore‑throat blockage, significantly reducing primary intergranular porosity in volcaniclastic rocks. [Luo 2005, pp. 1-2; 23-24]
- **Claim:** Chlorite grain rims inhibit quartz overgrowths and thus help retain primary intergranular porosity in volcanic horizons. [Luo 2005, pp. 23-24]
- **Claim:** The quality of Archean fracture/weathered zones is highly dependent on the lithology of immediately overlying strata: direct cover by volcanic or source rocks keeps fractures open, whereas mudstone cover results in infilling by mud and iron oxides. [Luo 2005, pp. 6-8; 24-26]
- **Claim:** Volcanic reservoir rock properties are closely tied to lithofacies; pyroclastic and volcaniclastic‑epiclastic facies typically exhibit higher permeability due to interconnected primary and secondary pore systems, while massive lavas may have high porosity but low permeability if vesicles are isolated. [Luo 2005, pp. 8-10; 14-15]
- **Claim:** Paleogeomorphic highs experience longer and more intense weathering, leading to greater development of vugs and fissures, and consequently better reservoir quality, compared to paleolows where pores are often filled by detrital material. [Luo 2005, pp. 8-10; 14-15; 24-26]
- **Claim:** Faults that link source rocks in adjacent sags to buried‑hill reservoirs act as effective hydrocarbon migration pathways when they form contemporaneously with hydrocarbon generation. [Luo 2005, pp. 3-6; 21-23]
- **Claim:** Carbonate cements in volcanic reservoirs show distinct paragenetic sequences: early microcrystalline calcite/dolomite (eogenetic), followed by blocky sparite (mesogenetic), and late calcite veins; their isotopic compositions (δ¹³C, δ¹⁸O) record progressive burial and fluid evolution. [Luo 2005, pp. 15-17; 17-19; 23-24]

## Open Questions
- How transferable are the observed diagenetic controls to other buried‑hill volcanic/metamorphic fields in different tectonic settings? Not confirmed.
- What is the precise relative contribution of organic acids versus hydrothermal fluids to mesogenetic dissolution? The paper suggests both, but no quantitative partitioning is provided [Luo 2005, pp. 21-23].
- The role of subvolcanic facies (nonreservoir diabase) as a possible seal or baffle is mentioned but not elaborated [Luo 2005, pp. 8-10].
- Further work could integrate seismic attributes to predict lateral distribution of fracture zones and weathered zones, as hinted by references to seismic‑volcanic prediction [Luo 2005, pp. 27-28].
- The long‑term production behavior and ultimate recovery factors for the different lithofacies remain unexplored in this paper.
