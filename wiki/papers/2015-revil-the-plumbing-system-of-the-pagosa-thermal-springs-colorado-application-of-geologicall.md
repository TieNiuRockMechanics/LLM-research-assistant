# The Plumbing System of the Pagosa Thermal Springs, Colorado: Application of Geologically Constrained Geophysical Inversion and Data Fusion.

## One-line Summary
This study uses geologically constrained geophysical inversion and data fusion of DC resistivity, self-potential, and seismic data to map the fault-controlled plumbing system of the Pagosa Thermal Springs, identifying key faults and fluid flow pathways.

## Research Question
The research aims to determine the plumbing system of the Pagosa Thermal Springs, specifically identifying the position of the geothermal reservoir (if any) and the major tectonic faults controlling the flow of thermal water in the area, which is obscured by the surface Mancos Shale [Revil 2015, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
The study area is Pagosa Springs, Colorado, located approximately 325 km southeast of Denver and 30 km north of the New Mexico border [Revil 2015, pp. 1-3]. The Big Spring (Great Pagosa Hot Spring) has a surface temperature of ~58 °C and a flow rate up to 1 cubic meter per minute, with a conduit depth greater than 305 m, making it the deepest geothermal hot spring in the world [Revil 2015, pp. 1-3]. Data were collected during geophysical surveys in May 2012, October 2012, and May 2013, resulting in twenty-two DC electrical resistivity profiles and 1317 self-potential measurements [Revil 2015, pp. 8-10]. Additional data include a vintage seismic profile from 1977 [Revil 2015, pp. 6-7] and geological information from wells O-2 and P-1 [Revil 2015, pp. 4-6].

## Methods
The study employs three geophysical methods: DC resistivity tomography, self-potential measurements, and seismic reinterpretation [Revil 2015, pp. 1-1]. DC resistivity data were acquired using an ABEM Terrameter SAS 4000 with a Wenner-α array [Revil 2015, pp. 8-10]. Self-potential measurements used non-polarizing electrodes and a high-impedance multimeter [Revil 2015, pp. 8-10]. The methodology involves geologically constrained geophysical inversion, where a geological cross-section guides the inversion of resistivity data using image-guided inversion and interpolation [Revil 2015, pp. 10-10]. Data fusion with scaled attributes is used to combine self-potential and resistivity tomograms to define a Normalized Geothermal Index (NGI) [Revil 2015, pp. 10-11].

## Key Findings
The Big Spring and a warm spring 1.8 km south are located at the intersection of the Victoire Fault (a major normal crustal fault) and two north-northeast trending faults (Fault A and Fault B) [Revil 2015, pp. 1-1]. The Eight Miles Mesa Fault, a second major tectonic feature, is responsible for warm springs further west and south [Revil 2015, pp. 1-1]. The Dakota formation acts as a local confined aquifer for hot water, sealed by the overlying Mancos Shale and underlying Morrison formation [Revil 2015, pp. 15-17]. The geothermal heat source is likely a large concealed batholith beneath the San Juan Mountains, 60-100 km northwest, with heat transported via deep crustal faults [Revil 2015, pp. 6-7, 15-17].

## Limitations
No single geophysical method can fully characterize the plumbing system alone [Revil 2015, pp. 1-1]. The Mancos Shale complicates surface fault recognition [Revil 2015, pp. 1-1]. Permitting issues limited survey coverage in the northern section of the study area [Revil 2015, pp. 8-10]. The thermal part of the target needs in situ sampling of pore water to ascertain [Revil 2015, pp. 10-11].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The geothermal heat source is from deep circulation through crustal faults and an elevated geothermal gradient, not from young magmatic intrusions [Revil 2015, pp. 6-7]. The Dakota formation is not the geothermal reservoir but a conduit for locally upwelling hot water [Revil 2015, pp. 4-6]. The hanging walls of normal faults (e.g., Eight Mile Mesa Fault) are heavily fractured, providing permeable pathways for fluid flow [Revil 2015, pp. 11-14, 15-17].

## Reusable Claims
- The intersection of the Victoire Fault with transverse northeast-trending faults (A and B) controls the discharge location of the Big Spring and warm spring [Revil 2015, pp. 1-1, 15-17].
- The hanging wall of normal faults in the Pagosa area is heavily fractured, creating highly permeable pathways for thermal water flow [Revil 2015, pp. 11-14, 15-17].
- Combining self-potential and resistivity data through scaled attributes can define a Normalized Geothermal Index to localize hydrothermal fluid flow [Revil 2015, pp. 10-11].
- The Dakota sandstone formation acts as a confined aquifer for geothermal fluids when sealed by overlying and underlying clay-rich formations [Revil 2015, pp. 4-6, 15-17].

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on previous geological and geophysical work in the Pagosa Springs area from the 1970s and 1980s by the Colorado School of Mines [Revil 2015, pp. 1-3]. It uses techniques from image-guided inversion [Zhou et al., 2014] and data fusion with attributes [Revil et al., 2013]. It references regional tectonic studies [Brister and Chapin, 1994] and hydrogeological investigations [Galloway, 1980; Warren, 1986].

## Open Questions
- What is the exact depth and geometry of the conduit feeding the Big Spring?
- Do Faults A and B transmit hot water, or are they only structural controls for upwelling?
- What is the precise location and extent of the heat source (batholith) beneath the San Juan Mountains?
- How does the fracture network in the Dakota formation vary spatially and influence flow paths?
