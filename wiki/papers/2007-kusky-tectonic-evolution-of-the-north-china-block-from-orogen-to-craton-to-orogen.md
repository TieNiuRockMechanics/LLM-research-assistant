# Tectonic Evolution of the North China Block: From Orogen to Craton to Orogen.

## One-line Summary

The North China Craton records an Archean–present cycle from collisional orogen to stable craton to lithospheric root loss and renewed orogenic activity, driven by multiple phases of subduction, collision, and mantle hydration. [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2]

## Research Question

How did the North China Craton form, stabilize, and subsequently lose its lithospheric root, transitioning from a craton to a tectonically active orogenic region, and what are the implications for the orogen–craton–orogen cycle? [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data

The North China Craton (NCC) covers ~1.7 × 10⁶ km² in northeastern China, Inner Mongolia, the Yellow Sea, and North Korea. [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2] The craton is divided into Eastern and Western Blocks separated by the Central Orogenic Belt (COB), with the Western Block (Ordos) stable and thick-rooted and the Eastern Block currently seismically active, hot, and thin-lithosphere. [Kusky 2007, pp. 2-4] The study covers the entire craton and its margins, incorporating geological mapping, metamorphic petrology, geochronology, geochemistry, and geophysical data (gravity, seismic tomography, heat flow, and xenolith samples). [Kusky 2007, pp. 20-22, pp. 12-13, pp. 8-12]

## Methods

- Field structural and stratigraphic mapping of Precambrian basement and Phanerozoic cover [Kusky 2007, pp. 4-5, pp. 5-6]
- U–Pb zircon geochronology (SHRIMP, upper-intercept ages) on metamorphic and igneous rocks [Kusky 2007, pp. 4-5, pp. 5-6]
- Metamorphic petrology and P–T–t path determination, especially of high‑pressure granulites and UHT assemblages [Kusky 2007, pp. 5-6]
- Geochemistry (REE, isotopes) of igneous and metasedimentary rocks to infer tectonic settings [Kusky 2007, pp. 5-6]
- Mantle xenolith and garnet‑concentrate studies to reconstruct lithospheric composition, thickness, and thermal history [Kusky 2007, pp. 22-24, pp. 8-12]
- Geophysical surveys (Bouguer gravity, seismic velocity, heat flow) to map lithospheric structure [Kusky 2007, pp. 20-22]
- Regional tectonic synthesis and plate reconstructions [Kusky 2007, pp. 13-16]

## Key Findings

1. The NCC preserves one of Earth’s longest tectonic records from 3.8 Ga to the present. [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2]
2. The Eastern and Western Blocks amalgamated at 2.5 Ga through collision of a passive margin on the Eastern Block with an oceanic arc–ophiolitic assemblage, forming the 1600 km-long Central Orogenic Belt. [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2, pp. 5-6, pp. 6-7]
3. A second major collisional event at 1.85 Ga along the northern margin of the craton produced widespread granulite‑facies metamorphism, a foreland basin (Changcheng Series), and post‑collisional extension. [Kusky 2007, pp. 7-8, pp. 24-25]
4. The craton interior remained stable from ~1.8 Ga to the late Paleozoic, but thousands of kilometres of subduction beneath its northern, southern, and eastern margins hydrated and weakened the subcontinental lithospheric mantle. [Kusky 2007, pp. 13-14, pp. 24-25]
5. Lithospheric root loss occurred in the Mesozoic, likely triggered by near‑simultaneous collisions on the Solonker suture (north) and Dabie–Sulu suture (south), resulting in delamination or thermal–chemical erosion. [Kusky 2007, pp. 13-14, pp. 24-25, pp. 26-27]
6. The Eastern Block now exhibits thin lithosphere (≥80 km), high heat flow, abundant seismicity (six of the ten deadliest earthquakes), and lacks a typical cratonic mantle keel. [Kusky 2007, pp. 2-4, pp. 20-22]
7. Cretaceous widespread magmatism, gold mineralization, and metamorphic core complexes mark post‑collapse extension and asthenospheric upwelling. [Kusky 2007, pp. 22-24, pp. 16-17]

## Limitations

- The geological and tectonic histories of the NCC margins are still poorly understood due to incomplete exposure and conflicting interpretations. [Kusky 2007, pp. 8-10]
- The timing of the main collision between the Eastern and Western Blocks is debated (2.5 Ga vs. 1.85 Ga), and the mechanisms of root loss remain uncertain, with multiple possible triggers (hydration, thermal erosion, delamination). [Kusky 2007, pp. 6-7, pp. 26-27]
- Metamorphic ages show strong 1.85 Ga overprint, obscuring earlier events. [Kusky 2007, pp. 4-5]
- The exact role of ridge subduction and the Pacific plate in lithospheric thinning has not been fully analysed. [Kusky 2007, pp. 26-27]
- Correlation of the southeastern craton margin with Korea is disputed. [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2]

## Assumptions / Conditions

- The orogen–craton–orogen cycle is a recurring Earth process and the NCC is a modern analogue. [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2, pp. 27-28]
- Subduction‑released fluids hydrate the mantle wedge, weakening the lithospheric mantle and promoting delamination. [Kusky 2007, pp. 13-14]
- Negative buoyancy from eclogite‑facis transformation in the lower crust drives lithospheric foundering. [Kusky 2007, pp. 24-25]
- The geochemical signature of Mesozoic magmas (adakites) records crustal foundering. [Kusky 2007, pp. 24-25]
- Geophysical gradients (gravity, seismic velocity) faithfully reflect lithospheric thickness. [Kusky 2007, pp. 20-22]

## Key Variables / Parameters

- Lithospheric thickness (seismic low‑velocity zone depth): >170 km (Ordovician) → ~80 km (present) [Kusky 2007, pp. 22-24]
- Heat flow: Low in Western Block, high in Eastern Block [Kusky 2007, pp. 2-4, pp. 20-22]
- Metamorphic P–T conditions: HP granulites in Hengshan: 1.2–0.9 GPa, 700–800 °C; UHT granulites: 975 °C at 9 kbar [Kusky 2007, pp. 5-6, pp. 7-8]
- Timing of collisions: 2.5 Ga (COB), 1.85 Ga (north margin), 250 Ma (Solonker), 230 Ma (Dabie–Sulu) [Kusky 2007, pp. 6-7, pp. 8-10, pp. 12-13]
- Tan-Lu fault sinistral displacement: ~500 km (Triassic–Jurassic) to 740 km (Early Cretaceous) [Kusky 2007, pp. 12-13]
- Cumulative subducted oceanic lithosphere: ~18 000 km [Kusky 2007, pp. 26-27]

## Reusable Claims

- The North China Craton contains one of the longest, most complex records of magmatism, sedimentation, and deformation on Earth, spanning 3.8 Ga to present. [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2]
- The Central Orogenic Belt is an Archaean (2.5 Ga) orogen preserving remnants of oceanic basins that closed between the Eastern and Western Blocks. [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2, pp. 4-5]
- All rocks >2.5 Ga in the NCC underwent 2.5 Ga metamorphism and were intruded by 2.5–2.45 Ga granites and mafic dykes. [Kusky 2007, pp. 1-2]
- A 1.85 Ga Himalayan‑style collision along the northern margin overprinted the craton, producing widespread granulite‑facies metamorphism and a south‑prograding foreland basin. [Kusky 2007, pp. 7-8, pp. 24-25]
- The Eastern Block currently has thin lithosphere (~80 km), high heat flow, and frequent large earthquakes, lacking a typical cratonic mantle root. [Kusky 2007, pp. 20-22]
- Mantle xenoliths show that a thick (~180 km) Archaean lithospheric keel existed in the Ordovician, but was lost by the Mesozoic. [Kusky 2007, pp. 22-24]
- Long‑lived subduction beneath the craton margins hydrated and weakened the subcontinental lithospheric mantle, facilitating delamination. [Kusky 2007, pp. 13-14, pp. 26-27]
- Mesozoic gold deposits and granitoids in the Eastern Block form a ring corresponding to the area of root loss. [Kusky 2007, pp. 22-24]
- The Late Jurassic Yanshanian orogeny reflects three‑sided convergence (northern Solonker closure, southern Dabie collision, eastern Pacific subduction). [Kusky 2007, pp. 12-13, pp. 16-17]

## Connections To Other Work

- Zhao et al. (2001a, 2006) propose a 1.85 Ga “Trans North China Orogen” collision, while this work argues for a 2.5 Ga “Central Orogenic Belt” collision, with 1.85 Ga event tied to the northern margin. [Kusky 2007, pp. 4-5, pp. 6-7]
- Zhai (2004) suggests the 1.8 Ga event is intra‑cratonic plume‑related uplift, not a collision. [Kusky 2007, pp. 7-8]
- Maruyama et al. (2004) and Komiya & Maruyama (2006) advocate double subduction hydration as a cause of mantle weakening. [Kusky 2007, pp. 13-14]
- Meissner & Mooney (1998) and Marotta et al. (1998) provide physical models for lithospheric root detachment. [Kusky 2007, pp. 24-25]
- Gao et al. (1998a,b) use geochemical evidence (Eu anomalies) to suggest eclogite delamination from the lower crust. [Kusky 2007, pp. 24-25]
- Xiao et al. (2003) describe the Solonker suture as a Permian closure marking the end of the Central Asian Orogenic Belt. [Kusky 2007, pp. 12-13]
- The Dabie–Sulu ultrahigh‑pressure belt results from Triassic continental collision (e.g., Okay & Sengör 1992; Yin & Nie 1993). [Kusky 2007, pp. 13-14]

## Open Questions

- What was the exact timing and nature of the amalgamation of the NCC micro‑blocks? The 2.5 Ga vs. 1.85 Ga debate continues. [Kusky 2007, pp. 2-4, pp. 6-7]
- What mechanism(s) triggered the loss of the lithospheric root: hydration‑induced delamination, thermal‑chemical erosion, or a combination? [Kusky 2007, pp. 24-25, pp. 26-27]
- What is the role of the Pacific ridge subduction event in contributing heat or fluids to the root loss? [Kusky 2007, pp. 26-27]
- Can the orogen–craton–orogen cycle be extrapolated to other cratons, and how many cratons may have been “decommissioned” and recycled into younger orogens? [Kusky 2007, pp. 27-28]
- How does the spatial extent of lithospheric thinning in the Eastern Block relate to the distribution of Mesozoic gold deposits and granitoids? [Kusky 2007, pp. 22-24]
- What are the detailed relationships between Palaeoproterozoic volcanosedimentary groups (e.g., Liaohe) and the cratonic blocks, and what tectonic settings do they represent? Very little structural work exists. [Kusky 2007, pp. 7-8]
