# Injection-Induced Shear Slip and Permeability Enhancement in Granite Fractures.

## One-line Summary
Injection-induced dilatant shear slip in rough granite fractures leads to retainable permeability enhancement through self-propping by asperities, while smooth fractures show minimal and non-retainable increases.

## Research Question
How do the coupled hydro-mechanical responses and permeability evolve during injection-induced shear slip in granite fractures, and what is the fundamental mechanism for permeability enhancement in shear stimulation? [Ye 2018, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
The study used laboratory experiments on cylindrical samples of Sierra White granite. The granite's mechanical properties were: Young’s modulus (E=67 GPa), Poisson’s ratio (ν=0.32), uniaxial compressive strength (UCS=150 MPa), internal friction angle (ϕ=46º), tensile strength (T=11 MPa), and matrix permeability of 500-1000 nanodarcy. The granite consisted mainly of Quartz (43.5%) and Albite (46.1%). [Ye 2018, pp. 8-10]

Four samples were prepared, each containing a single fracture inclined at ~30 degrees to the sample axis:
*   **SW-T1 & SW-T2:** Tensile fractures induced by mechanical splitting (very rough, JRC ~15).
*   **SW-S3:** A saw-cut fracture (rough, JRC ~2).
*   **SW-S4:** A polished saw-cut fracture (smooth, JRC ~1.2). [Ye 2018, pp. 8-12, pp. 43-54]

## Methods
A novel triaxial shear test with injection was conducted using an MTS 816 frame. The experimental setup involved:
1.  **Sample Preparation:** Cylindrical granite cores with a single inclined fracture were prepared. Two vertical boreholes were drilled to the fracture to create an inlet and outlet for fluid flow. [Ye 2018, pp. 12-14]
2.  **Fracture Characterization:** A 3D laser scanner measured surface topography before and after testing. The Joint Roughness Coefficient (JRC) was estimated using the Z2 parameter and the empirical correlation JRC = 61.79 × Z2 - 3.47. [Ye 2018, pp. 10-12]
3.  **Experimental Control:** Tests used **constant piston displacement control**. A confining pressure (σ3) of 30 MPa was applied. The sample was loaded to a near-critical differential stress. Injection pressure (Pi) was then increased stepwise from 5 MPa to 28 MPa to induce slip, and subsequently decreased stepwise to 8 MPa. Production pressure (Po) was held constant at 5 MPa. [Ye 2018, pp. 14-18]
4.  **Measurements:** Axial and radial displacements (LVDTs), axial load, injection/production pressures, and flow rates were recorded. Steady-state flow rates were used to evaluate permeability. [Ye 2018, pp. 14-18]
5.  **Permeability Evaluation:** Fracture permeability (k) was calculated using the cubic law: k = a_h^2 / 12, where the equivalent hydraulic aperture (a_h) was derived from steady-state flow using Darcy's law: a_h = (-12μLQ / (WΔP))^(1/3). The flow region was simplified as a rectangle with the same area as the elliptical fracture surface. [Ye 2018, pp. 18-21]

## Key Findings
1.  **Retainable Permeability Enhancement:** Rough fractures (SW-T1, SW-T2, SW-S3) showed significant, retainable permeability enhancement after shearing. Permeability increased ~6x (SW-T1), ~5x (SW-T2), and ~3x (SW-S3) during slip and remained elevated during pressure unloading (average retention ratios of 5, 4, and 2, respectively). The smooth fracture (SW-S4) showed only a ~2x increase that was not retained (retention ratio ~1). [Ye 2018, pp. 21-25]
2.  **Hydro-Mechanical Coupling:** In the "stick" state, flow rate increase was primarily due to aperture opening from decreased effective normal stress. In the "slip" state, the major contributor to permeability enhancement was irreversible normal dilation caused by shear slip. [Ye 2018, pp. 18-21, pp. 25-27]
3.  **Slip Characteristics:** Fracture slip exhibited a "stick-slip" behavior. For rough fractures, slip occurred in intervals: a first quasi-static slip (velocity ~10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁶ m/s), a dynamic slip (velocity ~10⁻⁵ m/s), and a second quasi-static slip. The smooth fracture (SW-S4) slipped gradually at lower velocities (~10⁻⁹ to 10⁻⁷ m/s). [Ye 2018, pp. 27-29]
4.  **Stress Relaxation:** Fracture slip correlated with stress relaxation. Faster slip induced quicker stress relaxation. Dynamic slip intervals in rough fractures were associated with rapid stress drop rates (~7 to 20 MPa/s). [Ye 2018, pp. 27-29]
5.  **Asperity Degradation:** Rough tensile fractures (SW-T1, SW-T2) showed significant asperity degradation, slickensides, and gouge production after shearing. JRC values decreased (e.g., SW-T1 from 15.32 to 10.21). Smooth saw-cut fractures showed only minor surface damage. [Ye 2018, pp. 29-31]

## Limitations
1.  The fluid flow region was simplified as a rectangle for permeability calculation, which does not perfectly represent the actual elliptical flow path. [Ye 2018, pp. 18-21]
2.  The deformation of the rock matrix was neglected in calculating fracture dilation and slip, which may lead to an underestimation of fracture deformation. [Ye 2018, pp. 14-16]
3.  The study was conducted at laboratory scale on single fractures; direct scaling to field-scale, complex fracture networks is not confirmed. [Ye 2018, pp. 3-5]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Fracture flow follows the cubic law for parallel plates. [Ye 2018, pp. 18-21]
2.  The matrix permeability of Sierra White granite is negligible compared to fracture permeability. [Ye 2018, pp. 18-21]
3.  The Joint Matching Coefficient (JMC) of the prepared fractures was close to or equal to 1.0. [Ye 2018, pp. 10-12]
4.  Pore pressure (Pp) is estimated as the average of injection and production pressures. [Ye 2018, pp. 12-14]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Injection Pressure (Pi):** Increased stepwise to induce shear slip.
*   **Effective Normal Stress (σn'):** Decreased with increasing pore pressure, controlling aperture in the "stick" state.
*   **Shear Stress (τ):** Dependent on differential stress and fracture angle.
*   **Shear Slip (ds):** Displacement along the fracture plane.
*   **Normal Dilation (dn):** Opening of the fracture perpendicular to its plane.
*   **Joint Roughness Coefficient (JRC):** Quantifies fracture surface roughness.
*   **Steady-State Flow Rate (Q):** Used to calculate equivalent hydraulic aperture and permeability.
*   **Fracture Permeability (k):** Evaluated from hydraulic aperture using the cubic law.

## Reusable Claims
*   **Condition:** For rough granite fractures (JRC > ~2) under triaxial stress. **Claim:** Injection-induced dilatant shear slip leads to a significant (3x to 6x) and retainable increase in fracture permeability due to self-propping by degraded asperities. [Ye 2018, pp. 21-25, pp. 31-33]
*   **Condition:** During injection-induced shear slip in fractures. **Claim:** The process exhibits "stick-slip" behavior, where the "slip" state is characterized by irreversible normal dilation that is the primary contributor to permeability enhancement, not just effective normal stress reduction. [Ye 2018, pp. 18-21, pp. 25-27]
*   **Condition:** For rough fractures during dynamic slip intervals. **Claim:** Faster fracture slip velocities (~10⁻⁵ m/s) correlate with rapid stress relaxation rates (~7-20 MPa/s), indicating large energy release potentially linked to induced seismicity. [Ye 2018, pp. 27-29]
*   **Condition:** For smooth, polished fractures (low JRC). **Claim:** Injection-induced shear slip results in minimal, non-retainable permeability enhancement due to insufficient asperities for self-propping. [Ye 2018, pp. 21-25, pp. 31-33]

## Connections To Other Work
*   Contrasts with direct shear tests and manually displaced fracture tests, which do not capture true injection-induced slip and may overestimate flow enhancement. [Ye 2018, pp. 3-5]
*   Builds upon prior triaxial-injection shear tests on saw-cut fractures (e.g., Nemoto et al., 2008; Bauer et al., 2016) by using both rough tensile and saw-cut fractures, measuring steady-state flow, and studying permeability retention. [Ye 2018, pp. 5-8]
*   The observed slip velocities (~10⁻⁶ to 10⁻⁵ m/s) are consistent with field-scale measurements from Guglielmi et al. (2015a). [Ye 2018, pp. 27-29]
*   The study addresses the theoretical understanding of shear stimulation for EGS, where fracture self-propping is viewed as key. [Ye 2018, pp. 1-3]
