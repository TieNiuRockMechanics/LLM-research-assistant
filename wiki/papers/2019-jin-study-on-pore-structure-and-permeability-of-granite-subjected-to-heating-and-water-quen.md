# Study on Pore Structure and Permeability of Granite Subjected to Heating and Water Quenching.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the changes in pore structure and permeability of granite after heating to temperatures between 20°C and 600°C followed by water quenching, finding an exponential increase in both properties with a critical threshold between 500°C and 600°C.

## Research Question
To obtain the pore characteristics and permeability change law of water-quenched granite subjected to different temperatures from 20°C to 600°C [Jin 2019, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The rock samples are medium-fine grained monzogranite collected from Rizhao, Shandong, China. The main mineral composition (by volume fraction) is: plagioclase 35%, K-feldspar 40%-45%, quartz 20%-25%, biotite 3%-5%, and a small amount of amphibole. At room temperature, the bulk density is 2637 kg/m³, longitudinal wave velocity is 4087 m/s, uniaxial compressive strength is 130 MPa, and elastic modulus is 13 GPa [Jin 2019, pp. 2-4].

## Methods
1.  **Thermal Treatment:** Samples were heated in a muffle furnace at a rate of 2°C/min to target temperatures (20, 200, 400, 500, 600°C), held for 2 hours, then rapidly quenched in water with stirring to room temperature [Jin 2019, pp. 2-4].
2.  **Pore Structure Testing:** Mercury intrusion porosimetry (MIP) was used with an AutoPore IV 9500 instrument. The measurable pore diameter range is 0.003-1000 μm. The test followed the Washburn equation with mercury surface tension of 0.485 N/m and a contact angle of 130°. Cylindrical samples were 7 mm in diameter and 15 mm in length [Jin 2019, pp. 2-4].
3.  **Permeability Testing:** The transient pulse attenuation method was used with a Smartperm III gas permeameter. Cylindrical samples were 50 mm in diameter and 100 mm in length. Permeability was calculated using equations involving upstream/downstream chamber volumes, average pore pressure, sample dimensions, and the decay coefficient [Jin 2019, pp. 2-4].
4.  **Permeability Prediction:** The Katz-Thompson (K-T) model was used to calculate predicted permeability from MIP data, using parameters like maximum hydraulic conductivity pore diameter (D_max), critical pressure pore diameter (D_c), porosity (φ), and the proportion of connected pores larger than D_max (S_Dmax) [Jin 2019, pp. 4-5].

## Key Findings
1.  With increasing temperature, the porosity and permeability of water-quenched granite increase exponentially. The temperature range of 500-600°C is the threshold interval for significant changes in both porosity and permeability [Jin 2019, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
2.  The new pores formed inside granite after high-temperature water quenching are mainly mesopores. The increase in mesopore content is the main reason for the substantial increase in porosity and permeability [Jin 2019, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
3.  The predicted permeability based on the K-T model can reflect the variation trend of the true permeability quite well [Jin 2019, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
4.  At room temperature (20°C), porosity was 0.37%. At 600°C, porosity increased to 2.45%, a 6.62-fold increase [Jin 2019, pp. 4-5].
5.  Compared to 20°C, permeability increased by an average of 15.34 times at 500°C and 80.26 times at 600°C [Jin 2019, pp. 4-5].

## Limitations
1.  The number of samples in this test was relatively small, making it impossible to derive a definitive law; more tests are needed for verification [Jin 2019, pp. 4-5].
2.  There is a certain difference between the model predicted permeability and the measured value due to experimental errors and theoretical defects from simplifying the physical model [Jin 2019, pp. 4-5].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The Katz-Thompson model is assumed to be applicable for predicting permeability from mercury intrusion data for this granite [Jin 2019, pp. 4-5].
2.  The pore structure parameters (D_c, D_max, etc.) derived from MIP are representative of the sample's transport properties [Jin 2019, pp. 4-5].
3.  The rapid water quenching process simulates a thermal shock condition relevant to engineering scenarios [Jin 2019, pp. 1-2].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Temperature (T):** 20, 200, 400, 500, 600 °C [Jin 2019, pp. 2-4].
*   **Porosity (φ):** Measured via MIP, ranging from 0.37% (20°C) to 2.45% (600°C) [Jin 2019, pp. 4-5].
*   **Permeability (k):** Measured via transient pulse method and predicted via K-T model [Jin 2019, pp. 2-5].
*   **Pore Diameter (D):** Classified as micropores, small pores, mesopores, and large pores. Key parameters include D_c (critical pressure pore diameter) and D_max (maximum hydraulic conductivity pore diameter) [Jin 2019, pp. 2-5].
*   **Cumulative Mercury Intrusion Volume (V_t):** Increased from 1.4×10⁻³ mL/g (20°C) to 9.7×10⁻³ mL/g (600°C) [Jin 2019, pp. 2-4].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For granite subjected to heating and subsequent water quenching. **Claim:** Porosity and permeability exhibit an exponential increase with heating temperature. **Limitation:** This trend is particularly pronounced above 500°C, with 500-600°C identified as a critical threshold interval [Jin 2019, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
2.  **Condition:** In granite after high-temperature water quenching. **Claim:** The dominant newly formed pore type is mesopores, and the increase in mesopore content is the primary driver for the significant enhancement in porosity and permeability. **Limitation:** The effect on micropores and small pores is minimal [Jin 2019, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
3.  **Condition:** When using mercury intrusion porosimetry data. **Claim:** The Katz-Thompson model provides permeability predictions that correlate well with measured permeability trends for thermally treated and quenched granite. **Limitation:** Absolute values may differ due to model simplifications and experimental error [Jin 2019, pp. 4-5].
