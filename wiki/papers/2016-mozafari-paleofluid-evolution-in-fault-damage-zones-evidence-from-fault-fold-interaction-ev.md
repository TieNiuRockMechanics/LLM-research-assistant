# Paleofluid Evolution in Fault-Damage Zones: Evidence from Fault-Fold Interaction Events in the Jabal Qusaybah Anticline (Adam Foothills, North Oman).

## One-line Summary
This study uses petrographic, geochemical, and microthermometric analysis of calcite veins to reconstruct the paleofluid evolution within fault-damage zones of the Jabal Qusaybah Anticline, linking distinct fluid events to four tectonic stages of fault-fold interaction.

## Research Question
How did fluid circulation and cementation within fault-damage zones evolve during the structural development of the Jabal Qusaybah Anticline, and what do these paleofluid records reveal about the connectivity and interaction of fault and fold systems?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Jabal Qusaybah Anticline, an 8-km-long, E-W-trending anticline in the Adam Foothills of North Oman [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-2]. The exposed strata are marine Cretaceous carbonates of the upper Natih Formation (middle Cenomanian–Turonian) [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-2]. Data comprise calcite veins and fault-core infillings from fault-damage zones, host limestone samples from all Natih Formation members, and structural measurements of fault systems (NE–SW left-lateral strike-slip, N–S extensional, and E–W extensional faults) [Mozafari 2016, pp. 3-5].

## Methods
The methodology integrated structural field studies with detailed laboratory analyses [Mozafari 2016, pp. 3-5]. Petrographic analysis included staining (Alizarin Red S, potassium ferricyanide), transmitted/UV-fluorescent light microscopy, and cold cathodoluminescence on 384 thin sections [Mozafari 2016, pp. 3-5]. Geochemical analyses involved stable carbon and oxygen isotope (δ¹³C, δ¹⁸O) measurements via mass spectrometry, strontium isotope (⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr) analysis via MC-ICP-MS, and trace element (Mg, Ca, Sr, Fe, Mn, Na, K, Al) quantification via ICP-OES [Mozafari 2016, pp. 3-5]. Fluid inclusion microthermometry was performed on doubly polished wafers to determine homogenization temperatures and salinities [Mozafari 2016, pp. 5-5]. Additional methods included vitrinite reflectance (VRr) measurements and X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis [Mozafari 2016, pp. 5-5].

## Key Findings
Three main groups of calcite veins were identified, each linked to a distinct tectonic stage [Mozafari 2016, pp. 5-7].
*   **Group 1 Veins (Burial/Early-Tectonic):** Formed in a closed to semi-closed fluid system from intraformational fluids along low-connectivity E–W extensional faults. Cements are nonferroan with δ¹³C values of 3.0–3.5‰ and δ¹⁸O values of -10.3 to -8.1‰ V-PDB [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-1].
*   **Group 2 Veins (Syntectonic):** Precipitated during the culmination of the anticline along NE–SW strike-slip and N–S extensional faults. Fluids evolved from modified seawater to a mixture with evaporitic brines (likely from the Ara Group), showing variable δ¹⁸O (-11.2 to 0.3‰) and salinities (3.4–12.8 eq. wt% NaCl) [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-1].
*   **Group 3 Veins (Late-Tectonic):** Associated with late fold amplification, uplift, and exhumation. Cements show higher δ¹³C (0.6–8.0‰) and δ¹⁸O (-3.6 to 1.5‰), with fluid inclusions indicating saline H₂O–NaCl–CaCl₂ brines (8.5–16 eq. wt% NaCl) and temperatures below 50°C. High δ¹³C values suggest bacterial methanogenesis or CO₂ degassing [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-1].
Fault connectivity progressively improved, allowing the migration of extraformational fluids (evaporitic brines, siliciclastic-derived fluids) late in the deformation history [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-1].

## Limitations
*   The study uses outcrop analogs, which may not perfectly represent subsurface reservoir conditions [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-2].
*   Fluid inclusion data from Group 1 veins and early Group 2 veins (BC2) are unreliable due to intense deformation twinning and potential stretching or secondary origin [Mozafari 2016, pp. 14-15].
*   The source of hydrocarbon inclusions in veins could not be confidently assigned to specific source rocks due to lack of supporting molecular data [Mozafari 2016, pp. 19-20].
*   The exact NaCl/CaCl₂ ratio in Group 3 fluid inclusions could not be determined due to small inclusion size [Mozafari 2016, pp. 14-15].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   A hydrostatic pressure gradient of 100 bar/km and a geothermal gradient of 30°C/km were used for pressure correction of fluid inclusion temperatures [Mozafari 2016, pp. 15-15].
*   Fluid systems are assumed to be H₂O–NaCl for Groups 1 and 2, and H₂O–NaCl–CaCl₂ for Group 3 [Mozafari 2016, pp. 14-15].
*   The middle to Upper Cretaceous seawater δ¹⁸O value is estimated at -1.2 to -0.5‰ V-SMOW [Mozafari 2016, pp. 15-15].
*   High δ¹³C values (>4.0‰) in Group 3 veins are interpreted as resulting from bacterial methanogenesis or CO₂ degassing [Mozafari 2016, pp. 17-17].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Geochemical:** δ¹³C, δ¹⁸O (‰ V-PDB), ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratios, trace element concentrations (Fe, Sr in ppm).
*   **Microthermometric:** Homogenization temperature (Th, °C), final ice melting temperature (Tm ice, °C), salinity (eq. wt% NaCl).
*   **Structural:** Fault orientation (NE–SW, N–S, E–W), fault type (strike-slip, extensional), vein orientation and spacing.
*   **Petrographic:** Cement type (e.g., BC1, FBC2, EC, GC), texture, staining (ferroan/nonferroan), cathodoluminescence pattern.

## Reusable Claims
*   In the Jabal Qusaybah Anticline, the interference between strike-slip and extensional fault systems during fold amplification enhanced the hydraulic conductivity of fault-damage zones, facilitating the late-stage migration of deep-sourced evaporitic brines [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-1].
*   The progressive increase in ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratios and salinity in syntectonic to late-tectonic calcite cements records the increasing contribution of evaporitic brines from the Ara Group to the fault-controlled fluid system [Mozafari 2016, pp. 17-17].
*   Bacterial methanogenesis or CO₂ degassing, inferred from highly positive δ¹³C values, occurred at shallow burial depths (<1000 m) during the final stage of fluid circulation in the anticline's fault-damage zones [Mozafari 2016, pp. 17-17].
*   Early vein cements (Group 1) precipitated from locally buffered formation waters in a closed system, while later veins (Groups 2 & 3) record the progressive opening of the system and mixing of extraformational fluids [Mozafari 2016, pp. 15-15].

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study builds on research documenting the role of fault-damage zones in fluid migration (e.g., Caine et al., 1996; Aydin, 2000; Paul et al., 2009) [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-2].
*   It uses Jabal Qusaybah as an outcrop analog for nearby subsurface fractured carbonate reservoirs like the Fahud and Natih fields [Mozafari 2016, pp. 1-2].
*   The interpretation of evaporitic brines from the Ara Group connects to work on the Huqf Supergroup in Oman (Burns et al., 1994) [Mozafari 2016, pp. 17-17].
*   The fluid evolution model relates to "fluid flushing" concepts in folds (Evans and Fischer, 2012) [Mozafari 2016, pp. 19-20].
