# Shear Behavior of Artificial and Natural Granite Fractures After Heating and Water‑Cooling Treatment.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the shear behavior of artificial and natural granite fractures from the Gonghe basin after slow heating to temperatures up to 300°C followed by rapid water-cooling, finding that thermal treatment enhances the peak shear strength of artificial fractures due to improved surface matching, while natural fractures exhibit weaker acoustic emission responses and lower shear properties.

## Research Question
What is the effect of heating and rapid water-cooling treatment on the shear behavior (strength, stiffness, dilation) and associated acoustic emission characteristics of artificial (Brazilian-induced) and natural (pre-existing) granite fractures, and what are the implications for Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) reservoir stimulation? [Chen 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study used fractured granite specimens from outcrops in the Gonghe basin, Qinghai, China, a potential region for EGS exploitation. The Gonghe granite is composed of 37% quartz, 33% microcline, 17% alkali, 8% Biotite, 3% amphibole, and 2% clinochlore by volume. Two types of specimens were prepared: 20 cubic (50 mm side) specimens containing a single Brazilian-induced artificial tensile fracture, and 12 cubic specimens containing a pre-existing natural fracture obtained via wire-electrode cutting from a larger block. The natural fracture surfaces had undergone weathering. [Chen 2020, pp. 2-3]

## Methods
1.  **Thermal Treatment:** Specimens were heated in a muffle furnace at 2°C/min to target temperatures (25°C, 150°C, 230°C, 300°C), held for 4 hours, then cooled by dipping only the fracture surface into a 25°C water tank until the specimen reached room temperature. [Chen 2020, pp. 3-5]
2.  **Surface Morphology Measurement:** A blue light 3D scanner (OKIO-5M) was used to measure fracture surface morphology before thermal treatment, after thermal treatment, and after shear. The Joint Roughness Coefficient (JRC) was calculated from the scanned data. [Chen 2020, pp. 2-3]
3.  **Direct Shear Test:** Tests were conducted using a YZW-50 apparatus under a constant normal stress of 5 MPa and a constant shear displacement rate of 0.2 mm/min until 5 mm shear displacement. [Chen 2020, pp. 3-5]
4.  **Acoustic Emission (AE) Monitoring:** Six AE sensors were attached to the specimen surfaces to capture AE signals (hits, ring down counts, energy) during shear. AE event locations were calculated. [Chen 2020, pp. 3-5]

## Key Findings
1.  **Fracture Type Comparison:** Artificial fractures have higher peak shear strength, residual shear strength, shear stiffness, and maximum shear dilation than natural fractures. Natural fractures exhibit lower surface roughness (initial average JRC of 13.5 vs. 16 for artificial). [Chen 2020, pp. 5-6]
2.  **Thermal Treatment Effect on Shear Strength:** Thermal treatment (up to 300°C) increases the peak shear strength of artificial fractures but has little influence on the shear properties of natural fractures. [Chen 2020, pp. 5-6]
3.  **Mechanism for Strength Increase:** The increase in peak shear strength for artificial fractures is attributed to better matching between the upper and lower fracture surfaces after thermal treatment, not to changes in surface roughness. [Chen 2020, pp. 11-15]
4.  **Surface Roughness Change:** The roughness variation after thermal treatment is insignificant (variation ratios between 0.95 and 1.05). Roughness generally decreases after shear, more significantly in natural fractures. [Chen 2020, pp. 5-6]
5.  **Asperity Damage:** The volume of sheared-off asperities is larger after thermal treatment for both fracture types. [Chen 2020, pp. 5-6]
6.  **AE Characteristics:** A strong correlation exists between shear stress and AE parameters. AE responses (energy, ring down counts) are significantly weaker in natural fractures. AE events concentrate near the fracture surface in artificial fractures but are dispersed throughout the specimen in natural fractures. [Chen 2020, pp. 6-7]

## Limitations
1.  The AE event location accuracy may be affected by the change in relative positions of the sensors during shear, an issue not addressed in the study. [Chen 2020, pp. 15-20]
2.  The study suggests the thermally induced changes in shear properties may be dependent on mineral compositions, which requires further investigation. [Chen 2020, pp. 11-15]
3.  The cooling method (dipping only the fracture surface) was designed to simulate EGS conditions but differs from other studies where the whole specimen is immersed. [Chen 2020, pp. 3-5]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Fracture closure under normal stress is assumed to be caused only by elastic deformation of surface asperities ("bed of nails" model). [Chen 2020, pp. 11-15]
2.  The contact area for matching degree calculation is defined as fracture apertures below 10 µm. [Chen 2020, pp. 11-15]
3.  All direct shear tests were conducted under a constant normal stress of 5 MPa. [Chen 2020, pp. 3-5]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Independent Variables:** Thermal treatment temperature (25°C, 150°C, 230°C, 300°C), Fracture type (artificial, natural).
*   **Dependent Variables:** Peak shear strength (τp), Residual shear strength (τr), Shear stiffness (Ks), Peak shear displacement (δsp), Maximum normal dilation (δmn), Joint Roughness Coefficient (JRC), Matching degree, AE parameters (hits, energy, ring down counts), Volume of sheared-off asperities.
*   **Controlled Variables:** Normal stress (5 MPa), Shear displacement rate (0.2 mm/min), Specimen size (50 mm cubes), Granite source (Gonghe basin).

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For Brazilian-induced artificial fractures in Gonghe granite. **Claim:** Slow heating to temperatures up to 300°C followed by rapid water-cooling of the fracture surface increases the peak shear strength under 5 MPa normal stress. **Limitation:** This effect is attributed to improved surface matching, not increased roughness. [Chen 2020, pp. 5-6, 11-15]
2.  **Condition:** For pre-existing natural fractures in Gonghe granite. **Claim:** The same thermal treatment (up to 300°C) has little influence on shear strength, stiffness, or dilation. **Limitation:** Natural fractures have lower initial roughness and more weathered asperities. [Chen 2020, pp. 5-6]
3.  **Condition:** During direct shear of granite fractures. **Claim:** Acoustic emission activity (energy, ring down counts) is strongly correlated with shear stress, peaking at the point of peak shear strength. **Limitation:** The magnitude of AE response is much lower for natural fractures compared to artificial ones. [Chen 2020, pp. 6-7]
4.  **Condition:** After shear of thermally treated granite fractures. **Claim:** The volume of sheared-off asperities is greater than in untreated specimens, indicating thermally induced reduction in asperity strength. [Chen 2020, pp. 5-6]
