# Study on Law of Longitudinal Wave Velocity Evolution of Dry Hot Rock after Thermal Shock.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the evolution of longitudinal wave velocity in four types of granite samples (parent rock, filling body, and composites with longitudinal/transverse cementation surfaces) from a dry hot rock mass after repeated thermal shocks up to 600°C, finding that the first shock causes the largest velocity drop and that water cooling induces greater damage than air cooling.

## Research Question
To study the evolution law of parent rock, filling body, and cementation surface properties after injecting low temperature water thermal shock in the geothermal development of actual dry hot rock, specifically focusing on longitudinal wave velocity changes.

## Study Area / Data
The rock samples were collected from a quarry in Luya Mountain, Shanxi, China. The granite is characterized by large crystal grains (millimeters to tens of millimeters), indicating deep slow cooling and crystallization, representing the characteristics of deep dry hot rock masses [Wu 2021, pp. 2-5]. Four types of standard cylindrical samples (Φ50 mm × 100 mm) were prepared: 1) Granite parent rock, 2) Filling body, 3) Granite with a longitudinal cementation surface (left-right structure), and 4) Granite with a transverse cementation surface (up-down structure) [Wu 2021, pp. 2-5].

## Methods
The study employed ultrasonic testing technology using an NM-4B non-metal ultrasonic detector to measure longitudinal wave velocity (Vp) [Wu 2021, pp. 2-5]. The experimental procedures included:
1.  **Natural Water Absorption and Wave Velocity Test:** Measuring sample dimensions, drying at 60±2°C for 48h, measuring dry mass and wave velocity, then soaking in water for 48h and measuring saturated mass and wave velocity [Wu 2021, pp. 2-5].
2.  **Effect of Shock Number on Wave Velocity:** Samples were heated to 300°C at a rate of 2°C/min, held for 4 hours, then rapidly cooled in 0°C ice-water mixture for 2 hours. This cycle was repeated 5 times, with wave velocity measured after each cycle after drying [Wu 2021, pp. 2-5].
3.  **Effect of Temperature under Different Cooling Methods:** Samples were divided into two groups. Group 1 was heated to target temperatures (150, 300, 450, 600°C) and cooled in 0°C water. Group 2 was heated similarly and cooled at room temperature. Each group underwent 5 heating-cooling cycles at each target temperature, followed by wave velocity measurement after drying [Wu 2021, pp. 2-5].

## Key Findings
1.  At room temperature, the open porosity of granite with a cementation surface is lower than that of the parent rock and filling body, while its wave velocity is higher [Wu 2021, pp. 1-2].
2.  During repeated thermal shocks from room temperature to 300°C with 0°C water cooling, the wave velocity of all four sample types decreased by 40%-50% after the first shock. The decrease diminished with subsequent shocks, dropping by about 3% [Wu 2021, pp. 1-2].
3.  At the same high temperature, the wave velocity drop after 0°C water cooling was greater than after room temperature cooling for all sample types, indicating that 0°C water cooling causes stronger rock damage [Wu 2021, pp. 1-2].
4.  Under the same thermal shock mode, the wave velocity drop of granite with a cementation surface was larger than that of the parent rock and filling body. This is attributed to the largest difference in mineral particle size near the cementation surface, leading to the strongest degree of thermal cracking [Wu 2021, pp. 1-2].
5.  The wave velocity decreased with increasing temperature for all samples under both cooling methods, with the rate of decrease slowing after 300°C [Wu 2021, pp. 5-7].

## Limitations
The study focuses on a specific granite type from Luya Mountain. The maximum temperature tested was 600°C. The research primarily uses wave velocity as an indicator of damage, with supplementary observations of visible cracking [Wu 2021, pp. 5-7]. The number of thermal shock cycles (5) was chosen to avoid complete sample failure for subsequent mechanical tests [Wu 2021, pp. 5-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The wave velocity is a reliable indicator of the overall internal damage degree of the rock [Wu 2021, pp. 2-5].
2.  The Luya Mountain granite samples represent the characteristics of deep dry hot rock masses [Wu 2021, pp. 2-5].
3.  The cementation surface between the filling body and parent rock is a weak surface that can be exploited for reservoir creation [Wu 2021, pp. 1-2].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Longitudinal Wave Velocity (Vp):** Primary measured response variable.
-   **Thermal Shock Temperature:** Ranges from room temperature to 600°C.
-   **Cooling Method:** 0°C water cooling vs. room temperature air cooling.
-   **Number of Thermal Shock Cycles:** Up to 5 cycles.
-   **Rock Type:** Four categories based on composition and structure (parent rock, filling body, longitudinal composite, transverse composite).
-   **Open Porosity (nO):** Measured property related to wave velocity.

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For granite samples from a dry hot rock mass undergoing thermal shock. **Claim:** The first thermal shock cycle induces the most significant reduction in longitudinal wave velocity (40%-50%), with subsequent cycles causing progressively smaller reductions (~3%). **Limitation:** This pattern was observed for shocks up to 300°C with water cooling [Wu 2021, pp. 5-7].
2.  **Condition:** When comparing cooling methods for thermally shocked granite. **Claim:** Rapid cooling with 0°C water causes greater internal damage (as indicated by larger wave velocity reductions) than slower cooling in air at room temperature. **Limitation:** The effect is particularly notable at a heating temperature of 150°C [Wu 2021, pp. 5-7].
3.  **Condition:** In composite rock samples containing a cementation surface between different lithologies. **Claim:** The region near the cementation surface is more susceptible to thermal cracking than the homogeneous parent rock or filling body, due to greater heterogeneity in mineral particle sizes. **Limitation:** This conclusion is based on wave velocity trends and visual observation of cracking [Wu 2021, pp. 5-7].
