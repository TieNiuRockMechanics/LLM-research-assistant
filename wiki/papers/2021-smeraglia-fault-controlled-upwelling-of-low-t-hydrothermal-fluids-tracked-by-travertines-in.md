# Fault-Controlled Upwelling of Low-T Hydrothermal Fluids Tracked by Travertines in a Fold-and-Thrust Belt, Monte Alpi, Southern Apennines, Italy.

## One-line Summary
Fossil Quaternary travertines in the Monte Alpi area record the past upwelling of low-temperature hydrothermal fluids along fault-controlled pathways, with fluid circulation localized at fault intersections within carbonates and inhibited by clay smearing in shales.

## Research Question
The study aims to assess: (1) the paleo-environmental depositional setting of the travertines, (2) the source of the past mineralizing fluids, and (3) the present-day, fault-controlled fluid circulation along high-angle extensional faults [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 2-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the SW edge of the Monte Alpi carbonate massif in the southern Apennines fold-and-thrust belt, specifically the La Calda area [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 2-4]. Data include geological mapping at 1:10,000 scale, field structural analyses, aerial photo interpretation, microstructural analyses of travertine samples, and stable oxygen and carbon isotope analyses (N=141 samples) [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 4-5, 8-10]. The area features Quaternary travertine deposits up to ~20 m thick, active low-temperature (~22°C) hydrothermal springs, and a complex fault network [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 2-4, 4-5].

## Methods
Methods included: (1) geological field mapping at 1:5000 scale, (2) field structural analyses, (3) aerial photo interpretation, (4) microstructural analyses via optical microscopy, and (5) carbon and oxygen stable isotope analyses of travertine powders [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 4-5]. Isotopic calculations used the Kele et al. (2015) equation for δ¹⁸O paleo-fluid composition and the Panichi and Tongiorgi (1976) equation for δ¹³C of parental CO₂, assuming equilibrium fractionation [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 10-12].

## Key Findings
1.  Travertines precipitated from CO₂-rich, meteoric-derived paleo-fluids with δ¹⁸O compositions (-7.7‰ to -10.1‰) overlapping those of active low-T hydrothermal springs [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 1-2, 10-12].
2.  Paleo-fluid temperatures ranged from 16°C to 27°C, consistent with the present-day spring temperature of 22°C [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 12-12].
3.  The parental CO₂ had a biogenic signature (δ¹³C ~ -8.6‰ to -17.4‰), though a deep-sourced component cannot be excluded [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 12-12].
4.  Travertine deposition occurred in a paleo-fluvial environment with cascades, pools, barrages, and caves, controlled by fault scarps and landslide escarpments [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 5-6, 10-12].
5.  Travertines occur only above carbonate rocks (Bagni Unit) and are absent along faults cutting shales and metapelites (Zia Santa and Ligurian Units) [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 1-2, 14-14].
6.  High-permeability fluid conduits localize at intersections of NW-SE and NE-SW striking faults, which are sub-parallel to the current maximum horizontal stress (σ_hmax) [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 1-2, 14-14].
7.  Clay smearing along fault segments cutting shales and metapelites created low-permeability barriers that hampered fluid upwelling [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 1-2, 14-14].

## Limitations
1.  Absolute radiometric ages for the travertines are not available [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 10-12].
2.  CO₂ degassing and calcite precipitation during travertine formation could lead to preferential loss of isotopically light species, preventing detailed estimation of pristine fluid δ¹⁸O and δ¹³C values [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 10-12].
3.  The input of deep-sourced CO₂ (magmatic/mantle or from limestone decarbonation) cannot be totally excluded by isotope composition alone [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 12-12].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Equilibrium fractionation was assumed for isotopic calculations [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 10-12].
2.  Paleo-temperatures during the Quaternary were assumed to be very similar to present-day values [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 10-12].
3.  The δ¹⁸O composition of the paleo-fluid was assumed to be similar to that measured at the present-day hydrothermal springs (~ -9‰) [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 12-12].
4.  A present-day geothermal gradient of 25°C/km and average surface water temperature of 10°C were used to estimate minimum fluid circulation depth [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 14-14].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Temperature:** Low-T hydrothermal springs (~22°C), calculated paleo-fluid temperatures (16-27°C), geothermal gradient (25°C/km) [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 4-5, 12-12, 14-14].
-   **Isotopic Composition:** δ¹⁸O and δ¹³C values of travertines and calculated paleo-fluids [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 8-10, 10-12].
-   **Fluid Chemistry:** Ca-HCO₃(SO₄) signature of springs, SO₄ content [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 4-5, 14-14].
-   **Structural Parameters:** Fault orientations (NW-SE, NE-SW, ENE-WSW), fault intersections, σ_hmax orientation [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 5-6, 14-14].
-   **Permeability:** High in fault damage zones/intersections in carbonates, low due to clay smearing in shales [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 1-2, 14-14].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** In fold-and-thrust belts with carbonate massifs. **Limitation:** Specific to the southern Apennines stress regime and lithology. **Claim:** Fault intersections, particularly between NW-SE and NE-SW striking high-angle faults, act as key high-permeability conduits for the upwelling of low-temperature hydrothermal fluids [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 14-14].
2.  **Condition:** Where faults juxtapose permeable carbonates against shales or metapelites. **Limitation:** Based on field absence of springs/travertines. **Claim:** Clay smearing along fault segments cutting fine-grained, low-permeability lithologies (shales, metapelites) creates barriers that inhibit cross-fault and fault-parallel fluid flow [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 1-2, 14-14].
3.  **Condition:** For meteogene to mixed meteogene-thermogene travertines. **Limitation:** Assumes equilibrium fractionation. **Claim:** Stable oxygen isotope (δ¹⁸O) compositions of travertines can be used to calculate the temperature and isotopic signature of the precipitating paleo-fluid, providing constraints on past hydrothermal systems [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 10-12].
4.  **Condition:** In areas with active low-T hydrothermal springs and fossil travertines. **Limitation:** Requires similar fluid sources. **Claim:** The overlap in δ¹⁸O values between fossil travertines and active hydrothermal springs indicates a long-term (since middle Pleistocene) persistence of the same fault-controlled fluid circulation system [Smeraglia 2021, pp. 1-2, 10-12].

## Open Questions
1.  What are the absolute radiometric ages of the travertine deposits, and how do they correlate with specific Pleistocene interglacial periods?
2.  What is the precise contribution of deep-sourced (mantle/magmatic) CO₂ versus biogenic CO₂ to the hydrothermal fluids?
3.  How does the in-situ stress field quantitatively influence the permeability of the fault zones, particularly the NW-SE striking conduits?
4.  What is the exact three-dimensional geometry and connectivity of the fault intersection zones at depth that serve as the primary upwelling pathways?
