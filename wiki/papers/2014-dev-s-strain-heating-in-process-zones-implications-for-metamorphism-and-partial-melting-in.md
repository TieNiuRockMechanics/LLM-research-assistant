# Strain Heating in Process Zones; Implications for Metamorphism and Partial Melting in the Lithosphere.

## One-line Summary
This paper proposes that distributed shear heating within large "process zones" of deformation, formed at kinematically incompatible fault junctions, can generate sufficient heat to cause metamorphism and partial melting in the lithosphere, explaining patchy volcanism in collisional settings.

## Research Question
Can lithospheric deformation itself produce enough heat through strain heating to cause high-temperature metamorphism or partial melting, and if so, how can this be reconciled with the negative feedback of temperature-dependent creep rheology? [Dev 2014, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The primary study area is the junction between the North Anatolian Fault (NAF) and the East Anatolian Fault (EAF) near Karliova in eastern Turkey. This site was chosen because the composition and age of emplacement of extensive volcanics are well known, as are the rates of motion on the associated strike-slip faults. [Dev 2014, pp. 1-2] The volcanism covers a zone of complicated deformation comprising fault branching, horsetail and reverse faulting. [Dev 2014, pp. 8-8] Data include geodetic fault slip rates (NAF: ~2 cm/yr, EAF: ~0.9 cm/yr), the angle of intersection (58°), and the volume and geochemistry of the Bingöl/Turna volcanics (~800 km³). [Dev 2014, pp. 8-8] The paper also references spatial correlations between volcanism and fault complexities in northern Tibet and Anatolia. [Dev 2014, pp. 4-5]

## Methods
The authors develop a model using a distribution of dislocations in an elastic medium to define the geometry and strain rate field of process zones formed at fault junctions. [Dev 2014, pp. 2-3] Off-fault strain rates are derived from the kinematic incompatibility between the faults. [Dev 2014, pp. 5-6] Heat production per unit volume is calculated as the product of strain rate and shear strength. [Dev 2014, pp. 5-6] The shear strength is determined using Byerlee's law for the brittle regime and dislocation creep power flow laws for the ductile regime. [Dev 2014, pp. 5-6] The heat flow equation, including radiogenic heating, is solved to track temperature evolution, with vertical diffusion considered but horizontal diffusion neglected in the initial model. [Dev 2014, pp. 6-7] A dimensionless analysis is used to scale the problem. [Dev 2014, pp. 6-7]

## Key Findings
1.  Kinematic incompatibilities at fault junctions (e.g., triple junctions, intersections) force deformation to distribute off the main faults, forming large process zones. [Dev 2014, pp. 2-3]
2.  The large volume of these process zones minimizes conductive heat loss compared to narrow shear zones, promoting strong heating. [Dev 2014, pp. 1-2]
3.  Under reasonable rheological assumptions, strain heating within process zones can raise temperatures to the solidus for both crustal and mantle rocks within a few million years after the onset of deformation. [Dev 2014, pp. 8-9]
4.  The model can explain the genesis of large volumes of magma (e.g., ~800 km³ for Bingöl/Turna) even at small melt fractions, due to the large volume of the heated zone. [Dev 2014, pp. 1-2]
5.  For the Karliova junction, the model predicts independent but coeval melting of the crust and mantle within 4-7 million years, consistent with the observed bimodal (mafic and silicic) volcanism. [Dev 2014, pp. 8-9]
6.  Process zone heating is more efficient than shear heating on planar faults or shear zones and can produce significant magma volumes more rapidly. [Dev 2014, pp. 10-11]

## Limitations
1.  Rheological uncertainties are large and affect all models of strain heating. [Dev 2014, pp. 8-9]
2.  The model neglects heat advection, which may play a role in some configurations. [Dev 2014, pp. 6-7]
3.  Horizontal thermal diffusion is neglected in the calculations, though it is argued to be relatively ineffective for major faults. [Dev 2014, pp. 6-7]
4.  The model assumes no localization of strain within the process zone on length scales comparable to the main faults. [Dev 2014, pp. 10-11]
5.  Predicted melt volumes are not directly equivalent to erupted magma volumes, as only a fraction melts and some may be left behind. [Dev 2014, pp. 9-10]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  100% of the mechanical work done during irreversible deformation is converted into heat once the plastic threshold is exceeded. [Dev 2014, pp. 5-6]
2.  The brittle strength is given by Byerlee's law, with effective normal stress equal to lithostatic pressure (no pore fluid pressure). [Dev 2014, pp. 5-6]
3.  Ductile strength follows dislocation creep power flow laws. [Dev 2014, pp. 5-6]
4.  Deformation within the process zone is treated as plane strain. [Dev 2014, pp. 5-6]
5.  The initial geotherm is calculated with average values for radiogenic heat production and thermal conductivity. [Dev 2014, pp. 8-8]
6.  The lithosphere-asthenosphere boundary is modeled as a diffuse thermal boundary with a fixed heat flux. [Dev 2014, pp. 8-8]

## Connections To Other Work
The paper challenges the prevailing view since the late 1970s that strain heating is self-limiting and insignificant for melting. [Dev 2014, pp. 1-2] It contrasts with two common modeling approaches: treating the lithosphere as a viscous fluid or as rigid blocks moving along localized faults. [Dev 2014, pp. 2-2] It builds on the concept of process zones from material mechanics and structural geology. [Dev 2014, pp. 2-2] The model provides an alternative to explanations for collisional volcanism that invoke asthenospheric processes like delamination, convective removal, or slab break-off. [Dev 2014, pp. 10-11] It is related to studies of strain partitioning and fault interaction. [Dev 2014, pp. 3-4]
