# Crack Evolution of Limestone Induced by Static Demolition at Axial Stress and Its Application.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the crack initiation and propagation patterns in limestone under uniaxial stress during static demolition using acoustic emission monitoring and develops a theoretical model for crack radius, which was successfully applied to roof caving in a high-gas coal mine.

## Research Question
What are the crack evolution laws (initiation location, propagation sequence, and velocity) in limestone under uniaxial stress during static demolition, and how can this knowledge be applied to engineering problems like forced roof caving in high-gas mines? [Hao 2014, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study used cubic limestone samples (200 mm × 200 mm × 200 mm) of K2 limestone. A central borehole (diameter 50 mm, depth 150 mm) was drilled perpendicular to the bedding. The static demolition agent was a self-made "mine-efficient static coal-rock swelling agent" with a maximum expansion pressure of 80 MPa. The engineering application was conducted in a high-gas coal mine working on the No. 15 coal seam, where the roof was K2 limestone with an average thickness of 7.18 m and a uniaxial compressive strength of about 50 MPa. [Hao 2014, pp. 1-2, 5-7]

## Methods
1.  **Experimental Setup:** A true triaxial rock mechanics testing machine was used to apply a uniaxial stress of 5 MPa to the limestone sample. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
2.  **Monitoring:** Acoustic emission (AE) spatial location technology (using a PAC disp multi-channel AE instrument) was employed to monitor and locate crack initiation and propagation in real-time. Four AE sensors were arranged on the sample faces. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
3.  **Procedure:** The sample was fixed with 0.5 MPa pressure, AE sensors were installed, the static demolition agent (mixed with water at a 7:3 mass ratio) was poured into the borehole, and then the axial pressure was increased to 5 MPa and held constant while AE monitoring began. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
4.  **Theoretical Modeling:** A model relating the expansion force from the static demolition agent to the crack propagation radius was established based on elastic mechanics and fracture mechanics theories. [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]
5.  **Engineering Application:** The model was used to determine drilling parameters (hole spacing, depth, angle) for forced roof caving in a high-gas mine. [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]

## Key Findings
1.  Under uniaxial stress, crack initiation occurs at the lower-middle part of the borehole wall. Two groups of cracks form sequentially, with each group appearing symmetrically along the borehole wall and roughly in a straight line. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
2.  The propagation rate of both crack groups follows a "fast then slow" trend, divided into a rapid increase phase and a rapid decrease phase. The rate during the decrease phase is lower than that during the increase phase. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
3.  The propagation rate of the first-formed cracks is greater than that of the later-formed cracks. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
4.  For a single crack, propagation is directional: the rate parallel to the borehole axis is greater than that perpendicular to the borehole axis. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
5.  The theoretical model for crack propagation radius showed good consistency with the experimental monitoring results. [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]
6.  The application of static demolition in the high-gas mine successfully reduced the hanging roof distance by 7.2 m, improving safety. [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]

## Limitations
1.  The study was conducted under uniaxial stress conditions, which simplifies the actual complex stress state in underground rock masses. [Hao 2014, pp. 1-2]
2.  The research used cubic limestone samples, and the results may differ for other rock types or sample geometries. The paper notes differences from studies on concrete. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
3.  The theoretical model assumes ideal conditions; in engineering applications, factors like pressure, temperature, and pre-existing fractures reduce the effective expansion pressure (a utilization coefficient of 0.6 was used). [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The experimental condition (uniaxial stress) is considered analogous to the stress state of a hanging roof in the arc-shaped triangular area of a working face, where horizontal forces are lost on the sides adjacent to the goaf. [Hao 2014, pp. 1-2]
2.  The static demolition agent's expansion pressure is initially uniform in all directions within the borehole. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
3.  The relationship between rock uniaxial compressive strength (σ_c) and fracture toughness (K_IC) is given by K_IC = 0.0265σ_c + 0.0014. [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Axial Stress (σ):** 5 MPa in the experiment. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
*   **Borehole Diameter (d):** 50 mm (lab), 40 mm (field). [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5, 5-7]
*   **Expansion Pressure (q(t)):** Time-dependent pressure from static demolition agent, max 80 MPa (lab), effective 48 MPa (field). [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5, 5-7]
*   **Crack Propagation Radius (a'):** Theoretical radius of first-formed crack. [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]
*   **Fracture Toughness (K_IC):** Material property derived from σ_c. [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]
*   **Acoustic Emission (AE) Events:** Used as a proxy for micro-cracking and crack propagation. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]

## Reusable Claims
1.  Under uniaxial stress, static demolition in limestone initiates cracks symmetrically at the lower-middle borehole wall, forming two sequential groups. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
2.  The propagation rate of cracks induced by static demolition under uniaxial stress follows a pattern of rapid increase followed by rapid decrease. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
3.  The propagation rate of cracks parallel to the borehole axis is greater than that perpendicular to the axis during static demolition. [Hao 2014, pp. 2-5]
4.  The crack propagation radius (a') can be modeled as a function of the time-dependent expansion pressure q(t) and the rock's fracture toughness K_IC, where a' = [q(t)]² / [π(0.0265σ_c + 0.0014)²]. [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]
5.  For engineering applications in high-gas mines, a utilization coefficient of 0.6 for the maximum expansion pressure of the static demolition agent is recommended to account for field conditions. [Hao 2014, pp. 5-7]
