# Gneissic Tors in the Central European Upland: Complex Late Pleistocene Forms?

## One-line Summary
Based on geomorphic analysis and terrestrial cosmogenic nuclide (¹⁰Be) inventories from thirteen Bohemian-Moravian Highland outcrops, this study reveals that gneissic tors and crags in a never-glaciated Central European upland have diverse morphologies strongly controlled by rock discontinuities, with Schmidt hammer measurements showing statistically significant within-tor weathering variability, and exposure ages condensing their shaping predominantly into the Late Pleistocene (107.5–7.4 ka), challenging assumptions of pre-Quaternary tor inheritance and suggesting complex, site-specific degradational pathways rather than a single evolutionary model. `[Zden 2023, pp. 1-14]`

## Research Question
How do litho-structural factors, topographical position, and Late Pleistocene periglacial conditions govern the spatial emergence, morphological evolution, and post-emergence degradation rates (summit downwearing versus cliff backwearing) of gneissic tors and crags in the never-glaciated Bohemian-Moravian Highland? `[Zden 2023, pp. 1-4]`

## Study Area / Data
* **Region**: Bohemian-Moravian Highland (BMH), eastern Bohemian Massif, Czechia (~6800 km²). Never-glaciated, repeatedly affected by periglacial conditions during the Quaternary. `[Zden 2023, pp. 2-3, 3-4]`
* **Climate Context**: Maximum ice sheet extent (Weichselian) dated ~22 ka BP did not reach this area. Pleniglacial (57–15 ka BP) characterized by mostly treeless vegetation; periglacial features include ice wedge casts and loess deposits. `[Zden 2023, pp. 3-4]`
* **Lithology**: Predominantly metamorphic rocks (various gneisses of Proterozoic to Palaeozoic age); one outcrop in quartz-pebble conglomerate. Dense foliation planes are typical in gneisses. Igneous rocks (Variscan granites, syenites) occur in central/eastern sectors. `[Zden 2023, pp. 3-4]`
* **Studied Outcrops**: Thirteen conspicuous bedrock outcrops: 9 summit or upper-slope tors; 4 steep-slope hillside crags. Distributed along a NW–SE transect (altitudes >750 m to ~350 m asl). `[Zden 2023, pp. 4-5, 6-7]`

## Methods
* **Geomorphological Analysis**: DTM-based (LiDAR 5G) derivation of crest distance and slope inclination for each outcrop. Field mapping of medium-scale landforms within a 100 m radius and documentation of small-scale weathering features. `[Zden 2023, pp. 4-5]`
* **Structural Measurements**: Strike and dip of foliation planes and joints measured on cliffs/summits using a geological compass (79 to 122 fractures per tor). Fracture spacing measured along horizontal and vertical transects at cliff bases. `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-5]`
* **Rock Hardness (Schmidt Hammer)**: Digital N-type Schmidt hammer (Proceq Digi-Schmidt ND) used on 30×30 cm plots (49 impacts each; lowest 9 disregarded; 40 values analyzed per plot). Stratified random sampling on gently inclined summits, vertical cliffs, transverse clefts, and basal overhangs (11–21 plots per tor). Shapiro-Wilk’s, Levene’s, and non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis ANOVA tests applied. R-values considered independent of fracture density (R² = 0.0051). `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-5]`
* **¹⁰Be Cosmogenic Nuclide Inventory**: Samples (≥0.5 kg) from surficial layer (3–5 cm). Summits sampled on most extensive foliation plane surface; cliff samples at 0.7–1.2 m above toe. Quartz isolation and ¹⁰Be extraction at LN2C, CEREGE; AMS measurement at ASTER. Denudation rates calculated using Eq. (1) with Stone (2000) scaling and 4.02 ± 0.32 at/g/a SLHL spallation production rate (Borchers et al., 2016). `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-6]`

## Key Findings
* **Structural Control on Morphology**: Tor/crag outlines governed by (sub)vertical joints; summit and cliff morphology dictated by inclination of foliation or bedding planes. Some tors developed in exceptionally densely foliated and jointed gneiss, contradicting the conventional view of tors as massive, weathering-resistant compartments. `[Zden 2023, pp. 1-2, 6-7, 7-8, 13-14]`
* **Within-Tor Weathering Variability**: Schmidt hammer R-values show statistically significant within-tor differences. Summit surfaces have significantly lower mean R-values than cliffed sides (e.g., Kamenný vrch summit 41.3 vs. cliff 52.8; Pivovar summit 34.7 vs. cliff 45.1), indicating longer subaerial weathering exposure of top parts. Hr´adky tor shows near-equal summit/cliff mean values but much greater dispersal on cliffs (29.0–59.6). Higher R-values (>50) are associated with rock niches/overhangs where recent rock detachments exposed fresh surfaces. `[Zden 2023, pp. 7-8]`
* **¹⁰Be Inventories and Ages**: TCN inventories indicate shaping predominantly within the Late Pleistocene and rarely Holocene (107.5 – 7.4 ka). Summit/upper-slope tors: denudation 8.1 ± 0.3 to 23.0 ± 1.5 m/Ma. Hillside crags: denudation 6.4 ± 0.2 to 105.6 ± 4.8 m/Ma. Tor summit apparent exposure ages: 31.2 ± 1.4 to 85.4 ± 2.7 ka (median 55.4 ka). Crag ages: 7.4 ± 0.3 to 107.5 ± 3.5 ka. Very old ages from resistant quartz conglomerate (Babí lom: 105.0, 74.6 ka) versus gneiss median 51.4 ka suggest lithological influence. `[Zden 2023, pp. 10-11, 11-12, 12-12]`
* **No Support for Pre-Quaternary Tor Inheritance**: TCN ages contradict earlier hypotheses of tors as pre-Quaternary landscape relics, indicating instead continuous decay during the Late Pleistocene. `[Zden 2023, pp. 11-12]`
* **Paired Summit-Cliff Samples – Three Post-Emergence Scenarios**: Eight outcrops with paired samples yield three cases: (a) summit older than cliff (Kamenný vrch, Pivovar, Babí lom; ~30 ka difference); (b) cliff older than summit (Tis˚uvka, ~30 ka difference – currently unexplained); (c) summit and cliff ages similar (~3–5 ka difference; Hr´adky, Milovské perničky, Pasecká skála, Jezerní stěna). These support scenarios of summit lowering with outline retention, balanced degradation, and backwearing dominance. `[Zden 2023, pp. 10-11, 12-13]`
* **Geomorphic Context Diversity**: Outcrops range from isolated tors in smooth soil-covered slopes to clusters accompanied by rock cliffs, debris-covered steps, benches, boulder fields, and nubbins. Surficial materials vary from debris-rich soils to autochthonous blocky mantles and scree. `[Zden 2023, pp. 6-7, 11-12]`

## Limitations
* **Sample representatives**: TCN inventories derived from single point samples per summit/cliff surface; do not necessarily represent average erosion rates across the surface. `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-6, 12-12]`
* **Limited structural and hardness data**: Detailed structural measurements and Schmidt hammer tests performed only at three tors (Hr´adky, Kamenný vrch, Pivovar), limiting generalization. `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-5, 7-8]`
* **Lithological control not fully resolved**: At-a-site grain size and mineral composition variations (potentially affecting R-values) not discernible by naked eye and not analyzed in thin sections. `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-5, 11-12]`
* **Statistical power**: 21 cosmogenic ages across 13 outcrops insufficient for robust statistical analysis of evolutionary histories by spatial position, morphology, or geology. `[Zden 2023, pp. 11-12]`
* **Environmental history**: Reliance on a single nuclide (¹⁰Be) without ²⁶Al does not allow addressing complex exposure/burial histories. `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-6]`
* **Mechanistic uncertainty**: Unable to determine whether summit-versus-cliff age differences result from long-term cliff retreat or gradual emergence from regolith in absence of multi-height samples. `[Zden 2023, pp. 13-14]`

## Assumptions / Conditions
* **Assumption: R-values reflect exposure duration**: Schmidt hammer rebound values are treated as proxies for relative weathering degree and hence relative exposure age, assuming identical bedrock mechanical properties. `[Zden 2023, pp. 11-12]` (Not confirmed — note caveat about potential lithological micro-variations.)
* **Assumption: TCN denudation rates assumed steady-state**: Denudation rates calculated assuming constant erosion and simplified inherited component (neglected for surface samples). `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-6]`
* **Assumption: Topographical shielding adequately characterized**: Shielding assessed in field considering distant horizons and nearby rock formations. `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-6]`
* **Condition: Never-glaciated landscape**: Results apply to upland terrains repeatedly affected by periglacial conditions but beyond Pleistocene ice limits; may not be transferable to glaciated tor populations (which show older ages often >300 ka). `[Zden 2023, pp. 2-3, 3-4]`
* **Condition: Lithological scope**: Outcrops studied are predominantly gneiss variants with dense foliation; findings may not extend to granitic tors without similar structural characteristics. `[Zden 2023, pp. 11-11]`
* **Condition: Sampling strategy**: Summit samples collected from most extensive foliation surface rather than highest/lowest points; cliff samples avoided notches/niches indicating recent block detachment. Thus TCN inventories represent "intermediate" events, not oldest or youngest. `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-6]`

## Key Variables / Parameters
* **¹⁰Be concentration** (×10³ at/g): ranged from 38.1 ± 1.7 (Sokolí skála) to 597.0 ± 19.5 (Výří skála). `[Zden 2023, pp. 10-11]`  
* **Minimum exposure age**: 7.4 ± 0.3 to 107.5 ± 3.5 ka. `[Zden 2023, pp. 10-11]`
* **Maximum denudation rate**: 6.4 ± 0.2 to 105.6 ± 4.8 m/Ma. `[Zden 2023, pp. 10-11]`
* **Schmidt hammer R-values (mean ± SD)**: Hr´adky 41.6 ± 6.6; Kamenný vrch 46.8 ± 6.7; Pivovar 43.8 ± 8.9. `[Zden 2023, pp. 7-8]`
* **Foliation dip inclination**: Mean 10–20° at three sampled tors. `[Zden 2023, pp. 8-10]`
* **Fracture density (vertical)**: Pivovar 4.50/m; Kamenný vrch 15.22/m (open: 7.41/m). `[Zden 2023, pp. 8-10]`
* **Crest distance and slope inclination**: Tors on 1°–12° slopes near crest; crags on 26°–43° slopes spanning up to 92% of slope length. `[Zden 2023, pp. 6-7]`
* **SLHL spallation production rate**: 4.02 ± 0.32 at/g/a (Borchers et al., 2016). `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-6]`

## Reusable Claims
* **Claim**: In never-glaciated Central European uplands, gneissic tors and crags record ¹⁰Be exposure ages predominantly within the Late Pleistocene (107.5–7.4 ka, median ~55 ka), demonstrating that their morphologies are not inherited from pre-Quaternary times but result from continuous degradation during the last cold phase. `[Zden 2023, pp. 11-12, 13-14]`
    * **Condition**: Applies to densely foliated metamorphic rocks (gneisses) in periglacial settings. Glaciated tor populations elsewhere show much older ages (>300 ka). `[Zden 2023, pp. 2-2]`
    * **Limitation**: Derived from single point samples per surface; represent "intermediate" erosion events, not oldest or youngest. `[Zden 2023, pp. 5-6]`
* **Claim**: Within-tor rock hardness shows statistically significant differences between gently inclined summits and vertical/overhanging cliff faces (e.g., means 34.7–41.3 on summits vs. 45.1–52.8 on cliffs), corresponding to longer subaerial weathering exposure of top surfaces. `[Zden 2023, pp. 7-8, 11-12]`
    * **Condition**: Measured on gneissic tors using Schmidt hammer with 40 valid impacts per 30×30 cm plot.
    * **Limitation**: Not confirmed whether micro-lithological variations contributed; thin section analysis not performed. `[Zden 2023, pp. 11-12]`
* **Claim**: Paired summit‑cliff ¹⁰Be inventories from gneissic tors yield three distinct relationships (summit > cliff age; cliff > summit age; approximately equal), indicating that post‑emergence degradation can proceed via summit downwearing, cliff backwearing, or balanced rates, with no single evolutionary pathway universally applicable. `[Zden 2023, pp. 10-11]`
    * **Condition**: Based on eight paired-sample outcrops; observed differences range from ~3–5 ka (similar) to ~30 ka (discordant).
    * **Limitation**: Unclear whether discordances reflect long‑term cliff retreat or gradual emergence from regolith. Multi‑height sampling lacking. `[Zden 2023, pp. 13-14]`
* **Claim**: Tor morphology is strongly litho‑structurally controlled: (sub)vertical joints dictate ground plan outlines; foliation or bedding inclination governs summit and cliff morphology; dense fracturing does not preclude tor formation, contrary to the conventional view that tors require massive, widely jointed rock. `[Zden 2023, pp. 1-2, 11-11]`
    * **Condition**: Documented on gneissic and conglomeratic outcrops; some gneiss tors exhibit exceptionally dense foliation and jointing (e.g., Kamenný vrch vertical fracture density 15.22/m). `[Zden 2023, pp. 8-10]`
* **Claim**: The geomorphic context of tors/crags (isolated vs. clustered, smooth slopes vs. stepped benches with block fields) reflects variable breakdown mechanisms from blocky disintegration to fine‑grained regolith production, with exposure age‑matched Schmidt hammer R‑values (younger surfaces show higher R‑values). `[Zden 2023, pp. 6-7, 10-11, 11-12]`
    * **Condition**: Derived from geomorphic mapping within 100‑m radius; R‑value‑age comparison available only for six dated surfaces at three tors. `[Zden 2023, pp. 12-12]`

## Connections To Other Work
* **Glaciated vs. non-glaciated tor inventories**: Tors in areas repeatedly ice-covered during the Pleistocene (Sweden, Scotland, Finland, Baffin Island) show much older apparent exposure ages (>300 ka in many cases) than populations beyond glaciation limits including this study (mostly <100 ka). `[Zden 2023, pp. 2-3]`
* **Dartmoor tors comparison**: Denudation rates for BMH tors (6.4–105.6 m/Ma; tors median ~16 m/Ma) best compare with Dartmoor granitic tors (5–77 m/Ma, Gunnell et al., 2013), although Dartmoor ages were reinterpreted as representing ephemeral features spanning at most Middle Pleistocene to present. `[Zden 2023, pp. 2-2, 12-12]`
* **Otago schist tors**: Denudation rates on Otago (New Zealand) schist tors (20–220 m/Ma, Raab et al., 2021) are larger than BMH gneissic tors, reflecting differences in bedrock resistance. `[Zden 2023, pp. 12-12]`
* **Pre-Quaternary inheritance debate**: Earlier BMH studies (Demek, 1964; Kirchner, 2016b) hypothesized tors as pre-Quaternary inherited forms shaped by deep weathering in Paleogene/Neogene and subsequent periglacial re-modelling; cosmogenic data now reassess this, showing Late Pleistocene ages dominate. `[Zden 2023, pp. 11-12]`
* **Sila Massif and Otago multi-height sampling**: Unlike those studies (Raab et al., 2019, 2021), this work cannot discriminate between emergence from regolith and cliff retreat scenarios due to lack of vertically resolved samples within individual tors. `[Zden 2023, pp. 13-14]`
* **Periglacial timing**: Absence of LGM and younger dates in this study parallels findings from blocky accumulation in Hrubý Jeseník (youngest 26.8 ka, Engel et al., 2020), suggesting limited geomorphic activity during coldest phases (polar desert). `[Zden 2023, pp. 12-13]`

## Open Questions
* What explains the reversed age pattern at Tisůvka tor (summit apparently much younger, 55 ka, than cliff at 84 ka) in a never-glaciated setting? `[Zden 2023, pp. 13-14]`
* How generalizable are the three degradation scenarios (summit lowering, backwearing, balanced) to other gneissic tor populations beyond the 8 paired-sample cases? `[Zden 2023, pp. 12-13]`
* What is the role of lithological micro-variations (grain size, mineral composition) in producing within‑tor R‑value variability, and can this be separated from exposure time effects? `[Zden 2023, pp. 11-12]`
* Did periglacial conditions during the Last Glacial Maximum significantly enhance mechanical weathering of tors, or did polar desert conditions suppress activity as suggested by the absence of LGM exposure ages? `[Zden 2023, pp. 12-13]`
* Can multi‑height ¹⁰Be sampling on individual tors differentiate between gradual emergence from regolith and cliff retreat as dominant post‑emergence processes in the BMH? `[Zden 2023, pp. 13-14]`
* To what extent are the exceptionally densely fractured gneissic tors geomorphic features shaped primarily by cryoplanation rather than deep weathering and stripping, as early regional studies suggested? `[Zden 2023, pp. 11-11]`
