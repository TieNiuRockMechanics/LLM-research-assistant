# Granite Deformation Characteristics in Drilling Process at 4 000 m Hydrostatic Pressure and High Temperature.

## One-line Summary
Under simulated 4000 m hydrostatic pressure and high temperature, granite exhibits clear creep deformation during drilling, with a critical temperature threshold between 150°C and 300°C and creep failure occurring at 500°C.

## Research Question
To investigate the deformation characteristics of large-scale granite surrounding a borehole during the drilling process under high temperature and high hydrostatic pressure conditions, in order to understand borehole stability for hot dry rock geothermal extraction.

## Study Area / Data
The study used large cylindrical granite samples (φ200 mm × 410 mm) of "Luhui granite" sourced from Pingyi, Shandong, China. The samples were dense, crack-free, and in a natural water-saturated state with a density of 2.71 g/cm³ [Feng 2010, pp. 1-3].

## Methods
The experiment utilized the 600°C 20 MN servo-controlled rock triaxial testing machine system from China University of Mining and Technology [Feng 2010, pp. 1-3]. The procedure involved: (1) loading the sample to a confining pressure of 25 MPa (simulating ~1000 m depth) at room temperature; (2) heating at 10°C/h to target temperatures (150°C, 300°C, 500°C) and maintaining for ~4 hours; (3) increasing the pressure to 100 MPa (simulating 4000 m hydrostatic pressure) and holding for ~40 minutes; (4) initiating drilling with a φ30 mm PDC drill bit while automatically collecting deformation data [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5]. The influence of the NaCl pressure medium on lateral deformation was corrected using a method from Wan et al. [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5].

## Key Findings
1.  Under constant temperature and pressure, granite deformation during drilling exhibits clear creep characteristics, with both axial and lateral deformations showing complete initial (decelerating) and steady-state (secondary) creep stages [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5].
2.  At 4000 m hydrostatic pressure, axial and lateral deformation amounts during drilling increase with rising temperature. A critical temperature threshold exists in the range of 150°C to 300°C; below this threshold, the deformation rate is low, and above it, the rate increases rapidly [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5].
3.  At 300°C, a clear accelerated (tertiary) creep process begins. At 500°C, creep failure of the granite occurs [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5]. The steady-state creep rate at 500°C is approximately 5 times that at 150°C [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5].

## Limitations
1.  Only four temperature points (20°C, 150°C, 300°C, 500°C) were tested due to the high difficulty, cost, and resource consumption of large-sample high-temperature high-pressure experiments [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5].
2.  The use of NaCl as a solid pressure medium required a correction method to eliminate its effect on lateral strain measurements [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5].
3.  The study simulates a specific drilling process; results may vary with different drilling parameters or rock types.

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The large sample size (φ200 mm × 400 mm) is assumed to better represent the in-situ rock mass structure (e.g., pores, fissures) compared to small laboratory specimens [Feng 2010, pp. 1-3].
2.  The experimental procedure (low-pressure heating followed by high-pressure loading) is assumed to be necessary to prevent equipment damage and achieve thermal equilibrium [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5].
3.  The injection of room-temperature water during drilling creates a significant thermal shock effect, which is a critical factor in the observed deformation behavior [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5].

## Key Variables / Parameters

-   **Sample Dimensions:** φ200 mm × 410 mm.
-   **Drill Bit:** φ30 mm PDC bit.
-   **Measured Outputs:** Axial strain, lateral strain, steady-state creep rate.

## Reusable Claims
1.  Under 4000 m hydrostatic pressure, granite exhibits a clear creep deformation process during drilling, characterized by distinct initial and steady-state creep stages. [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5]
2.  The deformation rate of granite during drilling under 4000 m hydrostatic pressure is highly sensitive to temperature, with a critical threshold range of 150°C to 300°C. Below this range, creep rates are low; above it, rates increase dramatically. [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5]
3.  For borehole stability in granite at 4000 m depth, 500°C is identified as a critical temperature condition leading to creep failure during the drilling process. [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5]
4.  The combination of thermal fracturing from high temperature and thermal shock from drilling fluid circulation significantly reduces the conditions (temperature and pressure) required for granite failure compared to static high-temperature tests. [Feng 2010, pp. 3-5]

The study builds upon and compares its findings with previous research:
