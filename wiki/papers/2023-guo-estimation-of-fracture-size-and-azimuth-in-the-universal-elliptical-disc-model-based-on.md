# Estimation of fracture size and azimuth in the universal elliptical disc model based on trace information.

## One-line Summary
The paper derives stereological formulae for trace length from universal elliptical disc (UED) fractures, validates them via Monte Carlo simulation, and develops an optimization-based inverse method to estimate fracture size and azimuth parameters from trace data on multiple sampling windows, demonstrating superiority over circular disc and non‑UED models.

## Research Question
How can the size‑related parameters (long axis length, short‑long axis ratio) and azimuth‑related parameters (rotation angle, orientation) of the universal elliptical disc (UED) fracture model be estimated from trace length information collected on sampling windows? [Guo 2023, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
No natural field site is used. The study relies on hypothetical 3D discrete fracture networks (DFN) generated by Monte Carlo simulations inside a block of size 50 m × 50 m × 50 m. Fracture geometric characteristics are assigned known distribution parameters (Tables 2, 5, 10) and virtual sampling windows of various orientations (Table 3) collect trace lengths. The “true” trace statistics are taken from infinite sampling windows, while the estimation method is tested with combinations of three finite‑orientation windows. [Guo 2023, pp. 1-1, 5-7, 10-11, 13-14]

## Methods
1. **Forward derivation** – The stereological relationship between trace length and UED parameters (long axis length \(a\), ratio \(k\), rotation angle \(\gamma\), orientation) is derived, leading to the mean and standard deviation of trace length (Eqs. 36‑37). [Guo 2023, pp. 1-2, 2-4, 4-5]
2. **Monte Carlo validation** – A 3D elliptical DFN with 250 000 fractures is generated; trace statistics from 30 infinite sampling windows are compared with the derived formulae. Error rate (\(ER = |TV - CV|/TV \times 100\%\)) is computed. [Guo 2023, pp. 5-7]
3. **Inverse estimation** – With pre‑assumed distribution forms (Fisher for orientation, von Mises for rotation angle, normal for \(k\), exponential for \(a\)), an optimization problem is constructed using trace means and standard deviations from three sampling windows (Eqs. 55‑56). Monte Carlo simulation approximates the integrals in the objective functions, and the set of parameters that minimizes the total error \(E_\text{sum}\) is selected. [Guo 2023, pp. 7-9, 9-10]
4. **Model comparison** – A hypothetical rectangular fracture network is represented by circular disc, non‑UED, and UED models. Trace statistics from 30 windows are compared with the actual network to assess model fidelity. [Guo 2023, pp. 12-13]

## Key Findings
- The derived analytical expressions (Eqs. 36‑37) were validated by Monte Carlo simulation: the error rate of mean and standard deviation of trace length is below 5 % for all 30 sampling windows. [Guo 2023, pp. 7-8]
- The optimization‑based parameter estimation method, using three sampling windows with different orientations, recovers the true parameters with a mean error rate less than 9.2 % (Table 7). [Guo 2023, pp. 11-12]
- The UED model significantly outperforms the circular disc and non‑UED models. In a hypothetical rectangular fracture network, the maximum error rates for the mean trace length are 99.5 % (non‑UED) and 20.9 % (UED), and for standard deviation 66.7 % (non‑UED) and 11.8 % (UED). [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14]
- The trace length distribution in the UED model depends on the distributions of orientation, rotation angle, long axis length, and ratio of short‑long axis lengths, whereas in the circular disc model it is independent of the sampling window. [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14]
- The third moment of trace length can be used to verify the assumed size distribution forms (Eqs. 63‑66) when the azimuth distribution is fixed. [Guo 2023, pp. 10-11]

## Limitations
- The forward and inverse derivations assume **infinite sampling windows**; in practice, finite windows introduce biases (size, truncation, censoring) that are not addressed here. [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14]
- The parameter estimation method requires **at least three sampling windows**, limiting its applicability to complex underground engineering where three faces exist (e.g., underground power plants, coal mines). For general tunnels or slopes with fewer windows, the method is not directly applicable. [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14]
- The distribution of size and azimuth parameters must be pre‑assumed; the relationship between trace length distribution and size/azimuth distributions has not been thoroughly investigated. [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14]
- The Monte Carlo integration used in the optimization (300 000 samples) may introduce numerical noise; the results are valid for the tested parameter ranges only. [Guo 2023, pp. 11-12]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Fractures are planar and modeled as **universal elliptical discs** with random central points (Poisson distribution, density \(P_{30}\)), independent size, azimuth, and location. [Guo 2023, pp. 1-1, 1-2]
- The sampling window is an infinite plane with known dip direction \(a_s\) and dip angle \(b_s\); the dihedral angle \(\epsilon\) between fracture and window is acute (0 to \(\pi/2\)). [Guo 2023, pp. 2-4]
- The orientation of the long axis follows a **von Mises distribution** (truncated on \([-\pi/2, \pi/2]\)), orientation follows a **Fisher distribution**, ratio \(k\) follows a **normal distribution** (truncated to [0,1]), and long axis length follows an **exponential distribution** for the parameter estimation illustration. [Guo 2023, pp. 8-9]
- The angle \(u\) between the trace line and the long axis is derived in Appendix A; its distribution depends on fracture and window orientations. [Guo 2023, pp. 7]
- The third‑moment verification assumes the azimuth distribution is already fixed. [Guo 2023, pp. 10-11]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Fracture geometry**: long axis length \(a\) (mean \(m_a\), standard deviation \(s_a\)), ratio of short‑long axis lengths \(k\) (mean \(m_k\), standard deviation \(s_k\)), rotation angle \(\gamma\) (location \(g_m\), concentration \(k_2\)). [Guo 2023, pp. 1-2]
- **Fracture orientation**: dip direction \(q\), dip angle \(d\) (or converted polar coordinates \(a\), \(b\)); Fisher distribution parameters \(a_m\), \(b_m\), \(k_1\). [Guo 2023, pp. 2-4, 8-9]
- **Trace length**: mean \(m_l\) and standard deviation \(s_l\) on each sampling window; third moment \(E(l^3)\). [Guo 2023, pp. 5-7, 10-11]
- **Stereological constants**: \(\lambda = \iint \cos\phi \, f(a,b)\, da\, db\), \(\sigma = \iint \sqrt{...} \, g(k)g(u)\, dk\, du\) (Eqs. 10‑11), \(m_a\) (mean long axis), and derived quantities \(\sigma\), \(\eta\) (Eqs. 38‑39). [Guo 2023, pp. 4-5]
- **Sampling window**: orientation (\(a_s,\,b_s\)) and resulting trace statistics. [Guo 2023, pp. 7-8]
- **Optimization objective**: total error \(E_\text{sum} = E_1 + E_2 + \dots + E_6\) (Eq. 61). [Guo 2023, pp. 10]

## Reusable Claims
- The mean and standard deviation of trace length produced by UED fractures on an infinite sampling window are given by Eqs. (36)‑(37), which are general and reduce to those of non‑UED and circular disc models when the appropriate variables are fixed. This relationship is valid provided the assumptions (planar, independent parameters) hold. [Guo 2023, pp. 5-7, 12-13]
- The parameter estimation method using an optimization algorithm and Monte Carlo integration can recover size‑ and azimuth‑related parameters of the UED model from trace statistics of three or more sampling windows, with a typical mean error below 10 %. The method requires pre‑specified distribution forms for the unknowns. [Guo 2023, pp. 9-10, 11-12]
- The trace length distribution under the UED model varies with sampling window orientation; this variability can be exploited to distinguish the UED model from simpler models (circular disc, non‑UED) and to verify the assumed size distributions using the third moment. [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14, 10-11]
- In a hypothetical rectangular fracture network, the UED model reduces the maximum error in mean trace length across orientations from ~100 % (non‑UED) to ~21 %, and in standard deviation from ~67 % to ~12 %, demonstrating its improved representativeness. [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14]

## Connections To Other Work
- The work extends the elliptical disc model of Zhang et al. (2002) from a non‑universal form (constant \(k\) and constant long‑axis direction) to a universal form where both can vary, and provides explicit stereological formulae that incorporate orientation variability, improving on the constant‑orientation assumption. [Guo 2023, pp. 1-2, 12-13]
- The derived trace length relationships for the UED model are shown to degenerate to the circular disc equations of Zhang and Einstein (2000) when \(k=1\), confirming internal consistency. [Guo 2023, pp. 12-13]
- The work of Jin et al. (2014), which considered orientation variance in the non‑UED model, is revised: the present derivation shows that the angle \(u\) is not constant but depends on fracture and window orientations, and the formulae (69)‑(72) provide the corrected version. [Guo 2023, pp. 12-13]
- The UED model’s representation accuracy ARI was shown by Zheng et al. (2022) to be ~20 % higher than that of the non‑UED model, a finding that this study supports through trace‑based comparisons. [Guo 2023, pp. 1-2, 13-14]
- The parameter estimation framework is built on the forward stereological relationships analogous to those used for circular discs (Warburton 1980a) and non‑UED models (Zhang et al. 2002, Jin et al. 2014). [Guo 2023, pp. 1-2, 8-9]

## Open Questions
- How can the mean and standard deviation of trace length be corrected for biases (size, truncation, censoring) that arise from finite sampling windows? [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14]
- What is the explicit form of the trace length distribution under different combinations of size and azimuth distributions for the UED model? Can unrealistic distributions be eliminated by comparing predicted and observed trace distributions? [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14]
- Can the parameter estimation method be extended to cases where fewer than three sampling windows are available, possibly by combining borehole data? [Guo 2023, pp. 13-14]
