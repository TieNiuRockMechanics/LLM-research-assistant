# Rift-to-Collision Transition Recorded by Tectonothermal Evolution of the Northern Pyrenees.

## One-line Summary
This study presents a tectonothermal model for the Pyrenees, showing that Late Paleozoic and mid-Cretaceous thinning events focused deformation in the northern Pyrenees, with main exhumation and cooling occurring at 50–35 Ma when less thinned, stronger proximal margins collided.

## Research Question
The study aims to evaluate the role of rifted margin architecture in the tectonic and thermal evolution of the French central Pyrenees, focusing on the Ariège area, to understand the transition from rifting to collision.

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the northern Pyrenees, specifically the Ariège region (Central NPZ), positioned along the ECORS profile. Data consist of new low-temperature thermochronological analyses (AHe, AFT, ZHe, ZFT) from 13 bedrock samples collected from the Arize and Trois-Seigneurs Paleozoic crystalline massifs, and one detrital sample from the Camarade basin. Additional constraints come from RSCM temperatures and existing literature data [Vacherat 2015, pp. 1-3, 8-9].

## Methods
The methods include apatite and zircon fission track (AFT, ZFT) analyses, (U-Th-Sm)/He (AHe, ZHe) analyses on bedrock and detrital samples, and inverse thermal history modeling using QTQt software. Forward modeling of ZHe data was performed using HeFTy to test damage-dependent diffusion models [Vacherat 2015, pp. 9-10, 13-14, 18-19].

## Key Findings
1.  Rifting and related cooling started in the Late Paleozoic-Triassic, followed by slow heating during Jurassic-Early Cretaceous post-rift subsidence [Vacherat 2015, pp. 1-1].
2.  A second episode of crustal thinning occurred at 130–110 Ma, controlled by south-dipping normal faults, leading to differential cooling and subsidence in the granitic massifs [Vacherat 2015, pp. 1-1, 21-23].
3.  Onset of convergence at ~83 Ma did not produce a clear cooling event in the studied massifs; instead, temperatures remained high (200–300°C) due to a persistent high geothermal gradient, with deformation focused in weak, hyperextended basins [Vacherat 2015, pp. 1-1, 23-24].
4.  The main phase of exhumation-related cooling is recognized across the Pyrenees at 50–35 Ma, with cooling rates of 12–15°C/Myr (potentially up to 50°C/Myr between 48–45 Ma), corresponding to exhumation rates of 0.5–1.6 km/Myr [Vacherat 2015, pp. 1-1, 23-24].
5.  This timing reveals that mountain building started when hyperextended rift basins closed and collision between proximal domains of the rifted margin occurred [Vacherat 2015, pp. 1-1].

## Limitations
1.  The damage-dependent diffusion model for ZHe (Guenthner et al., 2013) could not quantitatively reproduce the observed date-eU correlations, limiting full integration of ZHe data into inversions [Vacherat 2015, pp. 19-20].
2.  The exact original position of samples within each granite body is unknown; samples are assumed to be organized in a vertical column by current elevation [Vacherat 2015, pp. 16-18].
3.  The thermal structure (geothermal gradient) of the area is not independently constrained and is a free parameter in the models [Vacherat 2015, pp. 16-18].
4.  Higher-temperature thermochronometers are lacking to detail the cooling pattern between 300–200 Ma [Vacherat 2015, pp. 18-19].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The studied Variscan granites crystallized at ~305±5 Ma and cooled from ~900±50°C [Vacherat 2015, pp. 16-18].
2.  During the Triassic (~225±25 Ma), the granites were buried at less than 3 km depth (<100°C) based on proximity to Triassic cover [Vacherat 2015, pp. 16-18].
3.  Samples are treated as a vertical column with positions defined by current elevation for thermal modeling [Vacherat 2015, pp. 16-18].
4.  The vertical geothermal gradient is adjusted with time during model runs [Vacherat 2015, pp. 16-18].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** In the northern Pyrenees (Ariège region). **Claim:** The main phase of exhumation and mountain building, recorded by rapid cooling, occurred between 50 and 35 Ma, with rates of 12–15°C/Myr. **Limitation:** This timing is inferred from low-temperature thermochronology on granitic massifs and may not apply to all structural domains [Vacherat 2015, pp. 23-24].
2.  **Condition:** Following the onset of Iberia-Europe convergence at ~83 Ma. **Claim:** Initial convergence did not produce widespread cooling in the proximal European margin; instead, deformation and heating were focused in weak, hyperextended rift basins (e.g., Aulus basin) due to the persistence of a high geothermal gradient. **Limitation:** The thermal state of the hyperextended domain is inferred from RSCM and metamorphic data [Vacherat 2015, pp. 23-24].
3.  **Condition:** During the mid-Cretaceous (130–110 Ma). **Claim:** A second episode of crustal thinning occurred, controlled by south-dipping normal faults, leading to differential cooling (footwall exhumation) and subsidence (hangingwall burial) across the rifted margin. **Limitation:** The geometry and extent of these faults are interpreted from thermochronological patterns and basin stratigraphy [Vacherat 2015, pp. 21-23].

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on and compares with thermochronological work in the western Pyrenees [Vacherat et al., 2014], the central Pyrenees Axial Zone [Fitzgerald et al., 1999; Sinclair et al., 2005; Metcalf et al., 2009], and the southern Pyrenees [Beamud et al., 2011; Whitchurch et al., 2011]. It discusses the role of rift architecture in collision, drawing analogies to the Alps [Mohn et al., 2014] and Taiwan [Mesalles et al., 2014]. The tectonic model incorporates plate kinematics [Roest and Srivastava, 1991; Mouthereau et al., 2014] and deep seismic data [Chevrot et al., 2015].

## Open Questions
1.  What is the exact role and kinematic history (strike-slip vs. extension) of the North Pyrenean Fault (NPF) during rifting and collision? [Vacherat 2015, pp. 3-5]
2.  What mechanism—large-scale overthrusting or originally highly variable geothermal gradients—primarily controls the sharp discontinuities in peak metamorphic temperatures observed between adjacent basins? [Vacherat 2015, pp. 8-9]
3.  Can new experimental data and modeling approaches improve the calibration of damage-dependent diffusion models for zircon (U-Th)/He thermochronology to better resolve the 200–120°C thermal window? [Vacherat 2015, pp. 19-20]
