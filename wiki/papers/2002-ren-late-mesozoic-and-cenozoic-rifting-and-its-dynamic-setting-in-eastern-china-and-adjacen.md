# Late Mesozoic and Cenozoic Rifting and Its Dynamic Setting in Eastern China and Adjacent Areas.

## One-line Summary

The paper synthesizes the spatial-temporal distribution, subsidence history, and plate-tectonic/deep-lithosphere dynamic setting of Late Mesozoic–Cenozoic rifting in eastern China and adjacent areas, distinguishing four rifting stages and three rifting styles and proposing a combination model of plate interactions and asthenospheric processes.

## Research Question

How did Late Mesozoic and Cenozoic rifting evolve in eastern China and adjacent areas, and what plate-tectonic and asthenospheric dynamics drove its multiple stages and styles?

## Study Area / Data

The study area covers Eastern China and adjacent areas, including the Sino‑Korean Craton, Yangtze Craton, Siberia Craton, Mongol‑Okhotsk Fold Belt, and surrounding marginal seas (Japan Sea, South China Sea, etc.) [Ren 2002, pp. 1-2, pp. 2-4]. Data integrated include: geological and geophysical data from onshore and offshore Eastern China and adjacent areas; well and seismic profiles from oil, gas, and coal exploration; published works on basin fill, structures, subsidence history, and volcanism [Ren 2002, pp. 1-2, pp. 2-4, pp. 12-13]. Basins analyzed include Songliao, Bohaiwan, Yinggerhai, Qiongdongnan, Pearl River Mouth, Beibuwan, etc. [Ren 2002, pp. 12-13, pp. 14-17].

## Methods

- Compilation of a new distribution map of Late Mesozoic and Cenozoic extensional basins [Ren 2002, pp. 2-4].
- Backstripping analysis of selected basins (Songliao, Bohaiwan, northern South China Sea basins) to derive tectonic subsidence curves, using empirical porosity/depth relations and local isostasy [Ren 2002, pp. 12-13].
- Integration of seismic profiles, well data, isopach maps, and age determinations (fossil zones) to identify unconformities, rifting episodes, and structural styles [Ren 2002, pp. 13-14, pp. 14-17].
- Plate kinematic analysis using published convergence rate curves for Pacific–Eurasia and India–Eurasia [Ren 2002, pp. 18-19].
- Deep lithosphere modeling referencing lithospheric thickness estimates from geophysical data, paleogeotherm indicators, and volcanic geochemistry [Ren 2002, pp. 22-23].

## Key Findings

- Four rifting stages were identified: (1) Late Mesozoic (Late Jurassic–Early Cretaceous) rifting, (2) Early Tertiary (Paleogene) rifting, (3) Neogene intracontinental rifting and marginal sea spreading, (4) Quaternary rifting [Ren 2002, pp. 2-4, pp. 25-26].
- Late Mesozoic rifting produced the Northeast Asia fault basin system, covering >2 million km², with three phases: calc‑alkaline volcanism along NNE faults, Basin‑and‑Range‑type fault basin development, and a postrift thermal subsidence phase (e.g., Songliao basin) [Ren 2002, pp. 1-2, pp. 4-6].
- Dextral transtension along large strike‑slip faults (e.g., Tancheng–Lujiang) controlled many Late Mesozoic and Cenozoic basins [Ren 2002, pp. 6-8].
- Early Tertiary rifting initiated with alkaline basalts, forming continental rift belts (Bohaiwan basin up to 6 km fill) and continental marginal basins (South China Sea margin) [Ren 2002, pp. 8-11].
- Bohaiwan basin subsidence history shows two rifting episodes (first with E–W trending basins, second with NE–NNE trending), terminated by a regional unconformity and followed by thermal sag [Ren 2002, pp. 13-14].
- Northern South China Sea basins (Pearl River Mouth, Qiongdongnan, Yinggerhai) experienced episodic rifting with three episodes, breakup unconformities diachronous, and postrift rapid subsidence events [Ren 2002, pp. 14-17].
- Three rifting styles are distinguished: (1) intracontinental rifting within fold belts (pervasive extension, e.g., Northeast Asia fault basin system), (2) intracontinental rifting within cratons (controlled by large strike‑slip faults, e.g., Bohaiwan, Yinggerhai), (3) continental marginal rifting and spreading (back‑arc extension, pull‑apart, etc.) [Ren 2002, pp. 17-18, pp. 25-26].
- The timing of rifting correlates with variations in India–Eurasia and Pacific–Eurasia convergence rates: rapid India–Eurasia convergence in early Tertiary caused NW–SE extensional stress and dextral transtension; slower Pacific convergence during same period reduced transpression [Ren 2002, pp. 18-19].
- A combination model is proposed: interactions between plates and deep lithospheric processes (asthenospheric upwelling induced by lithospheric thickness steps and fault motion) drove rifting and volcanism [Ren 2002, pp. 1-2, pp. 22-25, pp. 25-26].
- Deep structure evidence: thin lithosphere (60–120 km) under rifted areas, elevated geothermal gradients (e.g., Songliao 3.11–4.8 °C/100 m, Bohaiwan high heat flow), low‑velocity upper mantle anomalies from tomography, and volcanism coincident with rifts [Ren 2002, pp. 22-23].

## Limitations

- The paper is a synthesis; individual basin histories rely on published data and interpretations that may be locally inconsistent [Ren 2002, pp. 1-2, pp. 2-4].
- Deep lithosphere models and asthenospheric upwelling mechanisms are speculative and based on indirect evidence (tomography, thermal anomalies, geochemistry) [Ren 2002, pp. 22-25].
- The correlation between plate convergence rates and rifting stages is qualitative and does not provide a quantitative stress model [Ren 2002, pp. 18-19].
- Limited data from the southern South China Sea and some offshore areas may affect completeness of rifting history [Ren 2002, pp. 8-11].
- Subsidence analysis assumes local isostasy and empirical porosity‑depth relations; deviations from local isostasy are not accounted for [Ren 2002, pp. 12-13].

## Assumptions / Conditions

- Local isostatic behavior of lithosphere during backstripping [Ren 2002, pp. 12-13].
- Porosity‑depth relations are representative for the lithologies encountered [Ren 2002, pp. 12-13].
- Stratigraphic age assignments from fossils and radiometric dating are correct [Ren 2002, pp. 2-4].
- Plate convergence rates from Engebretson et al. (1985), Northrup et al. (1995), and Lee & Lawver (1995) are accurate for the region [Ren 2002, pp. 18-19].
- The kinematic model that India–Eurasia convergence extruded blocks and caused dextral transtension along pre‑existing faults is valid [Ren 2002, pp. 18-19].
- Lithospheric thickness steps and fault motion induce secondary convection cells (after King & Anderson, 1995) [Ren 2002, pp. 22-25].

## Key Variables / Parameters

- Convergence rates: Pacific–Eurasia (varying from ~30 to 140 mm/yr), India–Eurasia (up to 180 mm/yr) [Ren 2002, pp. 18-19].
- Lithospheric thickness: pre‑rift ~200 km under Sino‑Korean craton; post‑rift 60–120 km under rift basins [Ren 2002, pp. 22-23].
- Tectonic subsidence rates: Songliao synrift 80–100 m/Ma, Bohaiwan synrift max ~140 m/Ma, postrift <20 m/Ma; South China Sea basins 40–250 m/Ma during rifting [Ren 2002, pp. 12-17].
- Heat flow: ranges from 1.6 to 2.53 HFU in Bohaiwan, 71.9–97.6 mW/m² in South China Sea basins [Ren 2002, pp. 22-23].
- Stretching factors: Songliao area up to 1.6; west of Daxing’anling 1.1–1.3 [Ren 2002, pp. 23-25].
- Geothermal gradient: Songliao 3.11–4.8 °C/100 m (max 6.2); South China Sea average 3.76 °C/100 m [Ren 2002, pp. 22-23].

## Reusable Claims

- Late Mesozoic rifting in Northeast Asia progressed from calc‑alkaline volcanism (J3–K1) to Basin‑and‑Range fault basin development and then to postrift thermal subsidence; this evolutionary sequence can serve as a template for wide intracontinental rifting [Ren 2002, pp. 4-6, pp. 12-13].
- Dextral transtension along major NNE‑trending strike‑slip faults (e.g., Tancheng–Lujiang) controlled the orientation and subsidence of Late Mesozoic and Cenozoic basins; the pattern of basin trends changing with distance from the fault indicates dextral shear [Ren 2002, pp. 6-8].
- The Bohaiwan basin is a superimposed basin with two distinct synrift episodes (first E–W trending, second NE–NNE trending), separated by unconformities, and cannot be explained by a simple pull‑apart model [Ren 2002, pp. 13-14].
- On the northern South China Sea margin, breakup unconformities are diachronous (Oligocene in Pearl River Mouth, Oligocene–Miocene boundary in Qiongdongnan/Yinggerhai) and postrift subsidence was episodic with rapid events in Miocene and Pliocene–Recent [Ren 2002, pp. 14-17].
- Convergence rate changes between India–Eurasia and Pacific–Eurasia correlate with extensional phases: rapid India convergence and slow Pacific convergence promoted NW–SE extension and rifting, while fast Pacific convergence led to compression and basin inversion [Ren 2002, pp. 18-19].
- Asthenospheric upwelling under rifted areas is evidenced by thin lithosphere, high heat flow, low‑velocity mantle anomalies, and volcanism; it may be induced by convective instabilities around lithospheric steps and fault‑related decompression [Ren 2002, pp. 22-25].

## Candidate Concepts

- [[episodic rifting]] – Multiple rift episodes within a single basin or margin, separated by unconformities or changes in stress regime [Ren 2002, pp. 13-14, pp. 14-17].
- [[wide rifting]] – Pervasive extension across an orogenic belt producing numerous small to medium fault basins, analogous to Basin and Range (Buck, 1991) [Ren 2002, pp. 17-18].
- [[dextral transtension]] – Strike‑slip faulting with a right‑lateral component that simultaneously accommodates extension, generating pull‑apart or oblique rift basins [Ren 2002, pp. 6-8].
- [[asthenospheric upwelling]] – Upward movement of hot asthenosphere, thinning the lithosphere, raising heat flow, and driving volcanism and uplift [Ren 2002, pp. 22-25].
- [[breakup unconformity]] – Regional unconformity separating synrift from postrift sequences, marking the transition from fault‑controlled subsidence to thermal sag [Ren 2002, pp. 12-13, pp. 14-17].
- [[intracontinental rifting within fold belt]] – Rifting style where pre‑existing orogenic fractures control basin formation, leading to widespread distributed extension [Ren 2002, pp. 17-18].
- [[intracontinental rifting within craton]] – Rifting style dominated by large strike‑slip faults, often producing pull‑apart basins or releasing bends [Ren 2002, pp. 17-18].
- [[continental marginal rifting and spreading]] – Back‑arc extension and seafloor spreading in marginal seas, often associated with subduction rollback or collision‑induced extrusion [Ren 2002, pp. 17-18].

## Candidate Methods

- [[backstripping]] – Removal of sediment load using decompaction and isostatic correction to reconstruct tectonic subsidence, applied with local isostasy assumption [Ren 2002, pp. 12-13].
- [[subsidence analysis]] – Derivation of subsidence rate curves to identify synrift and postrift phases and episodic subsidence [Ren 2002, pp. 12-13, pp. 13-14].
- [[convergence rate comparison]] – Using published plate motion models to correlate changes in convergence directions and rates with regional stress fields and rifting stages [Ren 2002, pp. 18-19].
- [[isopach mapping]] – Mapping sediment thickness to identify depocenter shifts and fault control [Ren 2002, pp. 13-14].
- [[seismic‑stratigraphic interpretation]] – Identifying unconformities, fault geometries, and basin fill sequences from seismic profiles [Ren 2002, pp. 13-14, pp. 14-17].
- [[geothermometry from xenoliths]] – Using xenolith‑derived paleogeotherms to infer pre‑rift lithospheric thickness [Ren 2002, pp. 22-23].

## Open Questions

- Why did Late Mesozoic rifting concentrate in the Mongol–Okhotsk fold belt while the cratons remained relatively stable? (Speculated to be due to lithospheric structure, but direct evidence remains limited) [Ren 2002, pp. 22-25].
- The exact mechanism by which India–Eurasia convergence translates into NW–SE extension in East China is not fully quantified; alternative models (e.g., mantle flow, slab pull) need further testing [Ren 2002, pp. 18-19].
- The role of pre‑existing Mesozoic structures in localizing Cenozoic rifting and the interaction between deep mantle upwelling and surface faulting requires more resolved geophysical imaging [Ren 2002, pp. 22-25].
- The cause of episodic postrift subsidence in the South China Sea margins (e.g., rapid Miocene and Pliocene events) is not fully understood; possibilities include renewed mantle convection or far‑field stress changes [Ren 2002, pp. 14-17].
- The relationship between volcanic pulses and specific rifting episodes, especially the transition from calc‑alkaline to bimodal volcanism, could constrain the thermo‑mechanical state of the lithosphere [Ren 2002, pp. 4-6].
