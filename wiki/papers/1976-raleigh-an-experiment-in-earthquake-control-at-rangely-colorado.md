# An Experiment in Earthquake Control at Rangely, Colorado.

## One-line Summary
An experiment at the Rangely Oil Field demonstrated that seismic activity could be controlled by manipulating subsurface fluid pressure, confirming the effective stress hypothesis for earthquake triggering.

## Research Question
Can earthquakes be controlled by altering fluid pressure in a fault zone, based on the hypothesis that increased pore pressure reduces effective normal stress and frictional resistance to sliding? [Mar 1976, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
The experiment was conducted in the Rangely Oil Field, a doubly plunging anticline in Colorado. The principal reservoir rock is the Pennsylvanian and Permian Weber sandstone, encountered at about 1700 m depth. The field had been under waterflood (high-pressure water injection for secondary oil recovery) since 1957, which raised reservoir pressures above the original 170 bars. A seismic network of 14 short-period seismometers was installed in 1969 to monitor microearthquakes. [Mar 1976, pp. 1-1, 1-2, 2-3]

## Methods
1.  **Seismic Monitoring:** A local network recorded and located earthquakes. Magnitudes were estimated using signal duration. [Mar 1976, pp. 2-3]
2.  **Stress Measurement:** Hydraulic fracturing was performed in a borehole at 2 km depth in the Weber sandstone to determine the in-situ state of stress. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4]
3.  **Reservoir Pressure Control & Monitoring:** Fluid pressure was varied by alternately injecting water into and back-flowing it from four experimental wells penetrating the seismic zone. Pressure was monitored continuously in observation wells and via periodic bottom-hole surveys. A reservoir simulation model was used to infer pressure distributions. [Mar 1976, pp. 4-5, 5-6]
4.  **Laboratory Testing:** The coefficient of static friction (μ = 0.81) and tensile strength (~100 bars) of Weber sandstone were measured on core samples. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4]

## Key Findings
1.  Earthquake activity was strongly correlated with fluid pressure in the Weber sandstone. When pressure was raised above a predicted critical value (~257 bars), seismicity increased; when pressure was lowered below it, seismicity ceased. [Mar 1976, pp. 5-6, 6-7]
2.  The experiment confirmed the Hubbert-Rubey effective stress principle: the critical fluid pressure (Pc) for triggering earthquakes on pre-existing fractures was successfully predicted using measured stresses and friction. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4, 6-7]
3.  The cessation of seismic activity within one day of initiating back-flow in May 1973 provided strong evidence for the direct causal link between fluid pressure and earthquakes. [Mar 1976, pp. 6-7]
4.  The fault zone was not a single surface but a broad zone of subparallel fractures with right-lateral strike-slip motion. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4]

## Limitations
1.  The vertical distribution of fluid pressure in the fault zone was not known. [Mar 1976, pp. 5-6]
2.  There are no data on the permeability of the fault zone at depths where earthquakes occur, which is critical for assessing the feasibility of control on larger faults. [Mar 1976, pp. 6-7]
3.  Earthquake locations had a systematic northward bias of 200-300 m due to lateral velocity variations, though they were presented uncorrected. [Mar 1976, pp. 2-3]
4.  The experiment was limited to a small, shallow fault zone within an oil field; applicability to major, deep faults like the San Andreas is unknown. [Mar 1976, pp. 6-7, 7-8]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The effective stress principle (τ = (Sn - P)μ) governs fault failure at the reservoir scale. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4, 6-7]
2.  The fluid pressure measured in well Fee69 was representative of the pressure in the earthquake zone beneath the injection wells. [Mar 1976, pp. 5-6]
3.  The coefficient of static friction measured on laboratory samples of Weber sandstone is applicable to the natural fault gouge. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4]
4.  The fault zone's permeability was sufficient to allow rapid pressure communication from the wells to the hypocentral region. [Mar 1976, pp. 6-7]

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Fluid Pressure (P):** The controlled variable, monitored in bars. Critical value Pc ≈ 257 bars. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4, 5-6]
-   **Effective Normal Stress (Sn - P):** The difference between total normal stress and pore pressure. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4]
-   **Shear Stress (τ):** Resolved on the fault plane (72 bars). [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4]
-   **Coefficient of Static Friction (μ):** 0.81 for Weber sandstone. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4]
-   **Seismicity Rate:** Number of earthquakes per month, used as the primary output measure. [Mar 1976, pp. 5-6, 6-7]

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** In a fault zone with known stress state and frictional properties. **Claim:** The fluid pressure required to trigger earthquakes can be predicted using the Hubbert-Rubey effective stress failure criterion. [Mar 1976, pp. 3-4, 6-7]
2.  **Condition:** When fluid pressure in a seismogenic fault zone is reduced below the critical threshold. **Claim:** Seismic activity can be arrested, demonstrating a direct causal control mechanism. [Mar 1976, pp. 5-6, 6-7]
3.  **Condition:** For induced seismicity caused by subsurface fluid injection. **Claim:** The hazard can potentially be mitigated by drilling wells to pump fluid from the fault zone, thereby reducing pore pressure and increasing frictional strength. [Mar 1976, pp. 6-7]

## Connections To Other Work
The experiment was motivated by the 1966 Denver earthquakes triggered by fluid injection at the Rocky Mountain Arsenal, which suggested earthquakes might be controllable. [Mar 1976, pp. 1-1] The work builds on laboratory studies of the effect of pore pressure on brittle failure and the theoretical framework of Hubbert and Rubey. [Mar 1976, pp. 6-7] The authors propose that the principles demonstrated could be applied to mitigate hazards from reservoir filling and suggest initial experiments on the San Andreas fault. [Mar 1976, pp. 6-7, 7-8]

## Open Questions
1.  Is the permeability of major fault zones at seismogenic depths sufficient to allow practical fluid pressure control? [Mar 1976, pp. 6-7]
2.  Can the scheme of creating fluid pressure barriers to limit earthquake size be applied to large, mature faults like the San Andreas? [Mar 1976, pp. 6-7, 7-8]
3.  What are the temperature, stress, pore pressure, and material properties at depth along major faults? [Mar 1976, pp. 7-8]
