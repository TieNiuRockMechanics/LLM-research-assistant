# Analysis of Shear Wave Splitting Anisotropy in the Tres Virgenes Volcanic Complex, Baja California Sur, Mexico.

## One-line Summary
This study uses shear wave splitting analysis of micro-earthquakes to reveal that seismic anisotropy in the Tres Virgenes Volcanic Complex is controlled by two superimposed fault systems, influencing geothermal fluid pathways.

## Research Question
The research aims to analyze seismic anisotropy via shear wave splitting to characterize the fracture systems, stress field, and their control on the geothermal reservoir in the Tres Virgenes Volcanic Complex [Chac 2021, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Tres Virgenes Volcanic Complex (TVVC) and its associated geothermal field in Baja California Sur, Mexico. The data comprise 558 micro-earthquakes recorded from 2009 to 2013 by a network of seismic stations, with depths ranging from 1 to 10 km and local magnitudes between 1.0 and 3.0 [Chac 2021, pp. 6-8]. The seismicity is located in granitic rocks of the Peninsular Range Batholith, which forms the substratum of the geothermal field [Chac 2021, pp. 1-2].

## Methods
The methodology involved a shear wave splitting (SWS) analysis. Hypocentral parameters were determined using the Seisan package with a specific velocity model [Chac 2021, pp. 4-5]. Seismic records were rotated into pseudo-vertical, radial, and transverse components. The fast shear wave polarization (ϕ) and delay time (δt) were estimated using cross-correlation techniques on the rotated components [Chac 2021, pp. 4-6]. Statistical analysis, including circular statistics and hypothesis tests (t-tests), was applied to assess spatial and temporal variations in the parameters [Chac 2021, pp. 8-13].

## Key Findings
- The analysis detected irregular spatial and temporal changes in birefringence parameters [Chac 2021, pp. 1-2].
- Fast shear wave polarizations (ϕ) show predominant NW-SE orientations, with secondary N-S and NE-SW directions [Chac 2021, pp. 8-10].
- Mean delay times (δt) range from 0.015 s to 0.058 s, with some individual values reaching 0.16 s [Chac 2021, pp. 1-2].
- The anisotropy is interpreted as being controlled by two superimposed systems: a regional, deep NW-SE system related to Gulf of California opening, and a local, shallower system with NW-SE, N-S, and NE-SW polarizations associated with faults affecting the TVVC [Chac 2021, pp. 18-18].
- The intersection of these fault systems at depths of 5-6 km is inferred to create fracture networks allowing geothermal fluid flow [Chac 2021, pp. 1-2].
- Seismicity is scarce between 1-2 km depth (the geothermal reservoir) and below 6 km (interpreted as the brittle-ductile transition at ~350°C) [Chac 2021, pp. 1-2, 18-18].

## Limitations
- The δt parameter is less robust than ϕ and involves greater uncertainty, especially in complex fractured rocks, making automatic analysis unreliable [Chac 2021, pp. 5-6].
- The study relies on a specific velocity model and the assumption of a Vp/Vs ratio of 1.70 [Chac 2021, pp. 4-5].
- The interpretation of anisotropy sources is complex, as seismic waves may be affected by multiple fault sets along their path [Chac 2021, pp. 13-14].
- The number of analyzed events per station and year varied, with some stations having limited data (e.g., TV05 in 2010) [Chac 2021, pp. 11-13].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The shear wave splitting is primarily controlled by aligned fractures and cracks, with the fast polarization (ϕ) parallel to crack planes and the maximum horizontal stress [Chac 2021, pp. 2-2].
- The delay time (δt) is proportional to the magnitude of anisotropy along the travel path [Chac 2021, pp. 2-2].
- Seismic events with an incidence angle higher than 45° were discarded to avoid surface effects [Chac 2021, pp. 6-8].
- The velocity model from Lermo et al. (2014) is assumed to be representative of the study area [Chac 2021, pp. 4-5].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Fast Shear Wave Polarization (ϕ):** The azimuthal direction of the fast shear wave, indicating the orientation of fractures or maximum horizontal stress.
- **Delay Time (δt):** The time difference between the fast and slow shear wave arrivals, indicating the magnitude of anisotropy.
- **Mean Polarization Direction (MPD):** The average ϕ for a station or region, calculated using circular statistics.
- **Mean Delay Time (MDT):** The average δt for a station or region.
- **Hypocentral Depth:** The depth of the seismic event, used to relate anisotropy to geological structures.

## Reusable Claims
- In the Tres Virgenes Volcanic Complex, the dominant NW-SE fast shear wave polarization direction is controlled by regional strike-slip faults associated with the transtensional tectonic regime of the Gulf of California opening [Chac 2021, pp. 17-18].
- The presence of secondary N-S and NE-SW polarizations at depths shallower than 5 km indicates a local anisotropic system controlled by faults affecting the volcanic complex, such as the El Cimarrón Fault [Chac 2021, pp. 15-18].
- The intersection of the regional NW-SE and local N-S/NE-SW fault systems at depths of 5-6 km likely creates fracture networks that facilitate geothermal fluid ascent to the surface [Chac 2021, pp. 1-2, 18-18].
- Observed spatial and temporal variations in shear wave splitting parameters in this study are primarily due to azimuthal anisotropy variations (changes in the relative event-station-fault geometry) rather than actual temporal changes in the stress field [Chac 2021, pp. 13-14, 17-18].

## Connections To Other Work
The study references numerous works applying shear wave splitting in geothermal fields (e.g., Evans et al., 1995; Elkibbi et al., 2005; Rial et al., 2005) and volcanic environments (e.g., Crampin et al., 1986; Bianco et al., 1998) [Chac 2021, pp. 1-2]. The interpretation of fault systems and tectonic context draws on regional studies of the Gulf of California opening (e.g., Stock and Hodges, 1989; Henry and Aranda-Gómez, 1992) [Chac 2021, pp. 2-3].

## Open Questions
- What is the precise role of magmatic intrusions (e.g., dikes) versus fault-controlled fractures in generating the observed anisotropy?
- How do temporal changes in geothermal injection-production operations directly influence the stress field and anisotropy, beyond the geometric effects analyzed?
- Can the two anisotropic systems be more precisely delineated in 3D using denser seismic arrays or other geophysical methods?
- What is the exact geometry and connectivity of the inferred fracture networks at 5-6 km depth that control fluid flow?
