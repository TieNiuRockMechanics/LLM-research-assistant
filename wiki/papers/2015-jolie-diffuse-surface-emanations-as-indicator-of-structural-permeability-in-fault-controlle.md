# Diffuse Surface Emanations as Indicator of Structural Permeability in Fault-Controlled Geothermal Systems.

## One-line Summary
This study demonstrates that a multi-parameter analysis of diffuse surface emanations (CO2, H2S, radon, thoron, gamma radiation) can successfully detect and characterize permeable fault zones that control fluid flow in geothermal systems, even in areas lacking obvious surface manifestations.

## Research Question
How can measurements of diffuse surface emanations be used to identify, map, and assess the relative structural permeability of fault zones in fault-controlled geothermal systems?

## Study Area / Data
The study was conducted at the Brady's geothermal system in the Basin-and-Range Province (Nevada, USA), an extensional amagmatic medium-enthalpy system with reservoir temperatures up to 212 °C [Jolie 2015, pp. 1-2]. The study area measures approximately 5.6 km × 3.9 km and is characterized by the NNE-oriented Brady's fault zone and its structural step-over [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-4]. Data were collected in March and April 2012 and include:
*   CO2 and H2S flux measurements at 925 sites using the accumulation chamber method [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-4].
*   Radon (222Rn) activity concentration at 476 sites and thoron (220Rn) at 474 sites using alpha-spectroscopy [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-4].
*   Gamma radiation at 10,521 sites using a roving principle and point measurements [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-4].

## Methods
A combination of in-situ geochemical and radiometric techniques was applied:
1.  **Accumulation Chamber Method**: Used a portable diffuse flux meter with a LICOR LI-820 NDIR analyzer for CO2 and a Draeger Polytron II electrochemical detector for H2S to measure soil gas flux [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-4].
2.  **Alpha-Spectroscopy**: Used RTM 2200 monitors to measure activity concentrations of 222Rn (radon) and 220Rn (thoron) in soil gas sampled from 1 m depth [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-4].
3.  **Gamma-Spectroscopy**: Used RTM 2200 monitors with a sodium iodide scintillator to measure gamma radiation dose rates and specific nuclides (40K, 214Bi, 208Tl) [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-5].
4.  **Data Processing**: Geostatistical analysis involved identifying data populations via probability plots (Sinclair, 1974), declustering, normal score transformation, variogram modeling, and Gaussian simulations using simple kriging for interpolation [Jolie 2015, pp. 5-6].

## Key Findings
*   Degassing anomalies of CO2, H2S, and radon are strongly correlated with mapped fault zones, particularly at fault intersections, areas of high fault density, and the Brady's fault step-over zone [Jolie 2015, pp. 6-7, 9-10].
*   The multi-parameter approach revealed a more diversified fault pattern than previously mapped, identifying permeable structures even in areas without geothermal surface manifestations [Jolie 2015, pp. 15-15].
*   Radon/thoron (222Rn/220Rn) ratios help distinguish potential deep upflow zones (high ratios) from shallow gas origins (low ratios) [Jolie 2015, pp. 12-13].
*   Gamma radiation anomalies correlate with geothermal surface manifestations and the infiltration area for geothermal process water, suggesting it can indicate fluid migration [Jolie 2015, pp. 10-11].
*   The total diffuse CO2 emission from the 3.2 km² study area was estimated at 99 t/yr ± 13 t/yr [Jolie 2015, pp. 12-12].

## Limitations
*   The survey represents a spatial snapshot; temporal (e.g., seasonal) variations in degassing were not studied [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-12].
*   Gamma radiation measurements are influenced by surface geology (e.g., sedimentary vs. volcanic rocks) and anthropogenic infrastructure, making it difficult to isolate deep geothermal signals [Jolie 2015, pp. 10-11, 13-14].
*   Some anomalies (e.g., near an abandoned acreage) were linked to non-geothermal sources (e.g., agricultural residuals) [Jolie 2015, pp. 7-9].
*   The method for installing radon/thoron probes was difficult on bedrock or stony ground [Jolie 2015, pp. 15-15].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   Faults in the Brady's system remain permeable and sustain gas migration to the surface despite geothermal production and associated pressure drawdown [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-12].
*   Local recharge to the Brady's geothermal system is assumed to be minimal, implying only small seasonal effects on degassing [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-12].
*   Gas migration to the surface occurs via a combination of diffusion and advection through deep-reaching fault zones [Jolie 2015, pp. 2-2].
*   The major source of radon and thoron is the uranium- and thorium-bearing Mesozoic basement rocks [Jolie 2015, pp. 14-15].

## Reusable Claims
*   In fault-controlled geothermal systems, areas of increased diffuse gas emissions (CO2, H2S, radon) are indicative of major upflow zones through deep-reaching, permeable fault zones [Jolie 2015, pp. 1-2].
*   The combination of accumulation chamber (CO2, H2S), alpha-spectroscopic (radon, thoron), and gamma-spectroscopic measurements provides a comprehensive tool for detecting and characterizing permeable structural elements in geothermal exploration [Jolie 2015, pp. 2-4].
*   Radon/thoron ratios can be used for a preliminary distinction between deep (increased ratio) and shallow (low ratio) origins of degassing anomalies in areas of active faulting [Jolie 2015, pp. 12-13].
*   Diffuse degassing measurements can identify permeable fault structures in areas without obvious geothermal surface manifestations, making them valuable for initial exploration phases [Jolie 2015, pp. 15-15].
*   Gamma radiation anomalies can be caused by geothermal fluid migration, as evidenced by high values at the infiltration area for geothermal process water [Jolie 2015, pp. 10-11].

## Open Questions

*   How can gamma radiation measurements be improved to reliably distinguish deep geothermal signals from near-surface lithological or anthropogenic effects?
*   What are the detailed recharge mechanisms and regional flow paths of geothermal fluids in the Brady's system?
