# Natural Fractures in a Metamorphic Buried Hill Reservoir, Bozhong 19–6 Area, Bohai Bay Basin, China.

## One-line Summary
Lithology, tectonic stress, and weathering collectively control the formation and vertical/horizontal distribution of interconnected open tectonic and dissolution fractures in the Archaean metamorphic buried hill reservoir of Bozhong 19-6, with leucogneiss, cataclasite, and gneiss near faults in weathered fracture zones representing the optimal reservoir.

## Research Question
What are the influences of lithology, tectonics, and weathering on the formation, development, and distribution of natural fractures in the Archaean metamorphic buried hill reservoir in the BZ19-6 area, and how do these factors evolve to control reservoir quality?

## Study Area / Data
- **Location and Setting:** Bozhong (BZ) 19-6 gas field, southwest of the Bozhong Depression, Bohai Bay Basin, a Mesozoic–Cenozoic rift basin on the Eastern China Craton (offshore part). [Zhang 2023, pp. 1-3] The study area has a proven reserve of over 1000 × 10⁸ m³. [Zhang 2023, pp. 1-1]
- **Target Reservoir:** Archaean metamorphic rocks at a depth of 4000–5500 m, thickness up to 983 m. [Zhang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- **Data Sources:** 50.89 m of cores and 460 sidewall cores from 17 exploration wells; 460 thin sections (impregnated with blue-dye resin, stained for carbonates); whole-rock X-ray diffraction (XRD) of 20 samples and clay-fraction XRD; conventional well logs (GR, CAL, SP, resistivity, CNCF, ZDEN, DT) and micro-resistivity imaging logs from 7 wells. [Zhang 2023, pp. 4-4]

## Methods
- **Fracture Identification and Characterization:** Core and sidewall core observation; thin-section petrography (polarizing microscope, manual point-counting). [Zhang 2023, pp. 4-4]
- **Imaging Log Analysis:** Manual interpretation of micro-resistivity imaging logs for fracture strike, dip angle, aperture, and filling status (open vs. filled). [Zhang 2023, pp. 4-4] [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-5]
- **Vertical Zonation:** Division of reservoir intervals into weathered fracture zone (WFZ), inner fracture zone (IFZ), and basement zone (BZ) based on trends in resistivity, density, acoustic, and neutron porosity logs. [Zhang 2023, pp. 7-7]
- **Mineral Composition and Lithology:** Petrographic lithology identification; XRD for whole-rock and clay mineral content; correlation of fracture linear density with brittle mineral content. [Zhang 2023, pp. 4-5]
- **Structural and Paleotopographic Analysis:** Comparison of fracture strike with fault orientations; analysis of fracture density vs. distance to faults and vs. thickness of overlying Shahejie Formation (as a proxy for paleo-topography). [Zhang 2023, pp. 10-11] [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-12]

## Key Findings
- **Fracture Types and Connectivity:** Both tectonic fractures (shear, tensile) and dissolution fractures are well developed; tectonic micro-fractures are intra- or intergranular. [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-5] 96.8% of fractures are open (from imaging logs of six wells), providing effective storage and fluid flow pathways. [Zhang 2023, pp. 1-1] [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-7]
- **Fracture Geometry:** Dominant fracture strike is ENE-WSW (from 2332 fractures). Oblique fractures (dip 30°–60°) constitute 49.89% of total; low-angle (26.85%) and high-angle (20.30%) are less common; sub-vertical (80°–90°) are only 2.96%. Fracture linear density is principally 1–8 m⁻¹ (maximum 17.5 m⁻¹). [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-7]
- **Lithology Control:** Fracture linear density decreases in order: leucogneiss (3.97 m⁻¹) > cataclasite (2.76 m⁻¹) > gneiss (2.61 m⁻¹) > migmatite gneiss (2.42 m⁻¹) > migmatite granite (1.83 m⁻¹). Fracture density increases with brittle mineral content (quartz, K-feldspar, plagioclase, calcite, dolomite) and decreases with biotite and clay content. High plagioclase in gneiss/cataclasite also promotes weathering and dissolution. [Zhang 2023, pp. 10-10] [Zhang 2023, pp. 10-11]
- **Tectonic Control:** Fracture strikes (ENE-WSW, WNW-ESE) are approximately parallel to adjacent normal faults. Mean fracture density decreases with increasing distance from faults due to reduced stress perturbation. The main fracture-forming events were the Indosinian (WNW-ESE thrust/shear), Late Yanshanian (ENE-WSW shear), and Himalayanian (EW tensile). [Zhang 2023, pp. 10-11] [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-11] [Zhang 2023, pp. 12-12]
- **Weathering Control and Vertical Zonation:**
    - Reservoir divides vertically into three zones based on weathering intensity: Weathered Fracture Zone (WFZ, top ~350 m), Inner Fracture Zone (IFZ, next ~350 m), and Basement Zone (BZ). [Zhang 2023, pp. 1-1] [Zhang 2023, pp. 7-7]
    - Fracture linear density and aperture decrease significantly with distance from the top of the buried hill (demarcation at ~350 m and ~700 m). Weathering-induced dissolution enlarges fractures; kaolinitic clay precipitation from strong weathering can clog pores/fractures at the very top of the WFZ. [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-12]
    - In the same WFZ, fracture linear density increases as the thickness of the overlying Shahejie Formation decreases; i.e., paleo-topographic highs experienced more intense weathering and have higher fracture density. [Zhang 2023, pp. 12-12]
- **Reservoir Evolution Model:** A five-stage evolutionary pattern from Pre-Indosinian basement formation to Himalayanian burial explains the combined effects of lithology and episodic tectonic fracturing and weathering, resulting in a ~700 m thick combined WFZ-IFZ reservoir zone. Prolonged subaerial exposure and weathering is a key reason for the far greater reserves in BZ19-6 (>1000 × 10⁸ m³) compared to the adjacent BZ13-2 area (~1 × 10⁸ m³). [Zhang 2023, pp. 12-13] [Zhang 2023, pp. 13-13]

## Limitations
- **Coring Artifacts:** Some secondary fractures may be induced during the coring procedure, and core-scale observation limits fracture network characterization. [Zhang 2023, pp. 4-4]
- **Thin Section Quality:** Badly weathered and broken samples make it difficult to produce reliable thin sections for petrographic or fracture analysis. [Zhang 2023, pp. 4-4]
- **Method-Specific Biases:** Manual point-counting on thin sections and manual picking on imaging logs are operator-dependent. [Zhang 2023, pp. 4-4]
- **Scope:** The study is limited to the BZ19-6 area; the specific lithological fracture susceptibility sequence may not be directly transferable to other basins with different migmatization histories. [Zhang 2023, pp. 10-10]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Brittleness Definition:** Brittle minerals are light-colored quartz, K-feldspar, plagioclase, calcite, and dolomite; dark minerals (biotite, clay) are ductile. [Zhang 2023, pp. 10-10]
- **Paleo-topography Proxy:** The thickness of the overlying Shahejie Formation is inversely related to paleo-topographic elevation. [Zhang 2023, pp. 12-12]
- **Weathering Depth Decay:** The intensity of physical and chemical weathering decreases with increasing burial depth from the paleo-surface (top of buried hill). [Zhang 2023, pp. 7-7]
- **Fracture-Fault Relationship:** Fracture density decreases with distance from a fault due to decay in stress perturbation, a relationship known from outcrop studies (e.g., Vitale and Isaia, 2014). [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-11]
- **Fluid Migration:** Open fractures act as both storage and fluid flow pathways; filled fractures are largely ineffective. [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-7]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Fracture Linear Density:** Number of fractures per meter interval (m⁻¹). [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-7]
- **Fracture Aperture:** Width of fracture opening (mm or μm), derived from imaging logs and thin sections. [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-5] [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-12]
- **Fracture Strike and Dip:** Orientation parameters from imaging logs. [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-7]
- **Vertical Zone Depths:** Distances from top of buried hill (WFZ: 0–~350 m; IFZ: ~350–~700 m; BZ: >~700 m). [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-12]
- **Mineral Composition:** Weight percent of quartz, K-feldspar, plagioclase, calcite, dolomite, biotite, and clay minerals from XRD. [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-5]
- **Distance to Fault:** Proximity of a well to the nearest major fault plane. [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-11]
- **Overlying Sediment Thickness:** Thickness of Shahejie Formation, used as a paleo-topography indicator. [Zhang 2023, pp. 12-12]

## Reusable Claims
- In the BZ19-6 Archaean metamorphic buried hill, leucogneiss, cataclasite, and gneiss host higher fracture linear densities than migmatite granite, because their higher brittle mineral (quartz, feldspar, carbonate) and plagioclase contents promote fracturing and dissolution. [Zhang 2023, pp. 10-10]
- Fracture linear density increases with proximity to faults in the BZ19-6 metamorphic reservoir, consistent with stress-perturbation models. [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-11]
- Weathering intensity in the BZ19-6 buried hill decays with depth, allowing vertical subdivision into a Weathered Fracture Zone (upper ~350 m, highest fracture density/aperture), an Inner Fracture Zone (next ~350 m), and a Basement Zone; both fracture density and aperture drop significantly at ~350 m and ~700 m depth from the unconformity. [Zhang 2023, pp. 1-1], [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-12]
- Within the Weathered Fracture Zone, higher paleo-topographic positions experienced more intense weathering and show higher fracture linear density (inferred from thinner overlying sedimentary cover in the BZ19-6 area). [Zhang 2023, pp. 12-12]
- 96.8% of natural fractures in the BZ19-6 metamorphic reservoir are hydraulically open based on imaging log interpretation, creating an interconnected fracture network. [Zhang 2023, pp. 1-1]

## Connections To Other Work
- **Global Buried Hill Analogs:** Buried hill reservoirs are globally significant; metamorphic rocks constitute 40% of volume and 75% of reserves of all buried hill reservoirs. [Zhang 2023, pp. 1-1]
- **Brittle Lithology in Other Basins:** In the Liaohe Basin, favorable lithologies are leptite and granulite; in South Jinzhou (Liaodong Bay), they are gneiss and migmatite granite—different from the BZ19-6 sequence (leucogneiss > cataclasite > gneiss). This is attributed to differences in mineral composition caused by migmatization. [Zhang 2023, pp. 10-10]
- **Weathering Zone Contrasts:** The Penglai oil field (Bohai Bay) weathering zone is the best reservoir, while the strong weathering zone of Bach Ho (Vietnam) is not favorable due to deep burial. [Zhang 2023, pp. 1-3]
- **Fault Proximity and Fracture Density:** The observed relationship matches the outcrop-based demonstration by Vitale and Isaia (2014) that fracture linear density increases closer to a fault. [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-11]
- **Adjacent Area Comparison:** BZ19-6 is compared to BZ13-2; BZ13-2 was covered by Mesozoic strata earlier and experienced shorter subaerial weathering, resulting in much smaller proven reserves. [Zhang 2023, pp. 13-13]
- **Methodological References:** The paper cites standard fracture-imaging-log interpretation workflows (Lai et al., 2018; Ameen et al., 2010) and weathering-regolith studies (Riber et al., 2016, 2017, 2019). [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-12] [Zhang 2023, pp. 15-15]

## Open Questions
- **Regional Weathering Duration vs. Lithology:** How does the duration and intensity of subaerial exposure interact with specific mineral compositions (e.g., plagioclase content) to control the thickness of the clay-sealing layer at the top of the WFZ? [Zhang 2023, pp. 11-12]
- **Fracture Connectivity Quantification:** Although 96.8% of fractures are open, the study does not explicitly quantify three-dimensional connectivity or permeability anisotropy of the fracture network. [Zhang 2023, pp. 5-7]
- **Predictive Modeling:** Can the qualitative “optimal principles” (lithology, fault proximity, WFZ-hilltop) be transformed into a quantitative predictive map or index for well placement? [Zhang 2023, pp. 13-13]
- **Upscaling:** The study spans core to well-log scales; fracture network properties at inter-well and field scales remain to be constrained.
- **Effect of Migmatization:** The distinct fracture susceptibility of migmatite granite in BZ19-6 versus Liaohe Basin suggests migmatization processes alter fracture behavior in ways that are not yet generalized. [Zhang 2023, pp. 10-10]
