# Deformation and Instability Failure of Borehole at High Temperature and High Pressure in Hot Dry Rock Exploitation.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates the deformation and critical failure conditions of boreholes in granite under high temperature and high hydrostatic pressure to inform Hot Dry Rock geothermal energy extraction.

## Research Question
What are the deformation behavior, failure modes, and critical instability conditions for boreholes in granite under the high temperature and high in-situ stress conditions encountered in Hot Dry Rock geothermal exploitation?

## Study Area / Data
The study uses laboratory experiments on granite samples. The granite, known as "Luhui" granite, was sourced from Pinyi, Shandong province, China, from a depth of 50 m [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3]. Three cylindrical specimens (200 mm diameter, 400 mm length) with a central 40 mm borehole were prepared [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3]. The average uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) at room temperature is 130 MPa [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
Experiments were conducted using a 20 MN servo-controlled high-temperature and high-pressure triaxial rock testing machine [Zhao 2015, pp. 1-2]. The setup can apply a maximum axial pressure of 318 MPa, lateral pressure of 250 MPa, and temperature of 600°C [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3]. Borehole radial deformation was measured using a specially designed optical instrument for temperatures above 240°C [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3]. Acoustic Emission (AE) was also monitored [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3]. The testing procedure involved incrementally increasing temperature and hydrostatic pressure to predetermined values and recording deformation over time [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
1.  When hydrostatic pressure is below 100 MPa and temperature below 400°C, specimens deform following the generalized Kelvin model (steady-state creep) and remain intact [Zhao 2015, pp. 1-2, 7-7].
2.  The critical condition for borehole instability is reached at a hydrostatic pressure of 125 MPa and temperature of 500°C, where creep deformation accelerates sharply [Zhao 2015, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
3.  The failure mode is shear failure or a combination of shear and tension failure [Zhao 2015, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
4.  The critical radial deformation ratio for borehole failure is about 20% [Zhao 2015, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
5.  Borehole radial creep deformation increases exponentially with increasing temperature and hydrostatic stress [Zhao 2015, pp. 3-4, 7-7].
6.  The ultimate drilling condition in granite is defined by the relationship: σ = -1.08442T + 643.5, where σ is hydrostatic stress (MPa) and T is temperature (°C) [Zhao 2015, pp. 4-5].

## Limitations
1.  The laboratory simulation uses pre-drilled, cooled rock samples reheated under stress, which weakens the rock more than in-situ conditions where cooling from drilling mud occurs [Zhao 2015, pp. 5-7].
2.  End effects due to friction between specimens and pressers impacted deformation over approximately 100 mm from each end [Zhao 2015, pp. 4-5].
3.  The study assumes hydrostatic stress, which is an approximation of deep in-situ stress conditions [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3].
4.  The effect of thermal shock from drilling fluid cooling on the borehole wall was not considered in the experiments [Zhao 2015, pp. 5-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The in-situ stress state in deep rock mass is close to hydrostatic [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3].
2.  The granite samples are homogeneous and without pre-existing fractures [Zhao 2015, pp. 2-3].
3.  The mechanical behavior observed in the lab is representative of field conditions, though the lab conditions are more severe due to the sample preparation history [Zhao 2015, pp. 5-7].

## Reusable Claims
1.  Under hydrostatic stress below 100 MPa (77% of UCS) and temperature below 400°C, granite boreholes exhibit steady-state viscoelastic creep and remain stable [Zhao 2015, pp. 1-2, 7-7].
2.  The critical instability condition for a borehole in granite is a hydrostatic stress of 125 MPa (96% of UCS) and a temperature of 500°C, beyond which accelerated creep and failure occur [Zhao 2015, pp. 1-2, 4-5, 7-7].
3.  The radial creep deformation of a borehole in granite increases exponentially with both temperature and hydrostatic stress, following the relation Urc = 0.22767exp(0.004454σ + 0.00483T) [Zhao 2015, pp. 3-4].
4.  The ultimate drilling depth in granite is constrained by the linear relationship between critical stress and temperature: σ = -1.08442T + 643.5 (MPa and °C), indicating that lower thermal gradients allow for deeper drilling [Zhao 2015, pp. 4-5].
5.  Borehole failure in brittle granite under high T and P occurs via shear or shear-tension composite failure, leading to wall fracturing and hole enlargement, distinct from necking in ductile rocks [Zhao 2015, pp. 4-5].

## Open Questions
1.  How do the critical conditions and failure modes scale from laboratory specimen size to full-scale boreholes in the field?
2.  What is the quantitative impact of the cooling effect from drilling fluids (thermal shock) on the critical instability conditions derived in this study?
3.  How do more complex, non-hydrostatic in-situ stress fields affect the deformation and failure criteria?
4.  Can the derived creep deformation equations be reliably used for long-term (decades) borehole stability prediction in HDR reservoirs?
