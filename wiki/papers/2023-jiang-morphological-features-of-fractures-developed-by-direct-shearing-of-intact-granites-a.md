# Morphological Features of Fractures Developed by Direct Shearing of Intact Granites after Water Cooling Cycles.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how water cooling cycles (1, 4, 7 cycles) at elevated temperatures (300, 400, 500 °C) affect the shear strength of intact granite and the 3D morphological characteristics (asperity height, apparent dip angle, and roughness) of the shear-induced fracture surfaces. [Jiang 2023, pp. 1-1, 1-3, 4-7]

## Research Question
- How do temperature and the number of water cooling cycles influence the shear strength and shear stiffness of intact granites?
- How do these treatments change the fracture surface morphology—specifically the asperity height distribution, apparent dip angle, and 3D roughness—of fractures formed by direct shearing?
- What mechanism at the mesoscopic scale explains the observed changes in fracture morphology? [Jiang 2023, pp. 1-1, 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
- Rock type: Granite from Weihai, Shandong Province, China; average density 2.69 g/cm³, initial porosity 0.49% [Jiang 2023, pp. 1-3].
- Specimen geometry: cubes 50 mm × 50 mm × 50 mm, prepared according to ISRM suggested method [Jiang 2023, pp. 1-3].
- Temperature levels: 300 °C, 400 °C, 500 °C; selected because an EGS is considered valuable only at ≥300 °C [Jiang 2023, pp. 1-3, 3-4].
- Water cooling cycles: 1, 4, and 7 cycles; each cycle = heating (5 °C/min) to target temperature, 6 h holding, quick immersion in room-temperature water, then 24 h drying at normal temperature [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4].
- Number of samples: 9 conditions (3 temperatures × 3 cycle numbers), each with one labeled sample, e.g., 300‑1 [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
- Heating and cooling: slow heating (5 °C/min) to target temperature, 6 h isothermal, rapid water quenching, and drying (24 h) before next cycle, repeated up to 7 cycles. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- Direct shear test: MTS 816.01 equipment; axial normal stress 20 MPa (loading rate 0.2 MPa/s), then shear force at 0.1 MPa/s until failure. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- 3D laser scanning: 3D-ML‑130 non-contact scanner (220,000 points/s, accuracy 0.02 mm, single scan 220 mm × 180 mm) with 0.3 mm sampling interval. Point clouds processed in Geomagic Studio (noise reduction, hole filling) and then discretised in MATLAB using Delaunay triangulation. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- Asperity height parameters: relative height Hᵢ, mean height, variance, skewness (Skew), kurtosis (Kurto) (Equations 1–5). [Jiang 2023, pp. 4-7]
- Apparent dip angle: geometric definition using normal vectors and sliding vector (Equations 6–8). [Jiang 2023, pp. 7-8]
- Initial contact area ratio: ratio of contact area in shear direction to that in reverse shear direction (Equation 9). [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
- 3D roughness metric: A₀ θ*ₘₐₓ/(C+1) after Liu et al. (refs 33–35). [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
- Thin-section microscopy: representative thin sections to examine mesoscale crack types (intergranular, intragranular, transgranular). [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]

## Key Findings
1. **Wave velocity**: Longitudinal wave velocity decreases significantly with increasing temperature and number of cycles, indicating more new and developed cracks. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
2. **Shear strength and stiffness**: Peak shear strength and shear stiffness decrease nonlinearly with both increasing temperature and cycle number. For example, from 1 to 7 cycles, the peak shear strength drops by 34.1% at 300 °C, 47.8% at 400 °C, and 58.1% at 500 °C. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
3. **Asperity height**: Both temperature and cycle number increase asperity height and improve overall uniformity (kurtosis decreases). Height distribution type shifts from right‑biased peak → left‑biased peak → left‑biased middle peak → left‑biased flat peak. At 300 °C, max height increased from 2.57 mm (1 cycle) to 3.51 mm (?) [Jiang 2023, pp. 4-7] (Note: fragment states at 1 cycle, max height increased from 2.57 to 3.51 mm as temperature increased from 300 to 500 °C, i.e., 36.6% increase). [Jiang 2023, pp. 4-7]
4. **Apparent dip angle asymmetry**: The maximum and average apparent dip angles in the reverse shear direction are systematically larger than those in the shear direction. Typical ranges: shear direction max 30–49°, average 5–15°; reverse shear max 32–62°, average 8–26°. This indicates an asymmetric asperity structure (θ₁ < θ₂), contrary to earlier symmetrical models. [Jiang 2023, pp. 7-8]
5. **Initial contact area ratio**: Ratio of shear‑direction to reverse‑shear‑direction initial contact areas fluctuates between 1.4 and 2, whereas previous studies reported around 0.6, confirming the asymmetric model. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
6. **3D roughness**: The roughness index A₀ θ*ₘₐₓ/(C+1) increases with both temperature and cycle times. Under constant cycles, the roughness increases by 40.7% (1 cycle), 47.6% (4 cycles), and 62.2% (7 cycles) when temperature rises from 300 to 500 °C. Under constant temperature, roughness increases by 2.05, 2.29, and 3.22, respectively, from 1 to 7 cycles. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
7. **Mesoscopic mechanism**: At 300 °C, intergranular cracks dominate; at 500 °C, intragranular and transgranular cracks become dominant due to loss of crystal/structural water and intensified thermal stress/quenching. With more cycles, superimposed damage promotes crack connection and larger transgranular cracks, which have higher roughness than intergranular/intragranular cracks. This explains why roughness increases while shear strength decreases. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]

## Limitations
- The study only tested one type of granite (Weihai, Shandong) and a single normal stress (20 MPa); results may not generalise to other lithologies or stress regimes. [Jiang 2023, pp. 1-3, 3-4]
- The number of water cooling cycles was limited to 1, 4, and 7; intermediate or longer cycle effects were not examined. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- The analysis is restricted to direct shear failure; the morphology of fractures formed under other loading modes (e.g., tensile, triaxial) is not addressed. [Jiang 2023, pp. 1-1, 1-3]
- Mesoscopic interpretation is based on thin-section observations; no quantitative mineralogical or microstructural parameters (e.g., crack density, mineral composition changes) were provided. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
- The fracture surface roughness metric A₀ θ*ₘₐₓ/(C+1) was adopted from Liu et al. (refs 33–35) but its sensitivity to the observed asymmetric asperity structures was not discussed. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
- No direct validation of the proposed asymmetric asperity model at field scales or larger specimen sizes was performed. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **EGS temperature threshold**: Temperature ≥300 °C is considered relevant for enhanced geothermal systems, guiding the selected temperature levels. [Jiang 2023, pp. 1-3, 3-4]
- **Normal stress**: A constant normal stress of 20 MPa was applied in all direct shear tests, representing a certain depth condition. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- **Heating rate**: Slow heating at 5 °C/min was assumed to avoid thermal cracking during the heating phase. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- **Water cooling cycle definition**: One cycle = heating to target temperature, 6 h constant temperature, rapid immersion in room‑temperature water, then 24 h drying. This standardises the thermal shock treatment. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- **Scanning resolution**: A 0.3 mm sampling interval was used for 3D laser scanning, considered sufficient to capture asperity morphology based on previous work (ref 29). [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- **Asperity height calculation**: Relative height is computed using Equation 1, with all heights measured from the same reference surface. The meaning of “minimum of absolute heights” is implicit. [Jiang 2023, pp. 4-7]
- **Apparent dip angle definition**: The apparent dip is calculated based on the angle between the triangle normal’s projection and the sliding vector, as defined in Equations 6–8. The sliding plane is presumably the mean fracture plane. [Jiang 2023, pp. 7-8]
- **Initial contact area ratio**: The ratio A_S/A_R‑S is computed from the asperity geometry; a ratio >1.4 is interpreted as confirmation of asymmetry. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
- **Mesoscopic mechanism**: It is assumed that crack types (inter‑, intra‑, transgranular) directly control fracture roughness, and that higher temperature / more cycles promote transgranular cracking due to crystal weakening and intensified thermal quenching. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]

## Reusable Claims
- For the tested Weihai granite under 20 MPa normal stress, peak shear strength after water cooling cycles decreases nonlinearly with both temperature (300–500 °C) and number of cycles (1–7); the decrease amplitude is larger at higher temperatures. [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- The height distribution of fracture surface asperities can be classified into four types: right‑biased peak, left‑biased peak, left‑biased middle peak, and left‑biased flat peak; increasing temperature or cycles shifts the distribution from right‑biased to left‑biased flat peak, indicating taller and more uniformly distributed asperities. [Jiang 2023, pp. 4-7]
- Fracture surfaces generated by direct shear after water cooling cycles exhibit an asymmetric asperity structure: the apparent dip angle (max and average) is larger in the reverse shear direction than in the shear direction (θ₁ < θ₂). The initial contact area ratio between shear and reverse shear directions lies between 1.4 and 2, contrasting with earlier symmetrical models. [Jiang 2023, pp. 7-8, 8-9]
- 3D roughness (A₀ θ*ₘₐₓ/(C+1)) increases with both temperature and cycle number. For the given conditions, the roughness increase is more pronounced at higher temperature and more cycles. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
- Mesoscopically, low‑temperature/low‑cycle treatments produce predominantly intergranular cracks, while high‑temperature/high‑cycle treatments lead to intragranular and transgranular cracks. Transgranular cracks are rougher, explaining the observed increase in fracture surface roughness and the decrease in shear strength. [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]

## Open Questions
- How do intermediate cycle numbers (2, 3, 5, 6) affect the transition between height distribution types? Is the shift gradual or abrupt? [Jiang 2023, pp. 4-7]
- Would the asymmetric asperity model hold under different normal stresses (not just 20 MPa) or for other rock types? [Jiang 2023, pp. 3-4]
- What is the quantitative relationship between mesoscopic crack parameters (e.g., crack density, orientation) and the morphological indices (height, roughness, asymmetry)? [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
- How does the observed roughness increase affect hydraulic properties (aperture, permeability) under confining pressure, relevant to EGS reservoir behaviour? [Jiang 2023, pp. 1-1, 1-3]
- Can the asymmetric asperity structure be systematically linked to the shear direction observed during formation, and does it influence frictional stability under cyclic loading? [Jiang 2023, pp. 7-8]
- Are there any mineralogical changes (e.g., phase transitions) at 500 °C that contribute to the change in crack type and roughness, beyond the suggested loss of crystal water? [Jiang 2023, pp. 8-9]
