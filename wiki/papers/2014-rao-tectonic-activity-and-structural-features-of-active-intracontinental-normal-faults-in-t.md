# Tectonic Activity and Structural Features of Active Intracontinental Normal Faults in the Weihe Graben, Central China.

## One-line Summary
High‑resolution remote sensing, field observations, and radiocarbon dating reveal that active normal faults in the eastern Weihe Graben form a narrow (<500 m), stepwise, discontinuous scarp system with pure dip‑slip, having vertically offset late Pleistocene–Holocene markers by up to ~~80 m at a non‑uniform throw‑rate of ~2.1–5.7 mm/yr (mostly 2–3 mm/yr) under ongoing NW–SE extension [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question
Earlier studies had traced the active faults of the Weihe Graben as a simple linear boundary but had not mapped their surface traces, geometric distribution, or structural features in detail; moreover, the kinematic sense (normal‑slip versus sinistral strike‑slip) was disputed [Rao 2014, pp. 1-4]. This study therefore investigates the recent activity, geometric pattern, and structural characteristics of the active normal faults in the Weihe Graben to clarify the nature of extensional deformation in intracontinental rift basins [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1, 1‑4].

## Study Area / Data
The Weihe Graben is one of several intracontinental graben systems around the stable Ordos Block in central China, formed by Cenozoic crustal extension since the Eocene [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1, 1‑4]. It is bounded to the south by the Qinling Mountains and contains up to 7000 m of Cenozoic sediments [Rao 2014, pp. 1-4]. The study focuses on the eastern part of the graben, specifically the northern margins of the Huashan Mountains and the Weinan Loess Tableland, which is the epicentral area of the 1556 M ~8.5 Huaxian earthquake [Rao 2014, pp. 4-6].
Data sources:
- 0.5 m WorldView and 1 m IKONOS satellite images, draped on 30 m ASTER GDEM to create 3‑D perspective views [Rao 2014, pp. 4-6].
- Field topographic profiles measured with a laser range‑finder [Rao 2014, pp. 6-8].
- Fault‑zone outcrops at four key locations (Locs 1–4) exposing shear zones, fault rocks, and displaced sediments [Rao 2014, pp. 6-8, 8‑11].
- Accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS) radiocarbon ages from shells and calcareous soil collected at the faulted sites [Rao 2014, pp. 6-8, Table 1].

## Methods
1. **Remote‑sensing analysis** – High‑resolution optical images were processed in 3‑D by draping on ASTER GDEM to identify geomorphic markers (e.g., terrace risers, alluvial fans) and active fault scarps [Rao 2014, pp. 4-6].
2. **Field mapping and structural observation** – Fault scarps, shear‑zone structures, fault‑rock fabrics, striations, and fault‑plane orientations were measured at outcrops and quarry exposures [Rao 2014, pp. 6-8, 8‑11].
3. **Topographic profiling** – Cumulative vertical offsets were obtained by summing the heights of individual fault scarps across six transects measured with a laser range‑finder [Rao 2014, pp. 6-8, 13‑13].
4. **Radiocarbon dating** – AMS ¹⁴C ages of shells and calcareous soil from loess and alluvial deposits constrained the timing of deformed landforms [Rao 2014, pp. 6-8, Table 1].
5. **Slip‑rate calculation** – Dip‑slip rates were computed by dividing the cumulative vertical offset by the radiocarbon age of the offset surface marker; the ages used range from ~5500 to 19 450 yr BP [Rao 2014, pp. 13-13, Table 2].

## Key Findings
1. **Fault distribution** – Active normal faults are concentrated in a narrow zone **<500 m wide** along the southern border of the eastern Weihe Graben [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1, 4‑6].
2. **Fault geometry** – The faults form a series of **stepwise scarps dipping 40°–71° into the graben**, with individual fault‑trace lengths of a few tens of meters to 450 m (generally <200 m), and spacing between scarps ranging from ~20 to 200 m (generally <100 m) [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1, 4‑6, 6‑8].
3. **Kinematics** – Topographic steps, fault‑plane striations, and the drape of fault‑zone materials indicate an **almost pure normal‑slip** sense; no evidence of a significant strike‑slip component was found [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1, 8‑11].
4. **Vertical offsets** – Late Pleistocene–Holocene terrace risers, loess slopes, and alluvial fans have been displaced by cumulative vertical amounts of **15–80 m** (generally 30–40 m); the largest single‑scarp displacement is ~19 m [Rao 2014, pp. 6-8, 13‑13].
5. **Slip rates** – The estimated dip‑slip (throw) rate is **~2.1–5.7 mm/yr, mainly 2–3 mm/yr**, and is **non‑uniform** along‑strike [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1, 13‑13, Table 2, Fig. 12].
6. **Tectonic regime** – The faults are developing under ongoing NW–SE extension, consistent with focal mechanisms and GPS velocities; the extension is probably linked to **pre‑existing spreading and rifting of the continental crust** that distinguishes the Weihe Graben from the stable Ordos Block and neighboring orogens [Rao 2014, pp. 13-15, Fig. 13].

## Limitations
- Slip‑rate estimates rely on radiocarbon ages of regional loess plateau surfaces rather than directly dated individual scarps; the exact age of initial displacement may be older or younger [Rao 2014, pp. 13-13].
- The presence of multiple stepped scarps makes it difficult to distinguish tectonic offsets from gravitational slope failures without detailed subsurface data; careful site selection was required [Rao 2014, pp. 13-13].
- The calculated slip rates reflect late Pleistocene–Holocene activity only; long‑term rates and variations linked to inherited Mesozoic–Cenozoic structures remain uncertain [Rao 2014, pp. 13-13].
- The subsurface geometry of the faults is inferred from earlier seismic reflection data (Xie, 2011) and was not directly imaged in this study [Rao 2014, pp. 11-13].
- The paper does not provide a detailed paleoseismic investigation of the 1556 Huaxian earthquake’s source fault, so direct linkage between the mapped scarps and that event is still interpretive [Rao 2014, pp. 15-15].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The measured cumulative vertical offsets represent tectonic faulting, not landslide‑related sliding; field evidence (foliated shear zones, striations, injection veins) supports a seismogenic origin [Rao 2014, pp. 13-13].
- Radiocarbon‑dated materials (shells, calcareous soil) from loess and alluvial deposits are contemporaneous with the formation of the offset landforms [Rao 2014, pp. 13-13].
- The stepwise scarps are the surface expression of a master listric fault at depth, as indicated by seismic reflection profiles [Rao 2014, pp. 11-13, after Xie 2011].
- The current NW–SE extensional stress regime, inferred from earthquake focal mechanisms and GPS, is the same tectonic regime that drove the late Pleistocene–Holocene faulting [Rao 2014, pp. 13-15].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Dip angle of main fault planes**: 40°–71°, mostly >50° [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1, 6‑8].
- **Fault trace length**: a few tens of meters to 450 m, generally <200 m [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1].
- **Fault zone width**: 10–500 m, generally <300 m [Rao 2014, pp. 4-6, 11‑13].
- **Spacing between individual fault scarps**: ~20–200 m, generally <100 m [Rao 2014, pp. 6-8].
- **Cumulative vertical offset**: 15–80 m, generally 30–40 m [Rao 2014, pp. 13-13].
- **Maximum single‑scarp offset**: ~19 m [Rao 2014, pp. 6-8].
- **Dip‑slip (throw) rate**: 2.1–5.7 mm/yr, mostly 2–3 mm/yr [Rao 2014, pp. 13-13, Table 2].
- **Age of offset markers**: 5500–19 450 yr BP (calibrated ¹⁴C) (Rao 2014, Table 1).

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim**: Active normal faults in the eastern Weihe Graben have dip angles of 40°–71° and exhibit pure dip‑slip kinematics at the surface, as shown by striations and fault‑rock fabrics.  
  *Condition*: Based on field exposures at four sites; applies to late Pleistocene–Holocene activity.  
  *Limitation*: Does not rule out deeper low‑angle detachments.
- **Claim**: The cumulative vertical offset across the fault zone is 15–80 m, and the associated throw‑rate is 2.1–5.7 mm/yr, with a modal value of 2–3 mm/yr.  
  *Condition*: Derived from six topographic profiles and radiocarbon ages of offset loess/alluvial surfaces.  
  *Limitation*: Rates may differ over longer timescales; assumes constant offset accumulation since the dated horizon.
- **Claim**: The surface rupture pattern is highly irregular, consisting of multiple discontinuous, parallel‑subparallel scarps within a zone <500 m wide, rather than a single linear trace.  
  *Condition*: Observed in the Weihe Graben, similar to co‑seismic normal‑fault ruptures (e.g., Dixie Valley, Pleasant Valley).  
  *Limitation*: May not apply to faults with strong vertical exhumation or thick sediment cover.
- **Claim**: Injected microbreccia veins within the fault shear zone indicate rapid, co‑seismic slip events during large earthquakes.  
  *Condition*: Observed in the cataclastic fault rocks at Loc 1; parallels findings from other active fault zones (Lin et al., 2012, 2013b).  
  *Limitation*: The age of the veins is not directly dated; association with a specific paleoearthquake is inferred.

## Connections To Other Work
- The observed stepwise, discontinuous scarps contrast with earlier maps that portrayed the Weihe Graben boundary as a single continuous lineament (e.g., Hou et al., 1998; Zhang et al., 1995; Li and Ran, 1983) [Rao 2014, pp. 1-4, 8‑11].
- The pure normal‑slip interpretation disagrees with studies that proposed a large sinistral strike‑slip component (e.g., Mercier et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 1998) and is consistent with the regional NW‑SE extensional stress derived from focal mechanisms (Ma, 1989) and GPS velocities (Wang et al., 2001) [Rao 2014, pp. 13-15].
- The surface rupture pattern mirrors co‑seismic normal‑fault ruptures in intracontinental rift environments, such as the 1954 Dixie Valley earthquake and the 1915 Pleasant Valley earthquake, and also the 2011 M 6.6 Fukushima earthquake surface rupture [Rao 2014, pp. 1-1, 11‑13, 13‑15].
- Subsurface structure inferred from seismic reflection data (Xie, 2011) shows the stepwise surface scarps merging into a master fault, supporting the listric geometry common in many extensional basins [Rao 2014, pp. 11-13].
- The paper links the extensional deformation to lithospheric mantle flow and crustal thinning, as revealed by geophysical studies (Bao et al., 2011; Huang et al., 2008), and suggests that similar processes may be important for other Ordos‑bounding grabens like the Yinchuan Graben [Rao 2014, pp. 15-15].

## Open Questions
- What are the detailed characteristics of the seismogenic source that produced the 1556 M ~8.5 Huaxian earthquake, and how can they be linked to the mapped scarps? [Rao 2014, pp. 15-15]
- How does strain accumulate in the brittle upper crust in the absence of magmatic activity, and what controls the recurrence of large normal‑faulting earthquakes in the Weihe Graben? [Rao 2014, pp. 15-15]
- What is the role of pre‑existing Mesozoic structures in the non‑uniform slip rates and along‑strike segmentation of the faults? [Rao 2014, pp. 13-13]
- How do the surface complexities of normal faults scale to the deeper seismogenic portion, and what mechanism triggers earthquakes on steeply dipping faults that may flatten at depth? [Rao 2014, pp. 11-13]
- How do the findings for the Weihe Graben transfer to other intracontinental rift basins around the Ordos Block, particularly those with different sediment thicknesses and basement characteristics? [Rao 2014, pp. 15-15]
