# Critical Temperature of Permeability Change in Thermally Cracked Granite.

## One-line Summary
This study identifies 300°C as the critical temperature (Tc) where the permeability of thermally cracked granite undergoes a sharp, order-of-magnitude increase, linked to the rapid formation and connection of micro-cracks.

## Research Question
What is the critical temperature at which the permeability of thermally cracked granite changes significantly, and what is the corresponding micro-crack evolution mechanism?

## Study Area / Data
The study used "Luhui" granite samples sourced from Pingyi, Shandong, China. The mineral composition is illite 25%, quartz 28%, feldspar 43%, and other 4%. The average quartz grain size is 0.6-0.7 mm, feldspar grain size is 1-2 mm, and initial porosity is 0.30%-0.55%. Samples were machined into cylinders of φ200 mm × 400 mm [Feng 2014, pp. 1-2].

## Methods
1.  **Permeability Measurement:** Permeability was measured under triaxial stress during heating using a "600°C 20 MN servo-controlled triaxial rock mechanics testing machine." High-pressure nitrogen gas was used as the fluid. Samples were heated at a rate of 10°C/h, held at target temperatures for 2 hours before measurement. Four samples were analyzed: one under 75 MPa confining pressure (simulating 3000 m depth) and three under 25 MPa (simulating 1000 m depth) [Feng 2014, pp. 2-5].
2.  **Micro-crack Analysis:** The evolution of micro-cracks in granite thin sections was quantitatively studied using an MPV-SP micro-photometer. The heating rate was 10°C/min, with a 1-minute hold at each target temperature for observation. The analysis focused on cracks with length l > 5 μm and l > 10 μm [Feng 2014, pp. 2-5].

## Key Findings
1.  A critical temperature (Tc) of 300°C was identified for permeability change in thermally cracked granite. Below 300°C, permeability magnitude is ~10⁻¹⁹ m² with small increases. Between 300-400°C, permeability increases drastically to a magnitude of 10⁻¹⁸ m². At 400°C, the magnitude reaches 10⁻¹⁷ m² [Feng 2014, pp. 1-2, 5-6].
2.  The permeability at 350°C is 5 to 40 times greater than at 300°C [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].
3.  Micro-crack quantity (for both l > 5 μm and l > 10 μm) shows two peaks within the 20-400°C range. For l > 5 μm cracks, peaks occur at 100-150°C and 250-300°C. For l > 10 μm cracks, peaks occur at 150-200°C and 300-350°C. The peak temperature for larger cracks (l > 10 μm) is higher than for smaller cracks (l > 5 μm) [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].
4.  Above 300°C, the quantity of l > 10 μm cracks increases sharply at a rate of approximately one per 10°C. This rapid increase in larger cracks is the main reason for the sharp permeability increase above 300°C [Feng 2014, pp. 1-2, 5-6].
5.  The critical temperature for permeability change (300°C) was the same for samples under different confining pressures (75 MPa and 25 MPa), though the absolute permeability values differed [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].

## Limitations
1.  The permeability measurement temperature intervals were relatively large (set at 50°C), so the exact range of the critical temperature could not be pinpointed, though it is confirmed to be around 300°C [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].
2.  The study acknowledges that the existence of a theoretical "critical temperature" for the onset of thermal cracking itself is questionable, as micro-cracks likely initiate at lower temperatures but may not be detected due to equipment sensitivity limits [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].
3.  One sample (No. 4) showed lower permeability values, possibly due to delayed measurement after reaching the target temperature, highlighting the time-dependent nature of permeability [Feng 2014, pp. 2-5].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The critical temperature for permeability change is independent of confining pressure (within the tested range of 25-75 MPa), as pressure primarily affects crack aperture, not the temperature at which crack connectivity causes a permeability jump [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].
2.  The permeability change is primarily governed by the development and connection of a micro-crack network, which can be explained by percolation models [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].
3.  The "Luhui" granite is representative for studying thermal cracking behavior relevant to nuclear waste disposal and geothermal energy development [Feng 2014, pp. 1-2].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Critical Temperature (Tc):** 300°C.
*   **Permeability (k):** Measured in m². Key values: ~10⁻¹⁹ m² (below Tc), ~10⁻¹⁸ m² (300-400°C), ~10⁻¹⁷ m² (at 400°C).
*   **Confining Pressure (σ₃):** 25 MPa (simulating 1000 m) and 75 MPa (simulating 3000 m).
*   **Heating Rate:** 10°C/h for permeability tests, 10°C/min for micro-crack observation.
*   **Micro-crack Length Thresholds:** l > 5 μm and l > 10 μm.

## Reusable Claims
1.  For "Luhui" granite under triaxial stress, a critical temperature of 300°C exists where permeability increases by at least one order of magnitude due to the rapid formation and interconnection of micro-cracks longer than 10 μm [Feng 2014, pp. 1-2, 5-6].
2.  The permeability of thermally cracked granite exhibits a multi-peak evolution with temperature, with a major, sharp increase occurring above 300°C, which is critical for modeling fluid flow in enhanced geothermal systems and nuclear waste repositories [Feng 2014, pp. 2-5, 5-6].
3.  The critical temperature for permeability change (300°C) is a material property for this granite type and is not significantly affected by confining pressure in the range of 25-75 MPa [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds upon and references prior research on rock thermal cracking, including work by Zhao et al. on the multi-peak nature of granite permeability [Feng 2014, pp. 2-5, 5-6], Chen et al. on thermal cracking thresholds in Westerly granite [Feng 2014, pp. 1-2, 5-6], and studies using SEM and CSLM for micro-crack characterization [Feng 2014, pp. 1-2]. It addresses a gap identified by Chen et al., who did not find a clear permeability threshold for granite below 200°C due to equipment limitations [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].

## Open Questions
1.  Does the critical temperature for permeability change (300°C) represent a similar threshold for other physical and mechanical properties (e.g., elastic modulus, Poisson's ratio) of granite? The authors suggest 200-300°C may be such a range [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].
2.  What is the precise temperature range of the critical zone, given the 50°C measurement intervals used in this study? [Feng 2014, pp. 5-6].
3.  How does the critical temperature vary with different granite types, mineral compositions, and initial porosities?
