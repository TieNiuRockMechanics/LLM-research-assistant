# Hydrogeochemical and Isotopic Constraints on the Pattern of a Deep Circulation Groundwater Flow System.

## One-line Summary
A hydrogeochemical and isotopic study in the Reshui River Basin reveals a fault-controlled deep circulation groundwater flow system where precipitation from southern mountains circulates to 3.8-4.3 km depth, is heated to ~170°C, and discharges via a hot spring, with mixing occurring during ascent.

## Research Question
How can hydrogeochemical and isotopic data be used to characterize the pattern, source, and flow path of a deep circulation groundwater flow system in a geothermal field?

## Study Area / Data
The study was conducted in the Reshui River Basin (E 113°54′, N 25°32′), southeast China, characterized by a subtropical monsoon humid climate, hills/mountains with elevations from 200 m to 1700 m, and an annual mean precipitation of 1670 mm [Long 2019, pp. 2-3]. The geology is within the plate collision zone between the Cathaysian and Yangtze blocks, with exposing Sinian and Precambrian strata and intrusive monzonite granite [Long 2019, pp. 2-3]. Four main fault systems (F1-F4) dominate the structure [Long 2019, pp. 2-3]. The Reshui hot spring geothermal field has an area of about 3 km², with spring temperatures around 90°C (max 98°C) and borehole production up to 2500 m³/d [Long 2019, pp. 3-4]. Water samples were collected from shallow groundwater, river water, and geothermal water (springs and boreholes) during field surveys in September 2016 and February 2017 [Long 2019, pp. 4-5].

## Methods
The study employed a combined geochemical and isotopic approach. Field surveys measured in-situ parameters (temperature, pH, EC, ORP) using a Horiba U-51 [Long 2019, pp. 4-5]. Water samples were filtered and preserved for laboratory analysis of major ions (ICP-OES, Ion Chromatography), trace elements (ICP-MS), and stable isotopes (δD, δ18O) via isotope ratio mass spectrometry (Finnigan MAT-253) [Long 2019, pp. 4-5]. Hydrogeochemical types were determined using Piper plots [Long 2019, pp. 9-10]. Mineral saturation indices were calculated using PHREEQCI [Long 2019, pp. 12-13]. Mixing ratios were estimated using a two end-member mixing model based on Na⁺ and SO₄²⁻ concentrations [Long 2019, pp. 10-11]. Geothermometers (Na-K, SiO₂) were applied to estimate reservoir temperatures [Long 2019, pp. 14-14]. The recharge elevation was estimated using the stable isotope altitude effect [Long 2019, pp. 12-13].

## Key Findings
1.  A fault-controlled deep circulation geothermal groundwater flow system exists. Precipitation infiltrating the weathering crust of granitic mountains on the south of the basin (average recharge elevation ~1150 m) is the source [Long 2019, pp. 12-13, 15-16].
2.  Water circulates to a depth of 3.8-4.3 km, where it is heated to approximately 170°C in a geothermal reservoir [Long 2019, pp. 14-14, 15-16].
3.  The regional active normal fault F3 acts as a primary pathway for groundwater ascent [Long 2019, pp. 15-16]. The geothermal water is obstructed by a thrust fault (F4) and forced upward by water pressure from an ~800 m elevation difference between recharge and discharge areas [Long 2019, pp. 15-16].
4.  The deep circulation geothermal water (samples G05, G07) is chemically distinct: alkaline (pH ~8.7), with high concentrations of Na⁺, SO₄²⁻, Cl⁻, F⁻, SiO₂, and trace elements (Li, Rb, Cs), and depleted isotopic compositions (δ18O ~-7.2‰, δD ~-45‰) [Long 2019, pp. 5-5, 10-11].
5.  Mixing with shallow, cooler groundwater occurs during ascent. Sample G06 is a mixture, with the deep thermal water proportion decreasing from 81% (dry season) to 71% (rainy season) [Long 2019, pp. 10-11].
6.  Two distinct geothermal systems were identified: the Reshui system (G02, G05, G06, G07) and the Nuanshui system (G15, G16, G17), differentiated by hydrogeochemistry [Long 2019, pp. 10-11].
7.  Mineral dissolution, hydrolysis aided by carbonic acid, and hydrothermal alteration in the reservoir contribute to the water's chemistry [Long 2019, pp. 12-13, 15-16].

## Limitations
1.  The two identified deep geothermal systems (Reshui and Nuanshui) cannot be separated using water isotope data alone due to the same precipitation isotopic input [Long 2019, pp. 12-13].
2.  Evaporation in the borehole G02 (sealed for a year) enriched its isotopic composition, hindering the use of its geochemical data to represent the deep reservoir fluid [Long 2019, pp. 12-13].
3.  The study relies on geothermometry, which assumes temperature-dependent water-mineral equilibrium in the reservoir [Long 2019, pp. 14-14].
4.  The estimated geothermal gradients (35-40°C/km) are referenced from another study and applied to the specific fault zone [Long 2019, pp. 14-14].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The local meteorological water line (LMWL) from Guilin (δD = 8.42 δ18O + 16.28) is applicable to the study area [Long 2019, pp. 12-13].
2.  The stable isotope altitude effect in South China is approximately -0.20‰ per 100 m for δ18O [Long 2019, pp. 12-13].
3.  The temperature of the constant temperature zone (T₀) is 16.7°C [Long 2019, pp. 14-14].
4.  Geothermal water samples G05 and G07 represent the unmixed deep reservoir fluid [Long 2019, pp. 10-11].
5.  The geothermal system is primarily heated by conduction, partially by steam [Long 2019, pp. 15-16].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Temperature:** Spring water (~88-98°C), borehole G05 (93°C), estimated reservoir (~170°C).
-   **pH:** Deep geothermal water (~8.7), shallow groundwater (~6.1-6.7).
-   **Isotopes (δ18O, δD):** Deep geothermal water (-7.2‰, -45‰), shallow groundwater (-6.1‰, -36‰).
-   **Major Ions:** Na⁺, SO₄²⁻, HCO₃⁻, Cl⁻, F⁻, SiO₂ concentrations.
-   **Trace Elements:** Li, Rb, Cs, Sr, As concentrations.
-   **Geothermal Gradient:** 35-40°C/km (in fault zone).
-   **Elevation Difference:** ~800 m between recharge and discharge areas.
-   **Mixing Ratio:** Proportion of deep thermal water in mixed samples.

## Reusable Claims
1.  In mountain regions with proper combination of water-conducting (e.g., normal fault F3) and water-blocking (e.g., thrust fault F4) faults, and significant topographic elevation difference, deep circulation groundwater convection can exist [Long 2019, pp. 15-16].
2.  The hydrogeochemical signature of deep circulation geothermal water in a granite-hosted system is characterized by high pH, high concentrations of Na⁺, SO₄²⁻, F⁻, SiO₂, and specific trace elements (Li, Rb, Cs), along with depleted stable isotope compositions [Long 2019, pp. 5-5, 10-11, 15-16].
3.  Mixing of ascending deep geothermal water with shallow groundwater is a common process that can be quantified using two end-member mixing models based on conservative ion tracers like Na⁺ and SO₄²⁻ [Long 2019, pp. 10-11].
4.  The recharge area for a deep circulation system can be identified using the altitude effect on stable isotopes in precipitation, requiring adjustment for orographic effects like rain shadow [Long 2019, pp. 12-13].
5.  Hydrothermal alteration in a geothermal reservoir is indicated by the saturation of deep geothermal water with respect to minerals like talc, chrysotile, and chlorite [Long 2019, pp. 12-13].
