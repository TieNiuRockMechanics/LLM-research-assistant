# Experimental Investigation of Thermal Cycling Effect on Physical and Mechanical Properties of Bedrocks in Geothermal Fields.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how repeated high-temperature (600°C) heating-cooling cycles degrade the physical and mechanical properties of marble and granite bedrocks, linking macroscopic weakening to the generation of micro-cracks.

## Research Question
How does treatment with different numbers of thermal cycles at a high temperature (600°C) affect the physical and mechanical properties (e.g., P-wave velocity, strength, deformation, failure mode) of bedrocks used in geothermal energy storage systems? [Rong 2018, pp. 4-7]

## Study Area / Data
The study used two rock types: dolomitic marble collected from Pingnan City and granite from Jinjiang City, both in Fujian Province, China. [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9] A total of 28 marble and 32 granite cylindrical specimens (50 mm diameter, 100 mm height) were prepared. [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9] Initial physical properties were measured, including P-wave velocity, density, and porosity. [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9]

## Methods
1.  **Thermal Cycling Treatment:** Specimens were heated to 600°C at 10°C/min, held for 4 hours, and then cooled naturally to room temperature (25°C). This constituted one cycle. Specimens were treated with 0, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 16 cycles. [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9]
2.  **Physical Property Measurement:** P-wave velocity was measured using an ultrasonic pulse transmission technique (1 MHz frequency) after treatment. [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9]
3.  **Microscopic Observation:** Thin sections from specimens were prepared and observed under an optical microscope using cross-polarized light to identify micro-cracks. [Rong 2018, pp. 9-12]
4.  **Mechanical Testing:** Uniaxial compression tests were performed using a hydraulic servo-controlled system at an axial displacement rate of 0.075 mm/min. [Rong 2018, pp. 9-12]
5.  **Acoustic Emission (AE) Monitoring:** During uniaxial compression, AE activity was recorded using four piezoelectric transducers (100-400 kHz resonance) mounted on the specimen surface. [Rong 2018, pp. 9-12]

## Key Findings
*   **P-wave Velocity:** Decreased significantly with the first thermal cycle and continued to decrease with more cycles, dropping by 73.2% for marble and 72.6% for granite after 16 cycles. [Rong 2018, pp. 9-12]
*   **Characteristic Stresses:** Peak strength, crack damage stress, and crack initiation stress decreased with increasing thermal cycles (up to 71.5% for marble, 51.5% for granite). Crack closure stress was less affected. [Rong 2018, pp. 12-15]
*   **Deformation Properties:** Young's modulus decreased (88.9% for marble, 67.6% for granite), while peak axial strain and maximum volumetric strain increased with more thermal cycles. [Rong 2018, pp. 15-19]
*   **Failure Mode:** Specimens treated with more cycles produced more fragments and showed lower integrity after failure. Granite failure was more violent than marble. [Rong 2018, pp. 22-26]
*   **AE Activity:** AE signals correlated with the stress-strain curve. Specimens with more thermal cycles showed more AE activity in the initial deformation stage due to pre-existing micro-cracks. [Rong 2018, pp. 19-22]
*   **Microscopic Observation:** Thermal cycling induced grain boundary and intra-grain micro-cracks. The number and aperture of these cracks increased with the number of cycles. [Rong 2018, pp. 26-29]
*   **Overall Trend:** The weakening effect of thermal cycling was most pronounced in the first several cycles and diminished with further cycles at 600°C. [Rong 2018, pp. 29-32]

## Limitations
*   Microscopic observation on thin sections provided only qualitative information on micro-crack development. [Rong 2018, pp. 26-29]
*   The study used a single high temperature (600°C). The effect of different temperature levels combined with cycling was not investigated. [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9]
*   Post-peak behavior for granite specimens was not captured due to violent failure. [Rong 2018, pp. 12-15]
*   The thin sections examined were not from the same specimen or position, limiting direct quantitative comparison. [Rong 2018, pp. 26-29]

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   The pre-determined temperature of 600°C was chosen to mimic a high-temperature environment for deep bedrocks in a thermal energy storage (TES) system. [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9]
*   A heating rate of 10°C/min and a hold time of 4 hours at peak temperature were used to ensure uniform heating. [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9]
*   Specimens were air-dried before ultrasonic testing. [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9]
*   The tangent modulus between 40% and 60% of peak stress was used as Young's modulus. [Rong 2018, pp. 15-19]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Independent Variable:** Number of thermal cycles (0, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 16). [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9]
*   **Controlled Variable:** Treatment temperature (600°C). [Rong 2018, pp. 7-9]
*   **Dependent Variables:** P-wave velocity, characteristic stress thresholds (peak strength, crack damage stress, crack initiation stress, crack closure stress), Young's modulus, peak strain, maximum volumetric strain, failure mode, AE activity. [Rong 2018, pp. 9-19]

## Reusable Claims
*   Under repeated thermal cycling at 600°C, the P-wave velocity, uniaxial compressive strength, and Young's modulus of marble and granite decrease significantly, with the most pronounced degradation occurring within the first few cycles. [Rong 2018, pp. 9-19, 29-32]
*   The degradation of macroscopic mechanical properties due to thermal cycling is primarily attributed to the generation and accumulation of grain boundary and intra-grain micro-cracks induced by thermal stress. [Rong 2018, pp. 26-29]
*   Acoustic emission monitoring effectively captures the micro-cracking process during loading, showing increased activity in the initial deformation stage for thermally damaged specimens. [Rong 2018, pp. 19-22]
*   The integrity of rock specimens after uniaxial failure decreases with an increasing number of prior thermal cycles, resulting in more fragmented failure. [Rong 2018, pp. 22-26]

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study builds on prior research investigating the effect of single high-temperature treatments on rock properties (e.g., hardness, P-wave velocity, strength). [Rong 2018, pp. 4-7]
*   It addresses a gap in understanding the effect of *multiple* heating-cooling cycles, which is relevant for the long-term operation of thermal energy storage systems. [Rong 2018, pp. 4-7]
*   The use of AE monitoring and microscopic observation follows established techniques in rock mechanics to link micro-scale damage to macro-scale property changes. [Rong 2018, pp. 4-7]
