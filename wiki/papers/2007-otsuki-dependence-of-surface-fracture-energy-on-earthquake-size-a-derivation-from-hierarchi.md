# Dependence of Surface Fracture Energy on Earthquake Size: A Derivation from Hierarchical Self-Similar Fault Zone Geometry.

## One-line Summary
The surface fracture energy of fault jogs and the mean fracture energy averaged over a rupture surface are both proportional to the rupture length raised to the power of 0.56, a relationship derived from the hierarchical self-similar geometry of fault zones and the fractal distribution of microfractures within jogs.

## Research Question
How does the surface fracture energy (Gs) depend on earthquake size, and can this dependence be derived from the geometric properties of fault zones?

## Study Area / Data
The study uses data from experimental fault zones [Otsuki and Dilov, 2005], natural large strike-slip faults [Aydin and Schultz, 1989], small strike-slip faults in Pliocene tuff breccia in Hokkaido, Japan (T. Dilov, unpublished data, 2006), and a high-quality photograph of a rock burst normal fault in Precambrian quartzite from a South African gold mine [Gay and Ortlepp, 1979]. Additional fractal analyses were performed on fault jogs in Jurassic Navajo Sandstone sketched by Ahlgren [2001] and Katz et al. [2004].

## Methods
The author formulates surface fracture energy based on the hierarchical self-similar geometry of fault zones, where fault segments and jogs of different ranks are nested [Otsuki and Dilov, 2005]. The distribution of microfractures within a fault jog is analyzed using the box-counting fractal method to determine its fractal dimension (D) [Otsuki 2007, pp. 1-3]. Mathematical derivations combine the geometric scaling relations with the fractal microfracture distribution to derive size-dependent expressions for fracture energy.

## Key Findings
1.  The distribution of microfractures in a fault jog is fractal, with a universal fractal dimension (D) of approximately 1.56 in 2-D measurements [Otsuki 2007, pp. 1-3].
2.  Both the surface fracture energy of individual jogs (GJ1) and the mean surface fracture energy averaged over the rupture surface (Gs) are proportional to the rupture length (L0) raised to the power of (D-1), which is approximately 0.56 [Otsuki 2007, pp. 1-1, 4-4].
3.  The ratio of fracture energy of the largest jogs to the smallest jogs can be as high as 8, suggesting large jogs act as effective barriers to rupture propagation [Otsuki 2007, pp. 3-4].
4.  The scaling exponent 'a' relating jog length (LJ) to jog thickness (TJ) appears to depend on the range width of the scale of observation, approaching 1 for a very wide range [Otsuki 2007, pp. 3-4].

## Limitations
1.  Estimates of surface fracture energy are strongly affected by the choice of xblim, the lower limit of the scale of observation. The author notes it should be replaced by the smallest grain size of natural fault gouge, which is not well understood [Otsuki 2007, pp. 4-4].
2.  The parameters CSL, CJL, and CJT may not be independent of rock properties, though their ratio CJT/CJL might be [Otsuki 2007, pp. 4-4].
3.  The derived Gs for a small earthquake was two orders of magnitude larger than the empirical Gc' from Abercrombie and Rice [2005], indicating potential discrepancies or different physical meanings [Otsuki 2007, pp. 4-4].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Fault zones have a hierarchical self-similar geometry where segment+jog structures of smaller size are nested within larger ones [Otsuki 2007, pp. 1-1].
2.  A seismic rupture initiates at a small jog of a low hierarchical rank (large i) and terminates at large jogs [Otsuki 2007, pp. 1-3].
3.  The fractal dimension D ≈ 1.56 for microfracture distribution in jogs is a universal constant [Otsuki 2007, pp. 4-4].
4.  For the simplified final equation for Gs, the scaling exponent a relating jog length to thickness is assumed to be 1 [Otsuki 2007, pp. 3-4].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **D**: Fractal dimension of microfracture distribution in a fault jog (≈1.56).
*   **L0**: Total length of the seismic rupture zone.
*   **Gs**: Mean surface fracture energy averaged over the rupture surface.
*   **GJ1(i)**: Surface fracture energy of a fault jog of hierarchical rank i.
*   **xblim**: Lower limit of the scale of observation for microfractures.
*   **γ**: Specific surface energy.
*   **CSL, CJL, CJT, a**: Geometric scaling constants for the hierarchical fault zone model.
*   **K**: Proportional constant in the fractal microfracture length equation.

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Claim:** The mean surface fracture energy (Gs) of an earthquake rupture is proportional to the rupture length (L0) raised to the power of 0.56, given a universal fractal dimension (D) of 1.56 for microfracture distribution in fault jogs and a hierarchical self-similar fault zone geometry. **Condition:** This derivation assumes seismic ruptures initiate at small jogs and terminate at large jogs. **Limitation:** The absolute value of Gs depends critically on the lower observation scale limit (xblim), which should correspond to the smallest grain size in fault gouge.
2.  **Claim:** The fracture energy of individual fault jogs varies by up to a factor of 8 depending on their hierarchical rank, with the largest jogs having the highest fracture energy and acting as effective barriers to rupture propagation. **Condition:** Derived under the assumption that the scaling exponent a=1. **Limitation:** The ratio is sensitive to the assumed value of the fractal dimension D.
