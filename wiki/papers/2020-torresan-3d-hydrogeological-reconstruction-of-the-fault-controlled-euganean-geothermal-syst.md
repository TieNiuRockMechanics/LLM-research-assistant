# 3D Hydrogeological Reconstruction of the Fault-Controlled Euganean Geothermal System (NE Italy).

## One-line Summary
A 3D hydrogeological model was constructed to evaluate the structural controls on the Euganean Geothermal System and estimate its thermal water resource volumes relative to exploitation.

## Research Question
How do the regional and local fault systems control the development and hydrogeology of the Euganean Geothermal System, and what is the estimated volume of thermal water in the reservoir compared to historical exploitation?

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Euganean Geothermal System (EuGS) in the central Veneto region of NE Italy, with the Euganean Geothermal Field (EuGF) as the exploitation area. The model domain is approximately 115 km long, 50 km wide, and 9 km deep [Torresan 2020, pp. 8-9]. Data used include gravimetric maps, geological and structural maps, well logs up to 5 km deep, and geological and seismic sections [Torresan 2020, pp. 8-9]. Specific sources include the Geological Map of Veneto Region (1:250,000), structural maps, gravimetric maps, seismic sections, and deep well logs from the VIODEPI project [Torresan 2020, pp. 9-12].

## Methods
The hydrogeological reconstruction was performed using the MOVE software (Midland Valley Exploration, Ltd.) [Torresan 2020, pp. 8-9]. The geological sequence was discretized into eight hydrostratigraphic units based on hydrogeological behavior [Torresan 2020, pp. 9-12]. The main regional faults were modeled as internal boundaries, and the model domain was subdivided into four structural domains (Eastern Southern Alps, Lessini-Berici-Euganei block, Veneto plain foredeep, and relay ramp), which were modeled separately and then merged [Torresan 2020, pp. 8-9]. Validation was performed using stratigraphic logs from deep wells and geological cross-sections not used in the modeling phase [Torresan 2020, pp. 12-17]. The volume of the thermal reservoir was estimated using a tetrahedral mesh, and groundwater availability was calculated using assumed effective porosity values [Torresan 2020, pp. 9-12].

## Key Findings
The Schio-Vicenza Fault System (SVFS) is fundamental to the EuGS, providing hydraulic connection between the recharge area and the exploitation field [Torresan 2020, pp. 1-3]. The relay ramp structure between the Schio-Vicenza (SV) and Conselve-Pomposa (CP) faults enhances local permeability and fluid convection, warming the thermal fluids [Torresan 2020, pp. 5-8, 22-23]. The 3D reconstruction validated the hydrogeological conceptual model, showing the recharge area is in the Veneto Prealps east of the SV fault [Torresan 2020, pp. 22-23]. The total volume of the regional thermal reservoir (Vtotal) is 1.91×10^6 M m³ [Torresan 2020, pp. 23-24]. At the regional scale, the exploited volume (1.18×10³ M m³) is a small percentage (0.8% to 6.1%) of the estimated groundwater volume, but at the EuGF scale, exploitation appears to exceed the estimated effective volume under all porosity scenarios [Torresan 2020, pp. 23-24].

## Limitations
The model simplifies fault geometries, using constant dip angles for the SVFS faults and relay ramp faults [Torresan 2020, pp. 9-12]. Minor local-scale faults impacting the recharge area were not reproduced as they were deemed not relevant for regional groundwater flow [Torresan 2020, pp. 9-12]. The volume estimation does not consider local hydraulic parameters, groundwater flow paths, or the real extent and properties of fault damage zones [Torresan 2020, pp. 23-24, 28-30]. The Quaternary cover reconstruction has higher errors due to local heterogeneities and data scarcity [Torresan 2020, pp. 12-17].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The thermal waters are of meteoric origin, infiltrating at ~1,500 m a.s.l. in the Veneto Prealps [Torresan 2020, pp. 5-8]. The EuGS is considered a closed system for volume comparison due to long water residence times (~several thousand years) relative to the exploitation period (~150 years) [Torresan 2020, pp. 23-24]. Effective porosity (ne) values for the reservoir lithologies were taken from literature (Pasquale et al., 2011) and well analysis, with minimum, mean, and maximum values of 1%, 4%, and 8% used in scenarios [Torresan 2020, pp. 9-12, 23-24]. The pre-Permian crystalline basement is assumed impervious (hydraulic conductivity of 10⁻¹⁰ m/s) [Torresan 2020, pp. 22-23].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Effective porosity (ne):** 1%, 4%, 8% [Torresan 2020, pp. 9-12, 23-24].
- **Fault dip angles:** SV fault: 85° NNE; CP fault: 87° NNE; Relay ramp faults: 85° [Torresan 2020, pp. 9-12].
- **Reservoir depths:** Maiolica Formation aquifer: 300-600 m; Calcari Grigi/Dolomia Principale aquifer: 800-1000 m [Torresan 2020, pp. 5-8].
- **Exploitation volume:** ~15 M m³/year currently; total historical exploitation ~1.18×10³ M m³ [Torresan 2020, pp. 3-4, 23-24].
- **Reservoir volumes:** Vtotal (regional) = 1.91×10^6 M m³; Vtotal (interaction zone) = 2.41×10^5 M m³; Vtotal (EuGF) = 1.04×10^4 M m³ [Torresan 2020, pp. 23-24].

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** The relay ramp structure between major fault segments is a critical control on the development of low-temperature geothermal systems by enhancing local permeability and fluid convection.
  **Condition:** In extensional to transtensional tectonic settings with inherited fault architectures.
  **Limitation:** The degree of permeability enhancement depends on the specific fracture network and damage zone development, which may vary.
- **Claim:** A 3D hydrogeological reconstruction can validate conceptual models and provide volumetric estimates of geothermal reservoirs, but these estimates are highly sensitive to assumed effective porosity and the scale of analysis.
  **Condition:** When sufficient geological, geophysical, and borehole data are available for model construction.
  **Limitation:** Volume estimates without coupled flow modeling may not accurately reflect exploitable resources or sustainable yields.

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on the hydrogeological conceptual model of Pola et al. (2015b) [Torresan 2020, pp. 5-8]. It references numerical simulations of the Euganean system by Pola et al. (2020) that quantify the importance of fault-controlled convection [Torresan 2020, pp. 22-23]. The work is contextualized within global studies on relay ramps as favorable structures for geothermal anomalies (e.g., Faulds et al., 2013; Fossen and Rotevatn, 2016) [Torresan 2020, pp. 22-23]. It uses geothermometer data from Gherardi et al. (2000) to infer circulation depth and recharge area [Torresan 2020, pp. 5-8, 22-23].
