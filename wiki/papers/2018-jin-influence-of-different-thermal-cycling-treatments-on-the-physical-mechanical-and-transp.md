# Influence of Different Thermal Cycling Treatments on the Physical, Mechanical and Transport Properties of Granite.

## One-line Summary

Slow cooling and rapid quenching thermal cycling treatments were applied to granite from 25–600 °C; rapid quenching induces more thermal gradient cracks, leading to lower density, P-wave velocity, UCS, Young’s modulus, and tensile strength, but higher permeability, with differences increasing up to 500 °C. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-1; 10-10]

## Research Question

How do two different thermal cycling treatments—slow heating followed by slow cooling versus slow heating followed by rapid quenching—affect the physical, mechanical, and transport properties of granite, and what are the underlying thermal cracking mechanisms? [Jin 2018, pp. 1-1; 2-3]

## Study Area / Data

- **Rock type**: Medium‑fine grained monzonitic granite collected from Shandong province, China. Modal composition: ~35 % plagioclase, 40 % K‑feldspar, 20 % quartz, 5 % biotite. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2; 2-3]
- **Sample geometry**: Cylindrical cores (φ = 50 mm, L = 100 mm for uniaxial compression; φ = 50 mm, thickness = 25 mm for Brazilian splitting). [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Data types**: Mass, volume, density, P‑wave velocity, uniaxial compressive strength (UCS), Young’s modulus, tensile strength, gas permeability (nitrogen), optical micrographs. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-1; 2-3]

## Methods

1. **Thermal cycling treatments**  
   - **Heating**: at 2 °C/min to peak temperatures 100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600 °C; held >2 h.  
   - **Slow cooling**: left in furnace, cooling rate 1–2 °C/min.  
   - **Rapid quenching**: taken out and dipped into water tank (~20 °C); average cooling rate ~100 °C/min. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2; 2-3]

2. **Physical property tests**  
   - Mass, volume, bulk density measured before and after treatment.  
   - P‑wave velocity along sample axis (NM‑4B analyzer, vaseline coupling, ~5 N load). [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3]

3. **Mechanical property tests**  
   - Uniaxial compression (L/D = 2:1) and Brazilian splitting (discs) per ISRM suggested method; constant strain rate 10⁻⁵ s⁻¹. Young’s modulus from linear portion of stress–strain curve. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3]

4. **Permeability tests**  
   - Pressure pulse decay method (Smart Perm III, nitrogen gas). Axial and confining pressure = 5 MPa; initial pore pressure = 2.5 MPa; equilibrium time 1 h. Klinkenberg effect negligible at mean pore pressure ~2.3 MPa. Permeability calculated by (Brace et al., 1968; Sander et al., 2017) equations. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3]

5. **Microstructural analyses**  
   - Thin sections (30 μm) from 30×20×5 mm slices undergoing identical thermal cycling. Cross‑ and plane‑polarized optical microscopy on sections treated at 25, 200, 400, 600 °C. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3; 7-8]

## Key Findings

- **Mass and volume**: Mass decreases continuously with temperature due to water loss; mass loss at 600 °C ~0.13 % (slow) and ~0.18 % (quench). Rapid‑quench mass loss slightly lower because samples absorbed water. Volume expansion increases gradually up to 400 °C, then sharply; at 600 °C expansion reaches ~2.8 % for rapid quench. [Jin 2018, pp. 3-4; 10-10]
- **Density**: Density decreases with temperature; sharper reduction after 500 °C. At 600 °C, density reduced by 2.3 % (slow) and 2.9 % (rapid). Rapid‑quench samples always have lower density. [Jin 2018, pp. 3-4]
- **P‑wave velocity**: Slow cooling: 1.2 % drop at 100 °C, then linear decrease to 69.6 % at 600 °C. Rapid quenching: approximately linear decrease; 73.4 % reduction at 600 °C. Difference between cooling methods increases up to 500 °C. [Jin 2018, pp. 4-5; 10-10]
- **UCS**: Decreases with temperature. Slow cooling: slight increase (~3 %) at 100 °C, then decline; at 500 °C reduced to 72 %, at 600 °C to 45 % of room‑temperature strength. Rapid quenching: at 500 °C reduced to 54 %, at 600 °C to 36 %. Difference between cooling methods increases up to 500 °C. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-6; 10-10]
- **Young’s modulus**: Gradual decrease, sharper after a transition temperature (500 °C slow, 400 °C rapid). At 600 °C, reduction 50 % (slow) and 64 % (rapid). [Jin 2018, pp. 5-6; 10-10]
- **Tensile strength**: Slow cooling: ~73 % reduction from 25 °C to 600 °C; inflection point ~400 °C. Rapid quenching: ~78 % reduction; always lower than slow‑cooling values. [Jin 2018, pp. 6-7]
- **Permeability**: Slow cooling: slight increase before 500 °C, then 35.6× increase at 600 °C (critical temperature 500 °C). Rapid quenching: critical temperature reduced to 400 °C; at 600 °C permeability ~79× higher than room temperature. Rapid‑quench permeability always larger. [Jin 2018, pp. 7-8; 10-10]
- **Microcracking**: Room‑temperature granite shows no visible microcracks. Rapid quenching from 200 °C already shows intragranular and intergranular cracks (not seen in slow cooling). At 400 °C slow cooling: inter‑ and intragranular cracks; rapid quenching: cracks coalesce throughout specimen. At 600 °C slow cooling: severe damage from quartz α‑β transition; rapid quenching: transgranular and wider cracks. [Jin 2018, pp. 7-8]
- **Mechanism**: Slow cooling → homogeneous temperature field → thermal cycling cracks from mineral thermal expansion mismatch. Rapid quenching → high radial temperature gradients → tensile tangential stress up to ~207 MPa at 500 °C, far exceeding tensile strength (~3.5 MPa), causing extensive thermal gradient cracks. [Jin 2018, pp. 8-9; 10-10]

## Limitations

- Only one granite type (medium‑fine grained monzonitic granite from Shandong) tested; results may differ for other rock types or grain sizes. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2; 6-7]
- Klinkenberg effect evaluated but found negligible at ~2.3 MPa pore pressure; not verified for all pressure ranges. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3]
- Permeability tests performed at low confining pressure (5 MPa); in‑situ stresses in EGS are higher. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3; 10-10]
- Microstructural analysis limited to 2D thin sections; 3D crack network characterization not performed. [Jin 2018, pp. 7-8; 8-9]
- The heterogeneous microcrack distribution pattern during rapid quenching requires more detailed investigation. [Jin 2018, pp. 8-9]

## Assumptions / Conditions

- Thermal diffusivity of granite ~10⁻⁶ m²/s → temperature equilibrium within 10 min for φ = 50 mm specimens; thus, slow heating/cooling induces negligible thermal gradients. [Jin 2018, pp. 8-9]
- Young’s modulus of slow‑cooled samples used for thermal stress estimation in rapid quenching; average E at 500 °C ~10.78 GPa from Table 2 (slow cooling). [Jin 2018, pp. 8-9; Table 2]
- Thermal expansion coefficient α=3×10⁻⁵ °C⁻¹, Poisson’s ratio ν=0.25 assumed for stress estimation. [Jin 2018, pp. 8-9]
- Water absorption during quenching influences mass loss but not the overall trend of property degradation. [Jin 2018, pp. 3-4]
- All samples air‑dried ≥48 h before mass measurement. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3]
- Mechanical tests at strain rate 10⁻⁵ s⁻¹; failure modes remain brittle across all temperatures. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-6]

## Reusable Claims

1. In granite, slow cooling from temperatures up to 600 °C induces primarily thermal cycling cracks (from mineral thermal expansion mismatch); rapid quenching adds thermal gradient cracks due to high‑magnitude tensile tangential stress at the sample surface. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-1; 8-9] Condition: assumes heating rate of 2 °C/min and quenching rate ~100 °C/min.
2. For this granite, the difference in P‑wave velocity and UCS between slow‑cooled and rapid‑quenched samples increases with peak temperature up to 500 °C, implying that higher temperature gradients during quenching cause progressively greater additional damage. [Jin 2018, pp. 4-5; 10-10]
3. A critical temperature for permeability enhancement exists: 500 °C for slow cooling, reduced to 400 °C for rapid quenching; above these temperatures permeability increases sharply. [Jin 2018, pp. 7-8]
4. The α‑β quartz transition at 573 °C leads to severe microcracking, causing a sharp drop in density, strength, stiffness, and a sharp increase in permeability irrespective of cooling method. [Jin 2018, pp. 4-5; 7-8]
5. Rapid quenching accelerates thermal deterioration: Young’s modulus transition temperature shifts from 500 °C (slow) to 400 °C (rapid). [Jin 2018, pp. 5-6]
6. Microcrack types evolve with peak temperature and cooling rate: intragranular and intergranular cracks appear at lower temperatures in quenched samples; transgranular cracks become widespread at 600 °C rapid quenching. [Jin 2018, pp. 7-8]
7. The pressure pulse decay method with nitrogen gas, at 5 MPa confining pressure and ~2.3 MPa mean pore pressure, provides Klinkenberg‑negligible permeability for thermally cracked granite. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3]
8. Volume expansion (permanent strain) is larger after rapid quenching than after slow cooling, driven by propagation and initiation of microcracks. [Jin 2018, pp. 3-4]
9. The initial nonlinearity of stress‑strain curves increases with treatment temperature, attributed to closure of more thermal microcracks. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-6]
10. Slow‑heated and slow‑cooled granite shows homogeneous, isotropically oriented cracks; rapid quenching produces heterogeneous crack distribution with highest density near the sample surface. (Not experimentally confirmed in this study but inferred from thermal stress field; stated as requiring further investigation.) [Jin 2018, pp. 8-9]

## Open Questions

- **Microcrack distribution pattern during rapid quenching**: The heterogeneous distribution (highest crack density near the surface) was inferred but requires detailed experimental investigation. [Jin 2018, pp. 8-9]
- **Thermal/hydraulic stimulation coupling**: The influence of thermal microcracking on hydraulic fracturing and reservoir stimulation is noted as meriting further study. [Jin 2018, pp. 9-10]
- **Generality across rock types**: The specific critical temperatures and transition behaviors observed in this monzonitic granite may differ in other granitoids or reservoir rocks (e.g., andesite, as shown by Heap et al., 2018a). [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2; 7-8]
- **Combined effect of heating rate**: This study only varied cooling rate; the influence of rapid heating is acknowledged but not tested here. [Jin 2018, pp. 8-9]
