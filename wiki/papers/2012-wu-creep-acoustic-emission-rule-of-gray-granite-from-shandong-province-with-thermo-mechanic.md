# Creep Acoustic Emission Rule of Gray Granite from Shandong Province with Thermo-Mechanical Coupling Effects.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the acoustic emission (AE) patterns during the creep of gray granite under high temperature and high pressure, finding that AE signals weaken with increasing temperature due to internal plastic deformation.

## Research Question
What are the rules of acoustic emission during the creep process of gray granite under thermo-mechanical coupling effects, specifically at different temperatures and pressures?

## Study Area / Data
The study used gray granite samples (Luhui granite) from Pingyi, Shandong Province, China. The samples were cylindrical, with dimensions of φ200 mm × 400 mm, in a natural water-saturated state [Wu 2012, pp. 1-2].

## Methods
The experiments were conducted using a 20 MN servo-controlled high-temperature and high-pressure rock triaxial testing machine [Wu 2012, pp. 2-5]. Acoustic emission was monitored using a PAC DiSP 24-channel AE system with four sensors [Wu 2012, pp. 2-5]. The test procedure involved applying a predetermined pressure, then heating the sample to a target temperature at a rate of 5 °C/h while collecting AE data [Wu 2012, pp. 2-5]. Three thermo-mechanical conditions were tested: 300 °C with 94 MPa axial and 75 MPa confining pressure; 400 °C with 125 MPa axial and 100 MPa confining pressure; and 500 °C with 175 MPa axial and 125 MPa confining pressure [Wu 2012, pp. 2-5].

## Key Findings
1.  Under 300 °C (94 MPa axial, 75 MPa confining pressure), the granite experienced both transient and steady-state creep. AE signals were stronger during the transient creep phase than the steady-state phase. In steady-state creep, AE frequency was stable but intensity gradually decreased [Wu 2012, pp. 1-2, 5-6].
2.  Under 400 °C (125 MPa axial, 100 MPa confining pressure), only steady-state creep was observed, and the AE pattern was similar to the steady-state phase at 300 °C [Wu 2012, pp. 1-2, 5-6].
3.  Under 500 °C (175 MPa axial, 125 MPa confining pressure), the collected AE signals were very weak, insufficient to characterize the creep AE rules [Wu 2012, pp. 1-2, 5-6].
4.  As temperature increased, the AE signals weakened. This is attributed to increased plastic deformation and local plastic damage within the granite at higher temperatures [Wu 2012, pp. 1-2, 5-6].

## Limitations
1.  The accelerated creep stage was not observed in the tests, likely due to the relatively low differential stress and large sample size, preventing failure within the test duration [Wu 2012, pp. 5-6].
2.  The AE system only collected characteristic parameters, not waveform data, preventing frequency spectrum analysis [Wu 2012, pp. 5-6].
3.  The same granite sample was used sequentially for all three temperature conditions, which may influence the results [Wu 2012, pp. 5-6].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The granite samples were assumed to be representative of the Luhui granite from Shandong Province.
2.  The test conditions (temperature, pressure) were assumed to be stable and accurately controlled.
3.  The AE coupling with the sample surface using grease was assumed to be adequate for signal transmission [Wu 2012, pp. 2-5].

## Reusable Claims
1.  For Luhui granite under triaxial stress, the creep behavior transitions from exhibiting both transient and steady-state phases to only a steady-state phase as temperature increases from 300 °C to 400 °C [Wu 2012, pp. 5-6].
2.  In high-temperature creep tests on granite, acoustic emission signal intensity decreases with increasing temperature, which is linked to the onset of internal plastic deformation and local plastic damage [Wu 2012, pp. 1-2, 5-6].
3.  The acoustic emission activity during the steady-state creep phase of granite is characterized by stable frequency and gradually decreasing intensity [Wu 2012, pp. 1-2].
