# Structure of the low permeable naturally fractured geothermal reservoir at Soultz.
Citation: Dezayes, Chrystel, et al. "Structure of the low permeable naturally fractured geothermal reservoir at Soultz." *Comptes Rendus Geoscience*, vol. 342, 2010, pp. 517-530. doi:10.1016/j.crte.2009.10.002.

## One-line Summary
Characterization of 39 fracture zones in six wells at Soultz EGS site, revealing three depth clusters that control fluid flow in a low-permeability granite reservoir.

## Research Question
How are the fracture zones in the Soultz granite organized geometrically, and how do they relate to fluid flow pathways prior to and after stimulation? see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-2]: "The goal of this study is to assess the geometry of the fracture network for a better understanding of the fluid circulation..."

## Study Area / Data
- Location: Soultz EGS site, Upper Rhine Graben, France.
- Geology: Paleozoic basement comprising porphyritic monzogranite and two-mica granite; overlain by Mesozoic (Triassic, Jurassic) and Cenozoic sediments. Hosts the geothermal reservoir at ~1.4–5 km depth. see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-2], [Dezayes 2010, pp. 1-2]
- Data sources: Six wells (EPS1, GPK1, GPK2, GPK3, GPK4, 4550). Data includes core (EPS1 fully cored; spot cores elsewhere), borehole image logs (UBI, BHTV), cuttings, geophysical logs (spectral gamma ray, calliper), drilling parameters, temperature/flow logs; supplemented by microseismicity and VSP surveys [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-5], [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-5].

## Methods
- Fracture zone identification via composite logs: petrographical description of cuttings, borehole image log analysis, gamma ray anomalies (K, Th, U), calliper, drilling parameters, and temperature/flow logs to locate fluid losses see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-5].
- Orientation determination from borehole images: overall zone orientation taken from border of brecciated zone or 3D average of sub-parallel planes; qualitative when image quality is poor see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-5].
- Fracture zones classified into three levels based on natural permeability and stimulation flow response:
	- Level 1: major zones; permeable before stimulation (total/partial mud losses during drilling).
	- Level 2: flow indication >20% of water losses during stimulation and/or significant hydrothermal alteration halo.
	- Level 3: poorly developed alteration halo; flow <20% of water losses during stimulation. see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-7]
- Comparison with microseismicity and VSP data for 3D fault model building see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 11-12].

## Key Findings
1. Fracture zones and mesoscale fractures have main strike ~N160°E–N0°E, clockwise rotated 10–15° relative to the regional Rhenish (N20°E–N45°E) fault direction. see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 11-12]
2. 39 fracture zones identified in six wells between 1400 and 5000 m depth, concentrated in three depth clusters:
	- Cluster I (1800–2000 m TVD): unaltered porphyritic granite; highly permeable.
	- Cluster II (3000–3400 m TVD): altered, densely fractured granite; forms the upper reservoir (stimulated 1997).
	- Cluster III (~4500–5000 m TVD): massive, low-permeability granite; hosts deep fractured reservoir with major isolated fracture zones (e.g., GPK3-FZ4770). see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 1-2], [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-11]
3. Average fracture zone strike is N160°E±15°, dip >60° (dominantly west-dipping), consistent between Buntsandstein sediments and granite. see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 11-12]
4. Fracture zone thickness ranges 0.5–60 m (avg. 12 m); interpreted as intermediate-scale normal faults with moderate offset. see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-9]
5. At depth, the balance of dominant fracture dip evolves: east-dipping dominates at 1420–2700 m TVD; conjugate sets at 2700–4800 m; west-dipping below 4800 m. see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-5]
6. Stress orientation (σH ≈ N169°E ±14°) aligns with fracture zones, suggesting critically oriented fractures may shear during hydraulic stimulation. see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 12-13]
7. Some fracture zones (e.g., GPK2-FZ3900, GPK3-FZ4770) correlate with microseismicity and VSP reflectors, interpreted as large-scale faults crossing the basement. see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 11-12]

## Limitations
- Fracture zone list is not exhaustive; may be updated with future data acquisition see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 12-13].
- Orientation estimation is uncertain when image quality is poor or structures are not well-defined planar features; some orientations are hypothetical (e.g., GPK2 deep zones) see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 7-9].
- Fracture type (extension vs. shear) and fracture length cannot be reliably estimated from borehole images alone see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-9].
- Permeability state before/after stimulation is inferred from mud losses and flow logs; geological data alone cannot directly indicate natural permeability see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-5], [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The border of a brecciated zone or the average orientation of sub-parallel individual fractures within a zone represents the overall fracture zone orientation see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-5].
- Fracture zones are steeply dipping (>60°) normal faults with moderate offset, analogous to structures in active graben settings see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-11].
- The three depth clusters reflect large-scale faults equivalent to those identified in the sedimentary cover from seismic reflection see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-11].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Fracture zone depth (Measured Depth and True Vertical Depth Sub Sea), dip direction, dip angle, and thickness (m) see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 7-9], Table 1.
- Fracture zone classification level (1–3) based on permeability/mud loss and alteration see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-7].
- Mesoscale fracture strike set directions (N-S, NW-SE, NE-SW) and dip distribution (east vs. west) as function of depth see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-5].
- Stress field orientation (σH ≈ N169°E) relative to fracture orientation see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 12-13].

## Reusable Claims
- At Soultz, 39 naturally fractured zones cluster into three depth intervals (1800–2000 m, 3000–3400 m, 4500–5000 m TVD) with distinct permeability and alteration characteristics; the intermediate cluster forms the upper reservoir in an altered, densely fractured granite see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 1-2], [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-11].
- Mesoscale fractures in the granite basement strike N0°E to N175°E, with a clockwise rotation of 10–15° relative to Rhenish faults; the dip distribution varies with depth, from east-dominant in the upper section to west-dominant below 4800 m see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-5], [Dezayes 2010, pp. 11-12].
- Average fracture zone orientation (N160°E±15°, steep west dip) aligns favorably with the present-day maximum horizontal stress (N169°E±14°), enhancing potential for shear reactivation during EGS stimulation see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 12-13].
- Fracture zone thickness ranges from 0.5 to 60 m (average 12 m), consistent with intermediate-scale normal faults; thickness is 5–10 times smaller than volcanic strike-slip fault analogues see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-9].

## Candidate Concepts
- [[fracture reservoir]] – The reservoir consists of three fracture-zone clusters embedded in low-permeability granite; permeability is related to fractured and altered zones see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 1-2].
- [[conjugate fractures]] – Two predominant N-S striking fracture sets dipping east and west form a conjugate system; their relative dominance changes with depth see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-5].
- [[hydrothermally altered and fractured zone (HAFZ)]] – Fracture zones with intense alteration (illite, calcite, quartz) and low natural permeability that can be enhanced by stimulation see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-9].
- [[stress-controlled shear reactivation]] – Fracture zones oriented close to σH are likely to undergo shear during hydraulic stimulation see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 12-13].

## Candidate Methods
- [[fracture zone identification via composite logs]] – Integrating borehole images, gamma ray (K, Th, U), cuttings petrography, caliper, and drilling parameters to delineate fracture zones see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-5].
- [[fracture zone classification by permeability level]] – Level 1: permeable before stimulation (drilling mud losses); Level 2: >20% flow during stimulation or significant hydrothermal alteration; Level 3: <20% flow and poor alteration halo see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 5-7].
- [[borehole image fracture orientation statistics]] – Deriving mean strike/dip from border of brecciated zone or 3D average of individual sub-parallel fractures; using Schmidt lower-hemisphere projections see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-5].
- [[3D fault modelling from borehole and seismic data]] – Correlating fracture zones at well scale with microseismicity and VSP results to define large-scale faults see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 11-12].

## Connections To Other Work
- The fracture zone dataset feeds into 3D models used for hydraulic stimulation and circulation simulations (Sausse et al., 2010; Rachez et al., 2006; Baujard and Bruel, 2005; Kohl and Megel, 2005) see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-2].
- Fracture zone classification builds on natural flow zones identified by Vuataz et al. (1990), Genter et al. (1995), and post-stimulation fluid pathways confirmed by Sanjuan et al. (2006) and Evans et al. (2005) see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 2-2].
- The observed fault spacing (∼500 m) matches fault spacing in the sedimentary cover from seismic reflection (Valley and Evans, 2007; Genter and Castaing, 1997) see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-11].
- Comparison of fracture zone thickness with fault zone architecture models (Caine et al., 1996; Micarelli et al., 2003; Gudmundsson et al., 2001) supports interpretation as normal faults see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 9-9].

## Open Questions
- What is the geometric and kinematic relationship between the fracture zone clusters in the granitic basement and the mapped major faults in the overlying sedimentary cover? see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 11-12]
- How do thermo-hydromechanical and chemical processes evolve along major fracture zones during long-term circulation (temperature drawdown, mineral dissolution/precipitation, reservoir clogging)? see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 12-13]
- Are there additional fracture zones that have not been detected with current log intervals? see [Dezayes 2010, pp. 12-13]
