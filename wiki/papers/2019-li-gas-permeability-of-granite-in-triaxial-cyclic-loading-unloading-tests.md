# Gas Permeability of Granite in Triaxial Cyclic Loading/Unloading Tests.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the evolution of gas permeability in granite specimens subjected to triaxial cyclic loading/unloading tests under different confining pressures.

## Research Question
To investigate the evolution of gas permeability of granite in triaxial cyclic loading/unloading tests [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].

## Study Area / Data
The granite specimens were prepared from cores obtained by the State Key Laboratory of Geomechanics and Geotechnical Engineering, Institute of Rock and Soil Mechanics, Chinese Academy of Sciences [Li 2019, pp. 1-4]. Specimens were cylinders with a diameter of 50 mm and a height of 100 mm [Li 2019, pp. 1-4]. X-ray diffraction analysis showed the mineral composition was approximately 29.39% quartz, 42.87% feldspar, and 26.40% mica [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].

## Methods
The tests were conducted using a rock T-H-M-C coupled testing system [Li 2019, pp. 1-4]. The experimental procedure involved: (1) applying and stabilizing a confining pressure (Pc = 2, 5, or 7 MPa); (2) performing cyclic loading/unloading on the deviatoric stress at specified percentages (0%, 30%, 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, and 100%) of the estimated peak strength; (3) measuring gas permeability at the lower stress limit of each cycle using the transient pulse method [Li 2019, pp. 1-4]. The gas permeability (k) was calculated using the formula: k = (2P0μLV) / (A(P1² - P2²)), where μ is the gas viscosity, L is the specimen length, A is the cross-sectional area, V is the reference volume, and P1, P2 are upstream and downstream pressures [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].

## Key Findings
- Plastic hysteresis loops form between the loading and unloading curves of successive cycles, and the lower limit of axial strain in each cycle increases with the number of cycles [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- Specimen volume initially compresses during the first several cycles, with main deformation along the axial direction. As deviatoric stress increases, volume strain changes from compression to dilatancy [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- Upon shear failure, specimens are divided by macroscopic cracks inclined to the axial direction [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- Gas permeability curves exhibit three stages: a stable decline, a slow rise, and a sharp rise [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- Gas permeability increases by 2-3 orders of magnitude after the brittle failure of the specimen [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- The inflection points on the volume strain and lateral strain curves can be used as important references to predict changes in permeability [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- The axial elastic modulus decreases with increasing cycle number [Li 2019, pp. 4-7].

## Limitations
The study focuses solely on granite. The tests were conducted under specific, controlled laboratory conditions with a limited range of confining pressures (2, 5, 7 MPa). The gas used was nitrogen, and its properties were assumed constant.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Gas flow through the rock follows Darcy's law [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- The viscosity of the nitrogen gas (μ) is constant at approximately 1.8×10⁻⁵ Pa·s at 20°C [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- The tests assume homogeneous and isotropic initial specimen properties.

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Confining pressure (Pc): 2, 5, 7 MPa.
- Deviatoric stress (σ₁ - σ₃).
- Axial strain, lateral strain, volume strain.
- Gas permeability (k).
- Cycle number (C0, C1, ..., C7).
- Axial elastic modulus (E).

## Reusable Claims
- Under triaxial cyclic loading, granite exhibits a transition from volume compression to dilatancy as deviatoric stress increases, which is associated with the initiation and coalescence of microcracks [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- The gas permeability of granite under cyclic loading follows a three-stage evolution (decline, slow rise, sharp rise), with the final stage showing an increase of 2-3 orders of magnitude upon brittle failure [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- The inflection points in the volumetric and lateral strain curves during cyclic loading can serve as precursors to significant permeability increases in granite [Li 2019, pp. 1-4].
- The progressive damage in granite under cyclic loading is evidenced by a continuous decrease in the axial elastic modulus with increasing cycle number [Li 2019, pp. 4-7].
