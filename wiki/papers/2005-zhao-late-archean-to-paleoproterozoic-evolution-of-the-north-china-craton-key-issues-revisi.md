# Late Archean to Paleoproterozoic Evolution of the North China Craton: Key Issues Revisited.

## One-line Summary
A refined tectonic model proposes that the North China Craton formed through the Paleoproterozoic collision of the Eastern and Western Blocks along the Trans-North China Orogen at ~1.85 Ga, incorporating new evidence for an earlier amalgamation within the Western Block and a rifting-closure event at the eastern margin. [Zhao 2005, pp. 1-3, 21-22]

## Research Question
What are the timing and tectonic processes involved in the amalgamation of the Eastern and Western Blocks of the North China Craton, and what is the nature of its internal subdivisions like the Khondalite Belt and the Jiao-Liao-Ji Belt? [Zhao 2005, pp. 1-4]

## Study Area / Data
The study encompasses the North China Craton, a ~1.5 million km² region covering northern China, southern northeast China, Inner Mongolia, the Bohai Bay, and the northern Yellow Sea. The data synthesized is from structural, petrological, geochemical, and geochronological investigations across several key areas, including the Trans-North China Orogen and the Eastern and Western Blocks. [Zhao 2005, pp. 1-2, 4]

## Methods
- **Geochronology:** SHRIMP and evaporation zircon U-Pb dating to determine magmatic crystallization and metamorphic ages. Sm-Nd and Ar-Ar dating on minerals like garnet and hornblende to constrain metamorphic events. [Zhao 2005, pp. 6, 13-14, 18-20]
- **Isotope Geochemistry:** Nd, Re-Os, and Hf isotopic analyses to constrain crustal formation ages and mantle evolution. [Zhao 2005, pp. 6-7]
- **Metamorphic Petrology:** Thermobarometric estimates combined with mineral assemblage analysis to define P–T paths (e.g., clockwise vs. anticlockwise). [Zhao 2005, pp. 5, 8-9]
- **Structural Geology:** Field-based structural mapping to identify deformation phases, shear zones, and fold geometries. [Zhao 2005, pp. 16-17]

## Key Findings
- A three-fold tectonic subdivision of the North China Craton into the Eastern Block, Western Block, and intervening Trans-North China Orogen is supported by new Nd, Re-Os, and Hf isotopic data. [Zhao 2005, pp. 4-7]
- The Western Block itself formed earlier through the Paleoproterozoic amalgamation of the Yinshan Block (north) and Ordos Block (south) along the Khondalite Belt, with metamorphism at 2.0–1.9 Ga. [Zhao 2005, pp. 1, 7-10, 21]
- The Eastern Block’s Jiao-Liao-Ji Belt does not represent a continent-arc-continent collision; evidence from bimodal volcanics, similar basement on both sides, and structural data favors a model of Paleoproterozoic rifting (2.2–1.9 Ga) followed by closure at ~1.9 Ga. [Zhao 2005, pp. 1, 13-18, 22]
- The Dongwanzi and Zunhua mafic-ultramafic complexes are not Archean ophiolites. Geochemical data show their chromites are not Alpine-type but are consistent with continental intrusions, and the central Dongwanzi belt is a Phanerozoic intrusion. [Zhao 2005, pp. 10-13]
- The main collision between the Eastern and Western Blocks occurred at ~1.85 Ga, not ~2.5 Ga. This is evidenced by consistent 1.85–1.80 Ga metamorphic and deformational ages from complexes across the entire Trans-North China Orogen. [Zhao 2005, pp. 17-21, 22]

## Limitations
- The tectonic model for the evolution of the Western Block (amalgamation of Yinshan and Ordos Blocks) is preliminary and based on data primarily from the peripheries, as the Ordos Block is largely covered by a Mesozoic-Cenozoic basin. [Zhao 2005, pp. 7-8, 10]
- The claim that the Dongwanzi complex is not an Archean ophiolite is partly based on interpretations of the central belt’s age without its own direct geochronological data; it relies on regional geological context and geochemical comparisons. [Zhao 2005, pp. 11-13]
- Few Neoarchean to Paleoproterozoic magmatic arcs are known where rocks remained undisturbed for ~700 Ma before being deformed, though global analogs are presented to argue this is possible. [Zhao 2005, pp. 20-21]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The interpretation that clockwise P–T paths from high-grade metamorphic rocks reflect continental collision and anticlockwise paths reflect magmatic underplating/intrusion is a fundamental assumption. [Zhao 2005, pp. 5-6]
- The correlation of the Fenzishan, North Liaohe, and Laoling Groups as the “northern belt” and the Jingshan, South Liaohe, and Ji’an Groups as the “southern belt” is based on stratigraphic correlation, supporting the rift-closure model. [Zhao 2005, pp. 13-14]
- The model assumes that the Dongwanzi and Zunhua chromitites, which plot outside the Alpine-type field, are diagnostic of continental mafic-ultramafic intrusions. [Zhao 2005, pp. 12-13]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **P–T path types:** Clockwise vs. anticlockwise paths as indicators of collisional vs. underplating settings. [Zhao 2005, pp. 5, 8-9]
- **Zircon CL texture and Th/U ratio:** Distinguishing structureless, highly luminescent, low-Th/U metamorphic zircons from oscillatory-zoned magmatic zircons. [Zhao 2005, pp. 17-19]
- **Chromite composition:** Cr# (100Cr/(Cr+Al)) and Mg# (100Mg/(Mg+Fe²⁺)) as discriminants between Alpine-type ophiolitic and continental intrusive chromitites. [Zhao 2005, pp. 12-13]

## Reusable Claims
**Claim 1:** Clockwise metamorphic P–T paths with isothermal decompression in granulite terrains are a key indicator of continental collisional orogenesis, as observed in both the Trans-North China Orogen and the Khondalite Belt. [Zhao 2005, pp. 5, 8-9]
- *Condition:* This is diagnostic when petrographic evidence confirms the sequence of mineral assemblages.
- *Limitation:* This interpretation does not apply for anticlockwise P-T paths with isobaric cooling, which are interpreted to reflect magmatic underplating.

**Claim 2:** SHRIMP U-Pb dating of low-Th/U, structureless metamorphic zircon rims or grains consistently yields an age of ~1.85 Ga for the peak metamorphism across the entire Trans-North China Orogen, irrespective of the protolith’s Archean or Paleoproterozoic age. [Zhao 2005, pp. 17-19]
- *Condition:* Applicable to high-grade gneisses and granulites within the central orogenic belt.
- *Limitation:* This age represents the timing of Paleoproterozoic collision and is not applicable to earlier events in the Eastern or Western Blocks.

**Claim 3:** The geochemical composition of chromites, specifically plotting outside the low-Fe, high-Cr/Mg field of Alpine-type podiform chromitites on a Cr# vs. Mg# diagram, indicates a non-ophiolitic, continental intrusive origin. [Zhao 2005, pp. 12-13]
- *Condition:* The assessment relies on comparing the chromite composition to known global ophiolite databases.
- *Limitation:* The conclusion was specifically applied to the Zunhua and Dongwanzi complexes in the North China Craton.

## Connections To Other Work
- The 1.85 Ga collision event in the North China Craton is correlated with a global pulse of orogenesis used to define the Paleo-Mesoproterozoic supercontinent Columbia. [Zhao 2005, pp. 21-22]
- The concept of a long-lived continental-margin arc in the Trans-North China Orogen is compared with similar 1.8–1.0 Ga convergent orogens in southeastern Laurentia and southern Baltica. [Zhao 2005, pp. 20-21]
- The controversy over the Dongwanzi ophiolite is directly linked to the debate on whether the North China Craton's final amalgamation was at 2.5 Ga (Kusky and Li, 2003) or 1.85 Ga (this model). [Zhao 2005, pp. 10-11, 17]

## Open Questions
- The exact timing and tectonic processes of the proposed collision between the Ordos and Yinshan Blocks remain under investigation. [Zhao 2005, p. 10]
- The Archean Dongwanzi and Zunhua complexes “need to be further examined before they can be considered as examples of Archean ophiolitic complexes.” [Zhao 2005, p. 13]
- A detailed characterization of magmatic arcs within the Trans-North China Orogen is needed to fully understand the long history of subduction before the 1.85 Ga collision. [Not explicitly stated as an open question but implied from the model's description on pp. 20-21]
