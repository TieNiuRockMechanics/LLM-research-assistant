# Structural and Physical Property Characterization in the Wenchuan Earthquake Fault Scientific Drilling Project — Hole 1 (WFSD-1).

## One-line Summary
This study characterizes the structural and physical properties of rocks from the 1201.15 m deep WFSD-1 borehole using integrated core analysis and geophysical downhole logging to investigate the faulting mechanism of the 2008 Wenchuan earthquake.

## Research Question
The research aims to investigate the faulting mechanism and physical properties of the seismogenic zone of the 2008 Mw 7.9 Wenchuan earthquake by characterizing the structures and physical properties of rocks in the WFSD-1 borehole [Li 2013, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
The study is located at the WFSD-1 drilling site in Bajiaomiao Village, Dujiangyan County, Sichuan province, on the hanging wall of the Yingxiu–Beichuan fault (YBF) in the Neoproterozoic Pengguan complex, approximately 385 m west of the surface rupture [Li 2013, pp. 2-3]. Data include continuous core samples recovered from 32 to 1201.15 m depth and a suite of geophysical downhole logs (P-wave velocity, natural gamma ray, self-potential, resistivity, density, porosity, temperature, magnetic susceptibility, and ultrasound borehole images) acquired during six logging sessions [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 4-6].

## Methods
The methods involve core investigation and integrated geophysical downhole logging. Logging was conducted six times using the Micro Logger II and JGS-1B digital logger [Li 2013, pp. 4-6]. Parameters measured include Natural Gamma Radioactivity (NGR), density, focused resistivity, apparent resistivity, natural potential, P-wave velocity, neutron porosity, borehole diameter, borehole deviation, temperature, well fluid conductivity, ultrasonic imaging, and magnetic susceptibility [Li 2013, pp. 4-6]. Sampling intervals are 10 cm for some parameters and 1 cm for others [Li 2013, pp. 4-6]. Cores were marked, scanned, and analyzed for structural features like fractures and bedding [Li 2013, pp. 6-7].

## Key Findings
1.  The geothermal gradient for the entire hole is 2.05 °C/100 m, with 1.85 °C/100 m in the Pengguan complex (above 585.75 m) and 2.15 °C/100 m in the Xujiahe Formation (below 585.75 m) [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 6-7].
2.  Logging parameters primarily depend on rock lithology. The Pengguan complex has relatively higher density, neutron porosity, and apparent resistivity than the Xujiahe Formation [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 7-8].
3.  All major fault zones are characterized by high magnetic susceptibility, low density, high porosity, mostly low resistivity, high natural gamma ray, and high P-wave velocity [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 11-12].
4.  The high magnetic susceptibility values in fault gouges most likely result from the transformation of magnetic minerals by frictional heating during the earthquake [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 12-13].
5.  The Yingxiu–Beichuan fault (YBF) zone exposed in WFSD-1 can be subdivided into five different parts based on logging responses, corresponding to specific fault rocks [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 12-13].
6.  The Wenchuan earthquake fault zone (Principal Slip Zone) is located at 585.75–594.5 m depth, with an average inclination direction of N305° and a dip angle of 71° [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 13-14].
7.  Fracture directions in the hanging wall (Pengguan complex) and footwall (Xujiahe Formation) are different, suggesting completely different stress field directions and implying the upper Pengguan complex may not be local [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 13-14].

## Limitations
1.  Density and neutron porosity logging data are absent for depths 594–780 m due to high mud weight (1.6 g/cm³) during the fifth logging session [Li 2013, pp. 4-6, 11-12].
2.  Ultrasonic imaging logging encountered problems, yielding a lack of data between 725 and 780 m depth [Li 2013, pp. 4-6].
3.  The exact plane or zone of slip activated during the 2008 Wenchuan earthquake was not definitively identified in this study, though a likely candidate is discussed [Li 2013, pp. 13-14].
4.  Some fracture density in cores may be overestimated due to drilling-induced cracks [Li 2013, pp. 13-14].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The high gamma radiation in WFSD-1 fault gouges is related to both the fault gouge itself (small grain size, high clay content) and its protolith lithology [Li 2013, pp. 11-12].
2.  The slight temperature increase (~0.15 °C) observed at 585–600 m depth reflects residual heat produced by friction during the Wenchuan earthquake [Li 2013, pp. 4-6, 13-14].
3.  The electron density of rocks approximates its volume density for density logging [Li 2013, pp. 4-6].
4.  The neutron logging measures the apparent porosity of wall rocks based on the hydrogen atom index [Li 2013, pp. 4-6].

## Reusable Claims
1.  The geothermal gradient in the Neoproterozoic Pengguan complex (volcanic rocks) is 1.85 °C/100 m, while in the Late Triassic Xujiahe Formation (sedimentary rocks) it is 2.15 °C/100 m [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 6-7].
2.  Major fault zones in the WFSD-1 borehole are consistently characterized by high magnetic susceptibility, low density, high porosity, low resistivity, high natural gamma ray, and high P-wave velocity [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 11-12].
3.  The Principal Slip Zone of the 2008 Wenchuan earthquake in WFSD-1 is located at 585.75–594.5 m depth, with an average structural orientation of N305°/71° [Li 2013, pp. 1-1, 13-14].
4.  Fault gouge layers in WFSD-1 exhibit anomalously high natural gamma radiation, a characteristic that differs from findings in the Taiwan Chelungpu fault Drilling Project (TCDP) [Li 2013, pp. 11-12].
5.  The difference in fracture orientations between the hanging wall (Pengguan complex) and footwall (Xujiahe Formation) of the YBF indicates distinct stress fields, suggesting the Pengguan complex is allochthonous [Li 2013, pp. 13-14].

## Connections To Other Work
The study compares its findings with other major fault-zone drilling projects, including the Nojima fault program (Japan), Taiwan Chelungpu fault Drilling Project (TCDP), San Andreas Fault Observatory at Depth (SAFOD), and Japan Trench Fast Drilling Project (J-FAST) [Li 2013, pp. 1-1]. It specifically notes differences in fault gouge gamma radiation compared to TCDP [Li 2013, pp. 11-12] and similarities in magnetic susceptibility anomalies with TCDP [Li 2013, pp. 12-13].

## Open Questions
1.  What are the precise mechanisms linking frictional heating to the transformation of magnetic minerals and the resulting high magnetic susceptibility in fault gouges?
2.  How do the physical property changes (e.g., density, porosity) within the fault zone evolve over time following an earthquake?
3.  What is the full three-dimensional geometry and connectivity of the fault zone structures identified in WFSD-1?
