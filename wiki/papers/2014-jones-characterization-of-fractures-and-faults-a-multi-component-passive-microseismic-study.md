# Characterization of Fractures and Faults: A Multi-Component Passive Microseismic Study from the Ekofisk Reservoir.

## One-line Summary
A multi-component passive microseismic study uses multiplet analysis and shear wave splitting to characterize active faults and aseismic fracture sets within the Ekofisk chalk reservoir.

## Research Question
How can passive microseismic monitoring data be used to characterize the geometry and properties of both seismically active faults and aseismic fracture networks within a hydrocarbon reservoir?

## Study Area / Data
The study uses microseismic data acquired over an 18-day period in April 1997 at the Ekofisk oil field in the North Sea [Jones 2014, pp. 1-2]. Data were recorded using a modified CGG SST 500 VSP consisting of six triaxial geophones spaced 20 m apart, deployed within well 2/4-C11a at depths from 2948 m to 3048 m [Jones 2014, pp. 5-6]. The Ekofisk reservoir is located on an elongate anticline in the Central Graben and comprises the Ekofisk and Tor Formation chalks separated by the Ekofisk Tight Zone [Jones 2014, pp. 2-3]. The reservoir's permeability is controlled by an extensive network of natural fractures, with three main categories identified: healed, tectonic, and stylolite-associated fractures [Jones 2014, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
The study employs two primary analytical methods on the microseismic data:
1.  **Multiplet Analysis:** Seismic multiplets (events with similar waveforms) were identified using a cross-correlation process [Jones 2014, pp. 6-7]. A semi-automated re-picking method was used to achieve high-precision P- and S-wave arrival times [Jones 2014, pp. 7-8]. Array-based polarisation analysis was performed on multiplet waveforms to improve directional constraints [Jones 2014, pp. 8-10]. Relocated multiplet hypocentres were used for cluster analysis and to fit fault planes via principal component analysis [Jones 2014, pp. 10-11].
2.  **Shear Wave Splitting Analysis:** An automated procedure was applied to estimate S-wave splitting parameters (fast polarisation direction Φ and time lag δt) to characterize seismic anisotropy, indicative of aligned fractures [Jones 2014, pp. 11-12]. A rock physics inversion method was also used to model the anisotropy in terms of fracture sets [Jones 2014, pp. 16-17].

## Key Findings
-   Multiplet analysis identified 213 groups comprising 637 events. Reprocessing of groups with ≥4 events (256 events) delineated five main clusters of seismic activity [Jones 2014, pp. 6-7, 10-11].
-   Four of these clusters (A, C, E, H) define planar features interpreted as active fault surfaces. The inferred faults include an antithetic pair striking NNE-SSW and a normal fault striking E-W, consistent with the dominant tectonic fracture orientation in the field [Jones 2014, pp. 10-11, 14-16].
-   Variations in waveform character and P-to-S amplitude ratios between co-located multiplet groups within a cluster suggest cyclic changes in fault motion associated with locked zones or asperities [Jones 2014, pp. 14-16].
-   Shear wave splitting analysis yielded 1125 reliable measurements. The fast S-wave polarisation shows predominant strikes of NNE-SSW and NNW-SSE, consistent with the multiplet-derived fault strikes and core data [Jones 2014, pp. 12-14, 16-17].
-   The dip of the S-wave splitting measurements is predominantly >60°, agreeing with the steep dip of the multiplet-inferred faults [Jones 2014, pp. 12-14].
-   Rock physics inversion of splitting data from the four fault clusters indicates the dominant source of anisotropy is a sub-vertical fracture set striking 36° (NNE-SSW) with a fracture density of 0.06 [Jones 2014, pp. 16-17].

## Limitations
-   The microseismic monitoring period was short (18 days), limiting the assessment of temporal changes in anisotropy or stress regime [Jones 2014, pp. 16-17].
-   The geophone array was deployed in a single vertical borehole, which can lead to challenges in resolving source locations and azimuthal coverage [Jones 2014, pp. 5-6].
-   The vast majority of S-wave splitting measurements were made along sub-horizontal ray paths, limiting sensitivity to vertical transverse isotropy [Jones 2014, pp. 12-14].
-   The rock physics inversion assumes a homogeneously anisotropic model for all ray paths included [Jones 2014, pp. 16-17].

## Assumptions / Conditions
-   Multiplet events are co-located, allowing for relative relocation and waveform comparison [Jones 2014, pp. 7-8].
-   Seismic anisotropy in the chalk reservoir is primarily caused by aligned fractures [Jones 2014, pp. 11-12].
-   The S-wave velocity model used a constant Vp/Vs ratio of √3, which is low compared to published estimates for limestone [Jones 2014, pp. 5-6].
-   The rock physics model for the inversion uses fixed average P-wave velocity (2925 m/s), S-wave velocity (1597 m/s), and density (2100 kg/m³) [Jones 2014, pp. 16-17].

## Reusable Claims
-   Multiplet analysis of microseismic data can provide high-precision locations that delineate the geometry of seismically active fault planes within a reservoir [Jones 2014, pp. 10-11].
-   Variations in waveform character and amplitude ratios between co-located multiplet groups can indicate changes in source mechanism along a fault, potentially related to asperities or locked zones [Jones 2014, pp. 14-16].
-   Shear wave splitting measurements from passive seismic data can be used to infer the orientation and density of aseismic fracture sets that control reservoir permeability [Jones 2014, pp. 11-12, 16-17].
-   A rock physics inversion of shear wave splitting data can constrain the properties of fracture sets, even when ray paths are not optimally oriented for simple interpretation [Jones 2014, pp. 16-17].

## Connections To Other Work
-   The study builds on earlier event location work at Ekofisk by Jones et al. (2010, 2014) [Jones 2014, pp. 5-6].
-   The multiplet analysis approach follows methods used at the Valhall field by De Meersman et al. (2009) [Jones 2014, pp. 1-2, 14-16].
-   The shear wave splitting analysis uses the automated procedure of Wuestefeld et al. (2010) and the inversion scheme of Verdon et al. (2009) [Jones 2014, pp. 11-12, 16-17].
-   Findings on fracture orientation are consistent with core data and seismic reflection studies from the Ekofisk field (e.g., Brewster et al. 1986; Toublanc et al. 2005) [Jones 2014, pp. 2-3, 10-11].

## Open Questions

-   Can the observed spatial variations in anisotropy (e.g., towards the Tor Formation) be confirmed and better constrained with longer-term monitoring and more complex anisotropy models?
-   What is the specific relationship between the microseismic activity on the diffuse cluster G and operations in the nearby well?
