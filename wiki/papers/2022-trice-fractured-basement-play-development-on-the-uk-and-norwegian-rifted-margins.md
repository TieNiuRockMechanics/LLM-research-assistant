# Fractured Basement Play Development on the UK and Norwegian Rifted Margins.

## One-line Summary
This paper reviews and compares the geological characteristics, evolution, and development challenges of fractured crystalline basement petroleum plays on the UK and Norwegian rifted margins, focusing on the Rona Ridge (UK) and Utsira High (Norway) as analogues for prospective plays like Frøya and Lofoten.

## Research Question
What are the key geological processes (faulting, fissuring, hydrothermal alteration, and weathering) that control porosity and permeability development in fractured basement reservoirs on the UK Continental Shelf (UKCS) and Norwegian Continental Shelf (NCS), and how can cross-border knowledge sharing accelerate the understanding and development of this underexplored play type? [Trice 2022, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
The study area encompasses four basement play types across the UKCS and NCS, all associated with prominent gravity highs: the Rona Ridge (UK Atlantic margin), the Utsira High (Norwegian North Sea), the Frøya High (North Atlantic margin), and the Lofoten / Vesterålen area (offshore northern Norway). [Trice 2022, pp. 4-5] Outcrop analogues are found in Scotland, the Outer Hebrides, Shetland, and the islands of Bømlo, Andøya, and Vesterålen in Norway. [Trice 2022, pp. 4-8]

Data sources comprise well data (including cores, drill stem tests, and wireline logs) from key wells such as the Lancaster, Clair, and Rolvsnes discoveries, alongside seismic surveys (broadband, beam-migrated 3D volumes, walkaway VSP) and regional gravity and aeromagnetic maps. Information from the Norwegian Petroleum Directorate (NPD) Fact Pages and the DAKS™ digital analogue database for global basement producing fields is also utilized. [Trice 2022, pp. 3-5, 11-13, 17-18]

## Methods
The study employs a comparative geological analysis of the four basement plays, examining:
- **Subsurface data interpretation:** Analysis of well logs, core (petrology and fracture characterization), seismic imaging (including KPSDM, CBM, FWI, and CRAM for the Utsira and Frøya highs), and drill stem test data [Trice 2022, pp. 17-19].
- **Outcrop analogue studies:** Use of onshore exposures to constrain fault and fracture network properties, structural deformation history, and the impact of weathering processes. Examples include field mapping and terrestrial laser scanning in the Lewisian Gneiss Complex and study of the Bømlo outcrops [Trice 2022, pp. 19-21].
- **Global analogue comparison:** Assessment of commercial oil and gas field data from the DAKS™ database to contextualize porosity ranges and common geological features (e.g., buried hill traps) [Trice 2022, pp. 11-14].
- **Reservoir classification:** Classification of fractured reservoirs according to the scheme of Nelson (2001) into Type I (porosity entirely from fractures) and Type II (fracture plus matrix porosity) [Trice 2022, pp. 4-5].
- **Geophysical well placement:** Integration of seismic fault mapping and interpreted regional joint trends to target high-angle wells for exploration and appraisal [Trice 2022, pp. 16-17].

## Key Findings
- The term “basement play” is defined in this paper as a play where the reservoir is a crystalline crustal rock with an igneous protolith, hosting a natural, hydraulically conductive fracture network developed through cooling, faulting, hydrothermal fluid ingress, and near-surface weathering. [Trice 2022, pp. 1-3]
- The four basement plays (Rona, Utsira, Frøya, Lofoten) share a common geological framework as long-lived, fault-bounded structural highs formed during late Paleozoic-Mesozoic extension, subsequently buried (“buried hill” traps) primarily during the Cretaceous, blanketed by marine mudstones that act as a regional seal. [Trice 2022, pp. 9-10]
- Near-surface tectonic fissuring during rifting, associated hydrothermal mineralization, and subaerial weathering are key controls on porosity and permeability development. Deeply penetrating open tensile fissures (Mode I fractures) are particularly important for creating well-connected void spaces. [Trice 2022, pp. 11-14]
- A critical distinction exists between plays: the Utsira High exhibits pervasive saprolite development from deep weathering resulting in average porosities near 10% (Type I-II fractured reservoir), whereas the Rona Ridge lacks pervasive saprolite and its porosity of around 4% is almost entirely fracture-hosted (Type I reservoir). [Trice 2022, pp. 13-16]
- Late Cenozoic uplift and tilting, coeval with hydrocarbon charge, are interpreted to enhance porosity and permeability through tensile failure, dissolution of cements (e.g., calcite) by acidic fluids, and prevention of mineral infill. [Trice 2022, pp. 10-11]
- Seismic imaging breakthroughs (broadband, CBM, CRAM) have significantly improved the resolution of complex geology and fault-fracture networks in basement highs, aiding well placement. [Trice 2022, pp. 17-18]
- Key drilling challenges include severe drilling fluid invasion leading to lost circulation and formation damage, exacerbated by overbalanced drilling in Type I reservoirs. At Rona, a specific calcium-brine mud interacted with drilling fines to create a damaging, glutinous paste. [Trice 2022, pp. 18-19]
- Knowledge sharing on consistent terminology, seismic processing workflows, optimized drilling techniques (e.g., controlled mud level methods), and formation evaluation (e.g., NMR effective porosity, sidewall-core handling) is critical for de-risking future basement exploration and development. [Trice 2022, pp. 19-21]

## Limitations
- Direct comparison between UKCS and NCS basement plays is challenging due to a “current paucity of public data available.” [Trice 2022, pp. 4-5]
- The age and burial history of the deep Paleozoic basins overlying the southern Frøya High remain unknown. [Trice 2022, pp. 9-10]
- The lithotype and classification (Type I vs. Type II) of the Frøya and Lofoten plays are uncertain due to insufficient well data. [Trice 2022, pp. 5-8]
- Comparing porosities between fractured basement analogues has “severe limits,” particularly for Type I reservoirs where measured porosity is governed by wellbore intersection with the fracture network. [Trice 2022, pp. 16-17]
- The suitability of Controlled Mud Level (CML) drilling methods for fractured basement has not been demonstrated. [Trice 2022, pp. 18-19]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The term “basement play” is specifically defined for this paper as a play in crystalline crustal rock with an igneous protolith, potentially having undergone regional metamorphism up to granulite facies. [Trice 2022, pp. 1-3]
- The paper assumes that high fracability is typical of granitic compositions (high quartz and feldspar content) common to many producing basement fields worldwide. [Trice 2022, pp. 9-10]
- A primary assumption is that the joint and fracture systems in the offshore Norwegian plays are analogous to those in onshore outcrops, which show deeply penetrating, depth-independent joint frequencies extending to depths of at least 1 km. [Trice 2022, pp. 9-10]
- It is assumed that uplift coeval with hydrocarbon charge enhances porosity and permeability, a process consistent with published data but without direct evidence of fracture reactivation from Rona Ridge cores. [Trice 2022, pp. 10-11]
- The absence of pervasive saprolite at Rona is speculated to result from its narrow ridge-like paleotopography or its metamorphic fabric, assuming that the paleoenvironment was not drastically different from other plays. [Trice 2022, pp. 14-16]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Reservoir Type:** Classification as Type I (fracture-only porosity) or Type II (fracture + matrix porosity) fractured reservoir.
- **Porosity:** Average values range from ~4% (Type I, e.g., Lancaster) to <10% (Type II, weathered, e.g., Utsira).
- **Oil Column Height:** Ranges from 30-40 m (Utsira/Rolvsnes) to >600 m (Lancaster).
- **Basement Composition:** Dominantly granitic to tonalitic orthogneisses, with variable metamorphic grade (amphibolite- to granulite-facies) and degree of weathering.
- **Fracture Network Properties:** Connectivity, aperture, and orientation of joints, shear fractures, and open tensile fissures (Mode I).
- **Well Path Orientation:** High-angle wells designed to be perpendicular to regional joint trends and to intersect seismically resolvable faults.
- **Drilling Fluid Invasion:** Degree of overbalance and fluid chemistry impact the severity of formation damage.

## Reusable Claims
- Fracture networks in crystalline basement are dominated by deep-penetrating, non-strata-bound joints formed from cooling, which can be reactivated by later tectonic and hydrothermal processes. [Trice 2022, pp. 9-10]
- Subconformity buried hill traps, formed by Cretaceous drowning and blanketing of paleo-highs by marine mudstones, are the most common trap type for global productive basement fields. [Trice 2022, pp. 11-13]
- Porosity in basement reservoirs is enhanced by a combination of near-surface tectonic fissuring (creating open tensile fractures), hydrothermal mineralization, and subaerial weathering which can form saprolite. [Trice 2022, pp. 11-14]
- In Type I fractured basement reservoirs, effective porosity and permeability are provided entirely by a hydraulically conductive subset of the natural fracture network, making well placement relative to this network the critical factor for productivity. [Trice 2022, pp. 16-17]
- Drilling fluid invasion is a significant operational challenge in fractured basement, causing total losses, deep formation damage, and complicating formation evaluation; the specific interaction between drilling fluid chemistry and basement lithology can create highly damaging byproducts. [Trice 2022, pp. 18-19]

## Open Questions
