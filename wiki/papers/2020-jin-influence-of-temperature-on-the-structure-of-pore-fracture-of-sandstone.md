# Influence of Temperature on the Structure of Pore–Fracture of Sandstone.

## One-line Summary
The study reveals that heating sandstone up to 800°C alters its pore-fracture structure, with 500°C identified as a critical threshold where porosity and permeability increase sharply due to enhanced pore connectivity and the formation of crack networks.

## Research Question
How does temperature (from 25°C to 800°C) influence the pore-fracture structure, including pore size distribution, connectivity, and porosity, of sandstone?

## Study Area / Data
Sandstone samples were collected from Datong, China. The samples had a density of 2.64-2.65 g/cm³, initial porosity of 2.65%, and helium permeability of 0.0699 mD. Mineral composition was 64.4% quartz, 17.5% clay, 6.4% calcite, 8.1% plagioclase, and 3.63% ankerite [Jin 2020, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM):** Used to observe surface morphological changes of pore-fractures after thermal treatment [Jin 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Low-Pressure Nitrogen Adsorption (LPNA):** Employed to quantitatively determine characteristics of micro- and transition-pores (pore size < 100 nm) using a V-Sorb 2800P apparatus at 77 K. The BET model calculated specific surface area, and the BJH model determined pore volume and size distribution from the desorption branch [Jin 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR):** A MicroMR12-025V spectrometer analyzed pore structure. Tests were conducted on water-saturated and centrifuged (0.68 MPa) samples to obtain T2 spectra, which relate to pore size distribution and connectivity [Jin 2020, pp. 3-4].
- **Thermal Treatment:** Samples were heated in a muffle furnace to target temperatures (100°C to 800°C in 100°C increments) at 5°C/min, held for 4 hours, and cooled at the same rate [Jin 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Fractal Analysis:** The modified Frenkel–Halsey–Hill (FHH) theory was applied to LPNA data (relative pressure 0.5–1) to calculate fractal dimensions of pore structures [Jin 2020, pp. 9-10].

## Key Findings
1.  **Thermal Cracking Evolution:** SEM showed pore-fracture development follows a trend of initiation–extension–connection–contraction with increasing temperature. At T < 200°C, primary microcracks open along grain boundaries. At 200–400°C, "desiccation cracking" of clay minerals increases slit-pores. At 400°C, transgranular cracking occurs. At 500°C, some microcracks shrink or close. At T > 500°C, extensive inter- and trans-crystalline microcracks form, leading to crack networks at 700°C. At 800°C, clay mineral corrosion and carbonate decomposition create round pores [Jin 2020, pp. 3-4].
2.  **Pore Structure Changes (LPNA):** Specific surface area increased to a maximum at 500°C, then decreased. Total pore volume generally increased with temperature. Pore size distribution was unimodal (peak ~4 nm) for 25–400°C, became bimodal at 500°C (peaks at 3.71 nm and 6.21 nm), and remained bimodal with a shifting second peak for 600–800°C. The volume of transition pores (10–100 nm) increased significantly above 500°C [Jin 2020, pp. 4-5].
3.  **Pore Connectivity and Porosity (NMR):** NMR T2 spectra under water-saturated condition showed trimodal distributions (micropores: T2 < 10 ms, mesopores: 10–100 ms, macropores/fractures: > 100 ms). At 500°C, peak amplitudes decreased, indicating a more compact structure. Above 500°C, spectra widened, with increased amplitudes for mesopores and macropores, indicating enhanced connectivity. NMR total porosity (φNMR) increased slowly below 500°C and sharply above it, following a Boltzmann function. Free fluid porosity (φF) increased more significantly than bound fluid porosity (φB) above 500°C [Jin 2020, pp. 5-8, 10-11].
4.  **Permeability:** Helium permeability (KHe) increased from 0.0699 mD at 25°C to a maximum of 0.8842 mD at 700°C [Jin 2020, pp. 8-10].
5.  **Fractal Dimension:** Fractal dimension (D) decreased from 25°C to 200°C, increased to a maximum of 2.606 at 500°C (indicating most complex pore structure), then decreased above 500°C, indicating a shift to a more homogeneous pore distribution [Jin 2020, pp. 9-10].
6.  **Threshold Temperature:** 500°C is identified as a threshold temperature for significant changes in pore-fracture structure, porosity, and permeability. The range 500–800°C is optimum for fluid transport in underground sandstone reservoirs [Jin 2020, pp. 10-11].

## Limitations
- The study used a single type of sandstone from Datong, China; results may vary for sandstones with different mineral compositions or initial structures.
- The thermal treatment involved a specific heating rate (5°C/min) and hold time (4 hours); different thermal histories could yield different results.
- NMR classification of pore types (micropores <10 ms, mesopores 10-100 ms, macropores >100 ms) is an adopted convention from prior work [Jin 2020, pp. 5-7].
- The centrifuge pressure for NMR irreducible water condition was fixed at 0.68 MPa [Jin 2020, pp. 3-4].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The relationship between NMR transverse relaxation time (T2) and pore size is proportional, as per the equation 1/T2 = ρ2(S/V) [Jin 2020, pp. 5-7].
- The modified FHH method applied to LPNA data at relative pressure 0.5-1 better reflects pore structure characteristics than data at 0-0.5 [Jin 2020, pp. 9-10].
- The Boltzmann equation adequately models the relationship between NMR porosity and temperature for this sandstone [Jin 2020, pp. 10-11].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Temperature (T):** Ranged from 25°C to 800°C.
- **Specific Surface Area:** Measured via LPNA using BET model.
- **Pore Volume and Size Distribution:** Measured via LPNA using BJH model.
- **NMR T2 Distribution:** Spectra under water-saturated (Sw) and irreducible water (Sir) conditions.
- **NMR Porosity (φNMR):** Total porosity from NMR, comprising bound fluid porosity (φB) and free fluid porosity (φF).
- **Helium Permeability (KHe):** Measured with ULTRA-PERMTM 200 Instrument.
- **Fractal Dimension (D):** Calculated from LPNA data using modified FHH theory.

## Reusable Claims
- Heating sandstone to temperatures above 500°C significantly increases its total porosity and permeability by creating interconnected crack networks and enlarging pore sizes [Jin 2020, pp. 10-11].
- The pore structure of sandstone becomes most complex (highest fractal dimension) at 500°C, after which it becomes more homogeneous with further heating [Jin 2020, pp. 9-10].
- The combination of SEM, LPNA, and NMR provides a comprehensive characterization of pore-fracture evolution across micro- to macro-scales in thermally treated sandstone [Jin 2020, pp. 1-2].
- For the studied sandstone, 500°C is a critical threshold temperature where the dominant pore-fracture development mechanism shifts from microcrack initiation and extension to crack network formation and connectivity enhancement [Jin 2020, pp. 10-11].
- The increase in free fluid porosity (φF) is more sensitive to temperature above 500°C than bound fluid porosity (φB), indicating the creation of larger, more connected pores [Jin 2020, pp. 10-11].
