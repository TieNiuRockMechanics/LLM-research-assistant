# 油页岩热破裂规律分形理论研究 [Theoretical Research on Thermal Cracking Fractals Laws of Oil Shale].

## One-line Summary
This study applies fractal theory to analyze the thermal cracking patterns of oil shale between 200°C and 600°C, finding that fissure distributions are self-similar with fractal dimensions ranging from 1.0 to 1.5.

## Research Question
The research addresses the lack of studies on the fractal evolution of rock fractures under high-temperature conditions, specifically aiming to use fractal theory to quantify the development and complexity of thermal fissures in oil shale during in-situ pyrolysis [Kang 2010, pp. 1-4].

## Study Area / Data
The oil shale samples were collected from the Fushun West Open-pit Mine in Liaoning, China, belonging to the Cenozoic Tertiary period. The test specimens were 7.0 mm cubic samples with an oil yield of 8.2% and fine primary bedding. Micro-CT images of three internal cross-sections (upper, middle, lower) perpendicular to the bedding were used as the primary data source [Kang 2010, pp. 1-4].

## Methods
The study employed the µCT225kVFCB high-precision micro-CT system to non-destructively observe the internal thermal cracking process. The fractal analysis used the fissure length-number method, where the number of fissures N(L) with length ≥ L follows a power-law distribution N(L) = A0 * L^(-D). The fractal dimension D and the logarithm of the initial distribution value lnA0 were calculated by fitting the linear relationship between lnN(L) and lnL for fissure lengths L ≥ 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 mm [Kang 2010, pp. 4-7].

## Key Findings
The fissure distributions in oil shale under different temperatures exhibit strong self-similarity, conforming to fractal laws. The fractal dimension D ranges from 1.0 to 1.5. D increases with temperature from 200°C to 400°C, reaching a maximum around 400°C (indicating the most complex fissure morphology), then decreases from 400°C to 600°C. The logarithm of the initial distribution value lnA0 increases gradually with temperature, with the most rapid increase occurring between 300°C and 400°C, indicating the fastest growth rate of macro-fissure proportion, which reaches its maximum at 600°C [Kang 2010, pp. 4-7].

## Limitations
The study focuses on a specific temperature range (200°C–600°C) and uses small cubic samples (7.0 mm). The fractal analysis is based on 2D CT cross-sections, which may not fully capture the 3D complexity of the fissure network. The results are specific to the oil shale from the Fushun deposit [Kang 2010, pp. 1-4, 4-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The analysis assumes that the fissure distribution within the oil shale samples is self-similar and follows a fractal pattern. It is assumed that the micro-CT images accurately represent the internal fissure structures and that the temperature range (200°C–600°C) is representative of the thermal cracking process relevant to in-situ pyrolysis [Kang 2010, pp. 4-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Temperature (T): 200°C to 600°C.
- Fractal Dimension (D): Quantifies fissure pattern complexity.
- Initial Distribution Value (A0) or its logarithm (lnA0): Relates to the proportion of large fissures.
- Fissure Length (L): Used as the scale parameter in the fractal measurement.
- Fissure Count (N(L)): Number of fissures with length ≥ L.

## Reusable Claims
- For the studied oil shale, the fractal dimension D of thermal fissures increases with temperature up to approximately 400°C, then decreases, indicating that fissure morphology is most complex at this temperature [Kang 2010, pp. 4-7].
- The logarithm of the fissure distribution initial value (lnA0) increases with temperature, with the steepest rise between 300°C and 400°C, signifying the most rapid increase in the proportion of macro-fissures during this interval [Kang 2010, pp. 4-7].
- The thermal cracking evolution in oil shale transitions from a mode dominated by the generation and rapid development of new fissures (200°C–400°C) to a mode dominated by the expansion, connection, and coalescence of existing fissures (400°C–600°C) [Kang 2010, pp. 4-7].
