# Production Capacity and Mining Plan Optimization of Fault/Fracture-Controlled EGS Model in Gonghe Basin.

## One-line Summary
This study develops and optimizes a fault/fracture-controlled Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) model for the Gonghe Basin using numerical simulation, identifying optimal parameters and a well layout for sustainable geothermal energy extraction.

## Research Question
How can the production capacity and mining plan for a fault/fracture-controlled Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) in the Gonghe Basin be optimized to enhance heat extraction efficiency and sustainability?

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Gonghe Basin, located on the northeastern edge of the Tibetan Plateau. It is characterized by complex tectonic structures, including several strike-slip faults (e.g., Qinghai South Mountain fault, Heka South Mountain fault), which provide natural high-permeability channels for geothermal fluid flow [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-3]. The basin has an area of 3092.89 square kilometers and abundant Hot Dry Rock (HDR) resources estimated at 4.3 × 10^6 EJ, accounting for over 20% of China's total HDR resources [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-3]. Key data include a temperature gradient of 6.7-6.8°C/100 m in basement granite, a maximum recorded temperature of 236°C at 3705 m depth, and a maximum gradient of 8.8°C/100 m at 3366 m depth [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-3]. The numerical model is based on geological data from boreholes DR3, DR4, GR1, and GR2 [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-3].

## Methods
A 3D thermal-hydraulic coupled numerical model was developed using COMSOL Multiphysics, based on local thermal nonequilibrium (LTNE) theory [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5]. The model simulates heat extraction over a 50-year period with a time step of 0.5 years [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5]. The physical model consists of a 500 × 500 × 500 m reservoir at 3300-3800 m depth, incorporating a horizontal injection well, a vertical production well, a natural fault (inclined at 55°), and an artificial fracture [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5]. Key assumptions include Darcy's Law for flow, constant water properties (liquid phase), and neglect of fracture aperture variation and chemical effects [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5]. The model was validated against analytical solutions and previous studies [Zhang 2019, pp. 5-6]. A comparative analysis was performed between the fault/fracture-controlled model (main model) and a reference doublet model from literature [Zhang 2019, pp. 6-8]. Optimization involved multiparameter analysis of injection rate, injection temperature, production pressure, number of fractures, vertical interval between storage zones, and storage permeability [Zhang 2019, pp. 11-12]. Well pattern layouts were also evaluated [Zhang 2019, pp. 15-16].

## Key Findings
1.  The fault-fracture-controlled doublet model is more suitable for long-term heat extraction than a common doublet model, maintaining higher production temperatures (199.0°C to 190.4°C over 50 years) and a lower heat extraction ratio (0.13), indicating longer reservoir life [Zhang 2019, pp. 8-10, 16-17].
2.  Optimal human-control parameters for the fault-fracture-controlled EGS model are: injection rate of 50 kg/s, production pressure of 15 MPa, and injection temperature of 60°C [Zhang 2019, pp. 12-14, 16-17].
3.  Optimal reservoir structure parameters are: vertical interval between storage zones of 310 m, storage permeability of 4 × 10^-14 m², and two fractures [Zhang 2019, pp. 14-16, 16-17].
4.  The best well pattern layout for the Gonghe Basin is one injection well and two production wells [Zhang 2019, pp. 16-17].
5.  Under optimized conditions, production temperature varies from 196.8°C to 192.7°C at a heat extraction ratio of 0.13 at the end of operation [Zhang 2019, pp. 16-17].

## Limitations
1.  The model neglects the variation of fracture aperture and chemical effects [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5].
2.  The interaction mechanism between faults and fractures is simplified; the natural fault is embedded as a plane without considering the mechanical behavior of hydraulic fracture and natural fault [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-3].
3.  The study is a numerical simulation; real-world EGS tests are extremely difficult and expensive [Zhang 2019, pp. 2-3].
4.  The model assumes constant porosity, specific heat, density, and thermal conductivity for the porous medium [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Water flow complies with Darcy's Law and is in a steady state in fractures and rock matrix [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5].
2.  Water remains liquid and does not vaporize under conditions of 40-80 MPa pressure and 323-513 K temperature [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5].
3.  The continuous porous media properties of the rock matrix are isotropic [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5].
4.  Water properties are described by equations related to temperature [Zhang 2019, pp. 3-5].
5.  The heat transfer coefficient between solid and fluid phase (hsfa) is 50 W/(m³ K) [Zhang 2019, pp. 5-6].
6.  The convection coefficient (h) in fractures and fault is 3000 W/m²/K [Zhang 2019, pp. 5-6].

*   **Vertical Interval (m):** Spacing between the two storage zones.
*   **Storage Permeability (m²):** Permeability of the stimulated storage zones.
*   **Production Temperature (°C):** Temperature of extracted water.
*   **Heat Extraction Ratio (α):** Measure of heat mined from the reservoir.
*   **Flow Impedance (MPa·s/kg):** Resistance to flow in the reservoir.
*   **Thermal Power (MW):** Heat production power.
*   **Electric Power (MW):** Generated electrical power.

## Reusable Claims
1.  For a fault/fracture-controlled EGS model in the Gonghe Basin, the fault-fracture system enhances connectivity and acts as a preferential channel for heat extraction, making it more suitable for long-term operation than a common doublet model [Zhang 2019, pp. 8-10].
2.  In the Gonghe Basin EGS model, increasing the injection rate beyond 50 kg/s leads to earlier thermal breakthrough and a significant drop in production temperature [Zhang 2019, pp. 12-14].
3.  A vertical interval of 310 m between storage zones in the Gonghe Basin EGS model provides better heat transfer along the fault and fractures compared to a 260 m interval [Zhang 2019, pp. 14-16].
4.  Using two fractures in the Gonghe Basin EGS reservoir optimizes the balance between heat transfer area and flow distribution, delaying thermal breakthrough compared to using one or three fractures [Zhang 2019, pp. 14-16].
5.  A well layout with one injection well and two production wells increases the heat transfer region and area, resulting in higher final production temperatures compared to other tested layouts for the Gonghe Basin fault-fracture EGS model [Zhang 2019, pp. 16-17].

## Open Questions
