# Solute Reactive Tracers for Hydrogeological Applications: A Short Review and Future Prospects.

## One-line Summary
This review systematically categorizes reactive solute tracers into partitioning, kinetic, and hybrid types, and advocates for a molecular design approach (tailor‑made tracers) to overcome existing limitations in hydrogeological tracer testing.

## Research Question
How can reactive solute tracers be systematically classified based on underlying processes, and what are the future prospects for designing novel tailor-made reactive tracers to access hydrogeological parameters not attainable with conservative tracers alone? [Cao 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
This is a review paper; it synthesizes laboratory and field‑scale tracer test results from multiple scientific fields. No single study area is used. The evidence base comprises published tracer experiments, including column experiments, field tests in aquifers, geothermal reservoirs, NAPL-contaminated sites, carbon capture and storage projects, and oilfields. [Cao 2020, pp. 1-2]; examples throughout [Cao 2020, pp. 5-20]

## Methods
The study employs a systematic literature review and conceptual synthesis. Reactive tracer compounds are categorized by the physical, chemical, and/or biological process they probe. The paper also outlines a molecular target design strategy: combining known structural elements and molecular features (functional groups, substructures, homologues) to create compounds with desired reactive behaviour (e.g., thermo-sensitive, interface-sensitive kinetics). The class of naphthalenesulfonates is presented as a promising backbone structure. [Cao 2020, pp. 2-3; 9-12]

## Key Findings
1.  **Three-category systematization.** Reactive tracers are classified into (1) equilibrium (partitioning) tracers, (2) kinetic (non-equilibrium) tracers, and (3) reactive tracers for partitioning (a hybrid form). [Cao 2020, pp. 3-5]
2.  **Equilibrium tracers.** Fluid–solid sorbing tracers (sensitive to uncharged or charged surfaces) can estimate organic carbon content (fOC) or surface area/charge; fluid–fluid partitioning tracers (volume‑sensitive or interface‑sensitive) can quantify residual saturation or fluid–fluid interfacial area. [Cao 2020, pp. 5-8]
3.  **Kinetic tracers.** Degradation‑sensitive tracers assess microbial activity or redox conditions; thermo‑sensitive tracers determine reservoir temperature and cooling fractions; kinetic interface‑sensitive (KIS) tracers exploit interfacial hydrolysis to characterize plume development. [Cao 2020, pp. 8-9]
4.  **Reactive tracers for partitioning.** Tracers such as reactive esters (e.g., triacetin, tripropionin) decay and the daughter products partition, enabling quantification of residually trapped CO₂ or oil saturation. [Cao 2020, pp. 9-10]
5.  **Tailor‑made tracer design.** The molecular design approach, exemplified by thermo‑ and interface‑sensitive groups incorporated into naphthalenesulfonate backbones, can produce compounds with predictable kinetics, high water solubility, low sorption, strong fluorescence, and low toxicity, enabling mass‑balance tracer tests without a conservative reference. [Cao 2020, pp. 10-13]

## Limitations
*   Completely ideal tracers do not exist; some prior knowledge of the investigated system is required to verify practicality and avoid test failure. [Cao 2020, pp. 2]
*   Various types of mixing always exist and should be interpreted with caution as they may influence tracer experiment results. [Cao 2020, pp. 3]
*   The selection of reactive tracers is complex due to the large number of requirements, the intricacies of natural systems, and the need to match tracer decay rates to the test time scale. [Cao 2020, pp. 12]
*   Earlier field experiments with thermo‑sensitive ester compounds failed due to pH dependence and vaporization. [Cao 2020, pp. 8-9]
*   The KIS tracer concept has currently only been tested in laboratory experiments with a supercritical CO₂ analogue fluid (n‑octane). [Cao 2020, pp. 9]

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   Reactive tracers are defined as compounds that undergo a chemical reaction or physicochemical interaction process in a predictable way under specific boundary conditions existing in the investigated system. [Cao 2020, pp. 2]
*   For quantitative interpretation, a reactive tracer must be combined with at least one conservative reference tracer in a multitracer experiment to normalize for hydrodynamic transport. [Cao 2020, pp. 3]
*   Linear sorption isotherm is assumed when relating retardation factor to organic carbon content or surface area. [Cao 2020, pp. 5-6]
*   Kinetic tracer decay mechanisms are usually desired to follow (pseudo) first‑order kinetics to limit ambiguity. [Cao 2020, pp. 8]
*   For thermo‑sensitive tracers, the reaction rate constant is assumed to be solely controlled by temperature, and the dependence follows the Arrhenius law. [Cao 2020, pp. 8]
*   For the KIS tracer concept, the hydrolysis reaction at the interface is simplified to (pseudo) zero‑order kinetics due to constant adsorbed reactant concentration. [Cao 2020, pp. 9]

## Reusable Claims
*   *Claim*. A neutral, uncharged organic tracer’s retardation factor can be used to estimate the average organic carbon content (fOC) between the injection and observation points, provided the log KOW of the tracer is known and a linear sorption isotherm is assumed. [Cao 2020, pp. 5-6] *Condition*. Requires prior empirical correlation between log KOC and log KOW for the compound class; deviations can be within one order of magnitude.
*   *Claim*. A charged tracer’s retardation, when sorption is mass‑ or surface‑area‑related, can be used to assess the specific surface area (A/V ratio) of an aquifer if the sorption coefficient (Kc) is known. [Cao 2020, pp. 6-7] *Condition*. Requires consideration of pH and the point of zero charge of the sorbent surface.
*   *Claim*. Volume‑sensitive partitioning tracers whose retardation is solely due to partitioning between two immiscible fluid phases can quantify the average residual saturation (Sr) of the immobile phase. [Cao 2020, pp. 7] *Condition*. Negligible sorption onto solids and a known partition coefficient between the two fluid phases are required.
*   *Claim*. Amphiphilic interface‑sensitive tracers can determine the specific fluid‑fluid interfacial area (aif) through interfacial adsorption, provided the interfacial adsorption coefficient (Kif) has been determined independently. [Cao 2020, pp. 7-8] *Condition*. Kif is calculated from the ratio of interfacial concentration to bulk concentration at equilibrium.
*   *Claim*. A thermo‑sensitive tracer with well‑defined, temperature‑driven (pseudo) first‑order hydrolysis kinetics can be used to infer the equivalent reservoir temperature (Teq) and cooling fraction (χ) of a geothermal reservoir by interpreting the tracer loss along the flow path. [Cao 2020, pp. 8-9] *Condition*. Reaction rate constant must be solely temperature‑dependent and follow the Arrhenius law; other influencing factors (e.g., pH) must be accounted for.
*   *Claim*. Reactive ester tracers that undergo in‑situ hydrolysis can determine the residual saturation of a non‑aqueous phase (e.g., oil, supercritical CO₂) by the differential breakthrough of the hydrophilic parent compound and the partitioning daughter product. [Cao 2020, pp. 9-10] *Condition*. Kinetic parameters of the hydrolysis are not used in the evaluation; daughter compound must have a markedly different partitioning coefficient.
*   *Claim*. The class of naphthalenesulfonates is a suitable backbone for molecularly designed reactive tracers due to its inherent properties: strong acidity (pKa<1), high water solubility, very low log KOW (non‑sorptive), thermal stability up to 250°C under anoxic conditions, strong fluorescence for low‑level detection, and low toxicity. [Cao 2020, pp. 10-12] *Condition*. Tailoring requires incorporation of specific reactive groups (e.g., thermo‑ or interface‑sensitive) while retaining the beneficial backbone properties.

## Connections To Other Work
*   This review builds on extensive existing literature on conservative tracers (e.g., bromide, fluorescein, rhodamine WT, ²H/¹⁸O) and solves the specific gap of a systematic review for reactive tracers. [Cao 2020, pp. 1-3]
*   The framework connects to column experiments by Schaffer et al. (2012, 2015) for sorbing tracer proof-of-concept. [Cao 2020, pp. 5-6; 15]
*   It references field demonstrations of volume-sensitive tracers in NAPL contamination (e.g., Jin et al., 1995; Annable et al., 1998) and CCS (e.g., LaForce et al., 2014; Stalker et al., 2015). [Cao 2020, pp. 7-8; 16-17]

## Open Questions
*   Potential for using uncharged surface-sensitive tracers (e.g., 8:2 fluorotelomer alcohol, short-chained alkyl phenols) is noted but has not yet been explicitly proposed and warrants further investigation. [Cao 2020, pp. 5-6]
*   The potential for reactive tracers is not yet fully exploited in the hyporheic zone, hydraulic fracturing applications, karst aquifers, and acid-mine drainage systems, due to their complex and heterogeneous conditions. [Cao 2020, pp. 9]
*   Further validation of KIS tracers in field-scale tests with actual supercritical CO₂, beyond laboratory analogues, is required. [Cao 2020, pp. 9]
*   The molecular target design of tracers for the aquatic environment has yet to be widely considered, despite its success in life sciences. [Cao 2020, pp. 10]
*   The concept of designing tracers with pre-selected fluorescent properties for both reactants and products could open the possibility of tracer tests without an additional conservative tracer; this needs practical demonstration. [Cao 2020, pp. 12-13]
