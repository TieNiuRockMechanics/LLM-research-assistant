# An Investigation on the Deterioration of Physical and Mechanical Properties of Granite after Cyclic Thermal Shock.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how the physical and mechanical properties of granite deteriorate after 0 to 20 cycles of thermal shock (heating to 450°C followed by water cooling) and analyzes the underlying mechanisms using experimental tests and numerical simulation.

## Research Question
How do the physical and mechanical properties of granite respond to cyclic thermal shock, and what are the internal deterioration mechanisms, particularly the formation and role of thermal cracks?

## Study Area / Data
The research used granite samples sourced from Jining City, Shandong Province, China. The samples were processed into standard cylindrical specimens (diameter 50 mm, height 100 mm) according to ISRM recommendations. The granite's mineral composition was identified as quartz, albite, plagioclase feldspar, and biotite via X-ray diffraction [Ning 2021, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
The experimental methodology involved several stages:
1.  **Cyclic Thermal Shock Test:** Samples were heated in a furnace to 450°C at a rate of 5°C/min, held for 2 hours, then immediately cooled in cold water until reaching room temperature. This cycle was repeated 0, 1, 5, 10, and 20 times [Ning 2021, pp. 3-4].
2.  **Physical Property Measurements:** Before heating and after each thermal shock cycle, measurements were taken for P-wave velocity (using an HC-U81 ultrasonic monitor), thermal conductivity (using a HotDisk TPS 500 analyzer), and porosity (via vacuum saturation and weighing) [Ning 2021, pp. 3-4].
3.  **Mechanical Testing:** Uniaxial compression tests were conducted using an MTS816 system at a loading rate of 0.002 mm/s, coupled with acoustic emission (AE) monitoring using a PCI-2 system to record counts and cumulative counts during loading [Ning 2021, pp. 3-4].
4.  **Numerical Simulation:** ANSYS software was used to simulate the temperature field and thermal stress distribution within the granite during a single thermal shock event, using parameters from the experiments and literature [Ning 2021, pp. 7-8].

## Key Findings
1.  **Deterioration Pattern:** With increasing thermal shock cycles, granite's porosity increased nonlinearly, while P-wave velocity, thermal conductivity, peak strength, elastic modulus, and deformation modulus decreased nonlinearly. Peak strain initially increased but decreased after 10 cycles [Ning 2021, pp. 4-5, 9-10].
2.  **Sensitivity to First Shock:** The first thermal shock caused the most severe deterioration in deformation modulus (59.7% of total decrease) and peak strain (25.2% increase). Other parameters like P-wave velocity and thermal conductivity were most sensitive to the first 10 cycles [Ning 2021, pp. 4-5, 6-7].
3.  **Mechanisms of Deterioration:** The decrease in thermal conductivity is attributed to increased porosity, higher air content in pores, and increased contact thermal resistance between mineral particles. The decrease in strength and increase in ductility are caused by increased thermal crack density/length, enhanced local stress concentration, and greater differences in local bearing capacity [Ning 2021, pp. 4-5, 9-10].
4.  **Acoustic Emission (AE) Characteristics:** Granite after cyclic thermal shock showed higher AE counts during the initial compaction stage but lower maximum counts at the beginning and during the entire crack propagation stage compared to natural granite. This is because thermal cracks provide additional pathways for energy release [Ning 2021, pp. 6-7].
5.  **Thermal Stress Mechanism:** Simulation showed that during cooling, the granite exterior experiences vertical tensile thermal stress (up to 9.18 MPa), while the interior experiences vertical compressive thermal stress (up to 7.59 MPa). This stress distribution leads to the propagation of pre-existing micro-cracks in the interior and the initiation of new thermal cracks on the surface [Ning 2021, pp. 8-9, 9-10].

## Limitations
1.  The study used a single heating temperature (450°C) and a single cooling method (water quenching). The effects of different temperatures or cooling rates (e.g., air cooling) were not investigated [Ning 2021, pp. 3-4].
2.  The maximum number of thermal shock cycles was 20. The long-term behavior under a higher number of cycles is not confirmed [Ning 2021, pp. 3-4].
3.  The numerical simulation modeled a single thermal shock event and used simplified boundary conditions and material parameters from literature [Ning 2021, pp. 7-8].
4.  The granite samples were from a single location (Jining, China), and results may vary for granite with different mineral compositions or textures [Ning 2021, pp. 2-3].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  A heating rate of 5°C/min was assumed to be a critical rate that does not cause damage to the granite during heating, based on prior literature [Ning 2021, pp. 3-4].
2.  The mineralogical characteristics of the granite (quartz, albite, plagioclase feldspar, biotite) did not change during the 450°C heating, as confirmed by XRD analysis [Ning 2021, pp. 4-5].
3.  The convective heat transfer coefficient in the numerical simulation was calibrated based on the time for the sample surface to cool to room temperature in the lab test [Ning 2021, pp. 7-8].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Independent Variable:** Number of thermal shock cycles (0, 1, 5, 10, 20).
*   **Dependent Variables:** P-wave velocity, thermal conductivity, porosity, peak strength, peak strain, elastic modulus, deformation modulus, acoustic emission counts.
*   **Controlled Parameters:** Heating temperature (450°C), heating rate (5°C/min), heating duration (2 hours), cooling medium (water), sample geometry (cylinder, 50mm diameter, 100mm height).

## Reusable Claims
*   **Claim:** The first thermal shock cycle causes the most significant increase in irreversible plastic deformation in granite, as evidenced by a 59.7% drop in deformation modulus after the first cycle out of a total 72.5% drop after 20 cycles. **Condition:** For granite heated to 450°C and water-cooled. **Limitation:** Specific to the tested granite and thermal shock parameters.
*   **Claim:** Cyclic thermal shock reduces the thermal conductivity of granite primarily by increasing porosity and contact thermal resistance between mineral particles, not by altering mineral composition at 450°C. **Condition:** For granite with the tested mineralogy and thermal shock history. **Limitation:** Does not apply if temperatures cause mineral phase changes.
*   **Claim:** Granite subjected to cyclic thermal shock exhibits lower acoustic emission counts during the crack propagation stage under uniaxial compression because pre-existing thermal cracks provide additional pathways for energy release. **Condition:** For granite with a history of thermal shock. **Limitation:** Specific to the AE monitoring setup and loading conditions used.

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds upon and references prior research on rock thermal damage, including work on the effects of maximum heating temperature [Ning 2021, pp. 2-2], single rapid cooling (thermal shock) [Ning 2021, pp. 2-2], and cyclic heat treatment [Ning 2021, pp. 2-3]. It notes that while the basic laws of damage from temperature and single rapid cooling are relatively complete, research on damage from cyclic thermal shock is insufficient, with existing studies lacking deep analysis of mechanisms and intuitive visualization of internal temperature/stress fields [Ning 2021, pp. 2-3].

## Open Questions
1.  How do the deterioration patterns and mechanisms change with different maximum heating temperatures (e.g., above 573°C for quartz inversion) or different cooling media (e.g., liquid nitrogen)?
2.  What is the long-term evolution of granite properties under a much larger number of thermal shock cycles (>20)?
3.  How do the findings translate to in-situ conditions in a geothermal reservoir with confining pressure and fluid saturation?
4.  Can the numerical simulation model be improved to account for the progressive development of thermal cracks over multiple cycles?
