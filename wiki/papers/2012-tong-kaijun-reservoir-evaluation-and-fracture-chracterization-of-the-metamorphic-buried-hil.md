# Reservoir Evaluation and Fracture Chracterization of the Metamorphic Buried Hill Reservoir in Bohai Bay Basin.

## One-line Summary
Using core, thin‑section, and imaging log data, the Archean metamorphic buried‑hill reservoir in the JZ25‑1S field (Bohai Bay Basin) is vertically zoned into weathered crust, semi‑weathered crust and base rock, with the pore‑fracture type in the semi‑weathered crust identified as the main pay zone; fracture effectiveness is assessed via crude‑oil inclusions, present‑day maximum horizontal stress direction, and Stoneley‑wave attenuation, leading to a qualitative‑semiquantitative three‑level reservoir classification.

## Research Question
How can fracture characteristics and reservoir quality of a highly heterogeneous Archean metamorphic buried‑hill reservoir be evaluated and classified using core, imaging‑log, geochemical and geomechanical data, and which fracture types constitute effective reservoir space?

## Study Area / Data
- **Location**: JZ25‑1S Oilfield, middle part of the Liaoxi low bulge, Liaodong Bay, Bohai Sea, east high belt of the Liaoxi‑2 fault system (Fig. 1a–c) [Tong 2012, pp. 1-1; 1‑3].
- **Reservoir**: Archean migmatization granitic gneiss (plagiogneiss and monzogneiss); content of brittle minerals (quartz + plagioclase) 40 %–70 %, dark minerals < 30 % [Tong 2012, pp. 1-3].
- **Overburden**: Paleogene mudstone of Sha‑3 Member [Tong 2012, pp. 1-1].
- **Data sources**: core observation, thin‑slice identification, imaging‑logging (FMI) from seven wells, full‑wavetrain acoustic logs, crude‑oil inclusion fluorescence on 14 samples from three wells, DST production tests, regional geostress orientation [Tong 2012, pp. 1-1; 1‑3; 3‑4; 4‑6; 6‑7].
- **Well examples**: JZ25‑1S‑2 (high deliverability), JZ25‑1S‑1 (low deliverability) used for fracture‑effectiveness calibration [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].

## Methods
- **Reservoir zonation** based on weathering degree and fracture development using core and conventional log responses (acoustic time, compensated neutron, density, resistivity) [Tong 2012, pp. 1-3].
- **Reservoir‑space typing** and porosity‑permeability trend analysis from core plugs; combined with FMI borehole images to classify three reservoir types [Tong 2012, pp. 3-4].
- **Fracture classification** by dip, filling degree and formation timing (early vs. late) from core and FMI interpretation; statistics of fracture density by zone [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
- **Fracture effectiveness evaluation**:
  - Crude‑oil inclusion fluorescence: number of hydrocarbon inclusion particles per vision and fluorescence colour in fractures of different orientations and filling states [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
  - Present‑day maximum horizontal stress direction: comparison of fracture strike with stress direction (< 30° angle deemed effective) and correlation with DST results [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
  - Acoustic wavetrain (Stoneley‑wave) analysis: Stoneley reflection coefficient, variable‑density imaging (“v” or “x” corrugations), and energy attenuation (centre‑frequency shift, dispersion) to detect open, permeable fractures [Tong 2012, pp. 6-7].
- **Quantitative fracture‑parameter calculation**: finite‑element method on resistivity image calibrated to flushed zone to compute fracture length, density, hydrodynamic width and apparent porosity [Tong 2012, pp. 6-7].
- **Reservoir classification**: qualitative‑semiquantitative criteria (Table 1) integrating porosity, fracture development, fracture effectiveness, fracture angle and strike relative to maximum stress, and actual oil‑production rates [Tong 2012, pp. 7-8].

## Key Findings
1. **Vertical zonation**: The buried‑hill reservoir is divided into three zones: weathered crust (5–32 m), semi‑weathered crust (30–120 m) and base rock (30–270 m). The semi‑weathered crust hosts the most developed reservoir, followed by weathered crust; base rock is least developed [Tong 2012, pp. 1-3; 7‑8].
2. **Reservoir space types**: Fractures (diagenetic, weathered‑dissolved, tectonic), dissolved pores (mainly in dark minerals and feldspar associated with fractures), and cataclastic intergranular pores. Macroscopic tectonic fractures control hydrocarbon migration and constitute the main reservoir space in a dual‑porosity system [Tong 2012, pp. 3-4].
3. **Reservoir types and main pay**: Three types defined — fracture‑pore, pore‑fracture, micro‑fracture‑compact — based on porosity‑permeability relationships and FMI images. The pore‑fracture type (dominant in the semi‑weathered crust) is the primary oil‑producing layer, characterized by well‑developed, largely unfilled tectonic and dissolved fractures, with high‑angle fractures abundant [Tong 2012, pp. 3-4].
4. **Fracture population**: Low–high angle inclined fractures (dip 15°–75°) account for ~80 % of all fractures. Fracture density is highest in the semi‑weathered crust, intermediate in the weathered crust, and lowest in the base rock [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
5. **Fracture effectiveness**:
   - **Crude‑oil inclusions**: Inclined open fractures show the highest hydrocarbon inclusion index (4.6–7.1 particles/vision, white fluorescence), followed by vertical semi‑filled fractures (1.18–4.80, yellow/blue‑white), and horizontal filled fractures are least effective (1.88–2.70, blue‑white) [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
   - **Geostress alignment**: Fractures whose strike is within 30° of the present‑day maximum principal stress direction (NE–SW) are effective. Wells with good alignment (e.g., JZ25‑1S‑2) produce at high rates (> 350 m³/d oil); wells with misalignment >60° (e.g., JZ25‑1S‑1) show much lower productivity [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
   - **Stoneley‑wave response**: Open, permeable fractures cause Stoneley‑wave reflection (high reflection coefficient), amplitude attenuation, dispersion, and “v”‑shaped patterns on variable‑density logs, all indicating effective fractures [Tong 2012, pp. 6-7].
6. **Reservoir classification (Level I–III)**: Qualitative‑semiquantitative criteria (Table 1) combining porosity, fracture development, fracture effectiveness, fracture angle, strike relative to stress, fracture porosity, fracture width, and oil production rate. Level I (pore‑fracture, porosity >8 %, fracture porosity >0.02 %, width >30 µm, production >200 m³/d) is the best; Level II (fracture‑pore, porosity 5–8 %, production 20–200 m³/d) is intermediate; Level III (micro‑fracture‑compact, porosity 2–5 %, production <20 m³/d) is poor. Fracture‑strike quality is ranked excellent (<30°), medium (30°–60°), poor (>60°) [Tong 2012, pp. 7-8].
7. **Practical application**: The classification scheme, verified by production data, reveals strong vertical and lateral heterogeneity in the buried‑hill fracture reservoir [Tong 2012, pp. 7-8].

## Limitations
- Imaging logging alone cannot evaluate fracture effectiveness because it does not reliably determine radial extension of fractures [Tong 2012, pp. 6-7].
- The study is confined to the JZ25‑1S field; applicability to other buried‑hill settings requires validation.
- Crude‑oil inclusion analysis was performed on a limited set of 14 samples from three wells; the representativeness for the entire reservoir may be constrained.
- Quantitative fracture parameters (length, density, width, apparent porosity) rely on finite‑element inversion of resistivity images, whose accuracy depends on the flushed‑zone calibration and model assumptions.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The ideal weathered crust profile (clay layer, semi‑weathered rock, unweathered base rock) applies, though boundaries are gradational and not sharply defined [Tong 2012, pp. 1-3].
- Fracture effectiveness is assumed directly correlated with the angle between fracture strike and present‑day maximum horizontal stress direction, with a threshold of <30° considered effective [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
- The Stoneley‑wave reflection coefficient and energy attenuation are interpreted as indicators of open fractures with flowing fluids; the “v” or “x” pattern on variable‑density images is diagnostic of open fractures intersecting the borehole [Tong 2012, pp. 6-7].
- Porosity and permeability relationships (three trend lines) from core plugs are used to infer reservoir type; however, upscaling from plug to formation scale carries inherent uncertainty.
- The qualitative‑semiquantitative classification criteria (Table 1) were developed specifically for the metamorphic buried hill of the study area, and the thresholds are tuned to local DST production values.

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Zonal thicknesses**: weathered crust (~5–32 m), semi‑weathered crust (~30–120 m), base rock (~30–270 m) [Tong 2012, pp. 1-3].
- **Porosity**: Level I >8 %, Level II 5–8 %, Level III 2–5 % [Tong 2012, pp. 7-8].
- **Fracture porosity**: Level I >0.02 %, Level II 0.01–0.02 %, Level III <0.01 % [Tong 2012, pp. 7-8].
- **Fracture width (hydrodynamic)**: Level I >30 µm, Level II 15–30 µm, Level III <15 µm [Tong 2012, pp. 7-8].
- **Oil production (DST)**: Level I >200 m³/d, Level II 20–200 m³/d, Level III <20 m³/d [Tong 2012, pp. 7-8].
- **Fracture dip population**: low–high angle inclined fractures (15°–75°) ~80 % of total [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
- **Geostress direction**: present‑day maximum principal stress NE–SW [Tong 2012, pp. 7-8].
- **Inclusion index**: inclined open fractures 4.6–7.1 particles/vision; vertical semi‑filled 1.18–4.80; horizontal filled 1.88–2.70 [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
- **Stoneley reflectivity**: high reflection coefficient signifying large fracture aperture; energy attenuation and “v”‑shaped reflection indicate permeable fractures [Tong 2012, pp. 6-7].

## Reusable Claims
1. In the JZ25‑1S Archean metamorphic buried hill, the semi‑weathered crust is the most productive interval, and the pore‑fracture reservoir type (high‑angle unfilled tectonic and dissolved fractures) forms the main pay zone [Tong 2012, pp. 1-1; 3‑4; 7‑8].
2. Low–high angle inclined fractures constitute about 80 % of the total fractures and are the dominant fracture population [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
3. Fracture effectiveness can be ranked by hydrocarbon inclusion index: inclined open > vertical semi‑filled > horizontal filled [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
4. A fracture is effective only if its strike lies within 30° of the present‑day maximum horizontal principal stress direction; this alignment correlates with high well deliverability [Tong 2012, pp. 4-6].
5. Stoneley‑wave parameters (reflection coefficient, energy attenuation, “v”‑shaped pattern) reliably identify open, permeable fractures and can complement FMI for radial extension evaluation [Tong 2012, pp. 6-7].
6. The qualitative‑semiquantitative classification criteria (porosity >8 %, fracture porosity >0.02 %, width >30 µm, production >200 m³/d for Level I) provide a reproducible framework for grading metamorphic buried‑hill reservoirs in the Bohai Bay Basin [Tong 2012, pp. 7-8].
7. The metamorphic buried‑hill reservoir exhibits strong vertical and lateral heterogeneity, making integrated logging‑core‑geomechanical evaluation essential for accurate reservoir characterization [Tong 2012, pp. 1-1; 7‑8].

## Open Questions
- How does the radial extension length of fractures (which imaging logging cannot determine) quantitatively affect productivity, and can the Stoneley‑wave method be calibrated to provide reliable extension estimates?
- What is the representativeness of the small inclusion dataset (14 samples) for the entire reservoir, and can inclusion‑based effectiveness be generalized to other buried‑hill plays?
- Are the classification thresholds transferable to other metamorphic buried‑hill reservoirs in the Bohai Bay Basin or globally, or are they field‑specific?
- How do multi‑phase diagenetic alterations (dissolution vs. filling) evolve over time and influence the preservation of effective fractures beyond the current static view?
- What is the link between the NE–SW present‑day stress and the palaeo‑stress fields that generated the fractures, and does fracture reactivation play a role in maintaining permeability?
