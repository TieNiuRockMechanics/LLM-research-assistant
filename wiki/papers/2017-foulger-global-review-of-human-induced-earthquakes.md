# Global Review of Human-Induced Earthquakes.

## One-line Summary
A comprehensive review and database (HiQuake) of over 700 cases of earthquakes postulated to be induced by human activities, analyzing their causes, magnitudes, and implications for hazard management.

## Research Question
What are the global characteristics, causes, and controlling factors of human-induced earthquakes, and how can this knowledge inform hazard assessment and management?

## Study Area / Data
The study utilizes the Human-induced Earthquake Database (HiQuake), a global catalog containing over 700 cases of earthquake sequences postulated to be induced by anthropogenic activity, spanning the period 1868–2016 [Foulger 2017, pp. 5-7]. Data were compiled from published papers, conference abstracts, books, reports, the world-wide web, and personal knowledge [Foulger 2017, pp. 7-9]. Cases cover all continents except Antarctica and include diverse industrial projects such as water reservoir impoundment, mining, hydrocarbon extraction, fluid injection (for waste disposal, enhanced oil recovery, hydrofracturing, geothermal energy, and carbon capture and storage), and nuclear explosions [Foulger 2017, pp. 6-7, 68-72].

## Methods
The authors constructed the HiQuake database through an extensive literature search [Foulger 2017, pp. 7-9]. The database includes all earthquake sequences proposed on scientific grounds to have been human-induced, regardless of credibility, as there is no rigorous way to define a credibility cut-off [Foulger 2017, pp. 7-9]. The methodology involved searching for case histories, downloading PDFs, and entering data into a standardized spreadsheet with columns for project details, seismicity parameters, and operational data [Foulger 2017, pp. 247-248]. Statistical analysis was performed to examine correlations between operational parameters (e.g., injection volume, project scale) and seismicity characteristics (e.g., maximum magnitude, MMAX) [Foulger 2017, pp. 69-72].

## Key Findings
1.  **Prevalence and Scale**: Human-induced earthquakes are reported from every continent except Antarctica, with 79% of cases occurring in intraplate regions [Foulger 2017, pp. 68-72, 72]. The largest reported induced earthquakes are the 2008 MW ~8 Wenchuan, China, earthquake (linked to reservoir impoundment), the 2016 MW 5.8 Pawnee, Oklahoma, earthquake (linked to wastewater disposal), and the 1976 MS 7.3 Gazli, Uzbekistan, earthquake (linked to gas extraction) [Foulger 2017, pp. 6, 17-19, 30-31, 41-43].
2.  **Controlling Factors**: The observed maximum magnitude (MMAX) correlates positively with project scale, fluid injection volume, and the yield of nuclear devices [Foulger 2017, pp. 69-72]. It correlates negatively with calculated inducing stress change, likely because the latter correlates inversely with project scale [Foulger 2017, pp. 6, 78-79].
3.  **Under-reporting**: Reporting is severely incomplete. Downward extrapolation suggests ~30% of M ~4 induced earthquakes, ~60% of M ~3 events, and ~90% of M ~2 events have gone unrecognized or unreported [Foulger 2017, pp. 73-74].
4.  **Stress and Triggering**: The minimum amount of anthropogenic stress needed to induce an earthquake is an unsound concept, as earthquakes occur naturally. Stress changes as small as a few hundredths of a megapascal (equivalent to a few tens of centimeters of water-table depth) have been observed to modulate earthquake activity [Foulger 2017, pp. 6, 76-78].
5.  **Diverse Causes**: The most common cause is mining (38% of cases), followed by water reservoir impoundment (24%) [Foulger 2017, pp. 21-22, 68]. Fluid injection for various purposes (wastewater disposal, enhanced oil recovery, hydrofracturing, geothermal energy, CCS) is a growing concern, with dramatic increases in seismicity rates observed in regions like Oklahoma, USA [Foulger 2017, pp. 37-38, 41-45].

## Limitations
1.  **Inclusion of All Cases**: The database includes all cases proposed on scientific grounds, regardless of credibility, because there is no rigorous way to define a credibility cut-off [Foulger 2017, pp. 7-9]. This means some included cases may not be truly induced.
2.  **Under-reporting**: The dataset is biased by severe under-reporting, especially for smaller magnitude events and projects in remote areas [Foulger 2017, pp. 73-74].
3.  **Data Quality**: The quality of data (locations, magnitudes, operational parameters) is non-uniform due to varying monitoring standards over time and between projects [Foulger 2017, pp. 12-15].
4.  **Attribution Difficulty**: Multiple industrial processes often occur simultaneously (e.g., extraction and injection), making it difficult to unambiguously attribute seismicity to a single cause [Foulger 2017, pp. 7-9, 33-34].
5.  **Magnitude Inconsistency**: Magnitudes are reported on different scales (ML, mb, Ms, MW), and rendering all to a single scale (MW) was beyond the scope of this work [Foulger 2017, pp. 13-15].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The term "induced" is used neutrally to mean "earthquakes related to human activities," without implying the origin of the total seismic stress change [Foulger 2017, pp. 9-11].
2.  Faults near to failure are pervasive in the continental crust, so induced earthquakes may occur essentially anywhere, including historically aseismic regions [Foulger 2017, pp. 6, 9].
3.  The amount of stress released in an induced earthquake is not necessarily the same as the anthropogenic stress added, as pre-existing tectonic stress may also be released [Foulger 2017, pp. 6, 9-11].
4.  The credibility of individual cases in HiQuake is the responsibility of the user [Foulger 2017, pp. 7-9].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Operational Parameters**: Injection/extraction volume, injection pressure, injection rate, project scale (e.g., reservoir volume, dam height, mine depth), duration of operations.
-   **Seismicity Parameters**: Maximum magnitude (MMAX), earthquake counts, focal mechanisms, hypocentral depth and location.
-   **Geomechanical Parameters**: Calculated stress change, pore pressure change, reservoir pressure change.
-   **Geological Parameters**: Pre-existing fault orientation and stress state, lithology, tectonic setting.

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition**: For fluid injection projects. **Claim**: The observed upper bound to the maximum magnitude of induced earthquakes (MMAX) correlates positively with the total volume of injected fluid [Foulger 2017, pp. 69-72, 95].
2.  **Condition**: In intraplate regions with no history of seismicity. **Claim**: Human-induced earthquakes can occur in historically aseismic areas because the crust is pervasively faulted and near failure [Foulger 2017, pp. 6, 9, 80-82].
3.  **Condition**: For wastewater disposal in Oklahoma, USA. **Claim**: The dramatic increase in seismicity rate since 2009 is confidently associated with massive wastewater injection, with the largest event being the 2016 MW 5.8 Pawnee earthquake [Foulger 2017, pp. 41-45].
4.  **Condition**: For water reservoir impoundment. **Claim**: The impoundment of water behind dams is a common cause of induced earthquakes, accounting for 24% of cases in the HiQuake database, with the largest postulated event being the 2008 MW ~8 Wenchuan earthquake [Foulger 2017, pp. 16-22, 68].
5.  **Condition**: For assessing hazard from induced seismicity. **Claim**: The minimum amount of anthropogenic stress needed to induce an earthquake is an unsound concept; stress changes as small as a few hundredths of a MPa have been observed to modulate seismicity [Foulger 2017, pp. 6, 76-78].

## Connections To Other Work
This review expands upon previous compilations, including lists by Nicol et al. [2011] and Suckale [2009], and a review of M > 1 events by Davies et al. [2013] which listed 198 cases [Foulger 2017, pp. 7-9]. It connects to research on specific industrial processes such as the geomechanics of hydrocarbon reservoirs (e.g., Segall, 1989), the seismicity of The Geysers geothermal field (e.g., Majer & Peterson, 2007), and the hazard from wastewater injection in the central USA (e.g., Ellsworth, 2013; Keranen et al., 2013). The work also relates to studies on natural stress modulation by processes like Earth tides and remote triggering [Foulger 2017, pp. 76-78].

## Open Questions
1.  Why are earthquakes induced by some industrial projects and not others, even when operations appear similar? [Foulger 2017, pp. 59, 82-84]
2.  Can we reliably predict *a priori* which projects will be seismogenic and what the maximum magnitude might be? [Foulger 2017, pp. 11-12, 60]
3.  What are the long-term seismic hazard implications after the cessation of industrial operations (e.g., mine abandonment, end of injection)? [Foulger 2017, pp. 41-43, 56-57]
4.  How can monitoring and management strategies be optimized to mitigate the nuisance of induced earthquakes while allowing beneficial projects to proceed? [Foulger 2017, pp. 61, 84-86]
5.  What is the role of hydraulic processes (fluid migration, pore pressure diffusion) in explaining why both mass addition and removal can induce earthquakes? [Foulger 2017, pp. 74-76]
