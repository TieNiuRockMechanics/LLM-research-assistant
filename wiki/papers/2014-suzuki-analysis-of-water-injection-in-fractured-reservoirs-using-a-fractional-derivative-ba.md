# Analysis of Water Injection in Fractured Reservoirs Using a Fractional-Derivative-Based Mass and Heat Transfer Model.

## One-line Summary
This study proposes a fractional heat transfer equation (fHTE) derived from the fractional advection-dispersion equation (fADE) to model cold-water injection and thermal breakthrough in fractured geothermal reservoirs, demonstrating its applicability through numerical simulations with varying surrounding rock permeability.

## Research Question
How can fractional-derivative-based models (fADE and fHTE) be used to evaluate the effects of cold-water injection into a geothermal reservoir, particularly regarding mass and heat transfer in a fault-related subsidiary structure with fractal geometry, and how do different permeability distributions in the surrounding rocks influence tracer and thermal responses? [Suzuki 2014, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study uses synthetic field performance data generated by the TOUGH2 reservoir simulator. The numerical model consists of a highly permeable fault zone (reservoir) surrounded by rocks with varying permeability distributions. The calculation domain is 80 m long and 20 m thick, with the reservoir thickness set to 2 m. Key parameters include a reservoir permeability of 1.0 × 10⁻¹³ m², surrounding rock permeability ranging from 0 to 1 × 10⁻¹⁴ m² (constant or spatially varying according to a power law), porosity of 0.1, initial temperature of 175°C, and injection temperature of 35°C. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 6-8]

## Methods
1.  **Mathematical Model Derivation:** A fractional heat transfer equation (fHTE) is derived based on the fractional advection-dispersion equation (fADE), which accounts for non-Fickian dispersion and diffusion into surrounding rocks with permeability that decays according to a power law (fractal geometry). [Suzuki 2014, pp. 2-4]
2.  **Numerical Simulation:** Synthetic tracer and thermal response data are generated using the general-purpose reservoir simulator TOUGH2 for different permeability distributions (constant and spatially varying) in the surrounding rocks. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 6-8]
3.  **Model Fitting:** The fADE and fHTE are fitted to the TOUGH2-generated data using a finite difference approach. Parameters (retardation factors b₁, e₁ and fractional orders β, β') are optimized by minimizing the root-mean-square error (RMSE). [Suzuki 2014, pp. 8-10]

## Key Findings
1.  The fHTE demonstrates good agreement with temperature profiles generated by TOUGH2 for both constant and spatially varying permeability distributions in surrounding rocks. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 15-18]
2.  For spatially varying permeability, the fADE provides a reasonable fit to tracer responses, capturing the gradual decrease in concentration. For constant permeability, the fADE captures the long tail but not the double-peak behavior. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 13-15]
3.  The retardation parameters (b₁ for fADE, e₁ for fHTE) increase with the permeability of the surrounding rocks. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 13-15]
4.  The orders of the temporal fractional derivatives (β in fADE, β' in fHTE) vary with the permeability patterns of the surrounding rocks. For spatially varying permeability, β and β' decrease as permeability increases. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 13-15]
5.  A spatially varying permeability distribution leads to a more gradual temperature decline compared to a constant permeability distribution. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 10-13]
6.  The equilibrium temperature (Tₑ) at the production well is highly negatively correlated with the tracer recovery rate (f). [Suzuki 2014, pp. 13-15]

## Limitations
1.  The fADE does not accurately describe the double-peak behavior observed in tracer responses for constant permeability of surrounding rocks. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 13-15]
2.  The study assumes the interior of the reservoir is uniform and focuses only on the effect of diffusion into surrounding rocks, neglecting processes like diffusion into secondary branched fractures and dispersion within the fault zone. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 4-6]
3.  The TOUGH2 simulations neglect thermal conduction for simplicity. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 6-8]
4.  The relationship between the parameters of the fADE and fHTE (e.g., β and β') and the physical structure of the surrounding rocks requires further investigation. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 13-15]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The fractured reservoir consists of a highly permeable fault zone surrounded by impermeable rocks, with the rock matrix inside the reservoir being porous media. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 2-4]
2.  The permeability of the surrounding rocks either remains constant or varies with distance from the fault zone according to a power law, based on the decay of fracture density in a damage zone. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 4-6]
3.  Local thermodynamic equilibrium between rock and fluid is assumed. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 6-8]
4.  The rock is incompressible, and both rock and fluid have constant thermal properties. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 6-8]
5.  Thermal conduction in the reservoir is neglected. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 4-6]

## Connections To Other Work
1.  The fADE is based on work by Fomin et al. (2005, 2011) on non-Fickian diffusion into surrounding rocks with fractal geometry. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 1-2]
2.  The study extends the concept of the fADE to heat transfer, analogous to mass transfer. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 2-4]
3.  It compares the fHTE with the heat loss model of Gringarten and Sauty (1975) and the tracer-based thermal breakthrough prediction method of Shook (2001), finding the fHTE superior for gradual temperature decline. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 15-18]
4.  The permeability model for surrounding rocks is based on the power-law decay of fracture density observed by Savage and Brodsky (2011). [Suzuki 2014, pp. 2-4]
5.  The research builds upon previous work by Suzuki et al. (2012) on using the fADE to predict tracer responses irrespective of well intervals. [Suzuki 2014, pp. 2-4]

## Open Questions

2.  Can the fHTE be used to predict thermal breakthrough irrespective of well intervals and to optimize well location and injection conditions? [Suzuki 2014, pp. 15-18]
3.  How can the models be extended to account for more complex reservoir heterogeneities and processes like heat conduction? [Suzuki 2014, pp. 15-18]
