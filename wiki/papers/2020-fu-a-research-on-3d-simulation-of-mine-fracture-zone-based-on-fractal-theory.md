# A Research on 3D Simulation of Mine Fracture Zone Based on Fractal Theory.

## One-line Summary
This paper presents a method for 3D simulation of overburden fracture zones in mines by using fractal theory to predict key fracture parameters from 2D experimental images and then generating a 3D model using a wire-frame, fracture-based approach.

## Research Question
How to effectively simulate the 3D distribution of fractures in a mine's overburden fault zone, given the difficulty of direct 3D simulation and the random, self-similar nature of the fractures [Robotics 2020, pp. 1-3].

## Study Area / Data
The study uses 2D geological graphics and pictures obtained from similarity simulation material experiments of overburden failure in a mining area [Robotics 2020, pp. 8-9]. The specific mining area is not named. The experimental platform used a Windows 7_x64 system with a Core i5-3230M CPU and NVIDIA GeForce GT 740M GPU [Robotics 2020, pp. 8-9].

## Methods
The research proposes a fractal-based framework for 3D simulation [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
1.  **Image Processing & Fractal Dimension Calculation:** 2D images from simulation experiments are processed (gray level, binarization). The fractal dimension (D) is calculated using the box algorithm [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
2.  **Fracture Parameter Prediction:** Using the calculated fractal dimension and self-similarity, key parameters are predicted:
    *   **Fracture Body Density (vfD):** Calculated using formula `vfD = A*D - B`, where A and B are coefficients reflecting geological characteristics [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
    *   **Fracture Linear Density (lfD):** Calculated from vfD, stress direction lengths (L1, L3), and the angle (θ) between crack strike and maximum tensile stress [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
    *   **Fracture Opening (a):** Calculated from lfD, maximum tension strain (ε), and current tension strain (εt) [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
    *   **Maximum Fracture Length (Rad):** Calculated from the predicted opening (a) and a proportional constant (p) using `Rad = a/p` [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8].
    *   **Fault Zone Height (Hf):** Calculated using an empirical formula involving mining height (H), roof bedding spacing (c), roof joint clearance (d), and mining width (q) [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8].
3.  **3D Simulation Generation:**
    *   A **fracture-based model** using a triangular prism as the basic unit is defined with a specific data structure [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8].
    *   A **single fracture body model** is generated by extending and connecting these base models, controlled by the predicted parameters and normal distribution [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8].
    *   **Multi-fracture simulation** is achieved by abstracting the fracture zone as a trapezoidal range and using an Iterated Function System (IFS) algorithm to generate a Sierpinski pyramid, mapping local fracture shapes to the whole zone via similar transformations [Robotics 2020, pp. 8-9].

## Key Findings
*   The fractal dimension (D) calculated for the experimental image was 1.2120 [Robotics 2020, pp. 9-12].
*   The prediction algorithm for maximum crack length and opening showed errors. When the proportional constant p=1/50, the error fluctuated between 40% and 50%; when p=1/100, the error fluctuated between 20% and 30% [Robotics 2020, pp. 9-12].
*   The selection of the proportional constant p is correlated with the geological environment, and its optimal value for the presented experiment was 1/50 [Robotics 2020, pp. 9-12].
*   The developed system can generate 3D simulation maps of the fracture zone based on the predicted parameters and geological conditions [Robotics 2020, pp. 9-12].

## Limitations
*   There are errors in the data predicted by the operating system due to the complexity of pre- and post-mining conditions in the fault zone [Robotics 2020, pp. 9-12].
*   The selection of the proportional constant p and its relationship with geological parameters requires further study [Robotics 2020, pp. 9-12].
*   The method relies on empirical formulas (e.g., for fault zone height) and coefficients (A, B) that are specific to certain geological conditions [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8, 9-12].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   Fractures in the fault zone exhibit random self-similarity and fractal characteristics in their distribution and geometry [Robotics 2020, pp. 1-3].
*   The mathematical relationships between fractal dimension and fracture parameters (density, opening, length) hold [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
*   The shape of the general fracture area can be abstracted as a trapezoidal range [Robotics 2020, pp. 8-9].
*   The product of crack radius (Rad) and a proportional constant (p) is positively correlated with crack opening (a) [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Fractal Dimension (D):** Quantifies the self-similarity and complexity of the fracture distribution [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
*   **Fracture Body Density (vfD):** Ratio of half the total fracture area to the volume of the fracture zone [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
*   **Fracture Linear Density (lfD):** Number of cracks per unit distance perpendicular to the fracture strike [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
*   **Fracture Opening (a):** Maximum transverse distance between fracture surfaces [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5].
*   **Maximum Fracture Length (Rad):** Maximum vertical and longitudinal distance of the fracture [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8].
*   **Proportional Constant (p):** A value between 0 and 1 relating fracture opening to radius, selected based on geological environment [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8].
*   **Fault Zone Height (Hf):** Approximate height range of the fracture zone [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8].

## Reusable Claims
*   The fractal dimension of a mine fracture zone can be calculated from 2D processed images using the box algorithm [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5, 9-12].
*   Fracture body density can be predicted from the fractal dimension using a linear relationship with site-specific coefficients A and B [Robotics 2020, pp. 3-5, 9-12].
*   The error in predicting maximum fracture length using the fractal method is dependent on the chosen proportional constant p, with lower p values (e.g., 1/100) yielding lower error ranges (20-30%) in the tested conditions [Robotics 2020, pp. 9-12].
*   A 3D fracture zone model can be generated by superimposing fracture-based models (triangular prisms) whose dimensions are controlled by predicted fractal parameters and an IFS algorithm [Robotics 2020, pp. 5-8, 8-9].
