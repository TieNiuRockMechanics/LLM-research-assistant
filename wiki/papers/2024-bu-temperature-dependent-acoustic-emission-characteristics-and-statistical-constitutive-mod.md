# Temperature-Dependent Acoustic Emission Characteristics and Statistical Constitutive Model of Granite under Uniaxial Compression.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the temperature-dependent mechanical behavior, acoustic emission (AE) characteristics, and proposes a coupled thermo-mechanical statistical damage constitutive model for granite under uniaxial compression after thermal treatment up to 600°C.

## Research Question
How do temperature-induced thermal damage and the resulting microcrack evolution affect the mechanical properties, acoustic emission characteristics, and failure process of granite, and how can these effects be quantitatively modeled in a constitutive relationship?

## Study Area / Data
- Granite specimens were collected from the Erlangshan Tunnel in Sichuan Province, China [Bu 2024, pp. 2-3].
- Specimens were processed into cylinders with a diameter of 50 mm and a height of 100 mm [Bu 2024, pp. 2-3].
- A total of 27 specimens were prepared: 6 for temperature curve measurement and 21 for grouped thermal treatment tests (3 specimens per group at target temperatures of 25°C, 100°C, 200°C, 300°C, 400°C, 500°C, and 600°C) [Bu 2024, pp. 2-3].
- Initial average density was 2.667 g/cm³ and P-wave velocity was 4340 m/s at room temperature [Bu 2024, pp. 2-3].
- Mineral composition at 25°C: quartz (31.8%), potash feldspar (13.8%), plagioclase (37.4%), biotite (15.3%), pyrite (1.2%), chlorite (0.5%) [Bu 2024, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Thermal Treatment:** Specimens were heated in an integrated muffle furnace. Internal temperature was monitored via three thermocouples drilled into one end. The heating rate was between 0.1°C/min and 2.4°C/min. After reaching the target temperature, specimens were held for 2 hours before cooling by opening the furnace door [Bu 2024, pp. 2-4].
- **Physical Property Testing:** Mass, volume, and P-wave velocity were measured before and after thermal treatment [Bu 2024, pp. 3-4].
- **Mechanical Testing:** Uniaxial compression tests were conducted using a hydraulic servo control system with a loading rate of 0.025 mm/min [Bu 2024, pp. 3-4].
- **Acoustic Emission (AE) Monitoring:** Four Nano30 AE sensors were attached to the specimen. The preamplifier gain and detection threshold were set to 40 dB. AE parameters including ring count, amplitude, frequency, and b-value were analyzed [Bu 2024, pp. 3-4].
- **Crack Initiation and Damage Stress Determination:** The method by Ranjith et al. (2008) using accumulative AE count and axial stress-time curves was used to determine crack initiation stress (σ_ci) and crack damage stress (σ_cd) [Bu 2024, pp. 9-10].
- **Constitutive Model Development:** A coupled thermo-mechanical (TM) statistical damage model was developed based on the Weibull distribution, Lemaitre's strain equivalence principle, and the Mohr-Coulomb criterion, incorporating AE parameters (accumulative ring count) [Bu 2024, pp. 12-20].

## Key Findings
1.  **Physical Properties:** Mass loss, volume expansion, and P-wave velocity decrease with increasing temperature. The most significant changes occur at 600°C, with a volume expansion rate of 3.21% and a P-wave velocity decrease of 81.75% [Bu 2024, pp. 4-6].
2.  **Mechanical Behavior:** Granite exhibits a temperature-strengthening effect (increased peak stress and elastic modulus) between 25°C and 200°C, followed by a thermal weakening effect above 200°C. At 600°C, peak stress is only 56.98% of that at 25°C [Bu 2024, pp. 6-8].
3.  **Thermally-Induced Cracks:** Optical microscopy shows intergranular cracks appear at 300°C-400°C, with transgranular cracks in feldspar at 500°C. At 600°C, the α/β quartz phase transformation at 573°C induces significant transgranular cracking [Bu 2024, pp. 8-9].
4.  **AE Characteristics:**
    - More AE ring counts appear in the early loading stage (Stage I) at higher temperatures [Bu 2024, pp. 9-10].
    - Crack initiation stress (σ_ci) decreases with temperature, while the stable crack propagation stage (Stage II) becomes longer [Bu 2024, pp. 9-10].
    - The proportion of high-frequency (HF) AE signals attenuates with temperature, while low-frequency (LF) signals increase significantly at 600°C [Bu 2024, pp. 10-12].
    - The global b-value increases significantly at 600°C. The dynamic b-value fluctuates more in early loading stages at higher temperatures and decreases sharply near peak stress [Bu 2024, pp. 12-14].
5.  **Constitutive Model:** The proposed coupled TM damage model, which introduces optimization parameters (θ, θ_TM, γ) based on AE accumulative ring count, can well fit the pre-peak nonlinear stress-strain curve, especially the compaction stage, of thermally treated granite [Bu 2024, pp. 14-20].

## Limitations
- The constitutive model can only reflect the pre-peak stage of the stress-strain curve and does not account for post-peak behavior or residual strength [Bu 2024, pp. 20-21].
- The study focuses on uniaxial compression; the model's applicability under confining pressure is not confirmed.
- Experimental conditions for thermal treatment (heating rate, holding time, cooling method) vary across studies and may affect results [Bu 2024, pp. 2-4].
- The model parameters (m, F₀) are derived for the specific granite tested and may require recalibration for other rock types.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The rock's mesoscopic element strength obeys a Weibull statistical distribution [Bu 2024, pp. 12-14].
- Thermal damage (D_T) is assessed based on the reduction in elastic modulus [Bu 2024, pp. 12-14].
- The total damage (D) is a coupled function of thermal and mechanical damage, with thermal damage occurring first [Bu 2024, pp. 14-20].
- The stress-strain relationship before the threshold stress (σ_t = 0.4σ_p) is dominated by crack closure and elastic deformation [Bu 2024, pp. 20-21].
- The AE accumulative ring count (N) is a valid proxy for the progression of mechanical damage during loading [Bu 2024, pp. 14-20].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Temperature (T):** Independent variable, ranging from 25°C to 600°C.
- **Peak Stress (σ_p):** Maximum axial stress at failure.
- **Elastic Modulus (E):** Slope of the linear elastic portion of the stress-strain curve.
- **Crack Initiation Stress (σ_ci):** Stress at which stable crack propagation begins.
- **Crack Damage Stress (σ_cd):** Stress at which unstable crack propagation begins.
- **AE Ring Count (RC) / Accumulative Ring Count (ARC):** Measures of acoustic emission activity.
- **b-value:** Seismological parameter derived from AE amplitude, indicating the relative size distribution of events.
- **Thermal Damage Variable (D_T):** Defined as 1 - E_T/E₀.
- **Mechanical Damage Variable (D_M):** Based on Weibull distribution: D_M = 1 - exp[-(F/F₀)^m].
- **Optimization Parameters (θ, θ_TM, γ):** Introduced to control damage evolution in the constitutive model, linked to ARC [Bu 2024, pp. 14-20].
- **Weibull Parameters (m, F₀):** Shape and scale parameters of the strength distribution. m represents brittleness, F₀ represents peak strength characteristics [Bu 2024, pp. 20-21].

## Reusable Claims
- Granite exhibits a thermal strengthening effect (increased σ_p and E) between 25°C and 200°C, followed by a thermal weakening effect above 200°C, due to competing mechanisms of crack closure and thermally-induced crack propagation [Bu 2024, pp. 6-8].
- The crack initiation stress (σ_ci) of granite decreases monotonically with increasing thermal treatment temperature, indicating that stress-induced cracking occurs at lower loads in thermally damaged rock [Bu 2024, pp. 9-10].
- At a thermal treatment temperature of 600°C, the AE signal frequency spectrum shifts significantly, with a marked increase in low-frequency components and attenuation of high-frequency components, suggesting a change in dominant fracture mode [Bu 2024, pp. 10-12].
- The global b-value derived from AE data increases significantly after thermal treatment at 600°C, indicating a higher proportion of small-scale microcrack events relative to large-scale macro-fractures [Bu 2024, pp. 12-14].
- A coupled thermo-mechanical statistical damage constitutive model that incorporates the accumulative AE ring count can effectively simulate the pre-peak nonlinear compaction and elastic stages of granite after thermal treatment [Bu 2024, pp. 14-20].

## Connections To Other Work
- The study builds on prior research on temperature-dependent physical and mechanical properties of granite [Bu 2024, pp. 1-2].
- It extends previous work on AE characteristics of heated granite, which often focused only on ring count and energy, by providing comprehensive amplitude, frequency, and b-value analysis [Bu 2024, pp. 1-2].
- The constitutive model approach follows the statistical damage theory initiated by Krajcinovic and Silva (1982) and incorporates concepts from later coupled TM models [Bu 2024, pp. 1-2].
- The method for determining σ_ci and σ_cd using AE follows Ranjith et al. (2008) [Bu 2024, pp. 9-10].
- The use of the Mohr-Coulomb criterion in the damage model incorporates temperature-dependent strength parameters as per Tian (2013) [Bu 2024, pp. 14-20].

## Open Questions
- How do different cooling methods (e.g., in-furnace cooling vs. rapid quenching) affect the AE characteristics and constitutive model parameters?
- Can the proposed constitutive model be extended to predict post-peak behavior and residual strength?
- How does the model perform under true triaxial or confined compression conditions?
- What is the specific mechanism linking the interaction of thermally-induced and stress-induced cracks to the observed changes in AE frequency spectra?
- How applicable are the model parameters (m, F₀) to granites of different mineralogical compositions or from different geological settings?
