# A New Computer Code for Discrete Fracture Network Modelling.

## One-line Summary
The paper presents **FracSim3D**, a comprehensive software package for two- and three-dimensional stochastic rock fracture simulation via marked point processes; fracture locations are generated by Poisson, non-homogeneous, cluster, or Cox point processes, while fracture geometry and properties are sampled from probability distributions, with built-in virtual sampling and statistical analysis tools [Xu 2009, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question
How to provide a **simple, freely available, and comprehensive tool** for the research community to generate 2D and 3D fracture networks stochastically, including realistic location models and sampling facilities, based on marked point process theory [Xu 2009, pp. 1-1, 1‑2].

## Study Area / Data
- **Conceptual data** for stochastic modelling typically come from surveys of rock outcrops, drill cores, borehole imaging, geophysical surveys, or seismic monitoring; these are essentially 2D biased samples, requiring correction before use in 3D modelling [Xu 2009, pp. 1-2].
- A **demonstration example** assumes: fracture locations follow a homogeneous Poisson distribution; fractures are circular discs lying in parallel planes; the sampling plane is perpendicular to the fracture planes; no truncation on the generated fractures and an infinite sampling window [Xu 2009, pp. 9-10].

## Methods
- **General approach**: treat fracture locations, size, orientation, and other properties as random variables; assume simple geometries—straight lines (2D) or ellipses (3D); construct model by Monte Carlo simulation after distribution parameters are inferred from data; fractures are divided into subsets by orientation [Xu 2009, pp. 1-2].
- **Marked point process (MPP)**: fracture locations are modelled by a point process; geometrical and other properties are marks assigned to the centroid points [Xu 2009, pp. 2-3].
- **Point process models for locations**:
  - *Homogeneous Poisson*: constant intensity λ, simulation via partitioned grid and Poisson random variates (Ross 1997 method using log‑uniform runs) [Xu 2009, pp. 2-3].
  - *Non‑homogeneous*: location‑dependent intensity λ(X), simulated by thinning a homogeneous process with λ_max; both **parametric** (user‑entered function, e.g., `600*exp(-2x‑y+z)`) and **non‑parametric** (gridded λ values file) intensity models supported [Xu 2009, pp. 3-4].
  - *Cluster (parent‑daughter)*: parent points from any point process; random number of daughters per parent; daughter locations relative to parent follow a distribution (e.g., uniform within an ellipse, possibly with preferential direction); edge effect handled by **periodic boundaries** (wrapping onto a torus) [Xu 2009, pp. 4-6].
  - *Cox (doubly stochastic)*: stochastic intensity field Λ(X); conditional on Λ(X)=λ(X), events form a non‑homogeneous Poisson process; FracSim3D offers exponential, normal, and lognormal models for Λ(X), with user‑defined location‑dependent parameters [Xu 2009, pp. 4-6].
- **Fracture generation**: 
  - Size distributions: uniform, exponential, lognormal (Table 2), or non‑parametric histogram [Xu 2009, pp. 6-7].
  - Orientations: in 2D, wrapped normal or von Mises; in 3D, Fisher distribution for fracture plane (dip direction, dip angle) and rotation angle (Fig. 4; Table 2); non‑parametric also allowed [Xu 2009, pp. 6-7].
  - Polygon creation uses **smoothing factor** (regulates approximation to ellipse) and **segmentation factor** (radial division for cell representation) [Xu 2009, pp. 6-7].
- **Additional properties**: Monte Carlo sampling of uniform, normal, exponential, lognormal, or non‑parametric PDF; up to five independent properties can be assigned [Xu 2009, pp. 7-9].
- **Virtual sampling**: plane sampling (3D→2D traces), window sampling (rectangular window for trace length/orientation analysis), scanline sampling (spacing analysis), core sampling (vertical boreholes through 3D network) [Xu 2009, pp. 7-9].
- **Statistical tools**: equal‑area/equal‑angle hemispherical projections, rose diagrams, histograms, normal and lognormal probability plots [Xu 2009, pp. 7-9].
- **Export**: fracture coordinates, polygon vertices, and properties can be saved as text or Excel‑readable spreadsheets [Xu 2009, pp. 9-10].

## Key Findings
1. **FracSim3D successfully implements MPP simulation** with four point‑process models, various size/ orientation distributions, and built‑in sampling facilities [Xu 2009, pp. 10-10].
2. In a **validation example** with parallel circular discs and a perpendicular sampling plane, the simulated trace‑length distribution **f(l)** closely matches the theoretical prediction of Warburton (1980) for both exponential and lognormal size distributions; averages of 40 independent simulations lie within the 95% confidence envelopes [Xu 2009, pp. 9-10].
3. When **truncation** is introduced (finite geographical region), the trace‑length distribution still follows the predicted model reasonably well, though with a greater proportion of smaller traces [Xu 2009, pp. 10-10].

## Limitations
- **No correlation between fracture properties** (e.g., size and thickness) in this version [Xu 2009, pp. 7-9].
- Only **simple fracture geometries** are implemented: straight lines (2D) and ellipses (3D); polygons are approximations [Xu 2009, pp. 1-2, 6‑7].
- The **generated point sets do not include in‑plane heterogeneity**; fractures are considered as simple marks attached to centroids [Xu 2009, pp. 2-3].
- **Bias correction** for 2D data is mentioned as necessary but not automated within the software [Xu 2009, pp. 1-2].
- The **Cox process models** are limited to exponential, normal, and lognormal stochastic intensities; more complex models are possible but not pre‑programmed [Xu 2009, pp. 4-6].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Rock fractures can be represented as **straight line segments (2D) or planar ellipses (3D)** [Xu 2009, pp. 1-2].
- Fractures are **divided into sub‑sets by orientation**, each modelled independently; if sub‑sets are correlated, a hierarchical or plurigaussian model may be used, but this is not the default [Xu 2009, pp. 1-2].
- **Marked point process** treats fracture locations as points, and all other properties as independent marks—no spatial correlation among marks unless governed by the point process [Xu 2009, pp. 2-3].
- For homogeneous Poisson simulation, the region is partitioned into a **regular grid** with independent Poisson counts per cell, then points uniformly distributed within each cell [Xu 2009, pp. 2-3].
- **Non‑parametric intensity models** must be supplied as a file with one value per grid cell, in a fixed order (x, y, z cycling) [Xu 2009, pp. 3-4].
- For cluster processes, **daughters are independent** of each other, and their location distribution around a parent is usually isotropic or with a preferential direction specified by an ellipse/ellipsoid [Xu 2009, pp. 4-6].
- The **validation example** explicitly assumes infinite sampling extent and no censoring, which is rarely met in field data; the truncation test shows the model still performs well under finite‑region constraints [Xu 2009, pp. 9-10].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Point process intensity**: λ (constant) for Poisson; λ(X) function for non‑homogeneous; parameters for parent process and daughter dispersion for clusters; stochastic intensity field parameters for Cox [Xu 2009, pp. 2-4].
- **Fracture size**: parameters of uniform (a,b), exponential (μ,λ), lognormal (a, μ_e, σ_e), or histogram‑defined non‑parametric distribution [Xu 2009, pp. 6-7].
- **Fracture orientation**: 
  - 2D: azimuth via wrapped normal (μ,σ) or von Mises (μ,κ).
  - 3D: dip direction, dip angle, rotation angle; Fisher distribution (mean plane via dip/dip‑direction or normal, κ) [Xu 2009, pp. 6-7].
- **Smoothing factor (SM)** and **segmentation factor (SG)**: control polygon shape and radial cell count [Xu 2009, pp. 6-7].
- **Additional property distributions**: choice of uniform, normal, exponential, lognormal, or non‑parametric, with corresponding parameters [Xu 2009, pp. 7-9].
- **Grid resolution** for Poisson simulation and non‑parametric intensity files [Xu 2009, pp. 2-4].

## Reusable Claims
1. **MPP simulation with point‑process locations and Monte‑Carlo marks** can generate realistic 2D/3D fracture networks suitable for flow or mechanical analysis, provided the underlying distributions are properly inferred from (biased) field data [Xu 2009, pp. 1-1, 2‑3, 10‑10].
2. **Non‑homogeneous Poisson processes can be simulated via thinning**, using the maximum intensity λ_max and retention probability λ(X)/λ_max; this is robust as long as λ_max is known or estimable [Xu 2009, pp. 3-4].
3. **Periodic boundary treatment** (toroidal wrapping) is effective for preserving all generated daughter points in parent‑daughter cluster models when simulating over a finite region, preventing edge‑effect loss [Xu 2009, pp. 4-6].
4. **Warburton’s (1980) stereological integral** relating the size distribution of parallel circular fractures to exposed trace length distribution holds under ideal conditions (Poisson locations, parallel discs, perpendicular plane, no truncation) and is empirically validated by FracSim3D simulations [Xu 2009, pp. 9-10].
5. Under **truncation**, the trace length distribution from a finite‑region sample still approximates the theoretical infinite‑window model, though with an increased frequency of shorter traces; this implies that the theoretical relationship is reasonably robust for moderate truncation [Xu 2009, pp. 10-10].

## Open Questions
- How to incorporate **correlations among fracture properties** (e.g., size–thickness, orientation–length) in future versions of the software? [Xu 2009, pp. 7-9]  
- What are the **appropriate 3D fracture size distributions** given the scarcity of true 3D surveys? The current implementation assumes similarity to 2D trace‑derived distributions, but more 3D datasets (e.g., Dowd et al., 2009) could refine these models [Xu 2009, pp. 6-7].
- Can **more complex fracture geometries** (e.g., non‑planar, polygonal, or irregular) be incorporated without excessive computational cost?
- How well does the **thinning method** perform when λ_max is not constant or when the intensity function has extreme variations? [Xu 2009, pp. 3-4]
- What is the impact of **correlated fracture subsets** (beyond hierarchical/plurigaussian) on network connectivity and flow, and how can such correlations be modelled within a general‑purpose simulator?
