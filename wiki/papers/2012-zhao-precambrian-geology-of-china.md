# Precambrian Geology of China.

## One-line Summary
This review synthesizes the current understanding and unresolved controversies on the tectonic architecture, assembly, and Precambrian evolution of the three major cratonic blocks of China—the North China, South China, and Tarim cratons—based on multidisciplinary data and competing models [Zhao 2012, pp. 2-3, 31-32].

## Research Question
What are the principal components, collisional histories, and tectonic settings of the North China, South China, and Tarim cratons, and how do the diverse geochronological, metamorphic, and geochemical datasets constrain or challenge existing models for their Precambrian amalgamation and breakup? [Zhao 2012, pp. 2-3]

## Study Area / Data
The study covers the three largest Precambrian blocks in China:  
- **North China Craton (NCC)** – including the Eastern and Western blocks, the Trans‑North China Orogen, and the Khondalite and Jiao‑Liao‑Ji belts [Zhao 2012, pp. 3-6].  
- **South China Craton** – subdivided into the Yangtze and Cathaysia blocks separated by the Jiangnan Belt, plus the Panxi‑Hannan Belt [Zhao 2012, pp. 12-13].  
- **Tarim Craton** – mainly exposed in the Kulukatage, Dunhuang, Akesu, and surrounding marginal complexes [Zhao 2012, pp. 28-30].  
Data consist of published structural, metamorphic, geochemical, geochronological, and geophysical results from the past two decades [Zhao 2012, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
The paper is a comprehensive literature review that integrates:  
- Field‑based structural and lithological mapping  
- Petrological and metamorphic P–T path analysis  
- Geochronology (SHRIMP, LA‑ICP‑MS, TIMS U‑Pb zircon; monazite; Ar‑Ar; Sm‑Nd etc.)  
- Whole‑rock geochemistry and isotope (Nd, Hf, Sr, O) systematics  
- Aeromagnetic and seismic imaging where available.  
Conflicting tectonic models are compared against the available evidence [Zhao 2012, pp. 2-3, 31-34].

## Key Findings
- The **North China Craton** consists of Archean to Paleoproterozoic basement, with rocks as old as 3.8 Ga. Neoarchean (2.8–2.7 Ga and 2.55–2.50 Ga) magmatism dominates, the latter forming ~80% of the Archean outcrop. The Eastern and Western blocks show gneissic domes, TTG–komatiite associations, anticlockwise P–T paths (isobaric cooling) and a 2.5 Ga metamorphic overprint, interpreted as reflecting mantle‑plume underplating. The Trans‑North China Orogen (TNCO) contains arc‑related rocks, linear belts, high‑pressure granulites/eclogites, and clockwise P–T paths, recording a 1.85 Ga continent–continent collision [Zhao 2012, pp. 1-1, 6-9].  
- The Western Block amalgamated via the Khondalite Belt at ~1.95 Ga before colliding with the Eastern Block at ~1.85 Ga along the TNCO. The Jiao‑Liao‑Ji Belt in the Eastern Block represents an earlier rift‑to‑collision cycle (~2.2–1.9 Ga) [Zhao 2012, pp. 9-10, 31-32].  
- Post‑collisional extension (1.80–1.75 Ga dyke swarms, 1.75–1.68 Ga AMCG suites) and later Mesoproterozoic‑Neoproterozoic rifting (Zhaertai‑Bayan Obo, Xiong’er Group) mark the response to supercontinent breakup (Columbia/Nuna) [Zhao 2012, pp. 11-12, 32].  
- The **South China Craton** (Yangtze + Cathaysia) assembled along the Jiangnan Belt. Archean‑Paleoproterozoic basement is sparse in the Yangtze Block but detrital zircons indicate widespread older crust. Late Mesoproterozoic to early Neoproterozoic folded belts (Jiangnan and Panxi‑Hannan) are composed of volcanic‑sedimentary successions and granitoids; multiple models (plume‑rift, slab‑arc, plate‑rift) explain their origin [Zhao 2012, pp. 15-21, 32-33].  
- The Cathaysia Block is dominated by Neoproterozoic rocks metamorphosed at 460–420 Ma, with minor Paleoproterozoic (1890–1830 Ma) and Mesoproterozoic (Hainan) units. Early Paleozoic deformation may reflect either intracontinental orogeny or continental collision with an unknown block [Zhao 2012, pp. 24-27, 33].  
- The **Tarim Craton** contains Neoarchean (2.6–2.5 Ga) TTG gneisses, Paleoproterozoic (2.45–2.35 Ga and 1.9 Ga) intrusions, and late Mesoproterozoic to Neoproterozoic meta‑sedimentary/volcanic rocks. Metamorphism at 1.9–1.8 Ga and 1.1–1.0 Ga relates to assembly of Columbia and Rodinia. The middle Neoproterozoic cover includes four tillite horizons (snowball Earth) and mafic‑ultramafic complexes linked to Rodinia breakup [Zhao 2012, pp. 28-31, 33-34].  
- Key controversies persist: timing and polarity of subduction in the TNCO, nature of the Jiao‑Liao‑Ji Belt, suture location and collision age of the Yangtze‑Cathaysia blocks, and the tectonic setting of Neoproterozoic magmatism in South China [Zhao 2012, pp. 27-28, 33].

## Limitations
- The review does not present new primary data; all interpretations rely on previously published work, the accuracy and completeness of which may vary [Zhao 2012, pp. 2-3].  
- Many critical areas (e.g., the interior of NCC Western Block Ordos, large parts of Tarim, and much of Cathaysia) are covered by younger sediments, limiting direct geological observation [Zhao 2012, pp. 9-9, 28-28].  
- The timing of several key events (collision ages in Jiangnan Belt, subduction polarity in TNCO, origin of early Paleozoic orogeny in Cathaysia) remains disputed, and no single model is universally accepted [Zhao 2012, pp. 27-28].  
- The paper focuses on the Precambrian; later (Phanerozoic) overprinting, particularly in Cathaysia (250–230 Ma and 460–420 Ma events), complicates the interpretation of older records [Zhao 2012, pp. 23-24].  

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Plate‑tectonic processes (subduction, collision, rifting) operated during the Archean and Proterozoic, even if styles differed from modern analogs [Zhao 2012, pp. 2-3, 8-9].  
- Geochronological ages (especially U‑Pb zircon) reliably record the timing of magmatic crystallization and metamorphic events [Zhao 2012, pp. 2-3].  
- Metamorphic P–T paths (clockwise vs anticlockwise) can be used to distinguish tectonic settings (collisional vs. magmatic underplating/plume) [Zhao 2012, pp. 6-6, 10-10].  
- The subdivision of cratons into blocks and orogenic belts is based on distinct lithological, structural, metamorphic, and geochronological contrasts [Zhao 2012, pp. 3-4].  

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Geochronology**: U‑Pb zircon ages (SHRIMP, LA‑ICP‑MS, TIMS), Sm‑Nd and Lu‑Hf isotope data, Ar‑Ar cooling ages.  
- **Metamorphic conditions**: Peak pressure–temperature (P–T) paths, mineral assemblages (e.g., kyanite‑K‑feldspar‑garnet for high‑pressure granulites; sapphirine‑quartz for UHT).  
- **Rock associations**: TTG gneisses, komatiites, bimodal volcanic suites, BIFs, S‑type and A‑type granites, calc‑alkaline arc rocks, ophiolitic mélanges.  
- **Structural style**: Gneissic domes vs. linear thrust belts, sense of shear (top‑to‑NW, top‑to‑SE).  
- **Isotopic signatures**: εHf(t), εNd(t), whole‑rock Sr‑Nd isotopes, zircon Hf model ages.  

## Reusable Claims
1. **Neoarchean mantle plume in NCC Eastern Block**: The large volume of 2.55–2.50 Ga TTG gneisses, komatiites requiring ~1600 °C melting, dominant domal structures, bimodal volcanism, and anticlockwise P–T paths are consistent with a mantle plume origin, provided that alternative arc models cannot simultaneously explain all these features [Zhao 2012, pp. 8-9].  
2. **Paleoproterozoic collision in Khondalite Belt**: High‑pressure pelitic granulites with kyanite‑K‑feldspar‑garnet and clockwise P–T paths with isothermal decompression, together with ~1.95 Ga metamorphic zircon ages, support continent–continent collision between the Yinshan and Ordos blocks [Zhao 2012, pp. 9-10].  
3. **Timing of Yangtze‑Cathaysia collision**: Detrital zircon ages show that the Sibao Group and equivalents in the Jiangnan Belt were deposited between 850 and 825 Ma; therefore the collision that formed the belt must have occurred after 825 Ma, most likely 825–815 Ma, if the unconformably overlying Banxi Group (815 Ma) marks post‑collisional deposition [Zhao 2012, pp. 27-28].  
4. **Cathaysia Block early Paleozoic event**: Widespread 460–420 Ma metamorphic ages and clockwise P–T paths in the Cathaysia Block indicate a continent–continent collisional event, possibly involving an unknown block that later rifted away along the Zhenghe‑Dapu Fault; absence of ophiolites in the current record does not refute this, as they may have been removed with the lost continental fragment [Zhao 2012, pp. 26-27].  
5. **Tarim Craton correlation with NCC**: Dunhuang Complex records Neoarchean TTG emplacement at 2.56–2.50 Ga followed by metamorphism at ~2.5 Ga and 1.9–1.8 Ga, analogous to the Western Block of the NCC; this suggests the Dunhuang Complex may be the western extension of the Alax Complex, separated from the rest of Tarim by the Xingxingxia Fault, but further work is required to test whether the entire Tarim Craton shared the same Archean–Paleoproterozoic history as the NCC [Zhao 2012, pp. 29-30].

## Connections To Other Work
- The subdivision of the NCC into Eastern and Western blocks, the Trans‑North China Orogen, and the Khondalite Belt builds on the models of Zhao et al. (1998, 2001a, 2005) [Zhao 2012, pp. 4-5].  
- Alternative NCC models (e.g., Kusky & Li 2003; Faure et al. 2007; Santosh 2010; Zhai & Santosh 2011) are discussed and contrasted with the preferred collisional sequence [Zhao 2012, pp. 4-6, 11-12].  
- The plume‑rift, slab‑arc, and plate‑rift models for South China (Li et al. 2003c; Zhou et al. 2002a,b; Zheng et al. 2007,2008a) are compared, highlighting their ability to explain different datasets [Zhao 2012, pp. 19-21].  
- The Tarim Craton’s evolution is compared to global supercontinent cycles, and its possible correlation with the North China Craton is explored via the Dunhuang Complex [Zhao 2012, pp. 29-30, 33-34].  
- The middle Neoproterozoic tillite sequences in Tarim are linked to the global snowball Earth hypothesis [Zhao 2012, pp. 31-31].  

## Open Questions
- What is the exact extent and nature of pre‑Neoarchean crust in the NCC and Yangtze Block, and why is it so poorly exposed? [Zhao 2012, pp. 5-6, 12-13]  
- Did the amalgamation of the Eastern and Western blocks along the TNCO involve a single 1.85 Ga collision or multiple events at ~2.1 Ga and ~1.85 Ga? [Zhao 2012, pp. 10-11]  
- What was the polarity of subduction during the final assembly of the NCC, and can present‑day seismic images reliably record Paleoproterozoic structures? [Zhao 2012, pp. 11-12]  
- Where exactly is the suture zone between the Yangtze and Cathaysia blocks, and when and how did they collide? [Zhao 2012, pp. 27-28]  
- What is the tectonic nature of the early Paleozoic (460–420 Ma) event in Cathaysia: intracontinental or continental‑margin collision? [Zhao 2012, pp. 26-27]  
- Do the Dunhuang Complex and the Alax Complex of the NCC share a common Archean–Paleoproterozoic history, implying that the Tarim and North China cratons were once joined? [Zhao 2012, pp. 29-30]  
- What drove the multiple phases of Neoproterozoic mafic–ultramafic magmatism in Tarim (820–800 Ma, 780–760 Ma, 740–735 Ma), and how do they relate to the breakup of Rodinia? [Zhao 2012, pp. 31-31]
