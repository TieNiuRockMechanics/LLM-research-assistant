# Three-Dimensional Thermal Structure of the Chinese Continental Crust and Upper Mantle.

## One-line Summary
An and Shi (2007) invert S-wave velocities to obtain a 3D upper-mantle temperature model for China, then solve the steady-state thermal conduction equation to derive crustal temperatures down to 80 km, finding higher temperatures in eastern China and a consistency between the seismological and thermal lithosphere. [An 2007, pp. 1-2, 9-10]

## Research Question
How can a three-dimensional thermal structure of the Chinese continental crust and upper mantle be determined by inverting seismic velocities to overcome the uncertainties and steady-state assumptions inherent in traditional heat-flow based methods? [An 2007, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
*   **Study Area:** The Chinese continent, including major tectonic units such as the North China craton (subdivided into the Ordos basin, central orogenic belt, and eastern rift basin), the Yangtze craton (including the Sichuan basin), the Tarim craton, the Qiangtang terrane, and the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau. [An 2007, pp. 4-8]
*   **Primary Data:** A 3D S-wave velocity model (CN03S) derived from fundamental-mode Rayleigh wave dispersion tomography, using over 4000 ray paths with a lateral resolution of 4°–6°. [An 2007, pp. 3-4]
*   **Validation Data:** Observed surface heat flow data for China and Asia, collected from a global database and a compilation by Wang and Huang (1990). [An 2007, pp. 7-8]

## Methods
*   **Upper-Mantle Temperature Inversion:** S-wave velocities at depths of 70–240 km are inverted for upper-mantle temperatures using a direct grid-search method. This seismic-thermal conversion, based on Goes et al. (2000), accounts for the effects of temperature, pressure, and mineralogical composition on elastic parameters and corrects for anelasticity at high temperatures. The composition is assumed to be off-cratonic. [An 2007, pp. 2-4]
*   **Crustal and Shallow Mantle Temperature Calculation:** Temperatures down to 80 km depth are calculated by solving the steady-state thermal conduction equation. The boundary conditions are the surface temperature and the upper-mantle temperature at 80 km depth inverted from seismic velocities. The global model CRUST2.0 is used for crustal structure and layered thermal parameters. [An 2007, pp. 1, 3-4, 9-10]
*   **Validation:** Surface heat flow is *calculated* from the derived crustal temperatures and compared with observed heat flow to validate the temperature model, rather than used as a primary constraint. [An 2007, pp. 1, 7-8]

## Key Findings
*   **Crustal Temperature (25 km):** Crustal temperature in eastern China (500–600°C) is higher than in western China (<500°C), with the Tarim craton showing the lowest temperature (~460°C). [An 2007, pp. 1, 7-9]
*   **Lithospheric Thickness Implications:** The temperature model shows that the depth where the 1300°C adiabat is crossed is consistent with the top of the seismic low-velocity zone, suggesting the seismological and thermal lithosphere are compatible. The lithosphere beneath eastern China is generally thinner than 100 km, whereas it may exceed 200 km beneath the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau. [An 2007, pp. 4-6, 8-9]
*   **Temperature at 100 km:** Temperatures beneath eastern and southeastern China are higher than the 1300°C adiabat. The Tarim craton and Sichuan basin show generally low temperatures (~1000°C). [An 2007, pp. 1, 8-9]
*   **Temperature at 150 km:** High temperatures (>1300°C adiabat) persist beneath the North China craton, South China, and the eastern Yangtze craton, and are also observed beneath the Qiangtang terrane. The lowest temperatures (~1130°C) are found near the Indian-Eurasian collision zone. [An 2007, pp. 1, 7, 9]
*   **Temperature at 200 km:** Very low temperatures (<1300°C) occur beneath the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau and the region south of the Tarim craton, areas strongly affected by the Indian-Eurasian collision. [An 2007, pp. 1, 7, 9]
*   **Model Validation:** The misfit between the model-calculated and observed surface heat flow is smaller than 20% for most regions with dense, high-quality measurements, indicating that the model represents the general crustal thermal state. [An 2007, pp. 1, 7-8]

## Limitations
*   The 3D S-wave velocity model (CN03S) has poor resolution at depths ≥250 km, limiting temperature analysis to depths ≤240 km. [An 2007, pp. 3-4]
*   The conversion from seismic velocity to temperature carries an uncertainty of ~150℃ due to velocity model uncertainties, compositional assumptions, and anelasticity corrections. The use of an off-cratonic composition means the inverted temperature may be underestimated by 15–120℃ for cratonic regions. [An 2007, pp. 3-4]
*   The calculation of crustal temperatures down to 80 km assumes a steady-state thermal conduction, which may not hold in tectonically active regions. The derived temperatures are considered a first-order approximation. [An 2007, pp. 9-10]
*   The study does not account for the thermal effects of fluids due to a lack of a deep fluid distribution model. If fluid effects were present but interpreted as temperature, the inverted temperatures would be an upper bound. [An 2007, pp. 3-4]
*   Crustal temperatures in western China have less reliability than in eastern China due to more complex crustal structure. [An 2007, pp. 8-9]

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   **Steady-State vs. Transient:** The upper-mantle temperatures represent the present (transient) state, while crustal temperatures down to 80 km are a first-order approximation based on a steady-state conductive solution between surface and deep boundary conditions. [An 2007, pp. 9-10]
*   **Composition:** A homogeneous off-cratonic mantle composition (68% olivine, 18% orthopyroxene, 11% clinopyroxene, 3% garnet, iron content 0.1) is used for all regions, justified by the young tectonothermal age of most of the Chinese continent. [An 2007, pp. 3-4]
*   **Temperature Dominance:** Assumes that temperature is the main factor influencing seismic velocities in the 50–250 km depth range, with compositional variations causing relatively small changes (~1%). [An 2007, pp. 2-3]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Key Variables:** 3D S-wave velocity (Vs), upper-mantle temperature, crustal temperature, surface heat flow, conductive heat flow, seismic quality factor (Q).
*   **Key Parameters (Mantle):** Elastic parameters (K, μ), pressure (P), iron content (X_Fe), olivine/orthopyroxene/clinopyroxene/garnet volume fractions, mantle composition type (off-cratonic), anelasticity model (Q1), adiabatic temperature of 1300°C.
*   **Key Parameters (Crustal Conduction):** Heat productivity (A) per crustal layer (sediment, upper, middle, lower), thermal conductivity (k) per layer, Moho depth from CRUST2.0, surface temperature.

## Reusable Claims
*   **Claim 1:** In regions where high-quality surface heat flow data densely sample the area, a crustal temperature model constrained by seismic-velocity-derived uppermost mantle temperatures yields surface heat flow predictions that match observations within 20%. **Conditions:** The method assumes steady-state conduction in the crust and relies on an accurate 3D seismic velocity model. **Limitations:** The misfit may increase in areas with sparse or low-quality heat flow data, such as Tibet, where high misfits may reflect unrepresentative measurements. [An 2007, pp. 1, 7-8]
*   **Claim 2:** The depth where the inverted upper-mantle temperature crosses the 1300°C adiabat is consistent with the top of the seismic low-velocity zone, implying a physical link and reliability of the seismic-thermometry method. **Conditions:** This finding is based on an off-cratonic mantle composition and the S-wave model CN03S. **Limitations:** This consistency was observed for the North China craton, Ordos basin, and Sichuan basin, but a significant discrepancy exists for the Tarim craton when compared to traditional steady-state models. [An 2007, pp. 4-7, 9]
*   **Claim 3:** The Qiangtang terrane exhibits a deep thermal anomaly at 110–150 km depth, where temperatures exceed the 1300°C adiabat, yet this heat has not propagated to the surface, resulting in relatively low observed surface heat flow (~45 mWm⁻²). **Conditions:** The deep high temperature is inferred from low S-wave velocities and is considered an upper bound as fluids were not accounted for. **Limitations:** This interpretation relies on the assumption that the lithosphere is not in thermal steady state. [An 2007, pp. 6]

## Connections To Other Work
*   **Goes et al. (2000):** Foundational work for the seismic-thermal method used to convert seismic velocities to upper-mantle temperatures. [An 2007, pp. 2-4]
*   **Huang et al. (2003):** The source for the primary 3D S-wave velocity model (CN03S) of the Chinese continent used in this study. [An 2007, pp. 3-4]
*   **Wang (2001) & Chi and Yan (1998):** Provide traditional steady-state thermal conduction models for China and the North China craton, which serve as the primary comparison for the new seismic-thermal model. Significant discrepancies at depth are noted, particularly for the Tarim craton. [An 2007, pp. 4-7]
*   **Artemieva and Mooney (2001):** Their global study of Precambrian lithosphere provides the methodological basis for the steady-state conduction calculation and some thermal parameters used in the crustal model. [An 2007, pp. 1-2, 3-4]
*   **An and Shi (2006):** A previous paper by the authors uses the upper-mantle temperature model from this study to estimate lithospheric thickness across the Chinese continent. [An 2007, pp. 3-4]

## Open Questions
*   What is the spatial distribution and thermal effect of fluids in the deep crust and upper mantle, and how would accounting for them alter the inverted temperatures? [An 2007, pp. 3-4]
*   The cause of the significantly different thermal state between the Qiangtang terrane's hot upper mantle and cold crust remains an interpretational model; the specific geological processes heating the mantle are not identified. [An 2007, pp. 6]
*   The large discrepancy between seismic-thermal and steady-state models for the Tarim craton suggests a more complex, non-steady-state thermal regime, but the dynamic causes remain an open question. [An 2007, pp. 6-7]
*   How can the first-order approximation of crustal temperatures down to 80 km be improved to account for transient thermal effects and lateral heterogeneities in shallow thermal properties? [An 2007, pp. 9-10]
