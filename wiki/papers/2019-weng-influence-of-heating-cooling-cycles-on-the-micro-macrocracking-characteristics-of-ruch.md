# Influence of Heating/Cooling Cycles on the Micro/Macrocracking Characteristics of Rucheng Granite under Unconfined Compression.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how repeated heating (350 °C) and rapid water cooling (21 °C) cycles affect the mechanical properties, microcracking, and failure mode of Rucheng granite under unconfined compression, using integrated acoustic emission and digital image correlation techniques.

## Research Question
How do varying numbers of heating/cooling cycles influence the micro/macrocracking characteristics, mechanical properties (strength, stiffness, deformation), and failure mode of Rucheng granite under unconfined compression?

## Study Area / Data
The study uses Rucheng granite collected from Rucheng County, Hunan Province, China, an area with active geothermal resources and an average geothermal gradient of 18.7 °C/100 m [Weng 2019, pp. 3-4]. The granite consists mainly of quartz (78%), albite (11%), microcline (7%), and muscovite (2.6%) [Weng 2019, pp. 3-4]. Specimens were cored and polished to a nominal height of 100 mm and diameter of 50 mm [Weng 2019, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
1.  **Cyclic Thermal Treatment:** Specimens were heated to 350 °C at 5 °C/min, held for 6 hours, then rapidly cooled in tap water (~21 °C) at an average rate of 322.8 °C/min. Groups were subjected to 0, 1, 3, 6, 10, and 15 cycles [Weng 2019, pp. 3-4].
2.  **Mechanical Testing:** Unconfined compression tests were conducted using a servo-controlled system at a displacement rate of 0.02 mm/min (strain rate ~3.33×10⁻⁶ s⁻¹) [Weng 2019, pp. 4-6].
3.  **Monitoring Techniques:**
    *   **Acoustic Emission (AE):** A PCI-2 system with a 45 dB threshold and 40 dB gain was used to record microcracking events [Weng 2019, pp. 4-6].
    *   **Digital Image Correlation (DIC):** A high-speed camera (50 fps) captured surface deformation. A speckle pattern was applied, and 2D DIC analysis was validated against strain gauges [Weng 2019, pp. 6-7].
4.  **Microstructural Analysis:** Surface cracks were visually inspected, and thin sections were examined using optical microscopy to identify intergranular and intragranular microcracks [Weng 2019, pp. 6-7].

## Key Findings
1.  **Damage Accumulation:** The number of surface cracks and internal microcracks (both intergranular and intragranular) increased with the number of heating/cooling cycles [Weng 2019, pp. 6-7].
2.  **Property Degradation:** P-wave velocity (Vp), unconfined compressive strength (UCS), and elastic modulus decreased with increasing cycles. Vp was more sensitive to damage than UCS (e.g., after 15 cycles, Vp decreased to 0.43 times initial, while UCS decreased to 0.61 times initial) [Weng 2019, pp. 7-9].
3.  **Stress-Strain Behavior:** Increased cycles led to more prominent crack closure stages, increased ductility, and a transition from brittle to slightly strain-softening post-peak behavior [Weng 2019, pp. 7-9].
4.  **Crack Propagation:** The onset of unstable crack propagation (crack damage stress, q_cd) initiated at a lower stress threshold with more cycles. The fracture formation stress (q_ff) relative to peak stress increased, indicating more pronounced softening [Weng 2019, pp. 14-16].
5.  **Failure Mode Transition:** The failure mode changed from a typical shear failure (failure angle ~63.5°) to axial splitting (failure angle ~90°) as the number of cycles increased [Weng 2019, pp. 14-16].

## Limitations
1.  Only one AE sensor was used, which may have failed to record all AE events, especially post-peak [Weng 2019, pp. 11-14].
2.  The study used a specific heating rate (5 °C/min) and rapid water cooling rate (322.8 °C/min); results may vary with different thermal treatment protocols.
3.  The research was conducted under unconfined compression only; confining pressure effects were not studied.
4.  The granite is from a specific location (Rucheng); generalizability to other rock types requires further investigation.

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The granite specimens are representative of the in-situ rock mass in the Rucheng geothermal area.
2.  The quasi-static loading condition (strain rate ~3.33×10⁻⁶ s⁻¹) is sufficient to capture the material's mechanical response.
3.  The 2D DIC technique is valid for measuring surface strains on cylindrical specimens given the small strains (<1%) involved [Weng 2019, pp. 6-7].
4.  The thermal damage is primarily caused by the mismatch in thermal expansion coefficients among mineral grains and thermal shock during rapid cooling [Weng 2019, pp. 16-18].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Independent Variable:** Number of heating/cooling cycles (0, 1, 3, 6, 10, 15).
*   **Thermal Parameters:** Heating temperature (350 °C), cooling medium temperature (21 °C), heating rate (5 °C/min), cooling rate (322.8 °C/min).
*   **Dependent Variables:** P-wave velocity (Vp), Unconfined Compressive Strength (UCS), Elastic Modulus (E), Poisson’s ratio (υ), Peak strain (ε_peak), AE hit counts, DIC strain fields, failure angle, crack density.

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For Rucheng granite subjected to cyclic heating (350 °C) and rapid water cooling. **Claim:** The sensitivity of P-wave velocity to thermally induced damage is greater than that of unconfined compressive strength, particularly after a low number of cycles [Weng 2019, pp. 7-9].
2.  **Condition:** For granite under unconfined compression after thermal cycling. **Claim:** An increase in the number of heating/cooling cycles promotes a transition in the macroscopic failure mode from shear-dominated to axial splitting-dominated [Weng 2019, pp. 14-16].
3.  **Condition:** For thermally damaged granite. **Claim:** The onset of unstable crack propagation (crack damage stress) occurs at a progressively lower fraction of the peak stress as the number of thermal cycles increases [Weng 2019, pp. 14-16].

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on previous work investigating the effects of single thermal treatments on rock properties [Weng 2019, pp. 1-2]. It specifically addresses the gap in understanding the effects of *cyclic* heating and cooling, which is more representative of conditions in Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) where cold water is repeatedly injected into hot rock [Weng 2019, pp. 2-3]. The observed shear-to-splitting failure transition is consistent with findings from other studies on thermally treated rocks at high temperatures [Weng 2019, pp. 18-19].

## Open Questions
1.  What is the quantitative relationship between the density of thermally induced microcracks and the reduction in macroscopic strength parameters?
2.  How do confining pressure and different pore fluid chemistries interact with cyclic thermal loading to affect damage evolution?
3.  What is the long-term behavior of thermally cycled rock under sustained loads relevant to geothermal reservoir conditions?
