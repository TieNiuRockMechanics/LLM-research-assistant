# Characterisation of Natural Fractures and Fracture Zones of the Basel EGS Reservoir Inferred from Geophysical Logging of the Basel-1 Well.

## One-line Summary
This study re-analyzes acoustic televiewer (UBI) logs from the Basel-1 well to characterize natural fractures and fracture zones in the crystalline basement, employing a novel stick-slip correction method that reveals a complex fracture set distribution and localized zones of high fracture frequency that do not consistently correlate with geophysical anomalies.

## Research Question
How can natural fractures and fracture zones within the Basel Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) reservoir be characterized using geophysical logging data from the Basel-1 well, and what are their spatial distributions, orientations, and relationships to other rock mass properties? [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Basel-1 well in Switzerland, located at the southeastern margin of the Upper Rhine Graben. The well was drilled to 5009 m depth, entering intact granite at 2507 m. Data analyzed include geophysical logs (ultrasonic televiewer (UBI), density, p-wave velocity, spectral gamma ray) run between 2602 m and 5000.5 m, along with drill cuttings analysis. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-2, 2-3, 3-4]

## Methods
- **UBI Log Analysis:** Natural fractures were identified by fitting sinusoidal curves to traces in the unwrapped UBI acoustic reflectivity log, distinguishing them from drilling-induced fractures (DIFs). [Ziegler 2015, pp. 4-5]
- **Stick-Slip Correction:** A novel correction method using accelerometer data from the UBI orientation tool (GPIT) was applied to improve image quality below 4677 m, where stick-slip motion distorted the depth scale. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 3-4, 4-5]
- **Fracture Set Identification:** Fuzzy (orientation) cluster analysis was performed on fractures in twelve non-overlapping 200 m intervals to identify fracture sets. A constant cluster search radius of 25° was used, and orientation bias was corrected using a minimum bias angle of 15°. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 4-5, 5-5]
- **Fracture Frequency & Zone Identification:** Fracture frequency was analyzed using 2 m and 10 m averaging windows after removing depth trends. Zones of high fracture frequency (FZ) were identified based on residual frequency thresholds. Zones of anomalously low density (LDZ) and low p-wave velocity (LVZ) were visually identified from log profiles. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-5, 7-10]

## Key Findings
- A total of 1164 natural fractures were identified, with 75% classified as certain and 25% as uncertain. The proportion of uncertain fractures increases with depth. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-7]
- Fracture frequency decreases with depth from 3.1 fractures/m near the top of the logged section to 0.3 fractures/m below 3.0 km. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-1, 5-5]
- Six potential fracture sets were identified, with only Set 1 (steeply dipping to W-SW) present along the entire borehole. Other sets occur over limited depth intervals. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-5, 5-7]
- The mean orientation of Set 1 does not coincide with prominent NNE-striking Rhenish lineaments but strikes subparallel to the maximum principal horizontal stress. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-1, 11-12]
- 29 zones of high fracture frequency (FZ-10m) with lengths up to 100 m were identified. Below 4 km, zones consisted predominantly of Set 1 fractures. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-5, 7-10, 11-12]
- Zones of high fracture frequency did not necessarily coincide with low density or low p-wave velocity anomalies. Over 54% of identified low velocity/density zones contained no fractures or had fracture frequencies similar to the local background. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-1, 10-11, 11-12]

## Limitations
- The primary limitation is the difficulty in distinguishing natural fractures from drilling-induced fractures (DIFs), which led to considerable differences between this and two previous analyses. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-2, 5-7, 11-12]
- The analysis assumes a cylindrical borehole, neglecting the effect of non-circular borehole geometry (breakouts) on dip angles, though this effect was found to be less than 3°. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 4-5]
- Fracture zone length cannot be inferred from a single borehole dataset; the study provides only point estimates of zone width. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 2-3, 11-12]
- The procedure for identifying low density/velocity zones was visual and provisional, and may not have identified all such zones. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-5]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The borehole is assumed to be cylindrical for fracture orientation analysis. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 4-5]
- A minimum bias angle of 15° was used in Terzaghi correction to avoid skewing of weighted orientation data. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 4-5]
- Fracture zones are defined as discontinuity structures of lengths tens of metres to a kilometre consisting of a quasi-planar connected network of single fractures. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 2-3]
- The background fracture frequency below 3.0 km was taken as 0.3 fractures/m for trend removal. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-5]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Fracture Frequency:** Fractures per metre (fractures/m), measured along the borehole. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-1, 5-5]
- **Fracture Set Orientation:** Dip direction and dip angle (e.g., Set 1: ~250°/70°). [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-5, 5-7]
- **Fracture Spacing:** Mean 'true' spacing between fractures within a set, in metres. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-5]
- **Zone Length:** Length of identified zones of high fracture frequency (FZ) or low density/velocity (LDZ/LVZ), in metres. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-5, 7-10]
- **Rock Properties:** Bulk density (ρb), p-wave velocity (vP), dynamic Young's modulus. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 7-10]

## Reusable Claims
- Applying a stick-slip correction using accelerometer data to UBI logs significantly improves image quality and fracture identification reliability in sections affected by tool motion. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-1, 3-4]
- In the Basel granite reservoir, fracture frequency exhibits a general decrease with depth, with a marked reduction below 3.0 km. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-1, 5-5]
- Fracture orientation analysis in crystalline basement can reveal multiple sets with discontinuous spatial occurrence, where one set may dominate at depth. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 5-5, 5-7, 11-12]
- Zones of high fracture frequency identified from borehole logs do not necessarily correspond to zones of anomalously low seismic velocity or density. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 10-11, 11-12]
- Narrow geophysical anomalies (low density/velocity) in borehole logs can be associated with single, highly altered or open fractures rather than broad fracture zones. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 10-11]

## Connections To Other Work
- This study re-analyzes UBI logs previously analyzed by Häring et al. (2008) and Fabbri (2011), finding considerable differences in fracture counts attributed to interpretation challenges. [Ziegler 2015, pp. 1-2, 5-7]
- The geological setting and lithological sequence build upon descriptions by Ladner et al. (2008) and Käser et al. (2007). [Ziegler 2015, pp. 2-3]
- The definition of fracture zones follows Valley (2007). [Ziegler 2015, pp. 2-3]
- The study relates fracture orientations to regional tectonic systems (Rhenish, Hercynian, Permian) described in earlier works (e.g., Gürler et al., 1987; Thury et al., 1994). [Ziegler 2015, pp. 11-12]
- The analysis of stress-induced breakouts and some DIFs references work by Valley and Evans (2009) and Sikaneta and Evans (2012). [Ziegler 2015, pp. 3-4, 4-5]
