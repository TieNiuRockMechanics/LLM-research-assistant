# Thermal Effects on Geologic Carbon Storage.

## One-line Summary
This review synthesizes knowledge on how temperature changes influence all stages of geologic carbon storage, from CO2 transport to leakage, emphasizing that non-isothermal processes are critical for successful CCS deployment.

## Research Question
The paper reviews the literature to address the overarching question: What are the thermal effects and their coupled processes related to Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS), and why is their comprehension crucial for successful project deployment? [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 1-5]

## Study Area / Data
This is a review paper synthesizing findings from numerous field sites and modeling studies. Referenced sites include the Ketzin pilot site (Germany) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 10-12], Sleipner (Norway) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 14-17], In Salah (Algeria) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 12-14], Cranfield (Mississippi) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 12-14], Frio (Texas) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 17-19], Nagaoka (Japan) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 17-19], Snøhvit (Norway) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 10-12], and the Alberta Basin (Canada) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 29-31]. Data sources include field measurements, laboratory experiments, and numerical simulations.

## Methods
The review covers analytical, semi-analytical, and numerical methods. Key numerical approaches include coupled wellbore-reservoir simulators (e.g., T2Well) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 12-14] and thermo-hydro-mechanical-chemical (THMC) models [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 35-38]. Analytical and semi-analytical solutions are used to estimate thermal stress and cold region positions [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 19-21]. Monitoring methods discussed include point measurements, wireline instruments, and fiber-optic distributed temperature sensing (DTS) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 33-35].

## Key Findings
1.  **Transport:** Optimal CO2 transport is in liquid state for both pipelines and ships due to higher density and lower pressure drops compared to supercritical or gaseous phases [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 5-8].
2.  **Injection:** CO2 heats up in the wellbore due to compression and friction but typically arrives at the storage formation colder than the surrounding rock [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 12-14]. Liquid CO2 injection is energetically efficient but induces cooling that can affect geomechanical stability [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 10-12].
3.  **Storage:** Temperature changes in the reservoir are caused by Joule-Thomson cooling, endothermic water vaporization, and exothermic CO2 dissolution [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 15-17]. Cold CO2 injection creates a cooled region that influences plume dynamics (viscous vs. gravity-dominated flow) and overpressure [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 17-19].
4.  **Geomechanics:** Thermal stresses from cooling can bring the stress state closer to failure, affecting caprock integrity. The impact depends strongly on the stress regime (normal, strike-slip, reverse faulting) [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 19-24].
5.  **Leakage:** CO2 leakage through wells or faults involves complex phase changes (supercritical to liquid to gas) and strong Joule-Thomson cooling, which can lead to self-limiting feedback mechanisms like three-phase flow and geysering [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 29-33].

## Limitations
The paper identifies several research gaps and limitations in current understanding:
*   The effect of impurities on pipeline corrosion is not well understood [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 35-38].
*   Wellbore and reservoir simulators are often decoupled, though coupling is needed for realistic injection conditions [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 35-38].
*   Thermo-mechanical effects of cold CO2 injection, especially on caprock stability, are not fully understood, with studies sometimes giving conflicting results [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 35-38].
*   Coupled THMC processes are computationally expensive and not yet investigated in detail [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 35-38].
*   Three-phase (water, liquid CO2, gaseous CO2) relative permeability and hysteresis in leakage pathways are poorly known [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 35-38].
*   The geomechanical implications of CO2 leakage involving liquid CO2 formation have not been investigated [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 35-38].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   CO2 properties (density, viscosity, solubility) are strongly dependent on pressure and temperature [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 5-8].
*   Non-isothermal effects play a major role in all stages of CCS, even if CO2 is injected in thermal equilibrium with the formation [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 5-8].
*   Storage formations are deep saline aquifers or depleted hydrocarbon reservoirs at depths where CO2 is supercritical [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 10-12].
*   Caprocks are typically softer (lower deviatoric stress) than storage formations [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 24-26].
*   For leakage analysis, wellbores are often the most likely conduit, especially in sedimentary basins with many legacy wells [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 29-31].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **CO2 Phase & Properties:** Density, viscosity, specific heat capacity, enthalpy, solubility [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 5-8].
*   **Thermal Parameters:** Temperature, geothermal gradient, thermal conductivity, thermal expansion coefficient [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 15-17, 19-21].
*   **Hydrological Parameters:** Pressure, overpressure, permeability, porosity, relative permeability [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 15-17, 24-26].
*   **Mechanical Parameters:** Stress state (normal, strike-slip, reverse faulting), tensile strength, shear strength [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 19-24].
*   **Operational Parameters:** Injection rate, wellhead pressure and temperature, wellbore geometry [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 12-14].

## Reusable Claims
*   **Condition:** For onshore CO2 pipeline transport. **Claim:** Transporting CO2 in liquid state is more efficient than in supercritical state due to lower compressibility and higher density, which reduces pressure drops and allows for smaller pipe diameters [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 5-8].
*   **Condition:** During CO2 injection into deep saline formations. **Claim:** The injected CO2 will typically reach the storage formation at a temperature lower than the ambient geothermal temperature, creating a cooled region around the wellbore that can persist for longer than the injection period [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 12-14, 17-19].
*   **Condition:** In a normal faulting stress regime. **Claim:** Thermal contraction of the storage formation due to cold CO2 injection can induce a stress redistribution that increases horizontal stresses in the overlying caprock, potentially enhancing its sealing stability [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 19-24].
*   **Condition:** For detecting CO2 or brine leakage from a storage formation. **Claim:** Combining pressure monitoring (for long-range detection) with temperature monitoring (for fluid identification near the leak) provides a more robust leakage detection strategy than using either method alone [Vilarrasa 2016, pp. 33-35].
