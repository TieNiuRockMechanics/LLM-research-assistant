# Deterioration of Equivalent Thermal Conductivity of Granite Subjected to Heating-Cooling Treatment.

## One-line Summary
The equivalent thermal conductivity (ETC) of granite decreases nonlinearly with increasing heating temperature, and this deterioration is significantly exacerbated by faster cooling rates due to increased porosity and microcrack density.

## Research Question
How do different cooling rates (air, water, liquid nitrogen) after heating affect the equivalent thermal conductivity (ETC) of granite, and what are the underlying meso-structural mechanisms for this deterioration?

## Study Area / Data
The experimental granite specimens were collected from the Xiamen Bay-Zhangzhou basin on China's southeast coast. The specimens were fine-grained, isotropic, and processed into discs (50 mm diameter, height-to-diameter ratio ~0.5). Mineral composition was approximately 32.4% quartz, 19.0% plagioclase, 45.2% K-feldspar, and 3.4% biotite. Initial average properties: P-wave velocity 4499 m/s, density 2.795 g/cm³, porosity ~0.55% [Bu 2023, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Heating Treatment:** Specimens were heated in a muffle furnace at 5°C/min to target temperatures (150°C, 300°C, 450°C, 600°C, 750°C) and held for 2 hours [Bu 2023, pp. 2-3].
- **Cooling Treatments:** Three methods were used: (I) air-cooling, (II) water-cooling (immersion in 20°C circulating water), and (III) liquid nitrogen (LN₂) cooling followed by natural heating to room temperature [Bu 2023, pp. 2-3].
- **ETC Measurement:** The transient hot-wire method (ISRM recommended) was used. Multiple surface measurements were averaged to obtain reliable ETC values [Bu 2023, pp. 3-6].
- **Meso-structural Analysis:** Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) was used to analyze pore structure and porosity. Optical microscopy was used to observe microcrack evolution and calculate microcrack density [Bu 2023, pp. 6-8, 10-12].
- **Numerical Modeling:** A CT-GBM (Computed Tomography and Grain-Based Model) discrete element numerical model was used to simulate microcrack propagation and heat conduction under different cooling rates (10°C/min to 200°C/min) [Bu 2023, pp. 12-14].

## Key Findings
1.  ETC decreases nonlinearly with increasing heating temperature. At 750°C, ETC decreased by 44.06%, 51.77%, and 61.56% after air-, water-, and LN₂-cooling, respectively [Bu 2023, pp. 3-6].
2.  At any given heating temperature, a faster cooling rate causes a greater decrease in ETC. LN₂-cooling produced the largest ETC reduction, followed by water-cooling and then air-cooling [Bu 2023, pp. 3-6].
3.  The deterioration in ETC is primarily caused by increased porosity and microcrack density resulting from the formation and propagation of pore structures and microcracks during heating and cooling [Bu 2023, pp. 1-1, 14-17].
4.  During heating, damage is induced by differences in thermal expansion and elastic properties of mineral grains. During cooling, a faster rate creates a higher temperature gradient, generating greater thermal stress that causes new cracks and aggravates existing damage [Bu 2023, pp. 1-1, 14-17].
5.  ETC shows a positive power-function correlation with P-wave velocity and density, and a negative power-function correlation with porosity [Bu 2023, pp. 6-8, 8-10].
6.  Microcrack density increases with heating temperature and cooling rate, showing a strong linear negative correlation with ETC [Bu 2023, pp. 10-12].
7.  Numerical simulations confirmed that higher cooling rates produce greater temperature gradients, higher thermal stress, more microcracks, and fewer effective thermal pipes, leading to reduced heat conduction [Bu 2023, pp. 12-14, 14-17].

## Limitations
- Only three discrete cooling methods (air, water, LN₂) were used; precise, continuous control of cooling rate in experiments is difficult [Bu 2023, pp. 14-17].
- The study focused on dry conditions; the effect of water-bearing state on ETC after heating-cooling was not investigated [Bu 2023, pp. 14-17].
- The numerical model was two-dimensional (2D); a 3D model is needed for more comprehensive study [Bu 2023, pp. 14-17].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Granite specimens were assumed to be isotropic in structure and composition [Bu 2023, pp. 2-3].
- The transient hot-wire method's measurement accuracy is approximately 3%, and it measures local ETC within a ~10 mm radial depth [Bu 2023, pp. 3-6].
- For NMR analysis, the surface relaxivity value for granite was taken as r₂FS = 1 × 10⁻⁸ m/ms [Bu 2023, pp. 8-8].
- The CT-GBM numerical model was calibrated to match the experimental granite's mineral composition and mechanical behavior [Bu 2023, pp. 12-14].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables:** Heating temperature (20°C to 750°C), Cooling method/rate (air, water, LN₂).
- **Dependent Variables:** Equivalent Thermal Conductivity (ETC), Porosity, Microcrack density, P-wave velocity, Density.
- **Controlled Variables:** Specimen size and preparation, heating rate (5°C/min), holding time (2 hours), drying procedure.

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** For granite, the equivalent thermal conductivity (ETC) decreases nonlinearly with increasing heating temperature, and this decrease is significantly amplified by faster cooling rates.
  - **Condition:** Under dry conditions, for heating temperatures from 20°C to 750°C and cooling via air, water, or liquid nitrogen.
  - **Limitation:** The relationship is specific to the granite type studied (fine-grained, isotropic, from Xiamen Bay-Zhangzhou basin).
- **Claim:** The primary mechanism for ETC deterioration in heated-cooled granite is the increase in porosity and microcrack density, which disrupts heat conduction pathways.
  - **Condition:** Observed via NMR and optical microscopy after thermal treatment.
  - **Limitation:** The quantitative contribution of porosity vs. microcrack density is not separated.
- **Claim:** A faster cooling rate induces a higher temperature gradient within the rock, generating greater thermal stress that promotes new crack formation and propagates existing cracks.
  - **Condition:** Demonstrated both experimentally and via numerical simulation (CT-GBM).
  - **Limitation:** The simulation used idealized cooling rates (10-200°C/min) rather than exact experimental conditions.

## Connections To Other Work
- The study builds on prior research on the effects of high-temperature treatment, temperature-pressure coupling, and freeze-thaw cycling on rock ETC [Bu 2023, pp. 1-2].
- It addresses a gap identified in the literature regarding the influence of different cooling rates on the ETC of heated rocks [Bu 2023, pp. 1-2].
- The experimental results are consistent with other studies showing that water-cooling produces lower ETC than air-cooling in granite [Bu 2023, pp. 10-12].
- The numerical model (CT-GBM) and its parameters are based on and validated against previous work by Guo et al. (2023c) [Bu 2023, pp. 12-14].

## Open Questions

- How can cooling rates be more precisely quantified and controlled in laboratory experiments?
