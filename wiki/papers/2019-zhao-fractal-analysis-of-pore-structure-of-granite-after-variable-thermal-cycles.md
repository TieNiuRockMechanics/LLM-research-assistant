# Fractal Analysis of Pore Structure of Granite after Variable Thermal Cycles.

## One-line Summary
This study uses mercury intrusion porosimetry and fractal analysis to investigate how variable thermal cycles (1-20 cycles) and temperatures (25-650°C) alter the pore structure of granite.

## Research Question
How do variable thermal cycles and temperatures affect the pore characteristics and fractal dimension of granite's pore structure?

## Study Area / Data
The granite samples originate from the city of Linyi in Shandong, China. The main mineral contents are quartz, feldspar, hornblende, and black mica. Samples were cut into cylinders of Φ 50 × 25 mm (diameter × height) [Zhao 2019, pp. 2-3]. A total of 52 granite samples were prepared, though samples after 20 cycles of 550 °C and 650 °C were damaged and could not be tested [Zhao 2019, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
Samples were subjected to five different temperatures (250, 350, 450, 550, and 650 °C) in a muffle furnace at a rate of 5 °C/min and maintained for 1 h, then cooled in water (about 25 °C) to room temperature. This process was repeated for 1, 5, 10, 15, and 20 thermal cycles for each temperature [Zhao 2019, pp. 2-3]. Pore characteristics were analyzed using a PoreMaster 60GT mercury intrusion porosimeter [Zhao 2019, pp. 2-3]. The fractal dimension (D) was calculated using equations relating pore volume (V(r)), total sample volume (V0), and mercury injection pressure (P) [Zhao 2019, pp. 2-3]. The relationship between pore diameter and pressure was established using the Washburn equation [Zhao 2019, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
1.  The pore characteristic distribution curve of granite shows obvious bimodal characteristics, allowing pores to be divided into two categories: 0.01 < r < 10 μm and 10 < r < 200 μm [Zhao 2019, pp. 1-2].
2.  Temperature has a great influence on pore structure, while the number of thermal cycles has little effect [Zhao 2019, pp. 1-2].
3.  Higher temperatures increase the total pore volume of granite, with 450 °C identified as a critical threshold temperature above which the total pore volume substantially increases [Zhao 2019, pp. 10-11].
4.  Micro pores (0.01 < r < 1 μm) are gradually transformed into large pores (100 < r < 200 μm) as temperature increases from 25 °C to 650 °C [Zhao 2019, pp. 10-11].
5.  As temperature increases, the fractal dimension of pores with diameters 0.01–10 μm decreases, reducing the complexity of that pore structure, while the fractal dimension of pores with diameters 10–200 μm increases, increasing the complexity of that pore structure [Zhao 2019, pp. 1-2, 10-11].
6.  The fractal dimension of the pores (0.01 < r < 10 μm) decreases with the number of thermal cycles, while that of the pores (10 < r < 200 μm) shows little change [Zhao 2019, pp. 10-11].

## Limitations
1.  Samples subjected to 20 thermal cycles at 550 °C and 650 °C were damaged and could not be tested, limiting data for the most extreme conditions [Zhao 2019, pp. 2-3].
2.  The study focuses solely on granite from a specific location (Linyi, Shandong, China), which may limit the generalizability of the findings to other rock types or granite from different geological settings.

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The mercury intrusion technique accurately represents the pore structure of the granite samples.
2.  The fractal dimension calculated from the mercury intrusion data is a valid measure of pore structure complexity.
3.  The thermal treatment protocol (heating rate of 5 °C/min, 1 h hold time, water cooling) simulates relevant conditions for dry-hot rock (HDR) development [Zhao 2019, pp. 1-2].
4.  The mineral composition (quartz, feldspar, hornblende, black mica) is representative for the analysis of thermal effects [Zhao 2019, pp. 2-3].
