# Thermally Induced Acoustic Emission in Westerly Granite.

## One-line Summary
A threshold temperature of 60-70°C exists for thermally induced acoustic emission in Westerly granite, above which emission rates depend strongly on heating rate, and a thermal Kaiser effect is observed where emissions only reoccur when the previous maximum temperature is exceeded.

## Research Question
What are the characteristics of thermally induced acoustic emission (A.E.) in Westerly granite under various heating rates (0.4 to 12.5°C/min) and cyclic temperature changes, and what mechanisms control the observed behavior? [Chen 1980, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
The test material was Westerly granite, chosen because most of its properties are known. Samples were drilled from a single block, with dimensions of 1.91 cm in diameter and 3.81 cm in length, and were left to dry in air for at least one week before testing. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-1]

## Methods
- Two sets of experiments were conducted: one investigating A.E. at different heating rates (0.4 to 12.5°C/min) and another examining A.E. under cyclic temperature changes with various heating rates and amplitudes. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-1]
- Samples were jacketed in copper foil, and temperature was measured by a copper-constant thermocouple soldered to the jacket with a resolution of about 0.5°C. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-1]
- A.E. signals were detected using a heavily damped PZT transducer (Panametrics V110) with a natural frequency of 5 MHz. Signals were preamplified (60 dB), band-passed at 5-200 kHz, and further amplified to an overall gain of 86 dB. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-2]
- Processed signals were counted by a digital counter (Data Precision 5740) set at a fixed sensitivity about twice the noise level. Gated counts at 10-second intervals were recorded by a PDP-11 minicomputer. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-2]
- No attempt was made to record the amplitude or waveform of the A.E. signals. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-2]

## Key Findings
- A threshold temperature of 60 to 70°C appeared to exist, above which A.E. began to occur profusely with increasing temperature. This threshold temperature seemed independent of the heating rate within the tested range (0.4 to 12.5°C/min). [Chen 1980, pp. 1-1, 2-3]
- Above the threshold temperature, the rate of A.E. depended strongly on the heating rate. The total number of A.E. events increased by a factor of ten as the heating rate changed from 0.4°C/min to 12.5°C/min. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-1, 2-3]
- A thermal 'Kaiser' effect was observed: in cyclic heating, very few A.E. occurred at temperatures below the maximum temperature reached in the previous cycle, regardless of the heating rate in subsequent cycles. No recovery of A.E. activity was apparent when the time interval between cycles was changed from 2 hours to 2 weeks. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-1, 3-4]
- The authors suggest an equilibrium state of thermally induced cracks exists, which is a function of temperature but independent of heating rate. In slower experiments, this equilibrium is achieved by slower crack growth, associated with smaller amounts of acoustic emission. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-1, 3-4]

## Limitations
- The recorded A.E. counts are strongly dependent on the monitoring system, so cross-comparison of absolute counts between different investigations may be less meaningful. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-2]
- No attempt was made to record the amplitude or waveform of the A.E. signals. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-2]
- Multiple events were probably not recorded due to the system setup, so recorded counts are somewhat lower than the number of actual countable events. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-2]
- Systematic measurement of thermally induced crack configuration under different heating rates is not yet available. [Chen 1980, pp. 3-4]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The concept of an equilibrium state for crack extension, initially proposed for mechanical loading, is assumed to also be appropriate for rocks subjected to thermal loading. [Chen 1980, pp. 3-4]
- The rate of A.E. from a propagating crack is proportionate to crack velocity, and greater crack velocity may be associated with higher temperature gradient (greater heating rate). [Chen 1980, pp. 3-4]

## Reusable Claims
- For Westerly granite, a threshold temperature of approximately 60-70°C exists for the onset of profuse thermally induced acoustic emission, and this threshold is independent of heating rate within the range of 0.4 to 12.5°C/min. [Chen 1980, pp. 1-1, 2-3]
- Above the threshold temperature, the total number of thermally induced acoustic emission events in Westerly granite increases significantly with increasing heating rate. [Chen 1980, pp. 2-3]
- Westerly granite exhibits a thermal Kaiser effect for acoustic emission, where emissions are suppressed during reheating to temperatures below the previously attained maximum, regardless of changes in heating rate or time intervals up to two weeks. [Chen 1980, pp. 3-4]

## Connections To Other Work
- The observed threshold temperature (60-70°C) is in agreement with findings by Johnson et al. (1978) who reported Tc ≈ 75°C for Westerly granite under slow heating. [Chen 1980, pp. 2-3]
- The thermal Kaiser effect is analogous to the mechanical Kaiser effect previously observed in rocks and other materials. [Chen 1980, pp. 2-3]
- The study contrasts with Todd (1973), who suggested a critical heating rate gradient (3-4°C/min) for major cracking, whereas this study found a gradual increase in A.E. activity with heating rate. [Chen 1980, pp. 2-3]
- The concept of an equilibrium crack configuration is extended from mechanical loading studies (e.g., Kurita and Fujii, 1979) to thermal loading. [Chen 1980, pp. 3-4]

## Open Questions

- How does the time interval between successive heating cycles beyond two weeks affect the recovery of acoustic emission activity? [Chen 1980, pp. 3-4]
