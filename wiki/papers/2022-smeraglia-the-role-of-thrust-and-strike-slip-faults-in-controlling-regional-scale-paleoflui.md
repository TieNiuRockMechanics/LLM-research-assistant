# The role of thrust and strike-slip faults in controlling regional-scale paleofluid circulation in fold-and-thrust belts: Insights from the Jura Mountains (eastern France).

## One-line Summary
Structural and isotopic analysis of syntectonic calcite veins in the Jura Mountains reveals that thrust and strike-slip faults acted as high-permeability conduits for the regional-scale downward infiltration of cold meteoric fluids, with limited evidence for deep fluid upwelling.

## Research Question
How do thrust and strike-slip faults control the regional-scale paleofluid circulation within a fold-and-thrust belt, and what are the sources, pathways, and temperatures of these fluids?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Jura fold-and-thrust belt (Jura FTB) in eastern France, a foreland belt of the Western Alps [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2]. The data comprise structural and microstructural analyses, along with stable (δ¹³C, δ¹⁸O) and clumped (Δ₄₇) isotope analyses of over 120 samples of syntectonic calcite veins and slickenfibers collected from five thrust faults (Arguel, Buron, Fuans, Montlebon, Goumois) and four strike-slip faults (Buron, Vue des Alpes, Morez, Pratz) [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 2-2, 4-5].

## Methods
The methodology integrated geological field mapping, structural analysis, and microstructural analysis with optical microscopy to characterize fault zone structures and vein/slickenfiber precipitation mechanisms [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 4-5]. Carbon and oxygen stable isotopes and carbonate clumped isotopes were analyzed on powdered calcite samples to determine precipitation temperatures and fluid compositions. Analyses were performed at ETH Zürich using a Thermo Fisher Scientific MAT253 mass spectrometer coupled to a Kiel IV device, following established methods [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 4-5]. Data processing used the Easotope software with revised IUPAC parameters for ¹⁷O correction [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 4-5].

## Key Findings
1.  Syntectonic fluid circulation occurred along high-permeability networks of breccias, foliated fault rocks, and discrete fault surfaces within both thrust and strike-slip faults [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2, 11-12].
2.  At the regional scale, fluid circulation was dominated by cold meteoric fluids that infiltrated downward along faults, interacting to various degrees with the carbonate host rocks [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2, 7-7].
3.  Fault-related calcite mineralizations precipitated from fluids with δ¹⁸O values between -0.8‰ and -7.8‰ (V-SMOW) at temperatures between 8°C and 54°C, with most temperatures between 8°C and 35°C [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2, 7-7].
4.  The δ¹⁸O values of the paleofluids partly overlap those of modern meteoric waters in the region [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2].
5.  Calcite with the highest calculated δ¹⁸O paleofluid values also yielded the warmest temperatures and heaviest carbon isotope signals, indicating strong host rock buffering and possible fluid heating at depth [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2].
6.  A contribution of deep fluids from Permo-Triassic rocks cannot be excluded but is considered unlikely, possibly due to the Middle Jurassic Opalinus Clay Formation acting as a regional low-permeability barrier [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2, 12-13].
7.  This suggests two distinct fluid circulation systems: one of cold meteoric fluids in Jurassic-Cretaceous rocks above the Opalinus Clay, and another of warm, basement-buffered fluids in Permo-Triassic rocks below it [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2, 12-13].

## Limitations
*   Due to low calcite precipitation temperatures, it was not possible to determine the salinity of the paleofluids by analyzing fluid inclusions [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 4-5].
*   The study relies on outcrop samples, which may not fully represent the conditions of deeply buried fault segments [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 7-8].
*   The contribution of deep, warm fluids is inferred to be minor but cannot be completely ruled out for some samples [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 7-8, 12-13].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   A surface water temperature of 10°C (present-day average for Jura springs) was assumed for depth calculations [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 7-8].
*   A geothermal gradient of 30°C/km (modern measured gradient) was assumed for depth calculations [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 7-8].
*   Burial depths of fault outcrops were estimated using the thickness range of eroded overlying Mesozoic and Cenozoic deposits [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 7-8].
*   The isotopic composition of the calcite is assumed to reflect the primary fluid from which it precipitated, with no significant later reordering [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 7-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **δ¹⁸O (‰, VSMOW):** Oxygen isotope composition of calcite and calculated paleofluids.
*   **δ¹³C (‰, VPDB):** Carbon isotope composition of calcite.
*   **Δ₄₇ (‰, CDES):** Clumped isotope value used to calculate precipitation temperature.
*   **Temperature (°C):** Precipitation temperature of calcite derived from clumped isotopes.
*   **Fault Zone Architecture:** Presence and type of high-permeability conduits (breccias, foliated fault rocks, discrete planes).

## Reusable Claims
*   In carbonate-dominated fold-and-thrust belts, thrust and strike-slip faults can act as regional-scale, high-permeability conduits facilitating the downward infiltration of cold meteoric fluids into the shallow crust [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 11-12, 12-13].
*   The presence of a thick, regionally extensive shale unit (e.g., the Opalinus Clay Formation) within the sedimentary succession can act as a low-permeability barrier, potentially compartmentalizing fluid flow into separate shallow (meteoric) and deep (warm/basement-buffered) systems [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2, 12-13].
*   The δ¹³C and δ¹⁸O trends in syntectonic fault calcites can be used to trace the interaction between infiltrating meteoric fluids and carbonate host rocks, where decreasing δ¹³C correlates with decreasing temperature, indicating an opening of the fluid system during fault propagation [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 11-12].
*   High-permeability fault zones in foreland fold-and-thrust belts may prevent the long-term entrapment of geofluids in reservoirs by promoting leakage [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 12-13].

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study's findings of meteoric fluid infiltration along faults are consistent with observations in other carbonate fold-and-thrust belts like the southern Pyrenees and the Apennines [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 12-13].
*   The conceptual model of fault-controlled fluid circulation is compared to modern fluid circulation inferred in the Swiss Molasse Basin [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 12-13].
*   The work builds upon and references prior studies on fault-fluid interactions in specific fault zones within fold-and-thrust belts [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 1-2].
*   The research addresses a gap noted in previous work, which rarely characterized paleofluid circulation along transects covering entire fold-and-thrust belts [Smeraglia 2022, pp. 2-2].

## Open Questions
*   What is the precise volume and extent of any deep, warm fluid contribution to the shallower fault systems?
*   How does the smearing of the Opalinus Clay along fault planes quantitatively affect its sealing capacity and the compartmentalization of fluid flow?
*   How applicable is this specific two-system model (separated by the Opalinus Clay) to other fold-and-thrust belts with different stratigraphic successions?
