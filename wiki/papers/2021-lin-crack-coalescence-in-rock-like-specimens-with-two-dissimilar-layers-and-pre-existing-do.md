# Crack Coalescence in Rock-Like Specimens with Two Dissimilar Layers and Pre-Existing Double Parallel Joints under Uniaxial Compression.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the crack coalescence mechanisms in rock-like specimens containing two dissimilar layers and double parallel joints under uniaxial compression, identifying seven coalescence patterns influenced by joint and rock bridge angles.

## Research Question
How do the joint angle and rock bridge angle influence the mechanical behavior, failure processes, and crack coalescence patterns in rock-like specimens composed of two dissimilar layers with pre-existing double parallel joints under uniaxial compression? [Lin 2021, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
The study uses laboratory-prepared rock-like specimens. The specimens are composed of two dissimilar layers: Layer A (medium hard rock) made from C32.5 Portland white cement, water, and fine sand (ratio 2:1:1), and Layer B (hard rock) made from C42.5 Portland black cement, water, and fine sand (ratio 2:1:1). [Lin 2021, pp. 2-2] The physical and mechanical properties are: Layer A has a density of 2182 kg/m³, uniaxial compressive strength of 35.32 MPa, Young's modulus of 4.35 GPa, tensile strength of 2.56 MPa, and Poisson's ratio of 0.21. Layer B has a density of 2355 kg/m³, uniaxial compressive strength of 70.24 MPa, Young's modulus of 5.78 GPa, tensile strength of 5.35 MPa, and Poisson's ratio of 0.17. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3] A total of 46 specimens were made, divided into 23 groups with two specimens each. The joint length (2a) is 15 mm and rock bridge length (2b) is 22 mm. Four rock bridge angles (α: 60°, 90°, 120°, 150°) and five joint angles (β: 30°, 45°, 60°, 75°, 90°) were tested. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3]

## Methods
Uniaxial compression tests were performed using a servo-hydraulic testing machine with a constant displacement control loading rate of 0.01 mm/s. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3] The crack coalescence process was monitored using two non-destructive techniques: Acoustic Emission (AE) and Digital Image Correlation (DIC). [Lin 2021, pp. 1-1] The AE system (PCI-II) used four Nano30 sensors with a threshold of 40 dB, main amplifier of 40 dB, and sampling frequency of 1 MHz. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3] The DIC system used a CCD camera (Basler/piA2400-17 gm) with a resolution of 2456 × 2058 pixels and an acquisition rate of 15 pictures per second. Speckle patterns were applied to the specimen surfaces, and images were analyzed using GOM Correlate software to obtain full-field strain fields. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3] Crack coalescence patterns were identified by analyzing the strain field contours from DIC and verified with photos from the CCD camera. [Lin 2021, pp. 3-5]

## Key Findings
1.  The peak strength of the specimens is dependent on both the joint angle and the rock bridge angle. It decreases and then increases with increasing joint angle, reaching a minimum at a joint angle of 45°. It first increases and then decreases with increasing rock bridge angle, reaching a maximum at a rock bridge angle of 120°. When the joint angle is 90°, changes in the rock bridge angle have no significant influence on peak strength. [Lin 2021, pp. 3-5, 12-13]
2.  Seven distinct types of crack coalescence were identified: (I) Coalescence of two coplanar secondary cracks (shear), (II) Coalescence of two wing cracks (tensile), (III) Coalescence of three wing cracks and an anti-wing crack (tensile), (IV) Coalescence of two coplanar secondary cracks and an out-of-plane tensile crack (mixed), (V) Coalescence of two coplanar secondary cracks and two wing cracks (mixed), (VI) Coalescence of two wing cracks and two oblique secondary cracks (mixed), and (VII) No coalescence. [Lin 2021, pp. 12-13]
3.  The type of crack coalescence is determined by both the rock bridge angle and the joint angle. The presence of two dissimilar rock layers makes the coalescence patterns more complex compared to homogeneous specimens. [Lin 2021, pp. 10-12, 12-13]
4.  When the angles of the rock bridge and joints are smaller, cracks initiate and coalesce across both dissimilar layers. When these angles are larger, crack coalescence mainly occurs in the rock layer with lower strength, and failure is primarily caused by crack propagation in that weaker layer. [Lin 2021, pp. 1-1, 12-13]

## Limitations
1.  The study uses artificial rock-like materials (cement mortar), not natural rock. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-2]
2.  The joint geometry is simplified (straight, through-going, double parallel joints). [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3]
3.  The contact angle between the two dissimilar layers is fixed at 0° (horizontal interface). [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3]
4.  The study is conducted under uniaxial compression only; confining pressure effects are not investigated. [Lin 2021, pp. 1-1]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The rock-like material within each layer is homogeneous and isotropic. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-2]
2.  The two layers are perfectly bonded at their interface. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3]
3.  The loading is quasi-static and uniaxial. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3]
4.  The AE and DIC measurements accurately capture the crack coalescence process. [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Independent Variables:** Joint angle (β), Rock bridge angle (α).
*   **Dependent Variables:** Peak strength, Crack coalescence pattern, Strain field evolution, AE characteristics.
*   **Controlled Parameters:** Specimen size, Joint length (15 mm), Rock bridge length (22 mm), Layer material composition and thickness, Loading rate (0.01 mm/s). [Lin 2021, pp. 2-3]

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Claim:** In specimens with two dissimilar layers and double parallel joints, the peak strength under uniaxial compression is minimized at a joint angle of 45° and maximized at a rock bridge angle of 120°.
    *   **Condition:** For the specific material properties and geometry tested (Layer A: UCS 35.32 MPa, Layer B: UCS 70.24 MPa; joint length 15 mm, bridge length 22 mm).
    *   **Limitation:** Does not apply when the joint angle is 90°, where strength is insensitive to rock bridge angle. [Lin 2021, pp. 3-5]
2.  **Claim:** The presence of two dissimilar rock layers introduces new crack coalescence types (e.g., Type III, V, VI) not typically observed in homogeneous specimens, due to asynchronous crack propagation between layers.
    *   **Condition:** Under uniaxial compression with pre-existing double parallel joints.
    *   **Limitation:** Specific to the tested layer strength contrast and joint geometry. [Lin 2021, pp. 10-12]
3.  **Claim:** When the rock bridge and joint angles are large, crack coalescence is suppressed, and failure is dominated by crack propagation within the lower-strength rock layer.
    *   **Condition:** For specimens with a clear strength contrast between layers (e.g., Layer A vs. Layer B).
    *   **Limitation:** The threshold angles for this behavior depend on the specific material strengths. [Lin 2021, pp. 12-13]

## Connections To Other Work
This study builds on and compares its findings to previous research on crack coalescence in homogeneous specimens with joints, notably the nine coalescence types identified by Wong and Chau (1998) [Lin 2021, pp. 10-12] and the numerical study by Zhang and Wong (2012) [Lin 2021, pp. 10-12]. It also references work on crack propagation in heterogeneous media, such as studies on shale with bedding planes by Morgan and Einstein (2017) [Lin 2021, pp. 1-2] and artificial layered rock by Wang et al. (2019) [Lin 2021, pp. 1-2].

## Open Questions
1.  How do the findings translate to natural rock masses with more complex layer interfaces, joint persistence, and different mineralogies?
2.  How would confining pressure (triaxial conditions) affect the identified crack coalescence patterns and failure mechanisms?
3.  What is the quantitative relationship between the strength contrast of the layers and the transition angles for different coalescence types?
