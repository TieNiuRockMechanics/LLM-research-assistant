# Fluid Flow and Fault-Related Subsurface Fractures in Slate and Metasandstone Formations: A Case Study of the Jentse Geothermal Area, Taiwan.

## One-line Summary
This study uses standard open-hole and FMI logs from well JT-3 to characterize subsurface fracture structures and identify a major permeable fault system controlling geothermal fluid flow in the tight slate and metasandstone formations of the Jentse geothermal area, Taiwan.

## Research Question
How do the properties of permeable structures within fault zones control geothermal fluid conduits in tight, low-grade metamorphic host rocks, and what is the subsurface architecture of these structures in the Jentse geothermal area?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Jentse geothermal area in the Slate Belt of the northern Central Range, Taiwan. The primary data source is the JT-3 well, a directional well drilled to 1475 m measured depth (MD). The analyzed interval is from 800 to 1475 m MD in the 8.5-inch-diameter section. Data include standard open-hole logs (Caliper, Gamma Ray, Deep Resistivity, Neutron Porosity, Bulk Density, Sonic Slowness), Fullbore Formation MicroImager (FMI) logs, a static borehole temperature log (measured 271 days after drilling), drill cuttings, and two short core intervals (839–841 m MD and 978–981 m MD) [Chen 2021, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
The workflow involved processing and interpreting standard open-hole and FMI logs using the Schlumberger Techlog Software Platform. Key steps included:
1.  **Quality checks and editing** of open-hole logs, including depth matching and removal of abnormal values. Washed-out intervals (800–950 m MD) required reconstruction of NPHI and RHOB logs using multi-linear regression [Chen 2021, pp. 3-4].
2.  **Lithological interpretation** by calculating shale volume (Vsh) from the Gamma Ray (GR) log to classify rock types: metasandstone (0 ≤ Vsh ≤ 0.3), sandy slate (0.3 < Vsh ≤ 0.7), and slate (0.7 < Vsh ≤ 1) [Chen 2021, pp. 4-5].
3.  **Effective porosity (PHIE) calculation** as an indicator of open fractures, derived from neutron porosity (NPHI) and bulk density (RHOB) logs after correcting for shale volume [Chen 2021, pp. 4-5].
4.  **FMI image interpretation** to identify bed boundaries, natural fractures (open and sealed), drilling-induced tensile fractures, and breccia zones. Fracture types were classified based on their appearance and offset characteristics [Chen 2021, pp. 5-6].
5.  **Computation of fracture parameters**: fracture density (number per meter, corrected via Terzaghi method), fracture aperture (using the Luthi and Souhaite method), and fracture porosity [Chen 2021, pp. 6-8].
6.  **Zonation** of the well section into fault zones and host rock intervals based on fracture density, characteristics, and abrupt changes in bed orientations [Chen 2021, pp. 8].

## Key Findings
1.  Four fault zones were identified in the studied interval (800–1475 m MD). Fault Zones 1–3 (800–1325 m MD) are interpreted as a major permeable fault system, while Fault Zone 4 (1400–1450 m MD) is a healed, non-permeable zone [Chen 2021, pp. 9-10].
2.  The permeable fault system comprises porous fault cores (marked by breccia) and damage zones (characterized by a high density of open fractures). The most permeable interval is interpreted to be the lower part of Fault Zone 1 (940–1030 m MD) [Chen 2021, pp. 9-10].
3.  The fault system has an estimated orientation of N60–70°E and dips 70–90° to the NW, with a calculated true width of approximately 116 m [Chen 2021, pp. 13].
4.  Fracture characteristics are controlled by lithology, fault displacement, fault activity, and the present-day stress field. Metasandstone beds tend to brecciate and form porous fault cores, while slate beds develop fracture planes or remain intact. Small fault displacements favor the development of permeable breccia over impermeable gouge [Chen 2021, pp. 10-13].
5.  The static borehole temperature log shows deflections that correlate with the interpreted permeable fault zones, supporting the structural interpretation [Chen 2021, pp. 10].
6.  The direction of maximum principal horizontal stress is inferred to be NE-SW, based on the strike of drilling-induced tensile fractures, which is consistent with the orientation of natural open fractures and regional tectonic studies [Chen 2021, pp. 13].

## Limitations
1.  The study lacks age data to determine the timing of fault activity and the period when Fault Zone 4 became healed [Chen 2021, pp. 10].
2.  Quantitative differences in permeability between the permeable fault system (FZ1-3) and the healed zone (FZ4) remain unclear due to a lack of flowing test data [Chen 2021, pp. 10].
3.  The method for calculating fracture aperture may underestimate values for fractures wider than 1 mm [Chen 2021, pp. 8].
4.  In high-Vsh slate beds, the open-hole log responses to fracturing are difficult to identify due to low fracture intensity [Chen 2021, pp. 13-14].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  All dark-colored fractures observed in FMI images are treated as natural open fractures, based on the correlation with low GR and high PHIE, and the assumption that clay mineral precipitation within fractures is rare in this setting [Chen 2021, pp. 6, 14].
2.  Water saturation is assigned as one (100%) because there are no hydrocarbons in the area [Chen 2021, pp. 4].
3.  The log interpretation workflow for siliceous sedimentary rocks is applied to the low-grade metamorphic slate and metasandstone, as their occurrence is similar to tight shale and sandstone [Chen 2021, pp. 4].
4.  The Terzaghi correction for fracture density is not reliable for fractures nearly parallel to the borehole; a maximum angle of 85° between fracture poles and the borehole was used [Chen 2021, pp. 7].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Shale Volume (Vsh)**: Calculated from GR log to determine lithology [Chen 2021, pp. 4-5].
- **Effective Porosity (PHIE)**: Calculated from NPHI and RHOB logs, used as an indicator of open fractures [Chen 2021, pp. 4-5].
- **Fracture Density**: Number of fractures per meter, corrected for borehole intersection bias [Chen 2021, pp. 7].
- **Fracture Aperture**: Width of fracture planes, calculated from FMI log conductivity [Chen 2021, pp. 8].
- **Fracture Porosity**: Volume of fracture apertures per unit borehole volume [Chen 2021, pp. 8].
- **Borehole Temperature**: Static temperature log used to infer fluid flow in permeable zones [Chen 2021, pp. 10].

## Reusable Claims
1.  In tight slate and metasandstone formations, permeable geothermal conduits are associated with fault zones that contain porous breccia cores and open-fracture damage zones, rather than clay-rich fault gouges [Chen 2021, pp. 10-11].
2.  The development of permeable fault structures is favored by the coexistence of faulting with small displacements and relatively brittle host lithologies (e.g., metasandstone) [Chen 2021, pp. 13].
3.  The orientation of natural open fractures and the present-day maximum horizontal stress (NE-SW) are similar, suggesting the regional tectonic stress field controls fracture development and orientation [Chen 2021, pp. 13].
4.  Static borehole temperature logs can be used to validate the location of permeable fracture zones identified through image and petrophysical log analysis [Chen 2021, pp. 10].
5.  The combination of standard open-hole logs (e.g., GR, RT, DT, PHIE) can distinguish different intensities of fracturing in metasandstone and sandy-slate formations, even without FMI images [Chen 2021, pp. 13-14].

## Connections To Other Work
The study references the general framework of fault zone architecture (fault cores and damage zones) from Caine et al. (1996) and others [Chen 2021, pp. 1]. It connects to regional geothermal studies in Taiwan's Slate Belt (e.g., Jentse, Chinshui, Tuchang areas) and prior drilling in the area [Chen 2021, pp. 2-3]. The inferred stress direction is consistent with GPS and geophysical studies of the northern Central Range [Chen 2021, pp. 13]. The workflow is presented as applicable to other fault-controlled geothermal areas in Taiwan and similar geological settings [Chen 2021, pp. 2].

## Open Questions
1.  What is the age and duration of activity for the permeable fault system (FZ1-3) and the healed Fault Zone 4?
2.  What are the quantitative permeability differences between the permeable fault zones and the host rock/healed zone?
3.  How does the subsurface fault system connect to the surface fault traces ("I" and "K" faults) and regional hydrothermal circulation?
4.  Can the established workflow be reliably applied to predict permeable zones in future exploration wells in the region without FMI data?
