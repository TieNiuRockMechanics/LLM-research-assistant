# Experimental Investigation on the Thermal, Mechanical and Cracking Behaviours of Three Igneous Rocks Under Microwave Treatment.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how microwave treatment affects the thermal response, mechanical properties, and cracking behavior of gabbro, monzonite, and granite.

## Research Question
The paper aimed to study the effect of microwave treatment on the thermal, physical, and mechanical properties of three igneous rocks (gabbro, monzonite, and granite), specifically examining different power levels and exposure times on temperature, ultrasonic wave velocity, Young’s modulus, load bearing capacity, and crack patterns [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study used three plutonic igneous rocks from Australia and China: gabbro, quartz monzonite (referred to as monzonite), and alkali feldspar granite (referred to as granite) [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-2]. The mineralogy and dielectric properties of the constituent minerals are provided [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-4]. Specimen dimensions were 84 mm wide by 41 mm high by 30 mm thick [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-4].

## Methods
A 2.45 GHz single-mode industrial microwave system with a maximum output power of 2 kW was used [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-4]. Output power levels were 0.5 kW, 1 kW, 1.5 kW, and 2 kW, with exposure times of 30 s, 60 s, 90 s, and 120 s [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-4]. Front surface temperature was measured using an infrared camera and an infrared gun [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-4]. Ultrasonic P-wave and S-wave velocities were measured before and after treatment [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-4]. Compression tests were performed using a 300 kN testing machine [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-4]. Crack patterns were extracted using a fluorescent resin technique and observed via petrographic thin-section analysis under UV, plane polarized light (PPL), and cross polarized light (XPL) [Zheng 2020, pp. 4-6].

## Key Findings
- The single-mode microwave system effectively weakened the three rocks by generating cracks or causing melting/shattering [Zheng 2020, pp. 1-2].
- Heating rates and thermal gradients increase as either exposure time or power level increases [Zheng 2020, pp. 1-2].
- When heated at 2 kW for 120 s, the P-wave velocities of gabbro and monzonite were reduced to 44% and 51% of their original values, respectively; S-wave velocities were reduced to 34% and 62%; Young’s modulus was reduced to 13% and 27%; and load bearing capacity was reduced to 34% and 43% [Zheng 2020, pp. 1-2].
- Granite specimens were more prone to violent failures (shattering, spalling) at high power levels [Zheng 2020, pp. 1-2].
- Petrographic observations imply the dominant crack types are: intergranular & transgranular cracks in gabbro, intergranular cracks in monzonite, and transgranular cracks in granite [Zheng 2020, pp. 1-2].
- For the same total energy output, heating at higher power levels for shorter durations yields better rock weakening results [Zheng 2020, pp. 12-14].

## Limitations
- No confinement was applied to the specimens during microwave treatment, which would suppress crack generation in a field scenario [Zheng 2020, pp. 10-12].
- Specimen thickness was not large enough to perfectly simulate field conditions; standing waves due to microwave reflection at the back surface may have exaggerated heating [Zheng 2020, pp. 10-12].
- It remains unclear how many cracks were generated during the heating process versus the cooling process [Zheng 2020, pp. 10-12].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Specimens were dried in an oven at 60 °C for 48 hours before microwave treatment [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-4].
- The microwave system's water load at the end was used to simulate a field scenario where the rock to be treated is infinitely thick [Zheng 2020, pp. 2-4].
- Young’s modulus was derived from ultrasonic wave velocities assuming the rock is homogeneous and isotropic [Zheng 2020, pp. 7-8].

## Reusable Claims
- Microwave treatment at 2 kW for 120 s can reduce the Young’s modulus of gabbro to 13% of its original value and its load bearing capacity to 34% of its original value [Zheng 2020, pp. 1-2, 7-8, 8-10].
- For the same total energy input, applying higher microwave power for a shorter duration results in greater rock weakening than lower power for a longer duration [Zheng 2020, pp. 12-14].
- The dominant thermal crack pattern induced by microwave treatment is controlled by rock mineralogy and texture: gabbro (mafic, medium-grained) develops both intergranular and transgranular cracks, monzonite (intermediate, fine-grained) develops predominantly intergranular cracks, and granite (felsic, coarse-grained) develops predominantly transgranular cracks [Zheng 2020, pp. 1-2, 10-12].
- Granite is more susceptible to violent, brittle failure (shattering) under high-power microwave irradiation compared to gabbro and monzonite, which tend to crack and melt [Zheng 2020, pp. 4-6].

## Open Questions

- What is the precise contribution of cracks generated during heating versus those generated during cooling after microwave treatment?
