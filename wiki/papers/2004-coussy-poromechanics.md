# Poromechanics

## One-line Summary
Poromechanics is the study of porous materials whose mechanical behaviour is significantly influenced by the pore fluid [Coussy 2004, pp. 9-10].

## Research Question
The book aims to provide a unified and systematic continuum approach to poromechanics for engineers and applied physicists, reconciling continuum mechanics with the microscopic discontinuities of porous media [Coussy 2004, pp. 9-10].

## Study Area / Data
The work is a theoretical treatise covering a broad range of materials, including rocks, soils, gels, and biological tissues, relevant to disciplines such as geophysics, geotechnics, biomechanics, physical chemistry, agricultural engineering, and materials science [Coussy 2004, pp. 9-10].

## Methods
The approach is based on two key concepts: 1) considering the porous medium as the superposition of several continua that move with distinct kinematics while interacting, and 2) transporting the governing equations from the current configuration to an initial reference configuration related to the solid skeleton [Coussy 2004, pp. 9-10]. The methodology extends continuum mechanics and uses the energy context of thermodynamics of open continua [Coussy 2004, pp. 9-10].

## Key Findings
The book establishes a unified framework for poromechanics, extending the continuum mechanics of solids to porous media. It systematically explores coupled processes such as hydro-diffusion, subsidence, hydration, swelling, drying, shrinkage, heating, pore pressure build-up, freezing, spalling, capillarity, and cracking [Coussy 2004, pp. 9-10]. The work extends to various thermo/hydro/chemo/mechanical couplings [Coussy 2004, pp. 9-10].

## Limitations
The book presupposes some basic mathematical knowledge of tensorial analysis and differential geometry [Coussy 2004, pp. 9-10]. It is a theoretical and self-contained treatise, not a report of specific experimental data or case studies.

## Assumptions / Conditions
The approach is based on the continuum hypothesis, treating the porous medium as superposed continua [Coussy 2004, pp. 9-10]. Specific chapters assume small perturbations for linearized problems [Coussy 2004, pp. 6-7] and the postulate of local state for thermodynamics [Coussy 2004, pp. 4-6].
