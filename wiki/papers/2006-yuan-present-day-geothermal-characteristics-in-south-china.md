# Present-Day Geothermal Characteristics in South China.

## One-line Summary
This study constructs geothermal gradient and heat flow maps for South China based on 418 data points, finding an average gradient of 24.1°C/km and heat flow of 64.2 mW/m², with high values in the east and southwest controlled by tectonic activity and subsurface hydrothermal processes.

## Research Question
The main purpose is to provide valuable geothermal parameters for the exploration of marine oil and gas in South China and to analyze the relationship between geothermal gradient, heat flow, and local tectonic evolution [Yuan 2006, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study area is South China (Huanan Plate), located south of the Qinling-Dabie orogenic belt, comprising the Yangtze block and Huanan orogen [Yuan 2006, pp. 1-2]. The database consists of 418 geothermal gradient and 418 heat flow values, compiled from previous works and newly collected data from 2001 onwards, including important marine petroleum provinces like the Lower Yangtze and Sichuan basin [Yuan 2006, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
The methodology involved compiling and revising geothermal data from prior compilations and new research, then constructing geothermal gradient and heat flow maps for South China [Yuan 2006, pp. 1-2]. Statistical analysis of the 418 values was performed to characterize distributions [Yuan 2006, pp. 3-5].

## Key Findings
- The geothermal gradients in South China range from 7.82 to 162.5°C/km with a mean of 24.1°C/km [Yuan 2006, pp. 1-2].
- Heat flows vary from 22 to 220 mW/m² with an average of 64.2 mW/m² [Yuan 2006, pp. 1-2].
- The southeast coastal regions and southwestern Yunnan have relatively higher geothermal gradients than the Yangtze block [Yuan 2006, pp. 1-2].
- South China can be divided into three thermal-tectonic domains: east and southwest domains with high heat flow (>70 mW/m²) and a central domain with low heat flow (<60 mW/m²) [Yuan 2006, pp. 5-6].
- Significant anomalies of high heat flow values distribute along deep faults or plate boundaries (sutures) [Yuan 2006, pp. 1-2].
- Geothermal gradients are controlled by tectonothermal settings and significantly affected by subsurface hydrothermal activities and rock thermal conductivities [Yuan 2006, pp. 1-2].
- Heat flow values are related to tectonic activities, the epoch of the last thermal event, lithosphere extension, crust thickness, and the burial depth/thickness of high conduction layers in the crust [Yuan 2006, pp. 1-2].

## Limitations
The study notes that the geothermal data, while comprehensive, may have gaps, and the interpretations of tectonic evolution in South China vary among scholars [Yuan 2006, pp. 5-6]. The influence of subsurface hydrothermal activity complicates the interpretation of conductive heat flow in anomaly areas [Yuan 2006, pp. 6-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The present-day geothermal gradient mostly reflects tectonic activities since the Cenozoic and is not notably affected by Mesozoic crust "activation" [Yuan 2006, pp. 6-7].
- Heat flow is primarily controlled by the intensity of tectonic activity, with other factors (last thermal event, lithosphere extension, crust thickness, high conduction layers) also playing significant roles [Yuan 2006, pp. 7-8].
- The stability of the Yangtze craton and Huanan orogen is inferred from their relatively low present-day geothermal gradients and fewer earthquakes [Yuan 2006, pp. 6-7].

## Reusable Claims
- The mean geothermal gradient in South China is 24.1°C/km, which is lower than in North China (33-35°C/km) but higher than in Tarim (20°C/km) [Yuan 2006, pp. 3-5].
- The mean heat flow in South China (64.2 mW/m²) is slightly higher than the China continent average (61 mW/m²) and nearly equal to the global average (65 mW/m²) [Yuan 2006, pp. 5-6].
- Anomalies of high geothermal gradient (>50°C/km) in tectonically active zones (e.g., Tengchong-Longling, Fuzhou) are primarily controlled by intensive subsurface hydrothermal activity rather than conduction [Yuan 2006, pp. 6-7].
- Heat flow distribution in South China, characterized by high values in the east and southwest and low values in the central Yangtze block, is a result of Cenozoic tectonic activity driven by Pacific plate subduction and Indian-Eurasian plate collision [Yuan 2006, pp. 7-8].
- In extensional basins like Subei and Jianghan, heat flow is higher on the side of a fault with more intensive extension and thicker K2-E sediments [Yuan 2006, pp. 7-8].

The study builds upon and revises data from "Compilation of the Heat Flow Data in the China Continental Area (3rd edition)" [Yuan 2006, pp. 2-3].
