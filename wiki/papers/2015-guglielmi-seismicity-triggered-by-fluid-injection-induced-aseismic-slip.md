# Seismicity Triggered by Fluid Injection–Induced Aseismic Slip.

## One-line Summary
Fluid injection into a natural fault primarily triggered aseismic slip, with micro-earthquakes occurring as a secondary, indirect effect mediated by aseismic creep [June 2015, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question
The study aims to directly measure fault slip and seismicity induced by fluid injection into a natural fault to understand the poorly understood mechanisms of induced seismicity and assess associated seismic hazards [June 2015, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
The experiment was conducted in an underground facility in southeastern France at a depth of 282 m, stimulating a preexisting, initially inactive fault cutting through a carbonate formation [June 2015, pp. 1-1]. Data were collected using the SIMFIP probe, which simultaneously measured fault-normal displacements, fault-parallel displacements, and fluid pressure during a step-increasing injection of 950 liters of water [June 2015, pp. 1-2]. Additional data included injection rate, induced seismicity (micro-earthquakes and tremors), and in situ stress state estimated via hydraulic tests on preexisting fractures (HTPF protocol) [June 2015, pp. 1-2].

## Methods
The researchers injected water into a 1.5-m-long chamber spanning the fault zone while monitoring pressure, flowrate, fault shear and dilation, and seismicity [June 2015, pp. 1-2]. They used hydromechanical modeling to determine the evolution of fault permeability [June 2015, pp. 1-2]. To estimate the friction law, they approximated the sliding zone by a circular crack under quasistatic equilibrium with a complete shear stress drop and tested rate-dependent friction models [June 2015, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
1.  Highly dilatant and slow aseismic slip (~4 micrometers per second) was observed, associated with a 20-fold increase in fault permeability [June 2015, pp. 1-1].
2.  This aseismic slip transitioned to faster slip (~10 mm/s) associated with reduced dilatancy and the onset of micro-earthquakes [June 2015, pp. 1-1].
3.  Most aseismic slip occurred within the fluid-pressurized zone and obeyed a rate-strengthening friction law: μ = 0.67 + 0.045 ln(v/v₀) with v₀ = 0.1 mm/s [June 2015, pp. 1-1].
4.  The accumulated seismic moment from aseismic slip (Mw ~1.17) was far larger than that from the microseismicity (magnitude < -2), confirming slip was mostly aseismic [June 2015, pp. 2-3].
5.  Micro-earthquakes were triggered only when the estimated size of the slip zone exceeded the pressurized zone, suggesting they occurred off the pressurized zone [June 2015, pp. 2-3].

## Limitations
The experiment was characterized by a particularly low effective normal stress, which may limit the direct applicability of the findings to seismic activity at greater depths [June 2015, pp. 3-3]. The simple model used to estimate friction ignores pore-pressure gradients and viscous forces associated with fluid flow [June 2015, pp. 2-3].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The analysis assumed quasistatic equilibrium with a complete shear stress drop for the sliding zone [June 2015, pp. 2-3]. Thermal effects were ignored as the in situ temperature (12.5°C) did not vary during the experiment [June 2015, pp. 1-2]. The fault was initially inactive [June 2015, pp. 1-1].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Slip Rates:** Aseismic ~4 μm/s; Seismic phase ~10-23 mm/s [June 2015, pp. 1-1, 1-2].
-   **Permeability:** Initial 0.07 × 10⁻¹⁰ m²; Final 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁰ m² [June 2015, pp. 1-2].
-   **Friction Parameters:** μ₀ = 0.67, a = 0.0447, v₀ = 0.1 mm/s [June 2015, pp. 2-3].
-   **Stress State:** σ₁ = 6 MPa (subvertical), σ₂ = 5 MPa, σ₃ = 3 MPa [June 2015, pp. 1-2].
-   **Fault Properties:** Initial bulk modulus 10-17 GPa; Fault zone permeability 25x higher than host rock [June 2015, pp. 1-2].

## Reusable Claims
-   Fluid injection can primarily trigger aseismic slip, with seismicity induced as a secondary effect mediated by aseismic creep [June 2015, pp. 1-1, 3-3].
-   Aseismic slip in this low-effective-stress context obeyed a rate-strengthening friction law, not conditional stability of a rate-weakening fault [June 2015, pp. 2-3].
-   The transition from aseismic to seismic slip correlates with the slip zone expanding beyond the fluid-pressurized zone [June 2015, pp. 2-3].
-   Fault permeability can increase dramatically (e.g., 20-fold) due to fracture opening during aseismic sliding [June 2015, pp. 1-2].

## Connections To Other Work
The study references theoretical models of earthquake nucleation suggesting slip becomes stable as pore pressure approaches lithostatic [June 2015, pp. 1-1]. It connects to laboratory measurements of rock friction showing rate-strengthening behavior in limestone at comparable sliding rates [June 2015, pp. 2-3]. The findings are discussed in relation to natural processes like deep afterslip, slow-slip events, and tremors [June 2015, pp. 3-3].

## Open Questions
-   How do the frictional properties and mechanisms observed at low effective normal stress translate to deeper, higher-pressure conditions relevant to geothermal or hydrocarbon reservoirs?
-   What is the precise role of heterogeneous fault properties (e.g., gouge-rich zones vs. solid rock) in controlling the partitioning between aseismic and seismic slip?
-   Can the observed relationship between the slip zone, pressurized zone, and seismicity be generalized to other fault systems and injection protocols?
