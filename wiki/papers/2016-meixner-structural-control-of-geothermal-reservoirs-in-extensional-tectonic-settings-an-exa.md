# Structural Control of Geothermal Reservoirs in Extensional Tectonic Settings: An Example from the Upper Rhine Graben.

## One-line Summary
This study uses slip and dilation tendency analyses on 3D fault models from two geothermal sites in the Upper Rhine Graben to show that high-angle transfer faults are likely naturally permeable but have low potential for shear reactivation, explaining observed tracer test and seismicity results.

## Research Question
How do the interplay and geomechanical behavior of graben-parallel normal faults and high-angle transfer faults control the localization and hydraulic properties of geothermal reservoirs in the extensional tectonic setting of the Upper Rhine Graben under present-day stress conditions?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on two geothermal doublet sites located near the Eastern Main Boundary Fault (EMBF) of the Upper Rhine Graben (URG): Bruchsal (Germany) in the central segment and Riehen (Switzerland) in the southern segment [Meixner 2016, pp. 3-3]. Data include six analogue, migrated post-stack seismic sections from the early 1980s and stratigraphic logs from ten boreholes for Bruchsal [Meixner 2016, pp. 3-4]. For Riehen, data include 2D seismic reflection surveys, geological cross-sections, and stratigraphic logs from 14 deep boreholes [Meixner 2016, pp. 3-4]. Stress field constraints for Bruchsal are based on borehole breakout analysis [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5], while for Riehen, they are inferred from nearby deep wells (BS-1 and OT-2) using borehole breakouts and drilling-induced tensile fractures [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5].

## Methods
The authors developed 3D structural fault models for both sites using the Corner Point Gridding workflow in Petrel® software, meshing faults into regular triangles [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7]. They calculated slip tendency (Ts) and dilation tendency (Td) for each fault patch using the software 3Dstress® [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7]. Ts is the ratio of effective shear stress to effective normal stress, while Td is the normalized difference between the maximum principal stress and the normal stress [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7]. Analyses were performed under different stress regimes: a pure normal faulting and a transitional normal/strike-slip regime for Bruchsal, and a pure strike-slip regime for Riehen [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7]. Sensitivity analyses were conducted by varying the SHmax orientation within its uncertainty range (±20° for Bruchsal, ±14° for Riehen) [Meixner 2016, pp. 10-12].

## Key Findings
1.  At both sites, graben-parallel faults (NE-SW to NNE-SSW) show generally low slip and dilation tendencies, as they are oriented subparallel to the regional maximum horizontal stress (SHmax) [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7].
2.  NW-SE striking transfer faults inferred from geological data exhibit high dilation tendencies (Td up to 0.97-1.0) but variable, often lower, slip tendencies depending on the stress regime [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7].
3.  Under the transitional stress regime at Bruchsal, transfer faults show a bimodal distribution of Ts, with about 20% of the fault surface having high values (~0.85) [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7].
4.  In the strike-slip regime at Riehen, maximum Ts (~0.6) is found on faults striking ±30° from SHmax, while vertical faults parallel to SHmax have Ts=0 and Td=1.0 [Meixner 2016, pp. 7-10].
5.  The geomechanical behavior of transfer faults is critical for tensile failure (high Td) but non-critical for shear failure (low or moderate Ts), suggesting they are naturally permeable structures with low stimulation potential [Meixner 2016, pp. 12-14].
6.  This interpretation is consistent with the absence of immediate tracer recovery and induced seismicity during tracer tests and reservoir operation at both sites [Meixner 2016, pp. 12-14].

## Limitations
- The analysis assumes a homogeneous stress state at each site, which may not capture local heterogeneities [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5].
- Fault geometry uncertainties, especially for the inferred transfer faults, could increase result uncertainty [Meixner 2016, pp. 10-12].
- The critical stress concept and slip tendency analysis are based on the same Mohr-Coulomb criterion, meaning critically stressed faults cannot be directly computed; only near-critical conditions are assessed [Meixner 2016, pp. 12-14].
- The study does not account for variations in fault cohesion or dynamic stress changes during fluid injection [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Stress magnitudes follow the critical stress concept with friction coefficients μ=0.85 for Bruchsal and μ=0.6 for Riehen [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5, 12-14].
- Pore pressure is assumed hydrostatic with a gradient of 10.2 MPa/km for Bruchsal and 9.91 MPa/km for Riehen, plus a mean overpressure of 0.2 MPa at Riehen [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5].
- The stress field is assumed to be uniform across the modeled areas of each site [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5].
- Pre-existing fault surfaces have negligible cohesion [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Slip Tendency (Ts):** Ratio of effective shear stress to effective normal stress on a fault plane [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7].
- **Dilation Tendency (Td):** Normalized difference between maximum principal stress and normal stress on a fault plane [Meixner 2016, pp. 5-7].
- **Maximum Horizontal Stress (SHmax):** Orientation (azimuth) and magnitude are critical inputs; mean orientations are N142°E ±20° for Bruchsal and N144°E ±14° for Riehen [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5].
- **Minimum Horizontal Stress (Shmin):** Magnitude gradient of 13.2 MPa/km for Bruchsal and 17.1 MPa/km for Riehen [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5].
- **Vertical Stress (Sv):** Magnitude gradient of 23.8 MPa/km for Bruchsal and 24.9 MPa/km for Riehen [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5].
- **Fault Orientation (Strike and Dip):** Determines resolved stresses; three main sets identified at each site [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-4].
- **Friction Coefficient (μ):** 0.85 for Bruchsal, 0.6 for Riehen, defining the critical stress state [Meixner 2016, pp. 4-5, 12-14].

## Reusable Claims
- In extensional settings like the Upper Rhine Graben, high-angle transfer faults striking subparallel to the maximum horizontal stress (SHmax) are geomechanically predisposed to high dilation tendency (Td) but low slip tendency (Ts), making them likely naturally permeable but with low potential for shear reactivation under the present stress field [Meixner 2016, pp. 12-14].
- The absence of immediate tracer recovery and induced seismicity in geothermal doublets aligned parallel to graben-bounding faults can be explained by the presence of intervening, steeply dipping transfer faults that act as high-permeability hydraulic boundaries rather than conduits for direct inter-well flow [Meixner 2016, pp. 12-14].
- The sensitivity of slip tendency (Ts) to the orientation of SHmax is high, meaning that small uncertainties in stress field characterization can significantly alter assessments of fault reactivation potential, whereas dilation tendency (Td) of NW-SE striking structures is relatively stable [Meixner 2016, pp. 10-12].
- A major reorientation of the regional stress field (by ~90°) in the Miocene means that pre-existing NW-SE striking faults in the Upper Rhine Graben are now favorably oriented for tensile failure and thus represent high-priority targets for geothermal exploration due to their expected high permeability and low stimulation potential [Meixner 2016, pp. 12-14].

## Connections To Other Work
- The study references the anisotropy of flow and geomechanical behavior observed at the Soultz-sous-Forêts EGS reservoir, where fracture propagation is parallel to SHmax (N-S) and an E-W fault may impede well connection [Meixner 2016, pp. 3-3].
- It builds on the concept that faults favorably oriented for frictional failure control fluid flow and reactivation potential in crystalline and sedimentary rocks [Meixner 2016, pp. 1-3].
- The work compares structural settings with other productive geothermal fields in extensional terrains, such as the Taupo Volcanic Zone in New Zealand [Meixner 2016, pp. 1-3].
- It applies and discusses the critical stress concept for determining stress magnitudes, as established by Zoback et al. (2003) [Meixner 2016, pp. 12-14].

## Open Questions
- How do dynamic stress changes during fluid injection and production affect the reactivation potential and permeability evolution of these transfer faults?
- What is the precise geometry (e.g., dip, connectivity) of the inferred transfer faults between the wells, and how does this influence local fluid flow paths?
- Can the findings from Bruchsal and Riehen be generalized to other segments of the Upper Rhine Graben or other continental rift systems with different pre-existing structural fabrics?
- How do variations in fault zone material properties (e.g., clay content, cementation) influence the relationship between dilation tendency and actual permeability?
