# Characteristics and Formation Mechanism of the Fractures in Archaean Buried Hill: A Case Study in the BZ19-6 Block, Bohai Bay Basin, China.

## One-line Summary
Four sets of tectonic fractures formed in Archaean buried hill reservoirs of the BZ19-6 Block during different Yanshanian to Himalayan events, with high-angle shear fractures in E – W and NW – SE orientations dominating fluid flow.

## Research Question
What are the types, formation stages, and permeability impacts of fractures in the recently discovered Archaean buried hill gas reservoirs of the BZ19‑6 Block, Bozhong Sag, Bohai Bay Basin? [Dai 2021, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
- **Location**: BZ19‑6 Block, an uplifted NE‑trending tectonic ridge between Bozhong Southwest and Main Sub‑Sags, Bohai Bay Basin, eastern China. [Dai 2021, pp. 2-4]
- **Stratigraphy**: Archaean buried hill overlain by Shahejie and Dongying source rocks; main gas layer in Archaean metamorphic/igneous rocks. [Dai 2021, pp. 4-5]
- **Well data**: Wells B1 to B4. Core lengths: 156 m (B1), 591 m (B2), 42.8 m (B3), 208.4 m (B4) of reservoir material. Lithologies include gneiss, granodiorite, monzonitic granite, cataclasite, diorite, diabase veins. [Dai 2021, pp. 4-5]
- **Core and sidewall cores**: 1.2 m total core, 228 sidewall cores, 1,038 thin sections. Porosity 1–16.7% (median <5%), permeability 0.003×10⁻³ μm² to 11.8×10⁻³ μm² (median 0.01–1×10⁻³ μm²). 97% reservoirs are fracture‑pore or pore‑fracture types. [Dai 2021, pp. 5-8]
- **Image logs**: FMI dynamic images used to determine fracture openness and orientation. [Dai 2021, pp. 5-8]
- **Outcrops**: 10 outcrops surveyed in Diaoyutai and Taili, including quartz diorite, granite pegmatite, moyite, amphibolite, biotite monzonitic granite, mylonite, gneiss, granitic dyke. Zircon U‑Pb ages from representative rocks. [Dai 2021, pp. 9-11]

## Methods
- **Core description**: Macroscopic classification of fractures by mechanical type (shear/tensile), dip (high ≥60°, moderate 30°–60°, low ≤30°), and aperture/openness. [Dai 2021, pp. 5-8]
- **Thin‑section petrography**: Microscopic fracture morphology and fill characteristics. [Dai 2021, pp. 5-8]
- **Image log interpretation**: Identification of open (conductive, dark) vs. closed/resistive (bright) fractures in FMI images; cross‑cutting relationships for relative timing. [Dai 2021, pp. 8-9]
- **Outcrop fracture survey**: Measurement of strike, dip, and cross‑cutting at 10 field sites. Stress orientations derived from conjugate shear fracture bisectors (σ₁, SHmax). [Dai 2021, pp. 11-12]
- **Permeability estimation**: Equivalent fracture permeability (kf) calculated using kf = A·k = D·h³/12 (Zimmerman & Bodvarsson, 1996), with A = contact surface area, D = fracture density, h = fracture width (excluding fills). Assumed constant widths of 0.5 mm or 1 mm for unmeasured fractures. [Dai 2021, pp. 4-5, 14-15]
- **Present‑day SHmax**: Inferred from drilling‑induced fracture orientations in FMI images. [Dai 2021, pp. 15-15]

## Key Findings
- **Two fracture types**: High‑angle (≥60°) shear fractures with smooth, straight surfaces and low‑ to moderate‑angle (≤60°) tensile fractures with irregular surfaces. 96% of fractures are open (conductive). [Dai 2021, pp. 5-8]
- **Four fracture sets** identified by cross‑cutting relationships and fill:
  - **Set I**: high‑angle shear, E‑W (90° ± 20° or 270° ± 20°), partially filled by calcite; formed in Early Yanshanian transpression. [Dai 2021, pp. 9-11]
  - **Set II**: moderate‑angle mesh‑like tensile, variable orientations (NNW‑SSE, NNE‑SSW), partially filled by asphalt and calcite; formed in Middle Yanshanian extension. [Dai 2021, pp. 9-11]
  - **Set III**: high‑angle shear, NW‑SE (140° ± 15° or 320° ± 15°), partially filled by asphalt; formed in Late Yanshanian transpression. [Dai 2021, pp. 9-11]
  - **Set IV**: high‑angle shear, NE‑SW (45° ± 10° or 225° ± 10°) with some ENE‑WSW, only partly filled by clay minerals; formed in Early Himalayan dextral strike‑slip. [Dai 2021, pp. 9-11]
- **Frequency**: Set III most abundant (47%), Set II (26%), Set I (21%), Set IV (6%). [Dai 2021, pp. 14-15]
- **Permeability control**: Widest fractures are those parallel or at small angle (<30°) to present SHmax. Sets I and III (E‑W and NW‑SE) generally wider and dominate permeability. A 3 mm‑wide fracture can yield up to 101.04×10⁻⁹ m² equivalent permeability; width has greater effect on productivity than density. [Dai 2021, pp. 14-15]

## Limitations
- Low exploration maturity: limited core coverage (1.2 m total conventional core, 228 sidewall cores) from four wells. [Dai 2021, pp. 1-2]
- Permeability calculation assumes smooth fracture walls, vertical flow, no confining pressure; unmeasured small fractures assigned constant widths (0.5 or 1 mm), which may not reflect true apertures. [Dai 2021, pp. 14-15]
- Absolute timing of fractures relies on regional tectonic correlations and limited zircon U‑Pb ages from outcrop analogues; no direct dating of fracture fills. [Dai 2021, pp. 11-12]
- Fracture width/density relationship to well productivity only available for B2–B4; B1 lacks production data. [Dai 2021, pp. 15-15]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Cross‑cutting relationships in core and FMI images record relative fracture chronology. [Dai 2021, pp. 8-9]
- Bisectors of conjugate shear fractures give the maximum horizontal principal stress (σ₁) direction at time of fracturing. [Dai 2021, pp. 11-12]
- Drilling‑induced fractures parallel present‑day SHmax. [Dai 2021, pp. 15-15]
- Fracture aperture (excluding fill) is the main parameter controlling fluid flow; fill reduces effective width. [Dai 2021, pp. 4-5, 14-15]
- Outcrop fracture patterns are representative of subsurface reservoir conditions. [Dai 2021, pp. 11-12]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Fracture orientation**: Strike/dip of each set (Set I E‑W, Set II variable, Set III NW‑SE, Set IV NE‑SW). [Dai 2021, pp. 9-11]
- **Fracture type**: shear vs. tensile, determined from surface features and dip. [Dai 2021, pp. 5-8]
- **Aperture (width)**: measured in core/outcrop or inferred from FMI; ranges from 0.1 mm to >3 mm. [Dai 2021, pp. 14-15]
- **Fill material**: calcite, asphalt, chlorite, clay minerals; used to identify set and relative timing. [Dai 2021, pp. 8-9]
- **Fracture density (D)**: 11.23–131.19 m/m², mean 42.83 m/m². [Dai 2021, pp. 14-15]
- **Equivalent permeability (kf)**: computed from width and density; e.g., 3 mm fracture with D=44.19 → kf=101.04×10⁻⁹ m². [Dai 2021, pp. 14-15]
- **Angle between fracture strike and present SHmax**: controls effective width and contribution to flow. [Dai 2021, pp. 15-15]
- **Porosity and permeability of matrix**: porosity 1–16.7%, permeability 0.003–11.8×10⁻³ μm²; reservoirs classified as fracture‑pore, pore‑fracture, or porous. [Dai 2021, pp. 4-5]

## Reusable Claims
- In the BZ19‑6 Archaean buried hill, fractures account for >85% of observed discontinuities and 97% of reservoirs are fracture‑controlled, indicating that fracture networks are the primary conduit for hydrocarbon flow in this crystalline basement play. [Dai 2021, pp. 5-8]
- High‑angle (≥60°) shear fractures are predominantly open (96% conductive) and exhibit greater lateral continuity than tensile fractures, making them the key permeability enhancers. [Dai 2021, pp. 8-9]
- Four fracture sets can be distinguished based on orientation, fill, and cross‑cutting: Set I E‑W (calcite), Set II mesh‑like (asphalt/calcite), Set III NW‑SE (asphalt), Set IV NE‑SW (clay). This sequence records evolving stress fields from Early Yanshanian transpression through Early Himalayan dextral strike‑slip. [Dai 2021, pp. 9-11, 12-14]
- The width of tectonic fractures, and consequently their permeability, is greatest when fracture strike is sub‑parallel (<30°) to the present‑day SHmax orientation. [Dai 2021, pp. 15-15]
- Fracture permeability scales with the cube of width (kf = D·h³/12); thus, rare wide fractures (>1 mm) dominate flow, while abundant thin fractures contribute less. Density alone is insufficient to predict reservoir quality. [Dai 2021, pp. 14-15]
- Outcrop analogue surveys show that conjugate shear fractures with strikes matching subsurface sets I, III, and IV occur in multiple Archaean lithologies (diorite, granite, gneiss) and can be used to validate subsurface fracture models when core is scarce. [Dai 2021, pp. 11-12]

## Connections To Other Work
- Buried hill fractured reservoirs globally share similar porosity‑forming processes (weathering, fracturing); examples include the Lancaster Field (UK), Lewisian Gneiss Complex, Sousa Basin (Brazil), Bach Ho (Vietnam). [Dai 2021, pp. 1-2, 2-4]
- In the Bohai Bay Basin, earlier discoveries (e.g., JZS oil field, PengLai oil field) also exhibit complex lithologies and fracture‑controlled reservoirs; this study extends the fracture characterisation to the BZ19‑6 gas accumulation. [Dai 2021, pp. 2-4]
- The tectonic evolution model draws on published regional stress reconstructions (Kimura et al. 1990; Dong et al. 2000; He et al. 1998; Hsiao et al. 2004) to link fracture sets to specific plate interactions (Izanagi subduction, India‑Eurasia collision). [Dai 2021, pp. 12-14]
- Permeability calculation follows Zimmerman & Bodvarsson (1996) and is applied similarly to tight sandstone reservoirs (Zeng et al., 2009). [Dai 2021, pp. 14-15]

## Open Questions
- Absolute age of each fracture set is not yet constrained by direct dating of fracture‑fill minerals; current timing relies on regional tectonic stages. [Dai 2021, pp. 11-12]
- How do weathering processes and overpressured sediment loading interact with tectonic fractures to modify porosity and permeability in the buried hill? [Dai 2021, pp. 1-2, 4-5]
- What is the 3D connectivity and topology of the mesh‑like Set II fractures, and how does it contribute to overall permeability relative to planar shear fractures? [Dai 2021, pp. 8-9, 9-11]
- The influence of variable fracture fill (calcite, asphalt, clay) on long‑term production behaviour is not quantified; the impact of diagenetic sealing requires further study. [Dai 2021, pp. 8-9]
- Upscaling from single‑fracture permeability to reservoir‑scale models while accounting for stress‑sensitive aperture changes remains a challenge. [Dai 2021, pp. 14-15]
