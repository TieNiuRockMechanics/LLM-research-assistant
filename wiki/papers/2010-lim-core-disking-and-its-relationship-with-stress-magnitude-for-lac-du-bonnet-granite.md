# Core disking and its relationship with stress magnitude for Lac du Bonnet granite.

## One-line Summary
Core disking thickness in granite is inversely correlated with in situ maximum principal stress; disking initiates when the maximum principal stress normalised by the tensile strength exceeds 6.5, and three thickness categories (thin, medium, thick) plus partial disking are defined to estimate stress magnitudes from core observations.

## Research Question
What is the quantitative relationship between the thickness of stress‑induced disk fractures in 45 mm diamond‑drilled core from massive unfractured granite and the magnitude of the in situ maximum principal stress \( \sigma_1 \)?

## Study Area / Data
- **Location:** 420 Level of AECL’s Underground Research Laboratory (URL), Lac du Bonnet granite batholith, Canadian Shield [Lim 2010, pp. 1-2].
- **Rock types:** Medium‑grained grey granite and fine‑grained granodiorite, with minor pegmatite; both are massive, unfractured, with no natural joints [Lim 2010, pp. 2-3].
- **Boreholes analysed:** 54 radially oriented 75‑mm‑diameter (NQ3) MVP boreholes, nominal core diameter 45 mm, drilled to depths of 2.50‑5.30 m from tunnel perimeters in Rooms 417, 418, 421, 423 [Lim 2010, pp. 2-3].
- **Core condition:** Extensive core disking was observed in 39 of the 54 boreholes; cores from both tunnels with and without a visible excavation damaged zone (EDZ, v‑shaped notch) were included [Lim 2010, pp. 2-3].
- **In situ stress:** Known with confidence; far‑field values on the 420 Level are \( \sigma_1 = 60\,\text{MPa} \), \( \sigma_2 = 45\,\text{MPa} \), \( \sigma_3 = 11\,\text{MPa} \) (trend/plunge 145/11, 054/08, 290/77) [Lim 2010, pp. 1-2].

## Methods
1. **Core logging:** All disked and solid cores were logged for rock type, disk thickness \( t \), fracture orientation, and fracture persistence (complete disks, partial disks, no disking) [Lim 2010, pp. 2-3].
2. **Stress analysis:** Three‑dimensional elastic boundary element analyses (Examine3D) were performed using the as‑built ovaloid tunnel geometries to compute the maximum principal stress \( \sigma_1 \) along every borehole [Lim 2010, pp. 4-5].
3. **Correlation:** Average disk thickness per 100 mm interval was plotted against the corresponding average \( \sigma_1 \) for each interval [Lim 2010, pp. 7-8].
4. **Normalisation:** Disk thickness was normalised by core diameter \( D \) (45 mm) and \( \sigma_1 \) was normalised by the Brazilian tensile strength \( \sigma_t \) (9.3 MPa for granite, 10.9 MPa for granodiorite) [Lim 2010, pp. 7-8].
5. **Curve fitting:** An exponential function \( \sigma_1 = A + B\,\exp(t/C) \) was fitted separately for granite and granodiorite data [Lim 2010, pp. 7-8].
6. **Stress‑path analysis:** A 3D elastic stress‑path analysis examined the evolution of principal stresses during core drilling at a location where disking was near initiation [Lim 2010, pp. 8-10].

## Key Findings
- **Inverse relationship:** As \( \sigma_1 \) decreases, disk thickness increases. Near the tunnel boundary (\( \sigma_1 \approx 100\,\text{MPa} \)) disk thickness is < 3 mm (thinner than average grain size); at \( \sigma_1 \approx 75\,\text{MPa} \) thickness ≈ 10 mm; at \( \sigma_1 \approx 68\,\text{MPa} \) thickness reaches ≈ 50 mm. [Lim 2010, pp. 7-8]
- **Initiation stress:** Core disking initiates when \( \sigma_1 \) is approximately 64 MPa in granite and 73 MPa in granodiorite. [Lim 2010, pp. 7-8, 8‑9]
- **Normalised threshold:** Disking begins when \( \sigma_1/\sigma_t \) exceeds 6.5, regardless of rock type. At \( \sigma_1/\sigma_t \approx 6.5 \) thick or partial disks are observed. [Lim 2010, pp. 8-9]
- **Classification scheme:** Three disking categories based on \( t/D \) and associated \( \sigma_1/\sigma_t \) ranges:
  - Thin disking: \( t/D < 0.2 \), \( \sigma_1/\sigma_t \) > ~9.
  - Medium disking: \( 0.2 \le t/D \le 0.4 \), \( \sigma_1/\sigma_t \) ≈ 8‑9.
  - Thick disking: \( 0.4 \le t/D \le 2.2 \), \( \sigma_1/\sigma_t \) ≈ 6.5‑8.
  - Partial disking: distinct white lines, \( t/D \) similar to medium, \( \sigma_1/\sigma_t \) ≈ 6.5‑7. [Lim 2010, pp. 8-9]
- **Effect of grain size:** A 17% increase in tensile/compressive strength from granite to granodiorite caused an approximately 300% increase in disk thickness under similar stress. [Lim 2010, pp. 3-4]
- **Fracture mechanism:** Stress‑path analysis shows that tensile stress develops in the core centre and at the outer surface near the drill bit; failure initiates when these tensile stresses exceed the rock’s tensile strength. Disk fractures may initiate either at the core centre or at the surface, explaining the occurrence of partial disks. [Lim 2010, pp. 8-10]

## Limitations
- Results are based on a single granite batholith at a specific depth and far‑field stress state; extrapolation to other rock types or stress regimes is uncertain.  
- Only the maximum principal stress \( \sigma_1 \) was correlated with disk thickness; the effects of intermediate and minimum principal stresses (\( \sigma_2, \sigma_3 \)) were not systematically investigated.  
- The correlation relies on 45‑mm‑diameter core; scaling to other core sizes is not addressed, though normalisation by core diameter is suggested.  
- The empirical fits are descriptive and do not constitute a mechanical model.  
- The distinction between stress‑induced disking and blast‑induced fractures required careful logging; misidentification could affect the data.  

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The in situ stress state at the 420 Level is accurately known from previous, independent measurements [Lim 2010, pp. 1-2].  
- Elastic stress analysis adequately represents the far‑field stress concentration around the tunnels and along the boreholes; plastic or fracture‑induced stress redistribution is ignored.  
- Core disking fractures are purely stress‑induced and not influenced by pre‑existing joints or heterogeneities beyond grain‑size variations.  
- The average disk thickness over a 100 mm interval is a meaningful parameter to correlate with the average \( \sigma_1 \) over the same interval.  
- The Brazilian tensile strength \( \sigma_t \) is the appropriate material property for normalising the stress when characterising tensile failure.  

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent:** Core disk thickness \( t \) (mm), normalised thickness \( t/D \) where \( D = 45\,\text{mm} \).  
- **Dependent:** Maximum principal stress \( \sigma_1 \) (MPa), normalised stress \( \sigma_1/\sigma_t \) (with \( \sigma_t \) = Brazilian tensile strength).  
- **Rock type / grain size:** Granite (medium‑grained) vs. granodiorite (fine‑grained), with corresponding strength differences.  
- **Fracture persistence:** Complete disking, partial disking, no disking.  
- **Curve‑fitting parameters:** \( A, B, C \) in \( \sigma_1 = A + B \exp(t/C) \).  

## Reusable Claims
- For Lac du Bonnet granite, core disking initiates when \( \sigma_1/\sigma_t > 6.5 \). Below this threshold, no disking is observed [Lim 2010, pp. 8-9].  
- The relationship between maximum principal stress (MPa) and disk thickness (mm) can be approximated by \( \sigma_1 = 63.70 + 75.11 \exp(t/3.20) \) for granite and \( \sigma_1 = 72.73 + 77.66 \exp(t/6.31) \) for granodiorite [Lim 2010, pp. 7-8].  
- A factor of 1.17 increase in tensile strength (granodiorite relative to granite) raised the disking initiation stress from ~64 MPa to ~73 MPa and increased disk thickness by a factor of ~4 at comparable stress levels [Lim 2010, pp. 3-4].  
- Core disk thickness can serve as a semi‑quantitative indicator of the in situ maximum stress magnitude when rock type and core diameter are known, using the provided classification table [Lim 2010, pp. 8-9].  
- The mechanism of core disking is tensile failure; stress‑path analysis shows that tensile stresses sufficient to exceed the rock’s tensile strength develop both at the core centre and at the core outer surface near the drill bit [Lim 2010, pp. 9-10].

## Connections To Other Work
- Obert & Stephenson (1965) [3]: Laboratory core‑disking tests on various rocks; found that Mohr‑Coulomb shear criterion best fitted initiation, but gave no thickness–stress relation. The present study provides field‑based thickness relationships. [Lim 2010, pp. 3-4]  
- Haimson & Lee (1995) [7]: Laboratory core‑disking experiments on Lac du Bonnet granite; their data, when normalised, show a different trend from the URL field data, suggesting that scale and in situ conditions affect the relationship. [Lim 2010, pp. 8-9]

## Open Questions
- What is the quantitative influence of \( \sigma_2 \) and \( \sigma_3 \) on core disk thickness, and can a multi‑axial stress criterion improve predictions?  
- Are the normalised relationships (\( \sigma_1/\sigma_t \) vs. \( t/D \)) valid for other core diameters (e.g., 100 mm) and other crystalline rock types?  
- Can the initiation of core disking be directly linked to a critical tensile stress threshold that is independent of rock type, or does grain‑size‑dependent fracture toughness play a role?  
- How does the drilling method (e.g., bit type, rotation speed, flushing) affect the onset and morphology of disking, especially partial disking?  
- Under what in situ conditions can core disking be used as a quantitative stress indicator without complementary stress measurements?
