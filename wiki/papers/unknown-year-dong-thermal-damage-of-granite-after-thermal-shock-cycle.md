# Thermal Damage of Granite after Thermal Shock Cycle.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the thermal damage of granite subjected to cyclic high temperature and thermal shock, finding that damage increases with temperature and cycle number and is strongly correlated when calculated from tensile strength and P-wave velocity.

## Research Question
The research aims to explore a general method for evaluating the thermal damage of granite after cyclic high temperature and thermal shock by examining the relationship between damage calculated from tensile strength and P-wave velocity [Dong unknown-year, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
Granite samples with a density of 2.68 g/cm³ were collected from Linyi City, Shandong province, China. The mineral composition was measured by X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis: Feldspar (58.4%), Quartz (32.0%), Amphibole (6.1%), Mica (3.4%), Others (<0.1%) [Dong unknown-year, pp. 1-3].

## Methods
Samples were cut into cylinders (Φ50 × 25 mm). They were heated to target temperatures (250°C, 350°C, 450°C, 550°C, 650°C) at a rate of 10°C/min and held for 1 hour, then immersed in 25°C water until cooled, constituting one thermal shock cycle. Cycles were set as 1, 5, 10, 15, and 20, except at 550°C and 650°C where only 1, 5, 10, and 15 cycles were tested to ensure sample integrity. After treatment, samples were dried at 50°C for 72 hours. P-wave velocity was measured using an RSM-SY5 wave detector, and tensile tests were performed on an MTS816 system at a loading rate of 2 mm/min. For each condition, two samples were tested, totaling 46 samples [Dong unknown-year, pp. 1-3].

## Key Findings
1.  Thermal damage, calculated from both tensile strength (Dσt) and P-wave velocity (Dv), increases significantly with increasing temperature and thermal shock cycle number [Dong unknown-year, pp. 1-1, 3-4].
2.  The increase in damage is significant before 5 cycles but tends to flatten after 5 cycles [Dong unknown-year, pp. 3-4].
3.  There is a good exponential correlation between Dσt and Dv, expressed as Dσt = 0.490exp(Dv/0.921) – 0.490, with a correlation coefficient R² = 0.980 [Dong unknown-year, pp. 3-4].
4.  The primary mechanisms of thermal damage are the expansion of mineral grains and the formation of cracks (boundary and transgranular) [Dong unknown-year, pp. 3-4].
5.  The critical temperature threshold for granite damage, often cited as 400°C, may decrease to 350°C and even 250°C due to the cumulative effect of thermal shock cycles [Dong unknown-year, pp. 4-5].

## Limitations
The study notes that to ensure sample integrity, only 1, 5, 10, and 15 cycles were investigated at the highest temperatures (550°C and 650°C), limiting the full cycle range data for those conditions [Dong unknown-year, pp. 1-3].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The thermal damage calculations assume that the reduction in tensile strength and P-wave velocity directly reflects internal structural damage [Dong unknown-year, pp. 1-1, 3-4]. The thermal shock process involves rapid cooling by immersion in 25°C water after heating [Dong unknown-year, pp. 1-3].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For granite from Linyi City subjected to thermal shock cycles (rapid water quenching from high temperature). **Claim:** Thermal damage, quantified by both tensile strength loss (Dσt) and P-wave velocity reduction (Dv), increases with increasing temperature and number of cycles, with the most significant damage increase occurring within the first 5 cycles [Dong unknown-year, pp. 3-4].
2.  **Condition:** For the granite and experimental conditions in this study. **Claim:** There is a strong exponential correlation (R²=0.980) between thermal damage calculated from tensile strength (Dσt) and from P-wave velocity (Dv), indicating that either parameter can be used to reliably evaluate thermal shock damage [Dong unknown-year, pp. 3-4].
3.  **Condition:** For granite under cyclic thermal shock. **Claim:** The cumulative effect of thermal shock cycles can lower the critical temperature threshold for significant thermal damage from commonly reported values around 400°C to as low as 250°C [Dong unknown-year, pp. 4-5].
