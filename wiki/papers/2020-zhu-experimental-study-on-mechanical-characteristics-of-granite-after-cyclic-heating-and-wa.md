# Experimental Study on Mechanical Characteristics of Granite after Cyclic Heating and Water Cooling.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the degradation of mechanical properties and changes in failure mode of granite subjected to cyclic heating and rapid water cooling, finding that temperature is the primary controlling factor for damage.

## Research Question
How do the temperature and the number of heating-water cooling cycles affect the mechanical characteristics (strength, modulus, failure mode) of granite? [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
The granite samples were collected from Zhangzhou City, Fujian Province, China. The rock is gray-white, uniform, and dense, with a natural moisture content of 0.31% and an average density of 2.97 g/cm³. Its main minerals are feldspar (35%), quartz (40%), amphibole (20%), and mica (5%). Samples were prepared according to ISRM standards: uniaxial compression specimens (Φ50×100 mm) and Brazilian split test discs (Φ50×25 mm). A total of 62 samples were used. [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]

## Methods
Samples were heated to preset temperatures (250°C, 350°C, 450°C, 550°C, 650°C) at a rate of 10°C/min and held for 2 hours, then rapidly cooled in water. This cycle was repeated 0, 1, 5, 10, 15, and 20 times. "0 cycles" indicates natural cooling in air after heating. Mechanical tests were then conducted using an MTS 816 servo-hydraulic testing machine with a displacement control loading speed of 0.02 mm/min. Uniaxial compression tests and Brazilian split tests were performed. [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]

## Key Findings
1.  Basic mechanical parameters (peak compressive strength, tensile strength, elastic modulus) degrade with increasing temperature and cycle number, with temperature exerting the dominant control. [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]
2.  At the same temperature, peak strength decreases with increasing cycle number. At 250°C and 550°C, degradation after the first cycle is not obvious, but for other temperatures, the first water cooling causes a significant strength drop. [Zhu 2020, pp. 3-6]
3.  Tensile strength degrades significantly when temperature exceeds 350°C. For temperatures between 350°C and 650°C, strength drops rapidly during the first 10 cycles and then stabilizes. [Zhu 2020, pp. 3-6]
4.  The average elastic modulus decreases with increasing cycle number and temperature. The effect of cycle number is more pronounced when cycles are less than 10. When temperature exceeds 550°C, the elastic modulus drops rapidly. [Zhu 2020, pp. 3-6]
5.  With increasing temperature and cycle number, the failure mode transitions from brittle (splitting failure) to plastic (flake and particle spalling). The sample color changes from gray-white to light yellow. Temperature is the decisive factor for the failure mode. [Zhu 2020, pp. 3-6]
6.  The damage mechanism involves thermal stress from temperature gradients during heating/cooling, which generates micro-cracks. Water cooling further softens the surface and expands existing fractures. [Zhu 2020, pp. 3-6]

## Limitations
The study focuses on a specific granite type from Fujian, China. The cooling method is rapid water immersion, which may differ from other cooling scenarios. The research examines short-term mechanical effects; long-term durability or fatigue under cyclic loading is not addressed. [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The granite samples are homogeneous and isotropic for the purpose of quantitative analysis. [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]
2.  The heating rate (10°C/min) and holding time (2 hours) are sufficient to achieve thermal equilibrium in the samples. [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]
3.  The damage is primarily caused by thermal stress from temperature gradients and mineralogical changes (e.g., quartz expansion). [Zhu 2020, pp. 3-6]

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Independent Variables:** Heating temperature (250°C, 350°C, 450°C, 550°C, 650°C), Number of heating-water cooling cycles (0, 1, 5, 10, 15, 20).
-   **Dependent Variables:** Peak compressive strength, Tensile strength, Average elastic modulus, Failure mode, Sample color.
-   **Controlled Variables:** Granite source and mineralogy, Sample dimensions, Heating rate, Holding time, Cooling method (rapid water immersion), Loading rate in mechanical tests. [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]

## Reusable Claims
-   **Claim:** For granite, the degradation of mechanical strength (compressive and tensile) is primarily controlled by the maximum heating temperature, with the number of thermal cycles playing a secondary, cumulative role. [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]
-   **Claim:** Granite undergoes a transition from brittle to plastic failure behavior when subjected to cyclic heating above 350°C and water cooling, with the failure mode shifting from axial splitting to surface spalling and particle disintegration. [Zhu 2020, pp. 3-6]
-   **Claim:** The elastic modulus of granite degrades significantly with thermal cycling, but the effect of cycle number diminishes after approximately 10 cycles, while temperatures above 550°C cause a sharp reduction in modulus. [Zhu 2020, pp. 3-6]
-   **Claim:** Rapid water cooling of heated granite induces greater surface cracking and mechanical degradation than natural air cooling, due to the intensified thermal shock. [Zhu 2020, pp. 1-3]
