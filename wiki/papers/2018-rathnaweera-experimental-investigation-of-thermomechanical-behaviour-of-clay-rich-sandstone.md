# Experimental Investigation of Thermomechanical Behaviour of Clay-Rich Sandstone at Extreme Temperatures Followed by Cooling Treatments.

## One-line Summary

This study investigates the mineralogical, microstructural, and mechanical responses of clay-rich Hawkesbury sandstone subjected to extreme temperatures (25–1000 °C) under three thermal conditions: continuous heating, heating followed by slow cooling, and heating followed by quenching, with a strengthening effect observed up to 600 °C and weakening above 600 °C linked to kaolinite phase transformations and thermal cracking. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1]

## Research Question

How do extreme temperatures (25–1000 °C) and subsequent cooling methods (slow air cooling vs. rapid water quenching) influence the thermomechanical behaviour, mineralogy, and microstructure of clay-rich sandstone? [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data

The tested material is Hawkesbury sandstone from the Gosford Basin, New South Wales, Australia, of early Triassic age. Its mineral composition is 74% quartz, 12% kaolinite, 7% siderite, 4% barite, 2% calcite, < 1% smectite, and < 1% mica. The rock has a porosity of 14% and a bulk density of 2300 kg/m³. The room-temperature compressive strength, Young’s modulus, and Poisson’s ratio are 35–50 MPa, 4–7 GPa, and 0.2–0.35, respectively. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2]

## Methods

- **Sample preparation:** Cylindrical specimens of 36 mm diameter and 76 mm length were cored, cut, and ground. Thermal couples were installed in centre-drilled holes. Four replicates per condition were used. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Heating and cooling:** Specimens were heated at 5 °C/min to target temperatures of 200, 400, 600, 800, and 1000 °C, held for 24 h, then cooled by either slow air cooling or water quenching. Quenched samples were oven-dried at 60 °C for 24 h before mechanical testing. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Continuous heating tests:** Separate specimens were tested at the same target temperatures inside an environmental furnace on a servo-controlled Instron machine, without prior cooling. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- **Uniaxial compression:** Cooled samples were tested on a Shimadzu AG 9 machine at 0.1 mm/min displacement rate. Strain was recorded via digital image correlation (ARAMIS®) with 3-D cameras. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- **Acoustic emission (AE):** A PCI 2-channel system (band-pass filter 250–750 kHz, resonant frequency 500 kHz, 60 dB amplification) recorded AE simultaneously during loading of cooled samples. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- **X-ray diffraction (XRD):** Powder XRD analysis at 25 °C, 600 °C, and 1000 °C using Cu Kα radiation (45 kV, 40 mA); matrix flushing and RIR-derived constants were used for quantification. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- **Scanning electron microscopy (SEM):** JEOL 700F FEG-SEM in low vacuum mode; EDS for composition mapping and EBSD for phase mapping; 0.3 mm thin sections coated with 3 nm titanium. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]

## Key Findings

1. **Strength evolution:** Regardless of cooling method, compressive strength, Young’s modulus, crack initiation stress, and crack damage stress increase when the preheating temperature rises from 25 °C to 600 °C, then decrease for temperatures > 600 °C. The maximum strength gain at 600 °C relative to intact strength is 22.76% for slow cooling, 1.3% for continuous heating, and below intact strength for quenching. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1, 8-11]
2. **Kaolinite transformation:** At preheating temperatures between 25 °C and 600 °C, hexagonal kaolinite transforms into a fibre-like (needle-type) tangled structure, which reinforces grain boundaries and increases mechanical integrity. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1, 13-15]
3. **Weakening mechanism > 600 °C:** Progressive dehydroxylisation of kaolinite begins at ~500 °C and accelerates above 600 °C, leading to disordered meta-kaolinite and further degradation that weakens the cementitious matrix. Thermal degradation of quartz, siderite, and barite also contributes. Inter-granular and intra-granular cracks become abundant. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1, 11-12, 15-15]
4. **Cooling-rate effect:** Quenching induces greater thermal shock, creating more micro‑cracks and a lower compressive strength than slow cooling. The failure mode shifts from shear to axial splitting earlier in quenched samples (at 400 °C) compared with slowly cooled samples (at 600 °C). [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12, 15-15]
5. **Mineralogical changes:** Quartz content decreases from 74% to ~69–70% at 1000 °C, while tridymite (α‑tridymite above 870 °C) and mullite precipitate. Dolomite appears at 1000 °C, and initial calcite disappears. Kaolinite decreases from 12% to ~9% at 1000 °C. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 5-7, 11-12]
6. **Crack development:** Inter-granular cracks dominate below 600 °C (between quartz and kaolinite). Above 600 °C, intra‑granular cracks in quartz grains become prominent, alongside continued inter-granular cracking. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11, 15-15]

## Limitations

- Only uniaxial compression tests were performed; no triaxial or confined-pressure tests were included. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2]
- The experimental cooling treatments included oven‑drying of quenched samples, which removed water‑saturation effects; real UCG reservoirs may remain fluid‑saturated. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- AE and ARAMIS systems could not be used during continuous heating tests inside the furnace. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- The study focuses on a single sandstone type (Hawkesbury sandstone); findings may not directly transfer to other clay‑rich sandstones. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2, 15-15]
- Acoustic emission analysis is based on a single resonant frequency (500 kHz) with a 250–750 kHz band‑pass filter. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]

## Assumptions / Conditions

- Heated samples were held at the target temperature for 24 h, and a modest heating rate of 5 °C/min was used to minimise thermal shock during heating. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- The oven‑drying of quenched specimens at 60 °C was assumed to remove all water without altering the thermally‑induced mineral changes. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- Temperature measurements from thermal couples assumed full contact between probe and specimen via ceramic paste. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- The three thermal processes (continuous heating, slow cooling, quenching) are taken to represent different thermal zones around a UCG gasifier. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2]

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Preheating temperature:** 25, 200, 400, 600, 800, 1000 °C. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Cooling method:** Slow cooling (air, ~39–52 h to room temperature) and rapid quenching (water, ~11–15 h to room temperature). [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Heating rate:** 5 °C/min. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Holding time:** 24 h at target temperature. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- **Mechanical properties:** Uniaxial compressive strength, Young’s modulus, Poisson’s ratio, crack initiation stress, crack damage stress. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11, 11-12]
- **Mineral contents:** Quartz, kaolinite, siderite, barite, calcite, mullite, tridymite, dolomite. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 5-7]
- **Microstructural features:** Grain morphology (hexagonal kaolinite, fibre‑type kaolinite, pseudo‑hexagonal tridymite), inter‑granular and intra‑granular cracks. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 5-7, 8-11]

## Reusable Claims

1. **Claim:** In clay‑rich sandstone, compressive strength and stiffness increase when preheated to 600 °C, primarily due to the transformation of kaolinite from a hexagonal to a fibre‑like structure that reinforces grain boundaries.  
   **Condition:** Observed under slow cooling, but strength gain is reduced or overridden by micro‑cracking in quenched or continuously heated conditions. Heating rate of 5 °C/min and 24 h hold. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1, 13-15]

2. **Claim:** Above 600 °C, clay‑rich sandstone undergoes progressive weakening because of dehydroxylisation of kaolinite cement, thermal degradation of quartz and other minerals, and intensification of inter‑ and intra‑granular cracking.  
   **Condition:** Valid for Hawkesbury sandstone (quartz‑kaolinite‑siderite composition); critical temperature identified as 600 °C. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1, 15-15]

3. **Claim:** Rapid cooling (quenching) induces more thermal shock damage than slow cooling, resulting in lower compressive strength and an earlier transition from shear to splitting failure.  
   **Condition:** Based on water quenching immediately after 24 h heating; dried before testing. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 8-11, 15-15]

4. **Claim:** The crack initiation stress of clay‑rich sandstone increases with preheating temperature up to 600 °C due to fibre‑reinforced grain boundaries, then decreases beyond 600 °C as mineral degradation and thermal cracks dominate.  
   **Condition:** Derived from AE energy–stress curves under uniaxial loading. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12, 15-15]

5. **Claim:** Quartz in sandstone converts to tridymite (α‑tridymite) at temperatures above 870 °C, and the process is irreversible; associated thermal degradation of quartz creates secondary porosity.  
   **Condition:** Confirmed by XRD and SEM on samples heated to 1000 °C. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 5-7, 11-12]

## Candidate Concepts

- [[fiber-reinforced sandstone]] – Induced kaolinite fibre structure that bridges micro‑cracks and enhances strength. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 13-15]
- [[thermal strengthening window]] – The temperature range (here 25–600 °C) over which clay‑rich sandstone gains strength due to mineral transformation. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-1]
- [[critical weakening temperature]] – The threshold (~600 °C for Hawkesbury sandstone) beyond which thermal degradation overcomes strengthening effects. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 13-15]
- [[cooling-rate damage]] – Enhanced mechanical degradation due to rapid thermal contraction and shock during quenching. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 13-15]
- [[kaolinite dehydroxylisation]] – Endothermic dehydration process (600–950 °C) that produces disordered meta‑kaolinite and weakens the rock cement. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 12-13]
- [[quartz-tridymite transition]] – Irreversible phase change of quartz to pseudo‑hexagonal tridymite at high temperatures, contributing to strength loss. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12]

## Candidate Methods

- [[high-temperature uniaxial compression with digital image correlation]] – Use of ARAMIS stereo cameras to capture full‑field strain on heated/cooled rock specimens. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- [[thermal treatment with controlled cooling]] – Systematically varying cooling method (air vs. water) after 24‑h high‑temperature hold to isolate cooling‑rate effects. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- [[AE-based crack stress thresholding]] – Cumulative acoustic emission energy vs. stress used to determine crack initiation and crack damage stresses. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12]
- [[combined SEM-EDS-EBSD on thermally treated rock]] – Low‑vacuum SEM with energy‑dispersive spectroscopy and electron backscatter diffraction for phase mapping and microstructural analysis. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]
- [[powder XRD with matrix flushing and RIR quantification]] – Quantification of mineral phases before and after thermal treatment. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 3-5]

## Connections To Other Work

- Prior studies (e.g., Ranjith et al., 2012; Rao et al., 2007; Zhang et al., 2009) reported contradictory trends of sandstone strength with temperature; this work attributes the discrepancies to differing clay contents. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2]
- The observed colour change above 400 °C (yellow to reddish‑brown) is consistent with Chakrabarti et al. (1996) and linked to siderite deoxidation. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12]
- The strengthening effect up to 600 °C is consistent with Duclos and Paquet (1991) and Rao et al. (2007), who explained it by thermal expansion closing pre‑existing cracks. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 13-15]
- The critical temperature of 600 °C and weakening mechanisms align with Paterson and Wong (2005) and Glover et al. (1995), who discussed thermal cracking and phase transitions. [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 13-15, 15-15]
- The study extends the limited literature on post‑cooling sandstone behaviour, which previously covered only up to 600 °C (Brotons et al., 2013; Lam dos Santos et al., 2011). [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2]

## Open Questions

- How do confining pressures influence the observed strengthening and weakening mechanisms? (Siddiqi and Evans, 2015 noted little effect at high confining pressure) [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 1-2]
- What are the mechanical responses under fluid‑saturated, in‑situ reservoir conditions, as opposed to the oven‑dried laboratory state? [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 2-3]
- Can the fibre‑like kaolinite structure be quantified and modelled to predict strength enhancement in other clay‑rich sandstones? [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 13-15]
- How does the duration of heating (beyond 24 h) affect the extent of dehydroxylisation and mullite formation? [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 11-12]
- What is the interplay between thermally‑induced porosity (secondary porosity) and permeability evolution? [Rathnaweera 2018, pp. 5-7]
