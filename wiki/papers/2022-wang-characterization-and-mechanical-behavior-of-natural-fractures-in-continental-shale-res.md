# Characterization and Mechanical Behavior of Natural Fractures in Continental Shale Reservoirs.

## One-line Summary
This dissertation proposes a new quantitative characterization method for natural fractures in continental shale reservoirs based on fractal topology theory, establishes new models for wellbore stress distribution, and for the first time clarifies the mechanical behavior of hydraulic fractures interacting with typical natural fracture groups, leading to optimized fracturing parameters.

## Research Question
The core research problem is to determine how the diverse and randomly distributed natural fracture systems in continental shale reservoirs affect wellbore stability during drilling and the complexity of fracture network formation during hydraulic fracturing, and to develop methods for their quantitative characterization and mechanical behavior analysis to guide safe and efficient development [Wang 2022, pp. 1-5].

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Yanchang Formation Chang 7 continental shale reservoir in the Ordos Basin, China. Data sources include core samples, micro-resistivity imaging logs (FMI), outcrop observations, and laboratory experiments. Physical simulations used large-scale true triaxial hydraulic fracturing systems, and numerical simulations employed the finite element method with 0-thickness Cohesive elements [Wang 2022, pp. 15-16, 111-116, 131-132].

## Methods
The research integrated multiple methods:
1.  **Quantitative Fracture Characterization:** Applied fractal geometry and topology theory to SEM and FMI images to quantify fracture development degree, type, connectivity, and distribution [Wang 2022, pp. 23-44].
2.  **Stress Field Modeling:** Established 3D stress distribution models for vertical and horizontal wells in isotropic and transversely isotropic reservoirs containing natural fractures, solved using the finite element method [Wang 2022, pp. 56-66].
3.  **Physical Simulation:** Conducted true triaxial hydraulic fracturing experiments on concrete samples with pre-embedded paper fractures to simulate natural fracture groups [Wang 2022, pp. 111-123].
4.  **Numerical Simulation:** Developed a coupled seepage-stress field model using the 0-thickness Cohesive zone method to simulate hydraulic fracture initiation, propagation, and interaction with natural fractures [Wang 2022, pp. 124-162].
5.  **Parameter Optimization:** Used single-factor analysis and orthogonal experimental design to analyze the influence of fracturing parameters (displacement, viscosity, stress difference) and determine optimal schemes [Wang 2022, pp. 163-206].

## Key Findings
1.  **Fracture Characterization:** The Chang 7 shale reservoir has low overall natural fracture development (fractal dimension ~1). Fractures are classified into Type I (isolated ends), Type II (crossing), and Type III (T-junction) nodes. The network is dominated by Type I nodes, indicating poor connectivity. Type I, II, and III fractures are distributed at angles of 10°, 5°, and 5° to the maximum horizontal principal stress (SHmax), respectively [Wang 2022, pp. 42-43].
2.  **Wellbore Stress:** Horizontal wells exhibit higher stress concentration and are less stable than vertical wells. Natural fractures significantly affect near-wellbore stress but have minimal impact on far-field stress. Type II and III fractures have the greatest influence on circumferential, radial, and tangential stresses, making the borehole wall most unstable [Wang 2022, pp. 105-107].
3.  **Fracture Mechanical Behavior:** Hydraulic fractures do not penetrate natural fractures but open them at intersection points and turn to propagate along them. Type II natural fractures have the strongest ability to induce hydraulic fracture propagation, followed by Type III, and Type I is the weakest. This leads to the most complex fracture networks in reservoirs with Type II fractures [Wang 2022, pp. 122-123, 157-162].
4.  **Parameter Optimization:** Fracturing fluid displacement is the primary factor affecting fracture behavior in most cases. Optimal parameters vary by fracture type and well orientation (horizontal/vertical). For example, for horizontal wells in Type II fracture reservoirs, the optimal combination is displacement 0.015 m³/s, viscosity 0.001 Pa·s, and stress difference 9×10⁶ Pa [Wang 2022, pp. 205-206].

## Limitations
1.  The quantitative fracture characterization and mechanical behavior studies are conducted on a 2D plane. The 3D spatial characteristics of natural fractures and the twisting behavior of 3D fracture propagation are not fully captured [Wang 2022, pp. 209].
2.  The wellbore stress distribution model assumes steady-state conditions and neglects the effects of fluid flow, heat exchange, and chemical reactions between drilling fluid and formation during drilling [Wang 2022, pp. 209].
3.  The interaction criteria between hydraulic and natural fractures are based on existing theories (e.g., Blanton, Warpinski) and may require further validation for the specific continental shale conditions [Wang 2022, pp. 127-140].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Rock is assumed to be a linear elastic, isotropic or transversely isotropic material [Wang 2022, pp. 57, 64, 132].
2.  The reservoir is impermeable, and pore pressure is hydrostatic in the stress distribution models [Wang 2022, pp. 57, 64].
3.  The influence of seepage field and temperature changes on the stress field is ignored in the wellbore stress models [Wang 2022, pp. 57, 64].
4.  In the hydraulic fracturing numerical model, the process is quasi-static and isothermal, and the fracturing fluid is incompressible [Wang 2022, pp. 132-133].
5.  Natural fractures are simplified as frictional interfaces with zero tensile strength [Wang 2022, pp. 133].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** In continental shale reservoirs with natural fractures dominated by Type I nodes. **Claim:** The overall fracture network connectivity is poor, and the reservoir's fractal characteristics are weak, with a fractal dimension close to 1 [Wang 2022, pp. 42-43].
2.  **Condition:** During hydraulic fracturing in shale reservoirs. **Claim:** Hydraulic fractures will not penetrate pre-existing natural fractures but will open them at intersection points and propagate along the opened fracture branches, with Type II fractures inducing the strongest diversion [Wang 2022, pp. 122-123, 162].
3.  **Condition:** For optimizing hydraulic fracturing in horizontal wells targeting Type II natural fracture zones. **Claim:** Fracturing fluid displacement is the primary factor controlling fracture network complexity, and a high displacement (e.g., 0.015 m³/s) combined with low viscosity (e.g., 0.001 Pa·s) and a specific stress difference (e.g., 9×10⁶ Pa) yields the most complex network and longest fractures [Wang 2022, pp. 193-195, 205-206].

## Connections To Other Work
This work builds upon and extends several research areas:
-   **Fracture Characterization:** It advances beyond qualitative classification (e.g., by Nelson, Wang Yuncheng) by introducing a quantitative method combining fractal and topological theories [Wang 2022, pp. 12-16].
-   **Stress Modeling:** It improves upon isotropic models (e.g., Thiercelin) by developing models for transversely isotropic shale and incorporating the effect of discrete natural fractures [Wang 2022, pp. 46-56].
-   **Fracture Interaction:** It validates and applies existing interaction criteria (e.g., Blanton, Warpinski, Renshaw) specifically for the context of continental shale with grouped natural fractures [Wang 2022, pp. 127-140].
