# Study on the Mechanical Properties of Coarse-Grained and Fine-Grained Granite Under High Temperature Triaxial Stress.

## One-line Summary
This study compares the thermal and mechanical properties of deep coarse-grained granite and shallow fine-grained granite under high-temperature (100-400°C) triaxial stress, finding that coarse-grained granite exhibits greater thermal expansion and lower elastic modulus due to its pre-existing fractures and extreme heterogeneity.

## Research Question
To reveal the true characteristics of deep granite to guide Hot Dry Rock (HDR) geothermal development, what are the differences in thermal and mechanical properties between deep coarse-grained granite and shallow fine-grained granite under high-temperature triaxial stress?

## Study Area / Data
- **Coarse-grained granite**: Sampled from Luya Mountain, Shanxi Province, China. This granite formed deep underground (~10 km) approximately 1.9 billion years ago and was later uplifted, representing true deep granite characteristics [Yin 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Fine-grained granite**: Sampled from Pingyi, Shandong Province, China (commercial name "Luhui granite"). This represents shallow, fine-grained granite [Yin 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Sample preparation**: Both types were processed into standard cylindrical specimens (Φ50 mm × 100 mm) [Yin 2020, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Equipment**: A self-developed 600°C high-temperature high-pressure rock triaxial testing machine at Taiyuan University of Technology [Yin 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Procedure**:
    1. Apply initial axial (σ_a) and confining pressure (σ_c) to 4 MPa.
    2. Heat to target temperatures (100, 150, 200, 250, 300, 350, 400°C) at a rate of 20°C/h and hold for 2 hours.
    3. Maintain confining pressure, increase axial pressure to 30 MPa in 2 MPa increments to measure elastic modulus.
    4. Use two grating external rulers to continuously measure axial displacement during heating to determine thermal strain and thermal expansion coefficient [Yin 2020, pp. 2-3].
- **Calculations**:
    - Thermal expansion coefficient (α) calculated using formula (1): α = (S_t2 - S_t1) / (t2 - t1) [Yin 2020, pp. 3-5].
    - Elastic modulus determined from the slope of the stress-strain curve within 10%-30% of the uniaxial compressive strength [Yin 2020, pp. 3-5].
- **Micro-observation**: Thin sections of coarse-grained granite were observed using a DMRX Leica polarizing microscope to analyze mineral composition and microstructure [Yin 2020, pp. 5-7].

## Key Findings
1.  **Thermal Expansion**: The thermal expansion coefficient of coarse-grained granite increases linearly with temperature (100-400°C). On average, it is 1.52 times that of fine-grained granite, with the maximum difference occurring at 400°C [Yin 2020, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
2.  **Elastic Modulus**:
    - For coarse-grained granite, elastic modulus first increases slowly (100-300°C) then decreases rapidly (300-400°C). The threshold temperature is ~300°C [Yin 2020, pp. 1-2, 3-5].
    - The elastic modulus of fine-grained granite is 1.4 to 2.6 times that of coarse-grained granite, and this difference increases with rising temperature and confining pressure [Yin 2020, pp. 1-2, 3-5].
3.  **Microstructural Cause**: Coarse-grained granite contains numerous pre-existing fractures and exhibits extreme heterogeneity due to its complex mineral composition (amphibole, plagioclase, etc.) and larger crystal grain sizes (up to 8 mm). This leads to greater thermal strain and more deteriorated mechanical properties under high temperature and pressure [Yin 2020, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
4.  **Engineering Implication**: The properties of coarse-grained granite result in higher permeability under high temperature and pressure, providing a favorable geological foundation for efficiently and cost-effectively constructing artificial reservoirs during HDR geothermal development [Yin 2020, pp. 1-2, 5-7].

## Limitations
- The study is limited to a temperature range of 100-400°C and a specific confining pressure condition (initial 4 MPa).
- The conclusions are based on two specific granite types from China (Luya Mountain and Shandong). Generalizability to other granite types is not confirmed.
- The elastic modulus measurement method (10-30% of UCS range) is a specific approach; other methods might yield different absolute values.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The coarse-grained granite from Luya Mountain is assumed to be representative of deep granite formed under high-temperature, high-pressure conditions [Yin 2020, pp. 2-3].
- The experimental procedure (heating rate, hold time) is assumed to allow for thermal equilibrium within the samples.
- The thermal stress model (Equation 2) assumes two adjacent mineral grains with different elastic moduli and thermal expansion coefficients [Yin 2020, pp. 5-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables**: Temperature (100-400°C), Confining Pressure (σ_c), Granite Type (coarse-grained vs. fine-grained).
- **Dependent Variables**: Axial Thermal Strain, Thermal Expansion Coefficient (α), Elastic Modulus (E).
- **Controlled Variables**: Sample dimensions (Φ50 mm × 100 mm), Initial stress state (σ_a = σ_c = 4 MPa), Heating rate (20°C/h), Hold time (2 hours).

## Reusable Claims
- Under triaxial stress, the thermal expansion coefficient of coarse-grained granite increases linearly with temperature in the 100-400°C range, and is on average 1.52 times greater than that of fine-grained granite [Yin 2020, pp. 1-2, 3-5].
- The elastic modulus of coarse-grained granite exhibits a non-monotonic response to temperature, with a threshold temperature of approximately 300°C, below which it increases and above which it decreases rapidly [Yin 2020, pp. 3-5].
- The presence of numerous pre-existing fractures and extreme mineralogical heterogeneity in coarse-grained granite leads to more severe thermal cracking and mechanical degradation under high-temperature conditions compared to fine-grained granite [Yin 2020, pp. 5-7].
- The difference in elastic modulus between fine-grained and coarse-grained granite is amplified by increases in both temperature and confining pressure [Yin 2020, pp. 1-2, 3-5].
