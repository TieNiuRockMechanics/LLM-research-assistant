# Neogene-Quaternary Normal and Transfer Faults Controlling Deep-Seated Geothermal Systems: The Case of San Agustín del Maíz (central Trans-Mexican Volcanic Belt, México).

## One-line Summary
The study identifies that the intersection of WSW-striking normal faults and NNW-striking transfer faults, active since the Pliocene under a NW-SE extensional regime, creates permeable rock volumes that control the ascent of deep-seated geothermal fluids to the surface in the San Agustín del Maíz area.

## Research Question
How can deep-seated geothermal systems buried under thick sediments be investigated from the surface, and what is the tectonic control on the circulation of geothermal fluids in the San Agustín del Maíz area? [Olvera 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study area is San Agustín del Maíz, located in the central sector of the Trans-Mexican Volcanic Belt (TMVB), a WNW-ESE oriented Neogene-Quaternary volcanic arc [Olvera 2020, pp. 2-3]. The area is characterized by an extensional tectonic depression filled with late Miocene-Holocene fluvio-lacustrine sediments unconformably resting on volcanic rocks [Olvera 2020, pp. 1-2]. Data were integrated from satellite images, topographic maps, aerial photographs, digital elevation models (15 m resolution), field measurements of hydrothermal manifestations (location and temperature), and structural/kinematic analysis of fault surfaces exposed in bedrock [Olvera 2020, pp. 3-3].

## Methods
The methodology integrated several datasets: 1) Interpretation of remote sensing data to define morpho-structural lineaments; 2) Morpho-structural analysis of Quaternary monogenetic volcanic vents using criteria from Paulsen and Wilson (2010) to assess tectonic control; 3) Detailed mapping and temperature measurement of hydrothermal manifestations using GPS and a mercury thermometer; 4) Structural and kinematic analysis of fault surfaces (striations, mineral fibers, fault-steps) processed with FaultKin 8 software [Olvera 2020, pp. 3-3].

## Key Findings
- Two main, almost orthogonal fault systems are present: WSW-striking normal faults and NNW-striking transfer faults, both active since at least the Pliocene under a NW-SE extensional regime [Olvera 2020, pp. 2-3, 8-9].
- Permeable rock volumes with enhanced permeability are located at the intersection of these fault systems, which channel deep geothermal fluids to the surface [Olvera 2020, pp. 1-2, 13-16].
- Thermal spring temperatures (30° to 92°C) are highest (>70°C) at the intersections of the two fault trends, indicating these are the most efficient structural channels for fluid flow [Olvera 2020, pp. 5-7, 9-10].
- Silica sinter deposits (opal-cristobalite-tridymite) are aligned along the main fault trends and show deformation (e.g., fissured ridges, rotated beds) linked to ongoing tectonic activity [Olvera 2020, pp. 5-7, 10-13].
- The kinematic evolution involves three deformational events (T1, T2, T3), with the NNW-striking faults acting as transfer faults accommodating the regional extension [Olvera 2020, pp. 7-9].

## Limitations
- Fault scarps are not continuously exposed; analysis relies on limited outcrops in road cuts, river incisions, and quarries [Olvera 2020, pp. 3-5].
- The age of the silica sinter deposits cannot be precisely defined, though the presence of pre-Hispanic pottery in a breccia indicates a hydrothermal eruption occurred after the 14th century [Olvera 2020, pp. 10-13].
- The study is primarily based on surface and near-surface data; direct evidence of the deep geothermal reservoir is inferred [Olvera 2020, pp. 1-2].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Measured striations on fault surfaces are assumed to be parallel to the main shear stress direction, allowing for stress analysis [Olvera 2020, pp. 2-3].
- The distribution and temperature of surface thermal manifestations are indicative of the underlying structural controls on fluid flow at depth [Olvera 2020, pp. 1-2].
- The regional stress field has been a persistent NW-SE extension since the Pliocene, overprinting earlier tectonic events [Olvera 2020, pp. 8-9].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Fault orientation: WSW-striking (normal faults) and NNW-striking (transfer faults) [Olvera 2020, pp. 2-3].
- Geothermal fluid temperature: Ranges from 30° to 92°C at surface springs [Olvera 2020, pp. 5-7].
- Permeability: Enhanced in damaged rock volumes at fault intersections [Olvera 2020, pp. 1-2].
- Tectonic regime: ~NW-SE extension active since Pliocene [Olvera 2020, pp. 2-3].
- Sinter mineralogy: Opal-cristobalite-tridymite, indicating subsurface temperatures >175°C [Olvera 2020, pp. 5-7].

## Reusable Claims
- In extensional tectonic settings, the intersection between normal faults and transfer faults creates highly permeable rock volumes that are optimal targets for geothermal fluid upflow [Olvera 2020, pp. 1-2, 13-16].
- The alignment and temperature distribution of thermal springs can be used as a surface indicator to infer the geometry of controlling faults at depth, especially when the geothermal system is buried under sediments [Olvera 2020, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
- Silica sinter deposits, analogous to travertine, can serve as morpho-tectonic indicators of active fault-controlled hydrothermal circulation [Olvera 2020, pp. 9-10].
- The kinematic evolution of fault systems in the central TMVB records a shift from Basin and Range-style tectonics to a regime of NW-SE extension with superimposed vertical uplift [Olvera 2020, pp. 8-9].

## Connections To Other Work
- The study applies the concept of "travitonics" (using travertine as a tectonic indicator) to silica sinter deposits [Olvera 2020, pp. 9-10].
- It builds on models of fluid flow in fault-controlled damaged rock volumes (e.g., Curewitz and Karson, 1997; Rowland and Sibson, 2004) [Olvera 2020, pp. 1-2].
- The tectonic framework references the evolution of the Basin and Range province and the Trans-Mexican Volcanic Belt (e.g., Suter et al., 1991, 2001; Ego and Ansan, 2002) [Olvera 2020, pp. 2-3, 8-9].
- The volcanic alignment analysis follows the criteria of Paulsen and Wilson (2010) [Olvera 2020, pp. 3-3].

## Open Questions
- What is the precise age and duration of the silica sinter deposition events?
- What is the exact geometry and connectivity of the fault zones at depths greater than those exposed at the surface?
- How does the local seismicity (M ≤ 5) specifically influence the maintenance of fracture permeability over time?
- Can the proposed conceptual model be directly applied to other geothermal areas within the TMVB with similar tectonic settings?
