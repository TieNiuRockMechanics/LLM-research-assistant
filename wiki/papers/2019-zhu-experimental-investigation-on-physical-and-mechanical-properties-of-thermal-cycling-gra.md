# Experimental Investigation on Physical and Mechanical Properties of Thermal Cycling Granite by Water Cooling.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how heating to 500°C followed by water cooling for 1 and 30 cycles affects the physical and mechanical properties of granite, finding significant property degradation due to microcrack development.

## Research Question
What are the effects of cyclic heating and water-cooling treatment on the physical and mechanical properties of granite, and what are the underlying microscopic change mechanisms?

## Study Area / Data
The study used gray, fine-grained granite blocks collected from a mine in Suizhou city, Hubei province, China [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3]. Cylindrical specimens with a diameter of 50 mm and a height of 100 mm were machined according to ISRM methods [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3]. The natural density was 2.603 g/cm³, and the average longitudinal wave velocity was 3770 m/s [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3]. Mineral composition was 77.68% feldspar, 10.58% quartz, 6.28% biotite, 3.24% chlorite, and 2.22% amphibole [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
Specimens were dried at 105°C for 24 hours, then heated in a furnace to predetermined temperatures (150, 300, 400, 500°C) at a rate of 5°C/min and held for 2 hours [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3]. They were then cooled by immersion in a water bath with distilled water [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3]. This process constituted one thermal cycle; half the specimens underwent 30 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3]. Physical properties (mass, volume, density, P-wave velocity) were measured after each cycle [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3]. Mechanical properties were tested via uniaxial compression at a displacement rate of 0.3 mm/min [Zhu 2019, pp. 3-4]. Microstructural analysis was performed using a Quanta250 SEM on thin sections enlarged 40 times [Zhu 2019, pp. 3-4].

## Key Findings
- At 500°C, granite volume increased by 1.73% and 2.55% after 1 and 30 cycles, respectively [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2].
- At 500°C, mass decreased by 0.16% and 0.31% after 1 and 30 cycles, respectively [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2].
- At 500°C, density decreased by 1.86% and 2.78% after 1 and 30 cycles, respectively [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2].
- Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and elastic modulus (E) decreased with increasing temperature for both 1 and 30 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2, 4-5].
- At 500°C, UCS decreased by 33.37% and 78.91% after 1 and 30 cycles, respectively [Zhu 2019, pp. 4-5].
- At 500°C, E decreased by 38.82% and 88.42% after 1 and 30 cycles, respectively [Zhu 2019, pp. 4-5].
- A yield platform appeared in stress-strain curves above 300°C, indicating increased ductility [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2, 3-4].
- P-wave velocity decreased with temperature and cycle number; most decrease occurred within the first 5 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 3-4].
- The normalized P-wave velocity showed a linear relationship with normalized UCS and E after 1 cycle, but an exponential relationship after 30 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 9-10].
- SEM observation revealed that property degradation is caused by the generation and development of microcracks, with more microcracks observed after 30 cycles than after 1 cycle [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
- Water cooling induces more severe thermal damage and microcracks compared to natural cooling [Zhu 2019, pp. 5-7, 9-10].

## Limitations
- The study used only one type of granite from a specific location [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- The maximum temperature was 500°C, and the maximum number of cycles was 30 [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- The cooling method was immersion in a water bath, which may not perfectly replicate all geothermal reservoir conditions [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- The study focused on uniaxial compression; other stress states (e.g., triaxial) were not investigated [Zhu 2019, pp. 3-4].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The heating rate of 5°C/min was assumed to be modest and consistent with previous studies [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- Specimens were held at the target temperature for 2 hours to ensure uniform heating [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- The water cooling process was assumed to represent rapid thermal shock relevant to geothermal energy extraction [Zhu 2019, pp. 9-10].
- The initial drying process at 105°C for 24 hours removed all natural moisture content [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-3].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Thermal Cycle Number**: 1 and 30 cycles.
- **Temperature**: 20°C (control), 150°C, 300°C, 400°C, 500°C.
- **Cooling Method**: Water cooling (immersion in distilled water).
- **Physical Properties**: Volume (V), mass (m), density (q), P-wave velocity (Vp).
- **Mechanical Properties**: Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS), elastic modulus (E), peak strain (ε).
- **Microstructural Properties**: Microcrack density (qf), thermal damage variables (DVT, DET).

## Reusable Claims
- Cyclic heating and water cooling to 500°C causes granite volume to increase by up to 2.55% and density to decrease by up to 2.78% after 30 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2, 3-4].
- The uniaxial compressive strength and elastic modulus of granite degrade significantly with increasing temperature and cycle number under water cooling, with reductions exceeding 78% and 88% respectively at 500°C after 30 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 4-5].
- The degradation of granite's physical and mechanical properties after thermal cycling with water cooling is primarily caused by the generation and development of internal microcracks, as confirmed by SEM observation [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
- Water cooling induces more severe thermal damage and microcracking in granite compared to natural air cooling, leading to greater property deterioration [Zhu 2019, pp. 5-7, 9-10].
- The relationship between normalized P-wave velocity and normalized mechanical properties (UCS, E) changes from linear after one thermal cycle to exponential after 30 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 9-10].
