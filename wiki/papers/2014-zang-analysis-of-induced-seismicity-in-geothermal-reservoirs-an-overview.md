# Analysis of Induced Seismicity in Geothermal Reservoirs – An Overview.

## One-line Summary

This paper provides a comprehensive overview of the analysis of fluid-induced seismicity in Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) across various tectonic settings, focusing on the relationship between reservoir hydraulics, in situ stress, and the occurrence of larger magnitude events.

## Research Question

What are the key reservoir parameters and physical mechanisms controlling the rate, magnitude, and spatiotemporal distribution of seismicity induced during the development and operation of Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS)?

## Study Area / Data

This study analyzes induced seismicity data from multiple global geothermal and reference sites investigated within the framework of the European Geothermal Engineering Integrating Mitigation of Induced Seismicity in Reservoirs (GEISER) project. Data includes seismic event catalogs, hydraulic parameters, and geological context.

- **European Geothermal Sites:** Soultz-sous-Forêts (France, Lower Rhine Graben), Basel (Switzerland, Upper Rhine Graben), Groß Schönebeck (Germany, North German Basin), Rosmanowes (Cornwall, UK), and Icelandic test sites [Zang 2014, pp. 3-3].
- **Non-European Geothermal Sites:** The Geysers (California, USA), Berlin (El Salvador), Cooper Basin (Australia), Paralana (Australia), Bouillante (Guadeloupe) [Zang 2014, pp. 3-3].
- **Reference Sites:** KTB deep drilling site (Germany, scientific injection) and induced seismicity related to gas production in The Netherlands [Zang 2014, pp. 3-3].
- **Supplementary Data:** For comparative context, data from wastewater disposal wells, hydraulic fracturing operations, and KTB scientific drilling are also included (Fig. 1, Table 2) [Zang 2014, pp. 3-3].

## Methods

Various analytical techniques were employed to investigate the seismic response to fluid injection:

- **Seismic Network Analysis:** Comparison of detection and location capabilities for different configurations, including surface versus down-hole borehole networks. Analysis of the impact of velocity models and the attenuation effects of near-surface layers [Zang 2014, pp. 3-5].
- **Hypocenter Location and Event Cataloging:** Utilization of 1D and 3D velocity models for precise event location. Catalog completeness and sensitivity are assessed, e.g., for Basel, minimum magnitude (Mw) was 0.9 for surface data vs. 0.1 for borehole data [Zang 2014, pp. 5-5].
- **Source Mechanism and Moment Tensor Inversion:** Full waveform moment tensor analysis to characterize the isotropic (tensile) versus deviatoric (shear) components of seismic events [Zang 2014, pp. 8-9]. High-resolution relative location and focal mechanism determination from high-quality borehole data [Zang 2014, pp. 7-8].
- **Gutenberg-Richter (b-value) Analysis:** Spatial and temporal mapping of b-values to link event size distributions to the state of stress [Zang 2014, pp. 7-8].
- **Deterministic and Probabilistic Hazard Modeling:** Utilization of fault-scaling relationships [Zang 2014, pp. 8-9], coupled hydro-mechanical discrete fracture network models to simulate dynamic fracture growth and seismicity [Zang 2014, pp. 9-10], and a hybrid approach known as Forward Induced Seismic Hazard Assessment (FISHA) [Zang 2014, pp. 9-10].
- **Statistical Methods:** Cross-correlation methods for improving event catalogs [Zang 2014, pp. 5-5].

## Key Findings

- **Relationship between Injected Volume and Maximum Magnitude:** The maximum observed magnitude (Mobs max) of induced seismicity increases with the total volume of injected fluid. Compared to long-term wastewater disposal, short-term EGS stimulations generally involve less fluid volume and produce smaller maximum magnitudes [Zang 2014, pp. 3-3].
- **Influence of Stress Regime and Rock Type on Seismic Cloud Orientation:** The in situ stress regime exerts a primary control on the orientation of fracture growth and the seismic cloud. In normal faulting regimes, seismic clouds are sub-vertical, whereas in thrust faulting regimes, they are sub-horizontal, as observed at Soultz-sous-Forêts and Cooper Basin, respectively [Zang 2014, pp. 10-11]. Induced fracture damage zones are expected to be narrower in sedimentary rocks than in crystalline rocks, potentially explaining the lower seismicity rates in sedimentary environments [Zang 2014, pp. 13-13].
- **Spatiotemporal Properties of Larger Magnitude Events (LME):** Larger magnitude events (LME) frequently occur after shut-in and at the periphery of the seismic cloud, distant from the injection point. This has been documented in Basel, Soultz-sous-Forêts, Paralana, and Berlin (El Salvador) [Zang 2014, pp. 7-8].
- **Spatial Variability of Source Parameters:** Near the injection well ("near-field"), high pore pressures correlate with events having significant isotropic (tensile) moment tensor components, high Gutenberg-Richter b-values, and low stress drops. Farther from the well ("far-field"), events are predominantly shear in character with lower b-values and higher stress drops, indicating a higher probability for the occurrence of LME [Zang 2014, pp. 13-13, 8-9].
- **Kaiser Effect in Crystalline Reservoirs:** During multi-stage stimulations in crystalline rock, seismicity is absent until the maximum stress level achieved during previous stimulations is exceeded [Zang 2014, pp. 11-11].
- **Reservoir Injectivity:** EGS sites are characterized by a wide range of injectivity values (ratio of max inflow to max wellhead pressure), which are generally higher compared to wastewater disposal wells where creating new fractures is not the primary goal [Zang 2014, pp. 13-13].
- **Seismic Hazard Assessment:** A hybrid Forward Induced Seismic Hazard Assessment (FISHA) approach, which couples deterministic fracture growth models with probabilistic computations, is recommended over traditional probabilistic methods alone [Zang 2014, pp. 13-14].

## Limitations

- Seismicity catalogs are network-dependent; event detection, location, and magnitude are highly sensitive to network geometry, velocity model quality, and location technique [Zang 2014, pp. 3-5].
- The observation period for geothermal reservoirs (decades) is too short to reliably estimate the maximum possible earthquake magnitude (Mmax) for probabilistic hazard assessment, making Mobs max a lower limit value only [Zang 2014, pp. 8-9].
- The scalar injectivity I (ratio of maximum inflow to maximum wellhead pressure) is a simplification and not a substitute for a proper injectivity index (II), which requires bottom-hole flow condition and far-field reservoir pressure corrections that are often unavailable [Zang 2014, pp. 5-6].
- Results are based on a selection of canonical geothermal sites, and the lack of seismicity at some sites (e.g., Bouillante, Groß Schönebeck) complicates a direct one-to-one correlation between hydraulic parameters and seismic response [Zang 2014, pp. 2-2].

## Assumptions / Conditions

- The causal relationship is based on Hubbert and Rubey (1959), where fluid injection into critically stressed faults with well-oriented failure planes weakens the crust and triggers seismicity within that zone [Zang 2014, pp. 10-11].
- Induced seismicity in both crystalline and sedimentary reservoirs can be described by a Gutenberg-Richter magnitude-frequency distribution [Zang 2014, pp. 6-7].
- The Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion and an inverse relationship between stress drop and b-value (Scholz, 1968) are assumed in models linking transient pressure fields to seismicity [Zang 2014, pp. 9-10].
- The direction of fracture propagation in both sedimentary and crystalline rocks is controlled by the orientation of the maximum horizontal compressive stress [Zang 2014, pp. 12-13].

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Mobs max:** Maximum observed seismic magnitude (local or moment magnitude).
- **Pmax:** Maximum wellhead pressure (MPa).
- **Qmax:** Maximum in-flow rate (l/s).
- **Vtot:** Total volume of injected fluid (m³).
- **Injectivity (I):** Ratio of Qmax to Pmax [Zang 2014, pp. 5-6].
- **b-value:** Slope of the Gutenberg-Richter magnitude-frequency distribution, used as a proxy for differential stress [Zang 2014, pp. 8-9].
- **Stress Regime:** Normal faulting (NF), strike-slip (SS), or thrust faulting (TF) conditions [Zang 2014, pp. 5-6].
- **Reservoir Depth:** Typically 2–5 km for EGS sites, with no direct relationship between depth and maximum magnitude observed [Zang 2014, pp. 5-6].
- **Vp/Vs Ratio:** Seismic velocity ratio used to track fluid pathways and structural changes in the reservoir over time [Zang 2014, pp. 5-6].

## Reusable Claims

- {“The total extension of the seismic network and the configuration of the seismic stations influence the completeness and accuracy of the event locations. Networks that include single borehole sensors or strings of deep borehole three-component sensors can detect and locate more events at a lower magnitude threshold than networks that consist solely of surface stations.” [Zang 2014, pp. 5-5]} Condition: Claims network sensitivity is improved by down-hole sensors, particularly for S-waves, due to reduced attenuation and noise.
- {“A widely observed feature in the spatiotemporal distribution of seismicity is a gradual migration from the vicinity of the borehole to distances farther from the borehole as fluid injection is progressing. In many cases as injection proceeds seismicity continues throughout the volume rather than in an expanding ring.” [Zang 2014, pp. 3-3]} Condition: Describes a common pattern in EGS stimulations.
- {“In crystalline reservoirs with multiple stimulation wells, seismicity is absent until the stress level of previous stimulations is exceeded (Kaiser Effect).” [Zang 2014, pp. 18-18]} Condition: Applicability confirmed in crystalline and meta-greywacke, but not in sedimentary basins.
- {“The damage zone around the hydraulic fracture in crystalline rock may be much larger compared to the one in sedimentary rock.” [Zang 2014, pp. 11-12]} Condition: This is based on the dominance of mode-II shear in crystalline rock and mode-I tensile failures in sedimentary rock during fluid injection.
- {“A change in isotropic components of the full waveform moment tensor is observed for events close to the injection well (tensile character) as compared to events further away from the injection well (shear character). Tensile events coincide with high Gutenberg-Richter b-values and low Brune stress drop values.” [Zang 2014, pp. 1-2]} Condition: Derived primarily from high-resolution analysis of the Basel dataset and provides a physical framework linking source mechanisms to stress state.

## Open Questions

- The physical mechanism of shear dilatation during high-pressure, high-volume fluid injection is not yet fully understood [Zang 2014, pp. 2-3].
- Why is induced seismicity generated at some sites and completely absent at others, even under similar operations (e.g., absence at Bouillante, low rate at Groß Schönebeck)? [Zang 2014, pp. 2-2]
- The causal relationship, if any, between the maximum magnitude observed at a project and a region’s long-term natural seismic hazard level remains unclear [Zang 2014, pp. 7-7].
- How can seismogenic permeability (Talwani et al., 2007) be used to understand the link between aseismic/relaxed and seismic/locked patches within a reservoir? [Zang 2014, pp. 12-13]
- How can poroelastic effects be deconvolved from anisotropic permeability effects on the shape of the seismic cloud? [Zang 2014, pp. 12-13]
- What is the role of long-term processes (heat transport coupling, geochemical effects like dissolution/precipitation) on induced seismicity during reservoir production? [Zang 2014, pp. 12-13]
