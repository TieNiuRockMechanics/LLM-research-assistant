# Regulated Transient Pool Boiling of Water during Quenching on Nanostructured Surfaces with Modified Wettability from Superhydrophilic to Superhydrophobic.

## One-line Summary
Transient pool boiling of water on stainless steel spheres is actively regulated by nanostructured coatings that modify wettability from superhydrophilic to superhydrophobic; superhydrophilicity enhances CHF by ~70% and accelerates cooling, while superhydrophobicity stabilizes the vapor film and suppresses boiling even at low wall superheats. [Fan 2014, pp. 1-2, 4-5, 9]

## Research Question
How do surface wettability extremes (superhydrophilic to superhydrophobic) affect transient pool boiling during quenching of hot spheres in saturated water, and can these effects be exploited to actively regulate the boiling process? [Fan 2014, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
- **Quenching object:** Polished 304 stainless steel spheres, diameter 10 mm. [Fan 2014, pp. 2-3]
- **Liquid:** Distilled water, saturated at atmospheric pressure (100 °C), without degassing; vessel dimensions 100×100×140 mm³ (large relative to sphere). [Fan 2014, pp. 3]
- **Initial conditions:** Sphere heated to 300 °C (wall superheat ~200 °C); immersion depth 40 mm. [Fan 2014, pp. 4-5]
- **Surfaces tested:**
  - Hydrophilic (clean, θ ≈ 23°) – baseline.
  - Hydrophobic (silane‑coated, θ ≈ 119°).
  - Superhydrophobic (Glaco nanoparticle coating, θ ≈ 165°, Cassie state).
  - Superhydrophilic (Glaco coating + plasma cleaning, θ ≈ 0°, Wenzel state).  
  Roughness (Ra, Rq) remained nearly unchanged across all cases. [Fan 2014, pp. 3-4, Table 1]
- **Thermophysical properties of water (atmospheric pressure):** see Table 3 [Fan 2014, pp. 6-7]; sphere properties (304 SS): ρ=7817 kg/m³, cp=460 J/kg·K, k=17 W/m·K [Fan 2014, pp. 4-4, Table 2].

## Methods
- **Quenching facility:** tube furnace (max 1000 °C, inner diam. 25 mm), electric slider (stroke 200 mm, speed ≤250 mm/s), quartz vessel, plate heater. [Fan 2014, pp. 2-3]
- **Temperature measurement:** Type‑K thermocouple (Ø 0.8 mm) at sphere center; data logged at 10 Hz. [Fan 2014, pp. 3]
- **Surface modification:**
  - Hydrophobic: vapor deposition of trichlorosilane. [Fan 2014, pp. 3]
  - Superhydrophobic: multiple cycles of coating with Glaco Mirror Coat Zero (silica nanoparticles functionalized with hydrophobizing surfactant), drying, curing at 300 °C. [Fan 2014, pp. 3-4]
  - Superhydrophilic: superhydrophobic spheres subjected to 5 min plasma cleaning, retaining nanostructure while removing the organic surfactant. [Fan 2014, pp. 4]
- **Wettability & roughness characterization:** sessile drop method (θ), SEM, AFM (on flat Si wafers), profilometry (Ra, Rq). [Fan 2014, pp. 3-4]
- **Heat flux reduction:** lumped capacitance model (Bi < 0.1 during film boiling); surface‑averaged heat flux calculated from cooling rate (Eq. 2). Reproducibility tested; systematic uncertainty in heat flux estimated < 5%. [Fan 2014, pp. 4-4]
- **High‑speed imaging:** NAC‑GX1 camera at 2000 fps, 1280×1024 pixels, to visualize vapor film and bubble dynamics. [Fan 2014, pp. 3]

## Key Findings
1. **Quenching curves:** Cooling time increases with contact angle. Superhydrophobic: ~90 s, monotonic; no reflection points (film boiling persists). Hydrophilic and hydrophobic show LFP/CHF reflections. Superhydrophilic: 18 s, ~260 °C pseudo‑LFP (actually transition boiling because real LFP > 700 °C). [Fan 2014, pp. 4-5]
2. **Boiling curves & CHF:** CHF decreases nearly linearly with contact angle. Superhydrophilic gives ~70% CHF enhancement over hydrophilic baseline, with associated wall superheat increase of 28 °C. CHF peak suppressed for hydrophobic surfaces, disappears for superhydrophobic (no nucleate boiling). [Fan 2014, pp. 5-6, 6-7]
3. **Film boiling & LFP/MHF:** LFP and MHF decrease with increasing contact angle. Experimental LFP for hydrophilic (~234 °C) matches literature. Measured MHF agrees with model predictions for θ = 23°–119°, but model deviates for extreme wettabilities. True LFP/MHF for superhydrophilic are expected to be very high. [Fan 2014, pp. 6-7]
4. **Vapor film dynamics (high‑speed imaging):**
   - Superhydrophilic: vapor film highly unstable even at high superheat, violent rewetting causes early collapse → enhanced CHF and film boiling heat transfer. [Fan 2014, pp. 5-6]
   - Hydrophilic: classical film formation, clear LFP/CHF transitions, spherical vapor dome. [Fan 2014, pp. 5-6]
   - Hydrophobic: stabilized film with wavy interface; vapor film collapses coherently (simultaneously over sphere). [Fan 2014, pp. 6]
   - Superhydrophobic: never‑collapsed vapor film, wavy interface, persistent Cassie state at low superheat. [Fan 2014, pp. 6]
5. **Heat transfer coefficient & vapor film thickness:** Nu during film boiling agrees with Frederking & Clark models (Eqs. 7, 10). Estimated vapor film thickness during film boiling decreases with improved wettability; critical thickness at LFP: 88.2 µm (hydrophilic), 46.8 µm (hydrophobic). [Fan 2014, pp. 7-9]
6. **Active regulation:** Nanostructured superhydrophilic/superhydrophobic surfaces provide an active means to either enhance CHF and cooling rate or preserve stable film boiling (e.g., for drag reduction). [Fan 2014, pp. 1-2, 9]

## Limitations
- The lumped capacitance model introduces significant error during nucleate boiling (high HTCs); therefore, absolute CHF and nucleate boiling heat transfer coefficients cannot be compared to classical steady‑state correlations. The data are valid only for relative comparison among the tested surfaces. [Fan 2014, pp. 4-4]
- Initial sphere temperature (300 °C) was not high enough to reach the true Leidenfrost point for the superhydrophilic surface (expected > 700 °C); consequently, the superhydrophilic data lack a genuine film boiling regime and true LFP/MHF. [Fan 2014, pp. 4-5]
- The stability of the organic hydrophobic coatings (hydrophobic and superhydrophobic) at elevated temperatures is uncertain, limiting the attainable initial temperature. [Fan 2014, pp. 4-5]
- Only saturated pool boiling at atmospheric pressure was studied; effects of subcooling or pressure variations were not investigated. [Fan 2014, pp. 3]
- The sphere diameter (10 mm) falls in the intermediate sphere mode (Dh=8.68 mm, Dt=13.36 mm), but generalisation to other sizes is not directly verified. [Fan 2014, pp. 6-7]
- Distilled water was used without degassing; dissolved gases may influence nucleation, though no degassing protocol was applied. [Fan 2014, pp. 3]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Quenching liquid is saturated distilled water at atmospheric pressure (100 °C). [Fan 2014, pp. 3]
- The sphere is considered a lumped capacitance system; the Biot number is sufficiently small during film boiling (Bi ≈ 0.1), justifying the calculation of surface‑averaged heat flux and wall superheat from centre temperature. [Fan 2014, pp. 4]
- Thermophysical properties of 304 stainless steel are constant (Table 2). [Fan 2014, pp. 4]
- Surface roughness remains unchanged among the different wettability treatments, so differences are attributed solely to wettability. [Fan 2014, pp. 3-4]
- Wall temperature is represented by the centre temperature; actual wall superheat at imaging instants may be slightly lower. [Fan 2014, pp. 5-6]
- Inclination angle φ in Kandlikar’s model is taken as 0° or 90°; the horizontal orientation likely used for comparison. [Fan 2014, pp. 6-7]

## Reusable Claims
- On nanostructured superhydrophilic surfaces (θ ≈ 0°), transient pool boiling CHF can be enhanced by approximately 70% relative to a clean hydrophilic surface (θ ≈ 23°), with a corresponding increase in wall superheat at CHF of about 28 °C. [Fan 2014, pp. 1-2, 6-7]  
- Superhydrophobicity (θ > 150°) stabilizes the vapor film, leading to the disappearance of the CHF peak and enabling film boiling to persist even at wall superheats below 10 °C. [Fan 2014, pp. 4-5, 5-6]  
- The cooling rate during quenching increases monotonically with surface hydrophilicity; the entire cooling period for a 10 mm steel sphere from 300 °C can be shortened from ~90 s (superhydrophobic) to ~18 s (superhydrophilic). [Fan 2014, pp. 4-5]  
- Violent rewetting, facilitated by superhydrophilicity, causes early collapse of the vapor film, which is responsible for the observed increase in film boiling heat transfer and CHF. [Fan 2014, pp. 1-2, 5-6]  
- In the film boiling regime, the Nusselt number for the intermediate sphere mode agrees with the natural convection film boiling model of Frederking and Clark, especially for surfaces where stable film is maintained. [Fan 2014, pp. 7-8]  
- The equivalent vapor film thickness during film boiling decreases with increasing surface wettability; for the hydrophilic case, film collapse occurs at a critical thickness of ~88 µm, whereas for the hydrophobic case it is ~47 µm. [Fan 2014, pp. 8-9]  
- Nanostructured superhydrophilic coatings (e.g., plasma‑cleaned silica nanoparticle layers) withstand temperatures up to at least 800 °C, allowing their use in quenching applications where true Leidenfrost phenomena are to be studied or suppressed. [Fan 2014, pp. 4-5]  
- Surface wettability modification via nanostructured coatings provides an active method to regulate transient pool boiling: either enhance heat transfer and CHF (superhydrophilic) or preserve stable film boiling for applications such as drag reduction (superhydrophobic). [Fan 2014, pp. 1-2, 9]

## Connections To Other Work
- **Vakarelski et al. (2012) [18]:** Provided the protocols for preparing nanostructured superhydrophobic and superhydrophilic surfaces (Glaco coating + plasma cleaning) and demonstrated stabilization of the Leidenfrost vapor layer by superhydrophobic textures. [Fan 2014, pp. 3-4, 4-5]  
- **Kim et al. (2009) [4]:** Quenching of stainless steel spheres in nanofluids; similar LFP (~250 °C) and MHF (~40 kW/m²) for hydrophilic surfaces of comparable size and wettability. [Fan 2014, pp. 6-7]

## Open Questions
- What are the true LFP and MHF for the superhydrophilic surface? Preliminary evidence suggests values > 700 °C, but they were not measured here because the initial temperature was only 300 °C. [Fan 2014, pp. 4-5]  
- How do surface roughness and porosity (nano‑ or micro‑scale) interact with extreme wettabilities to further regulate boiling? [Fan 2014, pp. 9]  
- What is the long‑term thermal stability and durability of the Glaco‑based nanostructured coatings under repeated quenching cycles? [Fan 2014, pp. 4-5, not confirmed]  
- Can an inverse heat conduction model correct the systematic errors of the lumped capacitance approach during nucleate boiling, thereby yielding boiling curves that are directly comparable to steady‑state data? [Fan 2014, pp. 4-4]  
- How do the findings extend to other fluids, higher pressures, or forced convection scenarios? [not studied in this work]  
- Is the coherent collapse of the vapor film on hydrophobic surfaces a general feature for intermediate spheres, and how does it influence the rewetting dynamics? [Fan 2014, pp. 6]  
- Could the combination of superhydrophilicity and nanoporosity lead to CHF enhancements beyond the factor of 1.7 observed here? [Fan 2014, pp. 9]
