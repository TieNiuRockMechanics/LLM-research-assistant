# Experimental Study on Physico-Mechanical and Transport Properties of Granite Subjected to Rapid Cooling.

## One-line Summary
Rapid cooling from high temperatures degrades the physical and mechanical properties of granite while significantly enhancing its permeability, with identified threshold temperatures for brittle-ductile transition and permeability change.

## Research Question
How do the physico-mechanical and transport properties of granite evolve when subjected to rapid cooling from elevated temperatures, and what are the underlying mechanisms and threshold temperatures for these changes? [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study used a medium-fine grained monzogranite (commercial name "Sesame White") sampled from Rizhao, Shandong, China. The main mineral composition is plagioclase (35%), K-feldspar (40%-45%), quartz (20%-25%), biotite (3%-5%), and minor hornblende, with grain sizes of 0.3-2 mm. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-5]

## Methods
- **Sample Preparation:** Cylindrical specimens (φ50 mm × 100 mm) for uniaxial compression and permeability tests, and Brazilian disc specimens (φ50 mm × 25 mm) for tensile strength tests were prepared according to ISRM standards. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-5]
- **Thermal Treatment:** Samples were heated to target temperatures (20°C, 100°C, 200°C, 300°C, 400°C, 500°C, 600°C) at a rate of 2°C/min in a muffle furnace, held for 2 hours, and then rapidly cooled by immersion in a water tank (~15 L) with stirring. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-5]
- **Physical Property Testing:** Mass loss, volume expansion, density, and longitudinal wave velocity (using NM-4B non-metallic ultrasonic detector) were measured after treatment. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-5]
- **Mechanical Property Testing:** Uniaxial compression and Brazilian tensile tests were conducted using a servo-controlled testing machine at a loading rate of 0.002 mm/s. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-5]
- **Permeability Testing:** Gas permeability was measured using the pressure pulse decay method with a Smart perm III instrument and nitrogen as the working fluid. A confining pressure of 5 MPa and initial pore pressure of 2.5 MPa were applied, with an initial pressure difference of 0.25 MPa. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-5]

## Key Findings
1.  **Physical Properties:** With increasing treatment temperature, the density, longitudinal wave velocity, compressive strength, elastic modulus, and tensile strength of rapidly cooled granite samples decrease monotonically. Permeability increases slowly at first and then sharply. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2, 5-7]
2.  **Brittle-Ductile Transition:** Under rapid cooling, the brittle-ductile transition temperature of granite is between 500°C and 600°C. Below this range, failure is predominantly brittle; above it, the behavior becomes more ductile. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2, 5-7]
3.  **Permeability Threshold:** Under the conditions of free state, slow heating, and rapid cooling, the threshold temperature for a significant change in granite permeability is 400°C. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2, 5-7]
4.  **Damage Assessment:** The damage factor based on elastic modulus accurately reflects the deterioration of mechanical properties, while the damage factor based on longitudinal wave velocity overestimates this deterioration. [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2, 5-7]
5.  **Thermal Cracking Mechanism:** During slow heating and holding, thermal cracking is caused by thermal expansion mismatch and anisotropy of mineral grains. During rapid cooling, large radial temperature gradients induce additional thermal stress, leading to more cracks and further property degradation. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-7]

## Limitations
- The study was conducted on a specific type of granite ("Sesame White" from Rizhao, China) under unconfined conditions. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-5]
- The thermal treatment involved a specific slow heating rate (2°C/min) followed by rapid water quenching. Results may differ under different heating/cooling regimes or confinement pressures. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-5]
- The permeability measurement used nitrogen gas; results might vary with different fluids. [Jin 2018, pp. 2-5]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The thermal cracking during slow heating and holding is primarily due to mineral thermal expansion mismatch and anisotropy. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-7]
- The temperature gradient during rapid cooling is the dominant cause of additional cracking. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-7]
- The damage factor formulas (based on E, Vp, σc, σt) are valid for quantifying the degree of thermal damage. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-7]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variable:** Treatment temperature (20°C to 600°C).
- **Dependent Variables:** Density, longitudinal wave velocity, uniaxial compressive strength, tensile strength, elastic modulus, permeability.
- **Controlled Variables:** Heating rate (2°C/min), holding time (2 hours), cooling method (water quenching), specimen dimensions, confining pressure for permeability tests (5 MPa).

## Reusable Claims
- Under rapid cooling conditions, the permeability of granite exhibits a sharp increase above a threshold temperature of 400°C. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-7]
- The brittle-ductile transition temperature for granite under rapid cooling is between 500°C and 600°C. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-7]
- For assessing thermal damage in rapidly cooled granite, a damage factor derived from elastic modulus is more reliable than one derived from longitudinal wave velocity, as the latter overestimates mechanical property degradation. [Jin 2018, pp. 5-7]
- Rapid cooling induces greater thermal cracking and property degradation in granite compared to slow cooling, as evidenced by a lower permeability threshold temperature (400°C vs. 500°C). [Jin 2018, pp. 5-7]
