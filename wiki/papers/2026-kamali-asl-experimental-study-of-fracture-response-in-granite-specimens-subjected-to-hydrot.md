# Experimental Study of Fracture Response in Granite Specimens Subjected to Hydrothermal Conditions Relevant for Enhanced Geothermal Systems.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how coupled Thermal-Hydrological-Mechanical-Chemical (THMC) processes, particularly pressure solution, affect fracture aperture and permeability in granite under varying effective stresses and temperatures relevant to Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS).

## Research Question
How do coupled THMC processes, with a focus on pressure solution, influence the evolution of fracture aperture and permeability in granite under hydrothermal conditions, and what is the effect of the temperature difference between the rock and injected fluid on these processes?

## Study Area / Data
The experiments used artificially-fractured specimens of Barre granite from Vermont, USA. The specimens were cylindrical (38.4 mm diameter, 38.5 mm length) with a dry density of 2.63 g/cm³ and porosity of 0.72%. XRD analysis showed the composition was 42.4% quartz, 47.9% feldspars (36.7% oligoclase, 11.2% microcline), 3.9% biotite, 3.2% muscovite, and 2.6% accessory minerals [Accessed 2026, pp. 4-4].

## Methods
A series of six flow-through experiments were conducted using an AutoLab 1500 triaxial system. Artificial tensile fractures were created via a modified Brazilian test. Experiments were performed under effective stresses of 5 MPa to 36 MPa, rock temperatures of 25 °C and 130 °C, and injected fluid (deionized water) temperatures of 25 °C, 75 °C, and 130 °C [Accessed 2026, pp. 4-6]. Hydraulic aperture and fracture permeability were estimated from pore pressure data using Darcy's law and the modified cubic law [Accessed 2026, pp. 4-6]. Effluent samples were analyzed via ICP-OES to quantify dissolved mineral concentrations. Pre- and post-test micro-CT scans (19.9 μm resolution) were used to map fracture surface topography changes [Accessed 2026, pp. 6-7].

## Key Findings
- Higher effective stresses led to higher fracture closure rates, highlighting the role of pressure solution [Accessed 2026, pp. 1-1].
- Fracture permeability and hydraulic aperture were not fully recoverable during unloading stages, indicating irreversible deformation due to pressure solution and mineral dissolution/precipitation [Accessed 2026, pp. 12-15].
- Increasing the temperature of the injected fluid increased the percentage of recovery of fracture aperture and permeability after unloading [Accessed 2026, pp. 1-1].
- Chemical analysis confirmed dissolution of quartz and feldspars, with higher dissolved element concentrations at higher injected fluid temperatures [Accessed 2026, pp. 12-15].
- Image analysis identified regions of dissolution and precipitation on the fracture surface [Accessed 2026, pp. 12-15].

## Limitations
- Experiments were relatively short-term (5-10 days), which may not fully represent long-term EGS reservoir behavior [Accessed 2026, pp. 12-15].
- Laboratory room temperature fluctuations affected specimen and fluid temperatures; reported temperatures do not reflect these fluctuations [Accessed 2026, pp. 4-4].
- The exact time to reach chemical equilibrium in the experiments was not specifically studied [Accessed 2026, pp. 4-6].
- Effluent analysis could not be conducted for Experiment #6 due to sample loss [Accessed 2026, pp. 12-15].
- Pre- and post-test CT images were obtained at atmospheric pressure and room temperature, not experimental conditions, preventing direct fracture volume change analysis [Accessed 2026, pp. 6-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- It was assumed that the concentration of dissolved quartz in the effluent reached steady-state within one day [Accessed 2026, pp. 4-6].
- A contact area ratio (Rc) between 0.4 and 0.8 was used to bound estimates of asperity contact area [Accessed 2026, pp. 6-7].
- Experiments #5 and #6 were designed to minimize the effects of thermal stresses (ΔT=0) to isolate chemical process effects [Accessed 2026, pp. 7-8].
- The modified cubic law with parallel plate approximation was used to estimate hydraulic aperture due to the very low matrix porosity (0.72%) [Accessed 2026, pp. 4-6].

## Reusable Claims
- Under constant effective stress, fracture permeability and hydraulic aperture in granite decrease over time due to coupled THMC processes, including pressure solution [Accessed 2026, pp. 8-9].
- Higher effective stress enhances the rate of fracture closure, primarily due to increased pressure solution at asperity contacts [Accessed 2026, pp. 12-15].
- The recovery of fracture permeability and hydraulic aperture after mechanical unloading is incomplete, with the irrecoverable portion attributed to pressure solution and mineral precipitation [Accessed 2026, pp. 16-18].
- Increasing the temperature of the injected fluid enhances the kinetic rate of mineral dissolution, leading to higher concentrations of dissolved elements in the effluent [Accessed 2026, pp. 12-15].
- When the temperature difference (ΔT) between the rock and injected fluid is minimized, the dominant mechanism for permeability reduction shifts, with pressure solution effects being more pronounced at lower temperatures [Accessed 2026, pp. 16-18].

## Open Questions

- How do the findings scale from laboratory specimens to reservoir-scale fracture networks?
