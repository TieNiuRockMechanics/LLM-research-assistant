# Experimental Investigation of Thermal Cycling Effect on Physical and Mechanical Properties of Heated Granite after Water Cooling.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how repeated heating to 500°C followed by water cooling affects the physical, mechanical, and microstructural properties of granite, finding significant deterioration that stabilizes after approximately 20 cycles.

## Research Question
What are the effects of thermal cycling (heating to 500°C and water cooling) on the physical properties (volume, mass, density, P-wave velocity), mechanical properties (uniaxial compressive strength, elastic modulus), and microstructure of granite?

## Study Area / Data
The granite used was collected from a mine in Suizhou City, Hubei Province, China. It has a dense massive structure and is composed of 77.68% feldspar, 10.58% quartz, 6.28% biotite, 3.24% chlorite, and 2.22% amphibole. Specimens were cored to a diameter of 50 mm and height of 100 mm. The natural density and average longitudinal wave velocity were 2.602 g/cm³ and 3792 m/s, respectively [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-2].

## Methods
Specimens were dried at 105°C for 24 hours, then divided into six groups (0, 1, 5, 10, 20, 30 cycles). Each cycle involved heating to 500°C at 5°C/min, holding for 2 hours, and then immersing in distilled water to cool to room temperature. After each cycle, dimensions, mass, and P-wave velocity were measured. Uniaxial compression tests were performed at a displacement rate of 0.3 mm/min. Microstructure was observed using a scanning electron microscope (SEM) at 100x magnification [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-2, 2-4].

## Key Findings
- Volume increases logarithmically with thermal cycles, reaching 2.55% after 30 cycles. Mass decreases slightly (0.31% after 30 cycles), leading to a density decrease of 2.78% [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-4, 8-9].
- P-wave velocity decreases sharply by 58.64% after the first cycle and by 76.91% after 30 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 4-5, 8-9].
- Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and elastic modulus (E) decrease with cycles. UCS decreases by 33.37% after 1 cycle and 78.91% after 30 cycles. E decreases by 38.82% after 1 cycle and 88.42% after 30 cycles. The rate of decrease slows and stabilizes after about 20 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 4-5, 8-9].
- Peak strain increases with thermal cycles, indicating increased ductility [Zhu 2019, pp. 4-5].
- SEM observations show microcrack generation and propagation. A microcrack network forms after 5 cycles, with crack density increasing slowly from 10 to 30 cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 4-5, 5-7].
- Failure mode transitions from axial splitting to shear failure with increasing cycles [Zhu 2019, pp. 5-7].

## Limitations
- All tests were performed under unconfined conditions, whereas geothermal reservoir rock is subjected to high confining pressure [Zhu 2019, pp. 7-8].
- The study focused on a single temperature (500°C) and a specific granite type [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-2].
- The maximum number of thermal cycles was 30 [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-2].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The heating rate of 5°C/min was used to minimize thermal shock during heating [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-2].
- Specimens were dried at 105°C for 24 hours before each cycle to eliminate moisture content [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-2].
- The temperature range (20°C to 500°C) is relevant for deep geothermal energy extraction (reservoirs shallower than 5 km) [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2, 2-2].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Thermal cycles (0, 1, 5, 10, 20, 30)
- Temperature (500°C)
- Cooling method (water cooling)
- Physical properties: Volume, mass, density, P-wave velocity
- Mechanical properties: Uniaxial compressive strength (UCS), elastic modulus (E), peak strain (εs)
- Microstructure: Microcrack density and network formation (observed via SEM)

## Reusable Claims
- The first thermal cycle (heating to 500°C and water cooling) causes the most significant deterioration in the physical and mechanical properties of granite, with subsequent cycles showing diminishing effects [Zhu 2019, pp. 2-4, 4-5, 8-9].
- The physical and mechanical properties of granite stabilize after approximately 20 thermal cycles of heating to 500°C and water cooling [Zhu 2019, pp. 4-5, 8-9].
- The primary mechanism for property degradation is the generation and propagation of microcracks induced by thermal stress from differential mineral expansion and rapid water cooling [Zhu 2019, pp. 4-5, 5-7, 7-8].
- Water cooling induces more severe thermal damage and microcracking in granite compared to air cooling, leading to greater reductions in strength and stiffness [Zhu 2019, pp. 5-7, 7-8].

## Connections To Other Work
- The study compares its results on water-cooled granite with prior work on air-cooled granite by Rong et al. (2018), showing that water cooling causes more significant reductions in UCS and E [Zhu 2019, pp. 7-8].
- It builds on previous research on the effects of high temperature on granite properties (e.g., Chen et al. 2012, 2017a,b; Yang et al. 2017; Zhao et al. 2018a,b) by introducing the combined effect of thermal cycling and water cooling [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2, 2-2].
- The findings are relevant to the stability of well boreholes during deep geothermal energy extraction, as discussed in works by Zaigham and Nayyar (2010) and Tomac and Sauter (2018) [Zhu 2019, pp. 1-2, 2-2].

## Open Questions
- How do confining pressures present in actual geothermal reservoirs affect the thermal cycling behavior of granite?
- What are the long-term effects of thermal cycling beyond 30 cycles?
- How do different cooling fluids (e.g., brines, supercritical CO2) affect the deterioration process?
- How do the findings apply to other rock types common in geothermal reservoirs?
