# Quantitative Multiparameter Prediction of Fault-Related Fractures: A Case Study of the Second Member of the Funing Formation in the Jinhu Sag, Subei Basin.

## One-line Summary
This study proposes a quantitative method to predict multiscale, multiperiod fault-related fracture parameters (density, aperture, porosity, permeability) by integrating fault-fracture self-similarity, core-based aperture distributions, and numerical geomechanical modeling of the in situ stress field.

## Research Question
How can the distribution, aperture, porosity, and permeability of multiscale fault-related fractures be quantitatively predicted in a reservoir, considering their scaling properties and the influence of the in situ stress field?

## Study Area / Data
The case study focuses on the reservoirs of the second member of the Paleogene Funing Formation in the Jinhu Sag, located in the western part of the Subei Basin, eastern China [Liu 2018, pp. 2-3]. The primary lithologies are argillaceous siltstone, siltstone, and fine sandstone with low permeability [Liu 2018, pp. 2-3]. Data includes observations and statistical analyses of fractures in cores from over 50 wells across the Biandong, Shigang, Qiaohekou, Minqiao, Yangjiaba, and Tongcheng regions [Liu 2018, pp. 2-3]. The study also utilizes data on 826 faults at different scales [Liu 2018, pp. 3-5], in situ stress orientations from hydraulic fracturing, borehole breakouts, focal mechanisms, and oriented core acoustic velocity experiments [Liu 2018, pp. 8-9], and rock mechanical parameters from triaxial experiments [Liu 2018, pp. 8-9].

## Methods
1.  **Fractal Analysis & Areal Density Prediction:** The information dimension (D) is calculated using fault data to quantify self-similarity. A model is proposed to calculate the multiscale areal fracture density (ρ_s) based on fault-fracture self-similarity theory [Liu 2018, pp. 2-3, 5-6].
2.  **Aperture Distribution Model:** Initial fracture apertures are determined using a constraint method with a skewed distribution model derived from core and thin-section observations [Liu 2018, pp. 1-2, 6-8].
3.  **Numerical Geomechanical Modeling:** A 3D finite element method (FEM) numerical model of the in situ stress field is established using a geological model, mechanical parameters from rock experiments, and boundary conditions from stress measurements [Liu 2018, pp. 8-9].
4.  **Underground Aperture Calculation:** The current aperture of fractures under the in situ stress field is calculated using an equation that considers effective normal stress and residual aperture [Liu 2018, pp. 8-9].
5.  **Porosity & Permeability Calculation:** Mathematical models are developed to calculate fracture porosity (φ) and the magnitude (K_max) and direction (θ_max) of maximum permeability for multiscale fractures [Liu 2018, pp. 9-11].
6.  **Fracture Development Pattern Classification:** Based on relationships among fracture aperture, density, porosity, and permeability, fracture development patterns are classified [Liu 2018, pp. 12-14].

## Key Findings
1.  Faults and fractures in the Jinhu Sag exhibit statistically significant self-similarity (R² > 0.97 for 84.6% of statistical units), providing a basis for predicting fracture density at different scales [Liu 2018, pp. 3-5].
2.  The calculated areal fracture density at the thin-section scale (d > 10⁻⁵ m) is 4–12 m/m², and at the core scale (d > 0.5 m) is 0.25–0.70 m/m² [Liu 2018, pp. 5-6].
3.  The present-day maximum horizontal principal stress (σ_H) is oriented ENE (75°–90°) with a magnitude of 37–39 MPa, and the minimum horizontal principal stress (σ_h) is oriented NNW (345°–360°) with a magnitude of 28–32 MPa [Liu 2018, pp. 14-15].
4.  Underground fracture apertures are concentrated in the range of (6–20) × 10⁻⁵ m [Liu 2018, pp. 9-11, 14-15].
5.  The predicted fracture porosity of the reservoirs is (35–95) × 10⁻³%, and the fracture permeability is (20–120) × 10⁻³ μm² [Liu 2018, pp. 11-12, 14-15].
6.  The main direction of fracture permeability generally converges toward the ENE, influenced by both the paleostress field controlling fracture formation and the current in situ stress field [Liu 2018, pp. 11-12, 14-15].
7.  Fractures in the Jinhu Sag are classified into five development patterns: low-porosity/low-permeability, high-porosity/high-permeability, low-porosity/high-permeability, high-porosity/low-permeability, and transitional [Liu 2018, pp. 12-14].

## Limitations
1.  The calculated fracture porosity does not consider corrosion or dissolution on fracture surfaces [Liu 2018, pp. 11-12].
2.  The prediction model showed a relatively large error in the Biandong region, possibly because the reservoir includes mixed deposits of lacustrine limestone and sandstone, which are more brittle and prone to fracture than the low-permeability sandstone assumed in the model [Liu 2018, pp. 12-14].
3.  The method relies on the assumption of fault-fracture self-similarity, which may not hold in all geological settings.

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Fractures develop in orientations that exhibit a high degree of similarity to nearby faults (fault-fracture self-similarity) [Liu 2018, pp. 3-5].
2.  Fracture apertures statistically follow a skewed distribution [Liu 2018, pp. 6-8].
3.  The current stress field no longer generates new fractures; it only affects the opening and permeability of existing, unfilled fractures [Liu 2018, pp. 8-9].
4.  The relationship between fracture length and aperture is positive [Liu 2018, pp. 6-8].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Information Dimension (D):** A fractal dimension quantifying fault/fracture network complexity and development [Liu 2018, pp. 2-3].
-   **Areal Fracture Density (ρ_s):** The total length of fractures per unit area at a given scale [Liu 2018, pp. 5-6].
-   **Fracture Aperture (b):** The opening width of a fracture, which follows a skewed distribution and is modified by in situ stress [Liu 2018, pp. 6-9].
-   **In Situ Stress (σ_H, σ_h):** The present-day maximum and minimum horizontal principal stresses [Liu 2018, pp. 8-9].
-   **Fracture Porosity (φ):** The pore volume contributed by fractures [Liu 2018, pp. 9-11].
-   **Maximum Permeability (K_max) and its Direction (θ_max):** The highest permeability value within a unit and its orientation [Liu 2018, pp. 9-11].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** In regions where faults exhibit self-similarity. **Claim:** The areal density of fractures at different scales can be predicted using the information dimension (D) of the fault network and a scaling model [Liu 2018, pp. 2-3, 5-6].
2.  **Condition:** For unfilled fractures in a given stress field. **Claim:** The current aperture can be calculated from the initial aperture distribution and the effective normal stress using a specific equation [Liu 2018, pp. 8-9].
3.  **Condition:** When fracture orientation, density, and aperture are known for a statistical unit. **Claim:** The fracture porosity and the magnitude and direction of maximum permeability can be quantitatively calculated using derived mathematical models [Liu 2018, pp. 9-11].

## Connections To Other Work
The study builds on the concept that tectonic fractures are closely related to fault development [Zhang et al. 2004; Jiu et al. 2013a; Liu et al. 2016, cited in Liu 2018, pp. 1-2]. It uses the self-similarity principle observed in fault systems [Barton and Zoback 1992; Aguilar-Hernández and Ramírez-Santiago 2010, cited in Liu 2018, pp. 2-3] and applies an aperture-stress relationship from previous studies [Willis-Richards et al. 1996; Jing et al. 1998, cited in Liu 2018, pp. 8-9]. The work addresses the challenge of evaluating fracture distribution and predicting porosity/permeability noted by other researchers [Zeng and Li 2009; Hennings et al. 2012, cited in Liu 2018, pp. 1-2].

## Open Questions
1.  How can the prediction accuracy be improved in areas with mixed lithologies (e.g., lacustrine limestone and sandstone) that deviate from the model's assumptions?
2.  How do diagenetic processes (e.g., cementation, dissolution) over geological time affect the long-term evolution of the predicted fracture parameters?
3.  Can this multiparameter prediction method be successfully applied to other sedimentary basins with different tectonic histories?
