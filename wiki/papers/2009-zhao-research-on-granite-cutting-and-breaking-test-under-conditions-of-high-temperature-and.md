# Research on Granite Cutting and Breaking Test under Conditions of High Temperature and High Pressure.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates the cutting and breaking laws of large-sized granite samples under high temperature (up to 300°C) and high confining pressure (100 MPa), finding that increased temperature enhances cuttability and reduces specific energy consumption when sufficient drilling pressure is applied.

## Research Question
The research aims to systematically study the cutting and breaking laws of granite under high temperature and high pressure conditions to provide a theoretical basis for developing efficient drill bits and improving drilling methods for high-temperature geothermal wells [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The experiments utilized large cylindrical samples of Luhui granite (φ200 mm × 400 mm, density 2.71 g/cm³) sourced from Pingyi, Shandong, China [Zhao 2009, pp. 2-5]. The testing was conducted using the "20 MN servo-controlled triaxial rock testing machine with high temperature and pressure" at China University of Mining and Technology [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].

## Methods
The experimental setup included a custom-designed drilling control system with precise pressure and rotation control [Zhao 2009, pp. 2-5]. A φ30 mm PDC drill bit was used. The study maintained a constant confining pressure of 100 MPa (approximating 4000 m depth) while varying temperature (20°C, 150°C, 300°C) and drilling parameters (Weight on Bit (WOB) and rotation speed) in an orthogonal test design [Zhao 2009, pp. 2-5]. Cutting speed and torque were measured, and unit rock-breaking energy consumption was calculated using the formula: a = W/V, where W is work done by the drill (primarily from torque) and V is the volume of rock broken [Zhao 2009, pp. 5-7].

## Key Findings
1.  Under high confining pressure (100 MPa), granite's cuttability increases with temperature. At a WOB of 755 N, the cutting speed at 300°C increased by 30% to 50% compared to room temperature [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].
2.  Under the same conditions, unit rock-breaking energy consumption decreased significantly with rising temperature. At 755 N WOB, the energy consumption at 300°C was 20% to 30% lower than at room temperature [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].
3.  In the high-temperature, high-pressure environment, cutting speed increased with both WOB and rotation speed. Unit energy consumption increased with rotation speed but decreased with WOB, a pattern consistent with room-temperature, no-confining-pressure conditions [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].
4.  Granite undergoes gradual failure in this temperature-pressure range. If WOB is too low, the effects of temperature on cutting speed and energy consumption are minimal. A sufficient WOB is required to achieve effective cutting at high temperatures [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].

## Limitations
1.  The maximum test temperature was 300°C, and confining pressure was fixed at 100 MPa; results may not extrapolate beyond these ranges [Zhao 2009, pp. 2-5].
2.  High temperatures may cause slight carbonization and strength reduction in the PDC drill bits, potentially affecting cutting performance [Zhao 2009, pp. 5-7].
3.  The study focused on a single granite type (Luhui granite) [Zhao 2009, pp. 2-5].
4.  The calculation of unit rock-breaking energy consumption ignored the work done by thrust (axial force), as it was estimated to be about 1/100 of the torque work [Zhao 2009, pp. 5-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Temperature is the primary factor influencing the brittle-ductile transition of granite within the tested range, with confining pressure being secondary [Zhao 2009, pp. 2-5].
2.  The granite samples were in a natural water-saturated state [Zhao 2009, pp. 2-5].
3.  The work done by the drill's axial force (thrust) is negligible compared to the work done by torque [Zhao 2009, pp. 5-7].

-   **Dependent Variables:** Cutting Speed (mm/s), Unit Rock-Breaking Energy Consumption (J/mm³), Torque (N·m).

## Reusable Claims
-   Under a confining pressure of 100 MPa, increasing temperature from 20°C to 300°C enhances the cuttability of granite, leading to a 30-50% increase in cutting speed when the WOB is at least 755 N [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].
-   Under a confining pressure of 100 MPa, increasing temperature from 20°C to 300°C reduces the unit rock-breaking energy consumption for granite by 20-30% when the WOB is at least 755 N [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].
-   The positive correlation between cutting speed and both WOB and rotation speed, and the negative correlation between unit energy consumption and WOB, observed under room temperature and no confining pressure, also hold true under high temperature (300°C) and high confining pressure (100 MPa) conditions [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].
-   To effectively exploit the temperature-induced reduction in granite strength for cutting, the applied WOB must exceed a certain threshold; below this threshold, temperature has a minimal effect on cutting performance [Zhao 2009, pp. 1-2].
