# 低渗透油气藏压裂水平井分形渗流理论研究

## One-line Summary

This dissertation establishes a comprehensive fractal seepage flow theory for multi-stage fractured horizontal wells in low-permeability oil and gas reservoirs by introducing a unified capillary bundle model for the matrix, a tree-shaped fractal fracture network for hydraulic fractures, and coupling them through semi-analytical potential superposition methods to predict well productivity.

## Research Question

How to accurately model and predict the seepage flow behavior and productivity of multi-stage fractured horizontal wells in low-permeability oil and gas reservoirs, considering the complex microstructure of the matrix (multi-scale flow, stress sensitivity, threshold pressure gradient) and the tree-shaped fractal branching structure of hydraulic fracture networks? [Tan 2015, pp. 1-6]

## Study Area / Data

- **Tight oil reservoir example**: A tight oil reservoir with mid-depth 3241 m, boundary pressure 38 MPa, formation permeability 0.06 mD, temperature 40°C, oil viscosity 0.5 mPa·s, density 0.84 g/cm³. The horizontal well had length 664 m, wellbore radius 0.1 m, 3 fractures with 200 m spacing, initial fracture width 0.02 m, and current flowing bottomhole pressure 31.9 MPa. [Tan 2015, pp. 102-106]
- **Tight gas reservoir example**: A tight gas reservoir with mid-depth 2893 m, boundary pressure 26.6 MPa, formation permeability 0.08 mD, temperature 353 K, gas viscosity 0.01 mPa·s, gas compressibility 0.26 MPa⁻¹, gas relative density 0.57, gas deviation factor 0.97. The horizontal well had length 561 m, wellbore radius 0.1 m, 4 fractures with 100 m spacing, initial fracture width 0.01 m, and current flowing bottomhole pressure 21.5 MPa. [Tan 2015, pp. 115-119]
- **Validation data**: Ghadami et al. experimental data for 6 core samples from the same layer with average permeability 1.2×10⁻¹⁵ m² and average porosity 0.065 [Tan 2015, pp. 28-33]; Roy et al. and Arkilic et al. experimental data for gas flow in nanotubes and micro-fractures [Tan 2015, pp. 42-45]; stress sensitivity experimental data for schist, sandstone, and limestone [Tan 2015, pp. 51-54].

## Methods

1. **Unified capillary bundle model for porous media**: Introduced capillary flux function, capillary length function, and capillary probability density distribution function to describe the complex pore structure of low-permeability reservoirs. [Tan 2015, pp. 22-25]
2. **Multi-scale flow model**: Combined Knudsen diffusion with continuum flow to model gas transport in nano/micro-scale pores, deriving a multi-scale permeability model and theoretically explaining the Klinkenberg slip effect. [Tan 2015, pp. 37-42]
3. **Stress-sensitive fractal model**: Based on fractal geometry and material mechanics (Hooke's law), established permeability and porosity prediction models under effective stress, treating the porous medium as composed of tortuous skeleton sets. [Tan 2015, pp. 45-51]
4. **Threshold pressure gradient model**: Derived an unsteady seepage model for power-law fluids in fractal porous media using Laplace transform and Stehfest numerical inversion, and established a threshold pressure gradient model. [Tan 2015, pp. 57-63]
5. **Tree-shaped fractal fracture network generation**: Established generation methods and numbering rules for tree-shaped fractal fracture networks with Y-shaped bifurcation structures, including length ratio, width ratio, height ratio, and bifurcation angle parameters. [Tan 2015, pp. 71-79]
6. **Potential function and superposition method**: Treated each fracture as a 3D line sink, derived potential distribution laws for single fractures in various reservoir types (infinite, bottom-water, gas-cap, edge-water, closed boundaries) using mirror image and superposition principles. [Tan 2015, pp. 83-99]
7. **Semi-analytical coupling model**: Coupled the matrix seepage model with the fracture network flow model through iterative solution, obtaining productivity and fracture network pressure distribution. [Tan 2015, pp. 99-102]

## Key Findings

1. The unified capillary bundle model can summarize previous research results and its correctness was verified against experimental data. [Tan 2015, pp. 25-28]
2. The multi-scale flow permeability model theoretically explains the Klinkenberg slip effect, and the derived gas slip factor expression relates to pore structure parameters (λmin, λmax, Df) and gas properties (μ, M). [Tan 2015, pp. 42-45]
3. The stress-sensitive fractal model shows that normalized permeability and porosity decrease with increasing effective stress; Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio are important parameters affecting permeability and porosity under stress. [Tan 2015, pp. 51-54]
4. The threshold pressure gradient increases sharply as porosity decreases; the tortuosity fractal dimension Dt has a greater influence than the pore fractal dimension Df; when n=1 (Newtonian fluid), the threshold pressure gradient approaches zero. [Tan 2015, pp. 67-71]
5. Fracture stage number (Mf) and fracture segment number are the primary factors affecting productivity; reservoir permeability and thickness are the dominant reservoir property factors. [Tan 2015, pp. 106-119]
6. The model predictions matched actual production data with average errors of 3.55% for the oil well and 1.86% for the gas well. [Tan 2015, pp. 102-106, 115-119]
7. End effects cause U-shaped flow distribution along the horizontal wellbore, with higher inflow at the heel and toe. [Tan 2015, pp. 102-106]

## Limitations

1. The unified capillary bundle model using normal distribution performs poorly for fitting multi-peak pore distributions. [Tan 2015, pp. 28-33]
2. The real fracture networks in multi-stage fractured horizontal wells contain many irregular fractures that are far more complex than the model assumes. [Tan 2015, pp. 119-120]
3. The threshold pressure gradient discussion is limited to single-phase power-law fluids; two-phase flow threshold pressure gradient has not been studied. [Tan 2015, pp. 57-59]
4. The existence of the threshold pressure gradient itself remains debated in the academic community. [Tan 2015, pp. 57-59]
5. The tree-shaped fractal fracture network model assumes idealized Y-shaped bifurcation structures, which may not fully capture real hydraulic fracture complexity. [Tan 2015, pp. 71-74]
6. The model assumes single-phase isothermal flow and neglects gravity effects in the matrix model. [Tan 2015, pp. 22-25]

## Assumptions / Conditions

- Capillaries are filled with single-phase fluid; seepage is isothermal; capillary forces and gravity are neglected; permeability and porosity do not change with pressure (base model). [Tan 2015, pp. 22-25]
- The porous medium is composed of a series of tortuous skeleton sets with capillary bundles between them. [Tan 2015, pp. 47-49]
- The multi-stage fractured horizontal well fracture network consists of multiple tree-shaped fractal fracture networks. [Tan 2015, pp. 71-74]
- The reservoir is homogeneous, uniform thickness, isotropic, and infinite (for the base potential distribution derivation). [Tan 2015, pp. 83-87]
- Single fracture is treated as a 3D line sink with uniform inflow. [Tan 2015, pp. 83-87]
- Rock and fluid are slightly compressible with small, constant compressibility coefficients. [Tan 2015, pp. 57-59]
- Power-law fluid is pseudo-plastic (0 < n < 1) for the threshold pressure gradient analysis. [Tan 2015, pp. 57-59]

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Df**: Pore fractal dimension (孔隙分形维数), 0 < Df < 2 in 2D, 0 < Df < 3 in 3D [Tan 2015, pp. 57-59]
- **Dt**: Tortuosity fractal dimension (迂曲度分形维数), 1 < Dt < 2 [Tan 2015, pp. 25-28]
- **Dcf**: Skeleton set fractal dimension (骨架集分形维数) [Tan 2015, pp. 47-49]
- **Mf**: Total bifurcation levels of tree-shaped fractal fracture network (裂缝分叉级数) [Tan 2015, pp. 71-74]
- **Nf**: Total number of initial fractures (初始裂缝条数) [Tan 2015, pp. 71-74]
- **RL, RW, RH**: Length ratio, width ratio, height ratio of fractal fracture network (长度比、宽度比、高度比) [Tan 2015, pp. 71-74]
- **θf**: Fracture bifurcation angle (裂缝分叉角度) [Tan 2015, pp. 71-74]
- **Lf0**: Initial fracture length (初始裂缝长度) [Tan 2015, pp. 71-74]
- **n**: Power-law index (幂律指数) [Tan 2015, pp. 25-28]
- **E**: Young's modulus (杨氏模量) [Tan 2015, pp. 45-47]
- **ν**: Poisson's ratio (泊松比) [Tan 2015, pp. 45-47]
- **Kn**: Knudsen number (克努森数) [Tan 2015, pp. 37-42]
- **b**: Gas slip factor (气体滑脱因子) [Tan 2015, pp. 42-45]
- **J**: Threshold pressure gradient (启动压力梯度) [Tan 2015, pp. 67-71]

## Reusable Claims

1. **The unified capillary bundle model with normal distribution fits single-peak pore distributions well but performs poorly for multi-peak distributions.** This is because the normal distribution function inherently represents unimodal distributions. [Tan 2015, pp. 28-33]
2. **The gas slip factor expression depends on pore structure parameters (λmin, λmax, Df, Dt) and gas properties (μ, M), indicating that both porous medium pore structure and gas properties are the main factors affecting the gas slip factor.** [Tan 2015, pp. 42-45]
3. **Young's modulus and Poisson's ratio are important parameters affecting permeability and porosity under stress conditions; higher Young's modulus leads to smaller normalized permeability, while higher Poisson's ratio leads to larger normalized permeability.** [Tan 2015, pp. 51-54]
4. **The tortuosity fractal dimension Dt has a greater influence on both pressure dynamic curves and threshold pressure gradient than the pore fractal dimension Df.** [Tan 2015, pp. 63-71]
5. **Fracture stage number (Mf) and reservoir permeability/thickness are the primary factors affecting multi-stage fractured horizontal well productivity, while fracture bifurcation angle has negligible impact at normal production pressure differentials with 3-stage fractures.** [Tan 2015, pp. 106-119]
6. **End effects cause U-shaped flow distribution along the horizontal wellbore, with fractures at the heel and toe producing more than those in the middle due to less mutual interference.** [Tan 2015, pp. 102-106, 115-119]

## Connections To Other Work

- The unified capillary bundle model generalizes previous models by Tan et al. [Tan 2015, pp. 25-28], Yu and Chen [Tan 2015, pp. 25-28], Yun et al. [Tan 2015, pp. 25-28], and Cai et al. [Tan 2015, pp. 25-28].
- The tree-shaped fractal network concept originates from Wechsatolet al. [Tan 2015, pp. 17-19] who proposed tree-shaped branching structures based on plant leaf bifurcation, and was further developed by Xu and Yu [Tan 2015, pp. 17-19] for fluid flow applications.
- The stress-sensitive model builds upon Gangi's [Tan 2015, pp. 45-47] phenomenological model and Walsh's [Tan 2015, pp. 45-47] fracture roughness model.
- The threshold pressure gradient discussion references the debate initiated by Li Chuanliang [Tan 2015, pp. 57-59] regarding its existence.
- The productivity model methodology follows the complex potential superposition approach pioneered by Lang Zhaoxin [Tan 2015, pp. 12-13] and the equivalent wellbore radius method of Raghavan and Joshi [Tan 2015, pp. 12-13].
- Fractal reservoir pressure transient analysis builds on Chang and Yortsos [Tan 2015, pp. 15-17] foundational work on fractal reservoirs.

## Open Questions

2. How can the model be extended to handle two-phase flow threshold pressure gradient phenomena? [Tan 2015, pp. 57-59]
3. How should the tree-shaped fractal fracture network model be modified to account for irregular, non-idealized fracture geometries observed in real hydraulic fracturing? [Tan 2015, pp. 119-120]
4. What new experimental methods and technologies are needed to better characterize the pore structure of low-porosity, low-permeability, highly heterogeneous reservoirs? [Tan 2015, pp. 119-120]
5. How do different hydraulic fracture morphologies and combinations affect the seepage behavior of multi-stage fractured horizontal wells? [Tan 2015, pp. 119-120]
