# Review: Thermal Water Resources in Carbonate Rock Aquifers.

## One-line Summary
This review synthesizes current knowledge on thermal water resources in carbonate rock aquifers, covering the geochemical processes that create reservoir porosity and various utilizations including thermal baths, geothermal energy, and CO2 sequestration.

## Research Question
The goals of this review are to: 1) outline thermal water resources in carbonate rock aquifers as parts of deep regional groundwater flow systems; 2) evaluate dissolution processes (hypogenic speleogenesis) crucial for creating porosity and permeability; 3) compile examples of thermal baths and geothermal power stations using thermal water from carbonate rock aquifers, with a focus on Central Europe; and 4) discuss the use of geothermal installations in carbonate aquifers for CO2 sequestration [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 2-3].

## Study Area / Data
The review compiles examples from numerous global regions, with a detailed focus on Central Europe. Key study areas include:
- The Buda Thermal Karst in Budapest, Hungary, supplied by Triassic carbonate rocks [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 1-2].
- The karst aquifer system in Stuttgart, Germany, associated with Middle Triassic Upper Muschelkalk Limestone [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 8-10].
- Thermal springs in Derbyshire, UK, discharging from Dinantian limestone [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 7-8].
- Geothermal installations in the Upper Malm Limestone aquifer below the Molasse Basin in Bavaria, Germany (e.g., Unterhaching) [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 10-12].
- Geothermal systems in the Upper Muschelkalk Limestone of the Upper Rhine Graben (e.g., Riehen, Switzerland) and the Dogger Limestone of the Paris Basin, France [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 10-12].

## Methods
This is a review paper that synthesizes existing literature, field observations, and theoretical considerations. Methods discussed in the reviewed studies include:
- Hydrogeochemical and isotopic analysis to determine water origin and evolution [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 7-8].
- Multi-tracer tests to determine flow velocities and conduit connectivity in karst aquifers [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 8-10].
- Numerical modeling of limestone dissolution along fractures by cooling thermal waters [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 5-6].
- Geochemical equations describing processes like calcite dissolution, mixing corrosion, and sulphuric acid generation [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 4-5].

## Key Findings
- Deep carbonate rock aquifers are probably the most important thermal water resources outside of volcanic areas [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 1-2].
- Porosity and permeability in these aquifers are created by processes summarized as hypogenic speleogenesis, including mixing corrosion, retrograde calcite solubility, and dissolution by geogenic acids [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 1-2].
- Regional fault and fracture zones are often the most productive zones for geothermal exploitation but are difficult to locate, leading to high exploration uncertainty [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 1-2].
- Geothermal installations in deep carbonate rocks offer low CO2 emissions and land consumption [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 1-2].
- Using geothermal installations for CO2 sequestration is a promising approach, as carbonate dissolution would partly neutralize the gas and increase reservoir porosity [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 1-2].

## Limitations
- There is no detailed and reliable global assessment of thermal water resources [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 1-2].
- The heterogeneity of carbonate rock aquifers results in a relatively high exploration uncertainty for geothermal drillings [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 1-2].
- Many hypogenic caves have been reshaped by meteoric waters and are difficult to recognize, leading to underestimation of hypogenic processes [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 4-5].
- The feasibility of using geothermal installations for CO2 sequestration requires careful evaluation due to possible drawbacks like subsidence [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 12-13].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Thermal water resources in continental carbonate rock aquifers outside volcanic zones are related to deep, regional flow systems characterized by cross-formational hydraulic continuity [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 2-3].
- Water circulation in thermal karst systems is generally gravity-driven, caused by topographic gradients [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 2-3].
- The dominant origin of waters in hypogenic karst-aquifer systems is meteoric, though connate and magmatic waters are sometimes involved [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 3-4].
- The efficiency of geothermal power plants directly depends on reservoir permeability and transmissivity [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 10-10].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Temperature**: Wellhead temperature determines resource use (e.g., >37°C for hot springs, 20-37°C for bathing) [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 2-3, 7-8].
- **Flow Rate**: Critical for economic viability of geothermal installations [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 10-10].
- **Transmissivity/Permeability**: Determines pumping energy and efficiency; deep carbonate rocks often have higher permeabilities than other reservoirs [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 10-10].
- **CO2 Concentration**: Key for karstification and potential sequestration; natural carbogaseous waters can include several g/L of CO2 [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 12-13].
- **Dissolution Kinetics**: Calcite dissolution rate is highly dependent on saturation state [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 5-6].

## Reusable Claims
- Carbonate aquifers probably constitute the most important thermal water resources outside of volcanic areas [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 1-2].
- Hypogenic speleogenesis enhances vertical hydraulic conductivity and cross-formational flow, creating a positive feedback between conduit development and the expression of thermal anomalies [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 4-5].
- The efficiency of geothermal installations in carbonate rock aquifers directly depends on reservoir permeability and transmissivity, which are often higher in karstified zones [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 10-10].
- Geothermal installations in deep carbonate rock aquifers could be used for CO2 sequestration, which would increase reservoir porosity and transmissivity while partly neutralizing the gas [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 12-13].
- The exploration risk for geothermal drillings in carbonate rock aquifers is relatively high due to heterogeneity, meaning drillings might miss high-permeability zones [Goldscheider 2010, pp. 13-13].
