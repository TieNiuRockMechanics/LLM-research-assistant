# Acoustic Emission and Damage Characteristics of Granite Subjected to High Temperature.

## One-line Summary
Xu and Zhang [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2] performed uniaxial compression tests on granite from 25°C to 1000°C with simultaneous acoustic emission (AE) monitoring to analyze mechanical properties, AE characteristics, failure modes, and damage evolution laws, aiming to provide references for predicting rock failure at high temperatures.

## Research Question
How do high temperatures (25°C to 1000°C) affect the acoustic emission (AE) characteristics, failure modes, and damage evolution processes of granite under uniaxial compression? The study further aims to establish a rock damage model using AE technology and verify its feasibility [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2, 10-11].

## Study Area / Data
- **Rock type:** Granite chosen from Shandong province of China [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Composition:** Mainly comprised of feldspar, quartz, mica, and others, based on X‑ray diffraction analysis [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Specimen dimensions:** 25 mm diameter rock specimens with a height‑to‑diameter ratio of 2, prepared according to the ISRM standard [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Initial density:** Average density of granite is 2.612 g/cm³ at room temperature [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Temperature points tested:** 25°C, 200°C, 400°C, 600°C, 800°C, and 1000°C. Three samples were tested at each temperature point [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Key property data across temperatures:** Tabulated values for peak stress, peak strain, accumulated AE counts, and elastic modulus are provided [Xu 2018, pp. 6-8]:
  - 25°C: Peak stress = 79.443 MPa, Peak strain = 1.239 %, Accumulated AE counts = 22.906 × 10⁵, Elastic modulus = 7.843 GPa.
  - 200°C: Peak stress = 66.278 MPa, Peak strain = 1.065 %, Accumulated AE counts = 14.777 × 10⁵, Elastic modulus = 7.084 GPa.
  - 400°C: Peak stress = 55.781 MPa, Peak strain = 0.983 %, Accumulated AE counts = 5.609 × 10⁵, Elastic modulus = 4.981 GPa.
  - 600°C: Peak stress = 30.161 MPa, Peak strain = 1.062 %, Accumulated AE counts = 1.354 × 10⁵, Elastic modulus = 3.248 GPa.
  - 800°C: Peak stress = 31.623 MPa, Peak strain = 1.288 %, Accumulated AE counts = 25.341 × 10⁵, Elastic modulus = 1.890 GPa.
  - 1000°C: Peak stress = 24.825 MPa, Peak strain = 1.985 %, Accumulated AE counts = 10.755 × 10⁵, Elastic modulus = 1.582 GPa.

## Methods
- **Heating procedure:** Rock samples were heated to preset temperatures (200°C, 400°C, 600°C, 800°C, and 1000°C) at a heating rate of 50°C/min in a MTS652.02 high‑temperature environment furnace; the high temperature was kept for 20 minutes to ensure uniform heating [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Loading procedure:** Uniaxial compression tests were carried out at high temperatures using a MTS810 material testing system with displacement control at a loading rate of 0.005 mm/s [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **AE monitoring:** AE signals were monitored and recorded simultaneously using a PCI-2 all‑digital AE monitoring system. The AE threshold was set to 45 dB, amplifier to 40 dB, sensor operating frequency 100–400 kHz, filter parameters 100–400 kHz, and the acquisition rate was 1 MHz [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Thermal damage (D_T) definition:** Defined by the elastic modulus, assuming D_T is zero at 25°C: D_T = 1 – E_T / E₀, where E₀ is the elastic modulus at 25°C and E_T is the elastic modulus at temperature T [Xu 2018, pp. 6-8].
- **Mechanical damage (D_M) derivation:** Based on the accumulated AE ring count and Weibull statistical distribution: D_M = Ω / Ω_m, where Ω is accumulated AE ring count at strain ε, and Ω_m is the accumulated AE ring count when the rock specimen is completely damaged [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6].
- **Total damage (D) calculation:** Derived from Lemaitre’s strain equivalence principle for thermomechanical coupling: D = D_T + D_M − D_T D_M [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10].

## Key Findings
- **Mechanical properties with temperature:** Peak stress decreases significantly with rising temperature, while peak strain generally increases, with notable changes around the quartz α‑to‑β phase transition at 573°C, dividing trends into four stages [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3, 3-4].
  - 25°C to 400°C: peak stress decreased from 79.44 MPa to 55.78 MPa ( −29.78 %), peak strain decreased from 1.24 % to 0.98 % ( −20.66 %) [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
  - 400°C to 600°C: peak stress decreased rapidly to 30.16 MPa ( −45.93 % compared to 400°C), peak strain increased to 1.06 % ( +8.16 %) [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3, 3-4].
  - 600°C to 800°C: peak stress had no obvious variation, peak strain increased to 1.29 % ( +21.28 %) [Xu 2018, pp. 3-4].
  - 800°C to 1000°C: peak stress decreased to 24.82 MPa ( −21.51 % compared to 800°C), peak strain increased rapidly to 1.99 % ( +54.11 %) [Xu 2018, pp. 3-4].
- **AE characteristics:** AE ring count rate corresponds to stress‑strain curves, showing maximum values at peak stress. AE events exhibit the law of “interval burst” and “relatively calm” throughout the test, explained from an energy point of view as rock energy release followed by “pressure relief” and return to a “balanced” state [Xu 2018, pp. 10-11, 1-2].
- **Accumulated AE counts vs. temperature:** From 25°C to 600°C, accumulated AE counts decreased from 22.91 × 10⁵ to 1.35 × 10⁵ (decrease of 94.10 %, while peak stress decreased 62.03 %). From 600°C to 800°C, counts increased rapidly, reaching a maximum of 25.34 × 10⁵ at 800°C. From 800°C to 1000°C, counts declined by 57.58 % while peak stress decreased by 21.51 % [Xu 2018, pp. 3-4].
- **Failure mode evolution:** From 25°C to 1000°C, the failure mode changes from single splitting failure (25°C–200°C) → multisplitting failure → incomplete shear failure (400°C, tension and shear combined, with surface stripping and loose grains) → ideal shear failure (600°C) → double shear rupture (800°C) → complete integral failure (1000°C) [Xu 2018, pp. 3-4, 4-6].
- **Thermal damage (D_T):** Shows logistic increase with the rise of temperature (fitting function provided, R² = 0.996) [Xu 2018, pp. 6-8].
- **Mechanical damage (D_M) stages:** The process can be divided into four stages: I (initial damage stage, D_M very small), II (stable development stage, D_M increases steadily), III (accelerated development stage, D_M increases dramatically), and IV (destructive stage, D_M tends to 1). The evolution slows down as temperature increases, indicating thermal damage [Xu 2018, pp. 6-8].
- **Total damage (D):** Increases with the rise of strain, reflecting the whole rock failure process from microcrack compaction to destruction. The growth trend of total damage slows down with rising temperature, and the maximum damage evolution rate shows a logistic decline with temperature (fitting function provided, R² = 0.875) [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10, 10-11].

## Limitations
- **Specimen size constraint:** Due to the internal volume limit of the high‑temperature environment furnace, 25 mm diameter specimens (height‑to‑diameter ratio of 2) were used, which may introduce size effects [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Sample count:** Only three samples were used at each temperature point, which may affect the statistical robustness of the average values reported [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Temperature range:** Tests were limited to 1000°C; behavior at higher temperatures, which are relevant for some applications (e.g., deep geothermal), was not investigated [Xu 2018, pp. 1-2].
- **Water and phase effects:** The influence of adsorbed water, crystal water, and mineral phase transitions is discussed qualitatively, but specific quantitative mineralogical changes during heating were not directly measured or reported beyond the initial X‑ray diffraction pattern [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3, 3-4].
- **AE “calm” interpretation:** The “relatively calm” state in AE events is noted as a relative concept, not an absolute zero event, and may depend on the chosen threshold (45 dB) and sensor characteristics [Xu 2018, pp. 10-11, 2-3].
- **Model assumptions:** The mechanical damage model relies on the Weibull statistical distribution assumption, and the total damage model relies on Lemaitre’s strain equivalence principle, which may have limitations for heterogeneous and anisotropic rocks [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6, 8-10].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Weibull distribution:** The probability of rock microscopic element damage φ(ε) is described using the Weibull statistical distribution, forming the basis for the D_M derivation from AE data [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6].
- **Lemaitre’s principle:** Total thermomechanical damage (D) is calculated using Lemaitre’s strain equivalence principle, assuming additive and interactive effects of thermal and mechanical damage [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10].
- **Thermal damage baseline:** D_T is assumed to be zero at 25°C (room temperature condition) [Xu 2018, pp. 6-8].
- **Loading rate consistency:** Displacement control at a constant rate of 0.005 mm/s was used across all temperature conditions [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Heating uniformity:** A 20‑minute holding time at the target temperature was assumed sufficient to heat the samples evenly [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **AE monitoring setup:** AE threshold of 45 dB, preamplifier 40 dB, and sensor frequency 100–400 kHz were assumed adequate to capture damage‑related signals while reducing ambient noise [Xu 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **AE correspondence to damage:** Accumulated AE ring counts are assumed to correspond to the slip of dislocations, initiation of microscopic cracks, and expansion of macroscopic cracks in rock materials [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6].

- **Elastic modulus (E₀, E_T):** Elastic modulus at 25°C (E₀) and at temperature T (E_T); used to define D_T (e.g., 7.843 GPa at 25°C, 1.582 GPa at 1000°C). [Xu 2018, pp. 6-8]
- **Thermal damage (D_T):** A measure of temperature‑induced damage, defined by elastic modulus change. [Xu 2018, pp. 6-8]
- **Mechanical damage (D_M):** A measure of loading‑induced damage, defined by normalized accumulated AE counts. [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]
- **Total damage (D):** Combined thermomechanical damage variable. [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]
- **Damage evolution rate:** Rate of total damage change with axial strain. [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]
- **Weibull parameters (m, α):** Parameters in the Weibull distribution function describing microintensity. [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim 1:** For the tested granite, the AE ring count rate during uniaxial loading exhibits a pattern of “interval burst” and “relatively calm,” which can be explained by sequential energy release and “pressure relief” states within the rock. [Xu 2018, pp. 10-11]
  - **Condition/Limitation:** Observed under uniaxial compression from 25°C to 1000°C for granite; “calm” refers to relatively low AE activity, not absolute zero.
- **Claim 2:** Thermal damage (D_T) in granite, defined by elastic modulus, increases with temperature following a logistic function (R²=0.996 up to 1000°C). [Xu 2018, pp. 6-8]
  - **Condition/Limitation:** Based on experimental data from 25°C to 1000°C; the specific logistic fitting function is provided in the paper.
- **Claim 3:** Mechanical damage (D_M) derived from normalized accumulated AE ring counts can characterize rock progressive failure through initial, stable, accelerated, and destructive stages. [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]
  - **Condition/Limitation:** Assumes Weibull statistics for micro‑element damage; requires AE monitoring at sufficient fidelity.
- **Claim 4:** The total damage (D) under thermomechanical coupling can be effectively modeled using D = D_T + D_M − D_T D_M, as derived from Lemaitre’s strain equivalence principle, and its evolution rate decreases with temperature. [Xu 2018, pp. 8-10]
  - **Condition/Limitation:** Verified for granite under conditions described; requires independent determination of D_T and D_M.
- **Claim 5:** Granite failure mode transitions from tensile‑dominated splitting to shear‑dominated and finally integral failure as temperature increases to 1000°C, with the quartz α‑β phase transition at 573°C being a critical threshold for shear failure development. [Xu 2018, pp. 4-6]
  - **Condition/Limitation:** Under uniaxial compression loading; precise transition temperatures may depend on heating rate and mineral composition.

## Open Questions
- The paper does not confirm the precise mineralogical and microstructural mechanisms causing the sharp increase in accumulated AE counts from 600°C to 800°C, other than suggesting the extension of microscopic cracks to macroscopic ones and enhanced plasticity [Xu 2018, pp. 3-4].
- The specific energy transfer mechanism behind the “interval burst” and “relatively calm” AE phenomenon before peak stress is only explained conceptually; quantitative energy partitioning analysis has not been confirmed in this study [Xu 2018, pp. 10-11].
- The relationship between AE frequency‑amplitude characteristics and the identified damage stages is not explored in this paper [connection not confirmed].
- The applicability of the established damage evolution model to triaxial stress states or different lithologies remains an open question [connection not confirmed].
