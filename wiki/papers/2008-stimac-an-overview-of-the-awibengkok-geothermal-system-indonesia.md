# An Overview of the Awibengkok Geothermal System, Indonesia.

## One-line Summary
The Awibengkok (Salak) geothermal system is a large, liquid-dominated, fracture-controlled reservoir hosted in andesitic-to-rhyodacitic volcanic rocks with benign chemistry and temperatures ranging from 240 to 312 °C [Stimac 2008, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
The paper provides a comprehensive geological and reservoir characterization of the Awibengkok geothermal system to establish a conceptual model for its management and to identify potential extensions [Stimac 2008, pp. 2-5].

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Awibengkok (Salak) geothermal field, located 60 km south of Jakarta on the southwestern flank of Gunung Salak volcano in West Java, Indonesia [Stimac 2008, pp. 1-2]. Data sources include:
*   Geological mapping and geochronology (K-Ar, 40Ar/39Ar, 14C dating) [Stimac 2008, pp. 5-6].
*   Subsurface data from 81 deep wells, including drill cuttings, spot cores, and 1067 m of continuous core from well Awi 1-2 [Stimac 2008, pp. 9-10].
*   Geophysical surveys: magnetotelluric (MT), time domain electromagnetic (TDEM), and microseismic monitoring [Stimac 2008, pp. 2-5, 21-22].
*   Geochemical analyses of fluids and rocks, including fluid inclusion studies [Stimac 2008, pp. 18-19].
*   Petrophysical measurements (porosity, permeability) on core samples [Stimac 2008, pp. 13-14].
*   Borehole image logs (BHTV, FMS, FMI, XRMI) [Stimac 2008, pp. 14-17].

## Methods
The study integrated multiple geoscientific methods:
*   **Geological Mapping & Geochronology:** Surface mapping constrained by aerial photos and dating to establish volcanic stratigraphy and structure [Stimac 2008, pp. 5-6].
*   **Subsurface Characterization:** Analysis of drill cuttings, core descriptions, and well logs (resistivity, gamma-ray) to define lithology and stratigraphy [Stimac 2008, pp. 9-10].
*   **Geophysical Surveys:** MT and TDEM surveys to map the smectite clay cap (low-resistivity zone) and infer reservoir extensions [Stimac 2008, pp. 21-22].
*   **Petrophysics:** Helium porosity and air permeability measurements on core samples; mercury injection capillary porosimetry (MICP) [Stimac 2008, pp. 13-14].
*   **Structural Analysis:** Interpretation of surface faults, subsurface offsets on marker horizons (e.g., Rhyodacite Marker), and fracture orientations from borehole image logs [Stimac 2008, pp. 14-17].
*   **Hydrothermal Alteration Studies:** Methylene blue analysis, X-ray diffraction (XRD), and petrography to map alteration mineral assemblages and zonation [Stimac 2008, pp. 17-18].
*   **Fluid Geochemistry:** Analysis of major ion chemistry, gas composition, and application of geothermometers to characterize reservoir fluids and define fluid sectors [Stimac 2008, pp. 18-19].
*   **Microseismic Monitoring:** A 10-station digital array used to track seismicity, define stress state, and infer permeability trends [Stimac 2008, pp. 23-25].
*   **Conceptual Modeling:** Integration of all datasets to develop a dynamic reservoir model [Stimac 2008, pp. 25-27].

## Key Findings
*   The Awibengkok reservoir is a liquid-dominated system with initial temperatures of 240–312 °C, benign fluid chemistry (salinity ~1.3 wt.%), and low-to-moderate non-condensable gas (NCG) content (<0.4 wt.% except in shallow zones) [Stimac 2008, pp. 1-2, 18-19].
*   The reservoir is hosted in andesitic-to-rhyodacitic volcanic rocks overlying Miocene marine sediments, with a stratigraphy divided into four major formations [Stimac 2008, pp. 9-10].
*   Permeability is primarily controlled by a highly interconnected network of open to partially sealed fractures, with a dominant N- to NE-trend [Stimac 2008, pp. 13-14, 14-17].
*   Matrix porosity averages 10.6% and declines with depth; matrix permeability is low (geometric mean 0.026 md) [Stimac 2008, pp. 13-14].
*   Hydrothermal alteration shows a zonation from a shallow smectite-rich argillic cap (low resistivity) to deeper propylitic and phyllic zones [Stimac 2008, pp. 17-18].
*   The field can be subdivided into four distinct fluid sectors (Western, Central, Eastern, Far Eastern Cells) based on temperature, chemistry, and tracer returns, bounded by major faults [Stimac 2008, pp. 18-19].
*   Deep upflow of high-temperature fluid occurs in the Western Cell, associated with shallow intrusions. Fluids ascend along N-NE structures, mix in the Central Cell, and flow laterally to the shallower Eastern Cell [Stimac 2008, pp. 25-27].
*   MT/TDEM surveys identified low-resistivity anomalies extending north and northwest of the proven reservoir, interpreted as possible extensions of the clay cap [Stimac 2008, pp. 21-22].
*   Drilling in the northwest extension (Cianten Caldera) confirmed smectite alteration but found subcommercial temperatures, indicating the area has cooled [Stimac 2008, pp. 29-30].

## Limitations
*   Dense tropical vegetation and rough terrain made detailed surface geological mapping difficult, requiring reliance on aerial photos [Stimac 2008, pp. 5-6].
*   The extent and connectivity of potential reservoir extensions to the north and west are not fully defined and require further drilling [Stimac 2008, pp. 29-30].
*   The conceptual model is based on integration of diverse datasets, but some flow paths (e.g., along NW/E-W faults) were not validated by tracer returns [Stimac 2008, pp. 17-18].
*   Petrogenetic studies have not been conducted on the key dacitic-to-rhyolitic lavas that dominate recent volcanism [Stimac 2008, pp. 6-9].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   The low-resistivity anomaly (<10 Ω-m) mapped by MT surveys is primarily caused by smectite and smectite-rich mixed layer clay alteration [Stimac 2008, pp. 21-22].
*   The local maximum horizontal stress is oriented NNE (N20°E), creating anisotropic permeability favoring N-NE trending fractures [Stimac 2008, pp. 14-17].
*   The propylitic alteration assemblage (epidote, illite, quartz, chlorite) corresponds to reservoir temperatures of 240–270 °C [Stimac 2008, pp. 17-18].
*   The commercial reservoir is underlain by low-permeability Miocene marine sedimentary rocks that act as a lower boundary [Stimac 2008, pp. 9-10].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Temperature:** 240–312 °C (reservoir), <180 °C (cap) [Stimac 2008, pp. 1-2, 17-18].
*   **Porosity:** Mean 10.6% (matrix) [Stimac 2008, pp. 13-14].
*   **Permeability:** Geometric mean 0.026 md (matrix); fracture-dominated bulk permeability [Stimac 2008, pp. 13-14].
*   **Fluid Salinity:** ~1.3 wt.% NaCl equivalent [Stimac 2008, pp. 18-19].
*   **Non-Condensable Gas (NCG):** <0.4 wt.% in liquid reservoir, up to 10 wt.% in shallow steam cap [Stimac 2008, pp. 18-19, 20-21].
*   **Resistivity:** <10 Ω-m (clay cap), >20 Ω-m (propylitic reservoir) [Stimac 2008, pp. 21-22].
*   **Stress Orientation:** Maximum horizontal stress ~N20°E [Stimac 2008, pp. 14-17].

## Reusable Claims
*   In volcanic-hosted geothermal systems, a smectite-rich argillic alteration cap is characterized by low electrical resistivity (<10 Ω-m) and can be effectively mapped using MT/TDEM surveys to infer the extent of the underlying reservoir [Stimac 2008, pp. 21-22].
*   Matrix porosity in volcanic geothermal reservoirs typically declines with depth due to compaction of fragmental deposits and hydrothermal alteration, with fragmental rocks (breccias, tuffs) exhibiting higher average porosity than crystalline rocks (lavas, intrusions) [Stimac 2008, pp. 13-14].
*   Permeability in liquid-dominated volcanic geothermal systems is primarily controlled by an interconnected fracture network, with orientations strongly influenced by the local stress field, leading to anisotropic flow [Stimac 2008, pp. 13-14, 14-17].
*   Fluid chemistry and temperature can be used to subdivide a geothermal reservoir into distinct sectors or cells, which are often bounded by major faults that act as barriers or conduits [Stimac 2008, pp. 18-19].
*   Microseismic monitoring in geothermal fields is spatially correlated with injection activities and can provide insights into stress state, permeability trends, and deep fluid circulation pathways [Stimac 2008, pp. 23-25].

## Open Questions
*   What is the full extent and injectivity of the low-resistivity anomalies identified north and northwest of the proven reservoir? [Stimac 2008, pp. 29-30].
*   What are the detailed petrogenetic relationships between the andesitic host rocks and the younger, economically important dacitic-to-rhyolitic intrusions and lavas? [Stimac 2008, pp. 6-9].
*   How do the NW- and E-W-trending fractures contribute to inter-compartment fluid flow, and what is their long-term permeability evolution under exploitation? [Stimac 2008, pp. 17-18].
*   What is the ultimate heat source and longevity of the system, given the evidence for deep (5 km) pressure connection and cooling in peripheral areas like the Cianten Caldera? [Stimac 2008, pp. 23-25, 29-30].
