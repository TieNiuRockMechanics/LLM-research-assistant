# Influence of Different Thermal Cycling Treatments on the Physical, Mechanical and Transport Properties of Granite.

## One-line Summary
This study compares the effects of slow cooling and rapid quenching thermal cycling treatments on granite, finding that rapid quenching induces more severe damage through thermal gradient cracking, leading to greater reductions in strength and stiffness and a more significant increase in permeability.

## Research Question
How do different thermal cycling treatments (slow heating followed by slow cooling versus rapid quenching) affect the physical, mechanical, and transport properties of granite, and what are the underlying microstructural damage mechanisms?

## Study Area / Data
The study used medium-fine grained monzonitic granite collected from Shandong province, China. The modal composition is approximately 35% plagioclase, 40% k-feldspar, 20% quartz, and 5% biotite [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2]. Cylindrical cores (50 mm diameter, 100 mm length) and discs (50 mm diameter, 25 mm thickness) were prepared for testing [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Thermal Cycling Procedure**: Samples were heated at 2 °C/min to target temperatures (100–600 °C, in 100 °C intervals), held for over 2 hours, then either slow-cooled in the furnace (1–2 °C/min) or rapid-quenched in water (~20 °C, ~100 °C/min cooling rate) [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2].
- **Non-destructive Tests**: Mass, volume, P-wave velocity (using an NM-4B analyzer), and permeability (using a Smart Perm III Ultra-Low Permeameter with nitrogen gas and the pressure pulse decay method) were measured [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Destructive Tests**: Uniaxial compression and Brazilian splitting tests were conducted at a constant strain rate of 10⁻⁵ s⁻¹ to determine uniaxial compressive strength (UCS), Young's modulus, and tensile strength [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3].
- **Microstructural Analysis**: Optical microscope observations (cross- and plane-polarized light) were performed on thin sections (30 μm thick) prepared from samples subjected to the same thermal treatments [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
1.  **Physical Properties**: Mass loss increases with temperature due to water loss. Volume expansion increases, especially above 400 °C, with rapid quenching causing greater expansion. Density decreases with temperature, with a sharper reduction after 500 °C; rapid-quenched samples have lower density than slow-cooled ones [Jin 2018, pp. 3-4].
2.  **P-wave Velocity**: Decreases with increasing temperature for both treatments. The reduction is greater for rapid quenching (73.4% at 600 °C) than for slow cooling (69.6% at 600 °C). The difference between the two methods increases with temperature up to 500 °C [Jin 2018, pp. 4-5].
3.  **Mechanical Properties**: UCS, Young's modulus, and tensile strength all decrease with rising temperature. Rapid quenching causes a more significant reduction in these properties compared to slow cooling. For example, at 600 °C, UCS is reduced to 45% (slow cooling) and 36% (rapid quenching) of room temperature values [Jin 2018, pp. 5-6].
4.  **Permeability**: Increases with temperature. For slow cooling, a critical temperature of 500 °C marks a sharp increase (35.6 times at 600 °C). For rapid quenching, the critical temperature is lower (400 °C), and the increase is more dramatic (79.3 times at 600 °C) [Jin 2018, pp. 6-7].
5.  **Microstructure**: Thermal cycling induces intragranular, intergranular, and transgranular microcracks. Rapid quenching produces visible microcracks at lower temperatures (200 °C) and more severe, wider cracking at higher temperatures compared to slow cooling [Jin 2018, pp. 7-8].

## Limitations
- The study acknowledges that the microcrack distribution pattern during rapid quenching requires more detailed investigation [Jin 2018, pp. 8-10].
- The research focuses on a single granite type from one location; results may vary for other rock types or granite compositions.
- The permeability tests were conducted at a low confining pressure (5 MPa), which may not fully represent in-situ reservoir conditions.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The thermal diffusivity of granite was assumed to be 10⁻⁶ m²/s for calculating temperature equilibrium time [Jin 2018, pp. 8-10].
- The Klinkenberg effect was evaluated and found to be negligible at the mean pore pressure (~2.3 MPa) used in permeability tests [Jin 2018, pp. 2-3].
- The heating rate (2 °C/min) was controlled to avoid significant thermal gradients during the heating phase [Jin 2018, pp. 1-2].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables**: Peak temperature (100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600 °C), cooling method (slow cooling vs. rapid quenching).
- **Dependent Variables**: Mass loss rate, volume expansion rate, density, P-wave velocity, uniaxial compressive strength (UCS), Young's modulus, tensile strength, permeability.
- **Controlled Parameters**: Heating rate (2 °C/min), hold time at peak temperature (>2 hours), sample dimensions, initial sample properties.

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim**: Rapid quenching induces more severe thermal damage in granite than slow cooling, characterized by lower density, P-wave velocity, and strength, but higher permeability.
  - **Condition**: For medium-fine grained monzonitic granite heated to temperatures between 100 and 600 °C.
  - **Limitation**: The magnitude of the difference increases with temperature, becoming most pronounced above 400 °C.
- **Claim**: A critical temperature exists where granite permeability increases sharply; this critical temperature is lower for rapid quenching (400 °C) than for slow cooling (500 °C).
  - **Condition**: Based on experimental results at ambient pressure.
  - **Limitation**: The critical temperature may shift under different confining pressures or for different rock types.
- **Claim**: The difference in P-wave velocity and UCS between slow-cooled and rapid-quenched granite increases with temperature up to 500 °C, reflecting greater thermal gradient cracking at higher temperatures.
  - **Condition**: For the tested granite and temperature range.
  - **Limitation**: The trend may not hold beyond 600 °C or for rocks with different mineralogies.

## Open Questions
- How does the spatial distribution of microcracks (e.g., surface vs. interior) differ quantitatively between slow cooling and rapid quenching?
- How do the observed property changes translate to coupled thermo-hydro-mechanical behavior in fractured geothermal reservoirs under realistic stress conditions?
- What is the effect of repeated thermal cycling (not just a single cycle) on the long-term evolution of granite properties?
- How do heating rate and cooling rate interact to influence the overall damage and permeability evolution?
