# Triggering of the Pohang, Korea, Earthquake (Mw 5.5) by Enhanced Geothermal System Stimulation.

## One-line Summary
Forensic analysis concludes that the 2017 Mw 5.5 Pohang earthquake was triggered by high-pressure fluid injection during enhanced geothermal system (EGS) stimulations, which activated a previously unmapped fault.

## Research Question
Did the hydraulic stimulations conducted at the Pohang Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) project trigger the 15 November 2017 Mw 5.5 Pohang earthquake?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Pohang EGS site in southeastern Korea, located within the Pohang basin. Data include:
- Drilling and stimulation data from two wells, PX-1 and PX-2, drilled to depths of ~4.2 km and ~4.3 km, respectively [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 1-2].
- Seismicity records from the Korea Meteorological Administration (KMA) and a local seismic network, including 519 earthquakes detected between 2009 and 2017 [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 5-6].
- Borehole geophysical logs, including dipole sonic logging from PX-2 [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 4-5].
- Drill cuttings and mud logs indicating fault gouge and breccia at ~3800 m depth in PX-2 [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 4-5].
- Injection parameters: maximum wellhead pressure of 89.2 MPa in PX-2 and 27.7 MPa in PX-1; net injected volume of 5841 m³ [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- Reanalysis of seismicity using a 1D velocity model, waveform cross-correlation, and a matched-filter technique to identify events [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 5-6].
- Determination of precise earthquake hypocenters and magnitudes (local ML and moment Mw) [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 5-6].
- Calculation of 53 focal mechanisms for events during and after stimulations [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 7-8].
- Stress field analysis using borehole data (dipole sonic logging) and regional focal mechanisms to develop preferred and regional stress models [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 4-5].
- Hydrogeologic modeling of pore pressure diffusion from injection intervals, considering two fault models (Case A and Case B) [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 10-11].
- Analysis of Coulomb failure stress changes from the 2011 Tohoku and 2016 Gyeongju earthquakes [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 9-10].

## Key Findings
1.  The Mw 5.5 Pohang earthquake was triggered by EGS stimulations, specifically by high-pressure injection into the PX-2 well, which activated a previously unmapped fault [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 1-2, 12-13].
2.  Seismicity induced by injection into PX-2 formed a tabular zone (strike 214°, dip 43°) that intersected the well at ~3800 m depth, corresponding to the mainshock fault plane [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 7-8].
3.  The mainshock was preceded by foreshocks that extended the fault zone activated by prior PX-2 stimulations and had similar focal mechanisms [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 8-9].
4.  The fault plane was critically stressed in the prevailing east-northeast–west-southwest compressive stress field, making it susceptible to slip from small pore pressure increases (>0.01 MPa) [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 9-10].
5.  Hydrogeologic modeling suggests pore pressure increases of ~0.07 MPa at the mainshock hypocenter by November 2017, sufficient to trigger slip [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 10-11].
6.  The earthquake's magnitude (Mw 5.5) contradicts the hypothesis that maximum induced earthquake size is bounded by injected volume, as the volume was far smaller than predicted for such an event [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 11-12].

## Limitations
- Comprehensive hydrogeologic modeling of fluid pressure perturbations was not fully completed and remains ongoing [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 10-11].
- The stress models rely on assumptions about friction coefficient (0.6) and hydrostatic fluid pressure [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 4-5].
- Some seismicity data, particularly from the mud loss event, could not be precisely located [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 6-7].
- The analysis was conducted after the mainshock; real-time risk assessment during the stimulation sequence was not performed [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 12-13].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The crust is in a state of frictional equilibrium governed by slip on faults with a coefficient of friction of 0.6 [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 4-5].
- An undisturbed, hydrostatic fluid pressure regime existed prior to stimulation [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 10-11].
- The preferred stress model corresponds to a critically stressed reverse stress state at 4.2 km depth [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 4-5].
- Fault zones have representative hydraulic properties based on analogous lithologies and fault zone models [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 10-11].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Injection Pressure**: Maximum wellhead pressure of 89.2 MPa in PX-2 [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 2-3].
- **Injection Volume**: Net volume of 5841 m³ remained in subsurface after stimulations [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 2-3].
- **Stress Orientation**: SHmax azimuth of 077° from dipole sonic logging [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 4-5].
- **Stress Magnitude**: S1=243 MPa, S2=120 MPa, Sv=106 MPa at 4.2 km depth [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 4-5].
- **Pore Pressure Increase**: ~0.07 MPa at mainshock hypocenter by November 2017 [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 10-11].
- **Fault Geometry**: Strike 214°, dip 43° for PX-2 seismicity plane [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 7-8].

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim**: High-pressure fluid injection during EGS stimulation can activate unmapped faults and trigger large earthquakes, even months after injection ceases.
  - **Condition**: When injection occurs into critically stressed rock containing pre-existing faults.
  - **Limitation**: Based on the specific Pohang case; generalizability requires further study.
  - **Source**: [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 1-2, 8-9, 12-13]
- **Claim**: The maximum magnitude of an induced earthquake may not be bounded by injected volume, as demonstrated by the Pohang earthquake exceeding volume-based predictions.
  - **Condition**: In tectonically stressed regions where triggered ruptures can grow beyond the stimulated volume.
  - **Limitation**: Contrasts with the volume hypothesis; applicability may depend on fault maturity and stress state.
  - **Source**: [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 11-12]
- **Claim**: Pore pressure increases on the order of 0.01 MPa can trigger slip on critically stressed faults.
  - **Condition**: For faults with friction coefficients ~0.6 and near-failure stress states.
  - **Limitation**: Threshold may vary with fault properties and stress conditions.
  - **Source**: [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 10-11]

## Connections To Other Work
- The study references the 2016 Mw 5.4 Gyeongju earthquake, concluding it did not trigger the Pohang event due to negligible static stress changes [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 6-7, 9-10].
- It compares the Pohang case to historical induced seismicity at the Rocky Mountain Arsenal (Denver) and Basel EGS projects, noting similar delays between injection and large events [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 8-9].
- The analysis engages with debates on maximum magnitude scaling, contrasting the volume hypothesis (McGarr, 2014) with statistical sampling models (van der Elst et al., 2016) [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 11-12].
- It builds on regional stress studies (Soh et al., 2018) and local borehole stress measurements (Kim et al., 2017) [Ellsworth 2019, pp. 3-5].
