# Physical Properties and Brittle Strength of Thermally Cracked Granite under Confinement.

## One-line Summary
Thermal cracking significantly increased granite permeability but did not reduce its brittle strength under 30 MPa effective confinement, and no water weakening effect was observed.

## Research Question
Why does thermal crack damage seem to have a large effect on rock properties under low confinement, while at high confinement, it seems not to matter? [Wang 2013, pp. 2-3]

## Study Area / Data
The material studied is La Peyratte granite, a granodiorite with an average grain size of 800 µm. Its modal composition is 38.5% plagioclase, 28.5% quartz, 20% K-feldspar, 8% biotite, and 5% muscovite. [Wang 2013, pp. 2-3] Four cylindrical specimens (diameter 40 mm, length 80 mm) were cored from the same block. Two samples were heat-treated at room pressure up to 700°C at a rate of 5°C/min, kept at 700°C for 2 hours, and then slowly cooled. [Wang 2013, pp. 2-3] The porosity of the intact specimen was <1%, while the thermally cracked specimen was about 3%. [Wang 2013, pp. 2-3]

## Methods
Experiments were performed in a triaxial cell allowing confining and pore pressures up to 100 MPa and deviatoric stresses up to 680 MPa. [Wang 2013, pp. 2-3] Permeability was measured using steady-state (for k > 10⁻¹⁸ m²) and oscillating pulse methods. [Wang 2013, pp. 3-5] Elastic wave velocities (P and S) were measured using an ultrasonic pulse transmission technique with 16 piezoelectric transducers. [Wang 2013, pp. 3-5] Acoustic emissions (AE) were recorded to locate hypocenters. [Wang 2013, pp. 3-5] The experimental procedure involved first increasing effective confining pressure (Pₑ = Pc - Pp) to 90 MPa under hydrostatic conditions, then decreasing it to 30 MPa, and deforming the sample to failure at a constant strain rate of 2×10⁻⁶ s⁻¹. [Wang 2013, pp. 5-6] Pore pressure was held constant at 5 MPa (nominally drained conditions). [Wang 2013, pp. 5-6]

## Key Findings
1.  **Permeability**: At low effective pressure (5 MPa), permeability of heat-treated specimens was 4–5 orders of magnitude larger than that of intact specimens. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2]
2.  **Brittle Strength**: At the experimental conditions (strain rate 2×10⁻⁶ s⁻¹, 30 MPa effective pressure), thermal cracking had no significant influence on the brittle strength (~500 ± 25 MPa differential stress). [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2, 6-8]
3.  **Water Weakening**: No obvious water weakening effect was observed on mechanical strength. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2, 6-8]
4.  **Elastic Anisotropy**: With increasing stress, elastic anisotropy appeared at lower differential stress in heat-treated specimens than in intact ones. However, close to failure, the magnitude of P wave anisotropy was approximately the same for both types. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2, 6-8]
5.  **Strain Localization**: AE hypocenter locations and P wave velocity anisotropy demonstrate that strain localization started at the onset of dilatancy (C₀) for heat-treated specimens, but later in intact specimens. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2, 8-9]
6.  **Crack Density at Failure**: Inverting wave velocities for crack density showed failure was reached for vertical crack densities of ~0.35 for dry specimens and possibly ~0.5 for water-saturated specimens. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2, 9-11]

## Limitations
*   The absence of a water weakening effect may be due to partial saturation at failure, as the characteristic hydraulic diffusion time (~600 s) was comparable to or longer than the final deformation stage (5-10 min). [Wang 2013, pp. 9-11]
*   The transverse isotropic (TI) approximation used for crack density inversion is insufficient to fully describe the elastic fabric, as anisotropy was observed in the horizontal plane (basal plane) before failure. [Wang 2013, pp. 8-9, 12-13]
*   The study was conducted at a single strain rate (2×10⁻⁶ s⁻¹) and confinement (30 MPa effective pressure). The results may not be universal for other conditions. [Wang 2013, pp. 12-12]

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   Experiments were conducted under nominally drained conditions with constant pore pressure (5 MPa). [Wang 2013, pp. 5-6]
*   The no-interaction approximation (NIA) was used to invert elastic wave velocities for crack density and aspect ratio. [Wang 2013, pp. 9-9, 12-13]
*   For the NIA, cracks were assumed to be penny-shaped and identical in size and shape within each population. [Wang 2013, pp. 12-13]
*   The thermal cracking process was assumed to arise from differential thermal expansions of neighboring grains and the quartz α-β transformation above 550°C. [Wang 2013, pp. 2-3]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Effective Confining Pressure (Pₑ)**: Pc - Pp. Hydrostatic tests up to 90 MPa; triaxial tests at 30 MPa. [Wang 2013, pp. 5-6]
*   **Differential Stress (Q)**: σ₁ - σ₃. [Wang 2013, pp. 5-6]
*   **Strain Rate**: 2×10⁻⁶ s⁻¹. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2]
*   **Heat Treatment Temperature**: 700°C. [Wang 2013, pp. 2-3]
*   **Permeability (k)**: Measured in m². [Wang 2013, pp. 3-5]
*   **Elastic Wave Velocities (Vp, Vs)**: Measured in m/s. [Wang 2013, pp. 3-5]
*   **Crack Density (η)**: Dimensionless parameter η = Nc³/V. [Wang 2013, pp. 9-9]
*   **Crack Aspect Ratio (χ)**: χ = w/c, where w is half-aperture and c is crack radius. [Wang 2013, pp. 9-9]

## Reusable Claims
*   Under 30 MPa effective confinement, thermal cracking up to 700°C does not reduce the brittle strength of fine-grained granite at a strain rate of 2×10⁻⁶ s⁻¹. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2, 6-8]
*   Thermal cracking increases permeability by 4-5 orders of magnitude at low effective pressure, but the effect diminishes with increasing pressure. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2, 3-5]
*   In thermally cracked granite, strain localization (as indicated by AE clustering and P-wave anisotropy) initiates at the onset of dilatancy (C₀), whereas in intact rock it initiates later. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2, 8-9]
*   The absence of a water weakening effect in low-permeability rocks at strain rates of 10⁻⁶ s⁻¹ or higher may be due to incomplete saturation during the final, rapid stage of deformation. [Wang 2013, pp. 9-11]
*   Elastic wave velocity and permeability probe different populations of cracks within the microstructure: velocities are sensitive to short, narrow intragranular cracks, while permeability is sensitive to long, opened intergranular cracks. [Wang 2013, pp. 9-9, 12-12]

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study builds on the foundational work of Walsh [1965a, 1965b, 1965c] on the influence of cracks on elastic rock deformation. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2]
*   It revisits the observation by Friedman et al. [1979] that thermally cracked granodiorite was as strong as unheated samples under 50 MPa confinement. [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2]
*   The experimental setup and acoustic system are described in detail in Brantut et al. [2011] and Ougier-Simonin et al. [2011a]. [Wang 2013, pp. 2-3, 3-5]
*   The crack density inversion method follows the approach of Schubnel et al. [2003, 2006] and Fortin et al. [2010]. [Wang 2013, pp. 9-11, 12-13]
*   The discussion on the absence of water weakening relates to studies on subcritical crack growth and stress corrosion (e.g., Atkinson and Meredith [1987]; Baud et al. [2000]). [Wang 2013, pp. 1-2, 9-11]

## Open Questions
*   What is the threshold pressure at which the brittle failure envelopes of unheated and thermally cracked samples of the same rock start to overlap? [Wang 2013, pp. 12-12]
*   How do confinement, initial grain size, strain rate, and cyclic loading influence the effect of initial crack damage on peak strength? [Wang 2013, pp. 12-12]
*   Why does the peak stress remain unchanged despite a reduced Young's modulus and increased initial damage in heat-treated specimens? Potential explanations include stress shielding and the role of pre-existing, longer flaws. [Wang 2013, pp. 9-11]
