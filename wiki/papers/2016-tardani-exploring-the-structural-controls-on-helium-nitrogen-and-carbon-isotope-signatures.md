# Exploring the Structural Controls on Helium, Nitrogen and Carbon Isotope Signatures in Hydrothermal Fluids along an Intra-Arc Fault System.

## One-line Summary
This study reveals that the regional distribution of the Liquiñe-Ofqui Fault System (LOFS) and the Arc-oblique Long-lived Basement Fault System (ALFS) exerts a first-order control on the helium, nitrogen, and carbon isotope signatures of hydrothermal fluids in the Southern Volcanic Zone of the Chilean Andes by influencing magma degassing, crustal assimilation, and fluid residence times.

## Research Question
How do the principal (LOFS) and subsidiary (ALFS) fault systems in the Southern Volcanic Zone control the isotopic composition (³He/4He, δ¹³C-CO₂, δ¹⁵N) of hydrothermal fluids, and what processes (e.g., crustal contamination, meteoric mixing, degassing) are linked to these structural controls?

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the central part of the Southern Volcanic Zone (SVZ) in the Chilean Andes, spanning approximately 37°S to 41°S latitude. The data comprise new helium, carbon, and nitrogen isotope analyses from gas samples collected at 23 volcanic/geothermal localities, including 5 fumaroles, 7 bubbling gas samples, and 11 hot springs (water-dissolved gas). These sites are spatially associated with either the NNE-striking, intra-arc, dextral strike-slip Liquiñe-Ofqui Fault System (LOFS) or the NW-striking, arc-oblique, sinistral-reverse Arc-oblique Long-lived Basement Fault System (ALFS) [Tardani 2016, pp. 1-2, 2-4, 4-5].

## Methods
Gas and water samples were collected using titanium tubes for fumaroles and pre-evacuated alkaline glass containers for hot springs. ³He/4He ratios were measured using a noble gas mass spectrometer (Helix-SFT) and corrected for atmospheric contamination using ⁴He/²⁰Ne ratios. δ¹³C and δ¹⁵N values were measured using a conventional gas source mass spectrometer (IsoPrim100). Chemical compositions (He, CH₄, N₂, O₂, Ar, H₂S, CO₂) were measured using a quadrupole mass spectrometer [Tardani 2016, pp. 4-5].

## Key Findings
1.  **Isotopic Variability:** The dataset shows a wide range of air-corrected ³He/4He (Rc/Ra) ratios (3.68 to 7.67 Ra), δ¹³C-CO₂ values (-7.44‰ to -49.41‰), and δ¹⁵N values (0.02‰ to 4.93‰) [Tardani 2016, pp. 5-5, 5-7].
2.  **Structural Control on Mantle Signature:** The highest, MORB-like Rc/Ra values (e.g., ~7.5 Ra) are found in fumaroles associated with the transtensional "horsetail" termination of the LOFS in the north, indicating minimal crustal contamination and efficient vertical fluid pathways [Tardani 2016, pp. 5-7, 9-11].
3.  **Crustal Contamination along ALFS:** Lower Rc/Ra values and higher ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratios in associated lavas are observed in systems controlled by NW-striking ALFS faults, indicating increased crustal assimilation of radiogenic ⁴He and sediment-derived nitrogen [Tardani 2016, pp. 5-7, 9-11, 14-16].
4.  **Secondary Processes:** Many hot spring samples, especially in the central segment, show evidence of CO₂ loss and ¹³C fractionation consistent with calcite precipitation during cooling/mixing, and mixing with meteoric water (air/ASW). These processes are enhanced along structures with lower vertical permeability [Tardani 2016, pp. 12-13, 13-13].
5.  **Conceptual Model:** The LOFS, with high vertical permeability, transmits more primitive, MORB-like signatures. The ALFS, misoriented for reactivation, promotes longer fluid residence times, enhanced fluid-rock interaction, and higher degrees of crustal contamination [Tardani 2016, pp. 13-14, 16-16].

## Limitations
-   Possible atmospheric contamination during sampling or analysis for some samples (e.g., samples RB, PU, RU) [Tardani 2016, pp. 5-5].
-   Difficulty in precisely quantifying the contribution of crustal vs. subducted sediment-derived nitrogen due to air contamination effects on N₂/He ratios [Tardani 2016, pp. 11-12].
-   The study focuses on regional-scale structural controls; local-scale fault-fracture mesh geometry is inferred but not directly mapped at all sample sites.

## Assumptions / Conditions
-   End-member isotopic compositions for mixing models: MORB (³He/4He = 8±1 Ra, δ¹⁵N = -5‰), sediments (δ¹⁵N = +7‰), crust (³He/4He ≈ 0.05 Ra) [Tardani 2016, pp. 9-11, 11-12].
-   Fractionation factors for calcite-CO₂ equilibrium are temperature-dependent (e.g., -10‰ at 25°C, 0‰ at 192°C) [Tardani 2016, pp. 13-13].
-   The ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr ratios in lavas are a proxy for the degree of crustal assimilation in the magmatic source region [Tardani 2016, pp. 5-5].

## Reusable Claims
-   In arc settings, the regional structural framework (e.g., orientation and kinematics of major fault systems) is a primary control on the isotopic signature of hydrothermal fluids by governing magma ascent pathways, crustal residence times, and fluid-rock interaction [Tardani 2016, pp. 1-2, 16-16].
-   Transtensional fault terminations (e.g., "horsetail" structures) promote high vertical permeability, allowing rapid ascent of mantle-derived volatiles with minimal crustal contamination, preserving MORB-like ³He/4He signatures [Tardani 2016, pp. 13-14].
-   Arc-oblique, transpressive basement faults misoriented to the regional stress field inhibit vertical permeability, leading to increased fluid residence times, enhanced crustal assimilation (higher ⁴He, sediment-derived N₂), and promoting secondary processes like calcite precipitation [Tardani 2016, pp. 14-16].
-   Low CO₂/³He ratios and light δ¹³C-CO₂ values in hydrothermal fluids can be explained by CO₂ loss via calcite precipitation during cooling and mixing, a process facilitated by longer fluid pathways and reaction times along certain fault structures [Tardani 2016, pp. 13-13].

## Connections To Other Work
-   Builds on studies using He-C-N isotopes to trace volatile sources and recycling in subduction zones (e.g., Sano and Marty, 1995; Fischer et al., 2002) [Tardani 2016, pp. 1-2].
-   Extends work on structural controls of fluid flow in geothermal systems (e.g., Sibson, 1996; Rowland and Sibson, 2004) to a regional isotopic dataset [Tardani 2016, pp. 2-2].
-   Integrates with prior geochemical surveys in the SVZ that noted variable ³He/4He ratios (e.g., Hilton et al., 1993; Ray et al., 2009) by providing a structural explanation for the variability [Tardani 2016, pp. 2-2].
-   Correlates fluid isotope data with ⁸⁷Sr/⁸⁶Sr in lavas, linking fluid signatures to magmatic source characteristics controlled by the same structures (e.g., Hildreth and Moorbath, 1988) [Tardani 2016, pp. 5-5].

## Open Questions
-   How do the specific geometries and connectivity of local fault-fracture meshes within the LOFS and ALFS influence the precise pathways and mixing processes of fluids?
-   What is the relative contribution of subducted sediments versus continental crustal rocks to the nitrogen and carbon budgets in these fluids?
-   Can the observed isotopic-structural relationships be used to prospect for geothermal resources or understand ore-forming processes in similar arc settings?
