# Rheological Heterogeneity, Mechanical Anisotropy and Deformation of the Continental Lithosphere.

## One-line Summary
The paper reviews how pre-existing rheological heterogeneities and mechanical anisotropy, primarily from the lithospheric mantle, control the large-scale deformation of continents, challenging models of a homogeneous, isotropic lithosphere.

## Research Question
How do pre-existing rheological heterogeneities and mechanical anisotropy influence the deformation of continents, and what is the relative importance of crustal versus mantle properties in governing lithospheric behavior?

## Study Area / Data
The study synthesizes geological and geophysical observations from multiple continental domains, including the Borborema Province (Brazil), Madagascar, Kenya rift, Baikal rift, Pyrenees, Appalachians, and the Atlantic margins. Data sources include field geology, seismic anisotropy (shear wave splitting, Pn anisotropy), gravity surveys, magnetotelluric soundings, and numerical models [Vauchez 1998, pp. 1-2, 3-4, 7-8, 13-15, 19-20].

## Methods
The approach combines: (1) Analysis of rheological strength profiles based on simplified lithospheric composition and experimental flow laws [Vauchez 1998, pp. 2-3]. (2) Review of geological and geophysical evidence for crust-mantle coupling and lithospheric fabrics [Vauchez 1998, pp. 3-5]. (3) Discussion of numerical models simulating deformation of heterogeneous lithospheres [Vauchez 1998, pp. 9-11, 15-16]. (4) Integration of experimental data on the mechanical anisotropy of olivine aggregates [Vauchez 1998, pp. 16-18].

## Key Findings
1.  Crust-mantle mechanical coupling is usually efficient, especially beneath major transcurrent faults, which likely crosscut the entire lithosphere and root into the sublithospheric mantle [Vauchez 1998, pp. 1-2, 3-5].
2.  In most geodynamic environments, the mechanical properties of the mantle govern the tectonic behavior of the lithosphere [Vauchez 1998, pp. 1-2, 2-3].
3.  Lateral rheological heterogeneity, primarily from variations in geothermal gradient, significantly influences continental deformation. Stiff cratons largely escape deformation, while strain localizes within or at the boundaries of weak rift basins [Vauchez 1998, pp. 1-2, 5-7, 13-15].
4.  A pervasive tectonic fabric in the lithospheric mantle, evidenced by seismic and electrical anisotropy, induces mechanical anisotropy due to the crystallographic preferred orientation of olivine. This is the source of "tectonic inheritance," explaining the reactivation of ancient structures and the tendency for continental break-up to parallel old orogenic belts [Vauchez 1998, pp. 1-2, 16-18, 19-20, 22-23].

## Limitations
1.  The rheological approach using strength profiles is an oversimplification, reducing the lithosphere to a few uniform layers [Vauchez 1998, pp. 2-3].
2.  There is a scarcity of reliable experimental rheological data for the lower crust, leading to uncertainty about its stiffness and the potential for decoupling [Vauchez 1998, pp. 4-5].
3.  Whether major faults root into the asthenosphere or vanish within the lithospheric mantle remains speculative and needs further confirmation [Vauchez 1998, pp. 4-5].
4.  The time-dependent evolution of rheological heterogeneities (e.g., thermal relaxation of rifts) and its effect on subsequent deformation is complex and not fully resolved [Vauchez 1998, pp. 8-9].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The lithosphere's composition is simplified to a quartz-dominated upper/middle crust, a plagioclase-dominated lower crust, and an olivine-dominated upper mantle [Vauchez 1998, pp. 2-3].
2.  The active deformation mechanism at a given depth is the one requiring the minimum work (brittle failure or dislocation creep) [Vauchez 1998, pp. 2-3].
3.  The mechanical properties of the stiffest layer (usually the mantle) determine the behavior of the whole lithosphere, assuming sufficient coupling [Vauchez 1998, pp. 2-3].
4.  Seismic anisotropy (shear wave splitting) is primarily caused by lattice-preferred orientation of olivine in the lithospheric mantle [Vauchez 1998, pp. 18-19].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** In most tectonic settings except those with very high thermal flow. **Claim:** The strength of the subcrustal mantle is the largest, and its mechanical properties determine the behavior of the whole lithosphere [Vauchez 1998, pp. 2-3].
2.  **Condition:** During continental collision involving pre-existing heterogeneities. **Claim:** Stiff heterogeneities (cratons) induce stress concentration at their tips, favoring the initiation of lithospheric-scale strike-slip faults, while weak heterogeneities (rifts) localize deformation [Vauchez 1998, pp. 9-11, 15-16].
3.  **Condition:** For olivine-rich upper mantle rocks. **Claim:** A pervasive crystallographic preferred orientation developed during past orogenic events induces a significant mechanical anisotropy that influences subsequent deformation patterns, a process termed tectonic inheritance [Vauchez 1998, pp. 16-18, 22-23].
4.  **Condition:** When rifting results in lithospheric mantle thinning greater than crustal thinning (β > δ). **Claim:** Rifting tends to generate a weak rheological heterogeneity that can localize subsequent compressional deformation if reactivated before thermal relaxation [Vauchez 1998, pp. 7-8, 8-9].

## Open Questions
1.  To what depth do major lithospheric faults penetrate? Do they root into the asthenosphere or dissipate within the lithospheric mantle? [Vauchez 1998, pp. 4-5].
2.  How does the interaction between fault kinematics and asthenospheric flow, driven by plate motion, influence deformation? [Vauchez 1998, pp. 5-7].
3.  What are the precise effects of lateral variations in lithospheric mantle composition (e.g., water depletion) on rheology? [Vauchez 1998, pp. 22-23].
4.  How do rheological heterogeneity and mechanical anisotropy combine quantitatively to produce the observed complex strain fields in continents? [Vauchez 1998, pp. 22-23].
