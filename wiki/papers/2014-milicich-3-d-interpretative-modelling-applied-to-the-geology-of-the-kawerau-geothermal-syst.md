# 3-D Interpretative Modelling Applied to the Geology of the Kawerau Geothermal System, Taupo Volcanic Zone, New Zealand.

## One-line Summary
This study applies 3-D visualisation and modelling software to integrate borehole stratigraphy and structural data, creating a geological framework model for the Kawerau geothermal system that provides new insights into its structure, stratigraphy, and controls on permeability.

## Research Question
How can 3-D interpretative modelling be applied to integrate geological and structural data from boreholes and surface mapping to better understand the geological framework, controls on deep-seated permeability, and evolution of the Kawerau geothermal system?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Kawerau Geothermal Field, located in the southern Whakatane Graben within the Taupo Volcanic Zone (TVZ), New Zealand. The model area is 5.8 by 3.9 km northeast of the township of Kawerau [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3]. Data sources include:
*   Geology datasets from relogged cores and cuttings of over 95 geothermal production, re-injection, and monitoring boreholes [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].
*   GIS datasets including fault locations from the Rotorua QMAP geological map, lakes, rivers, roads, and outcrop areas of geological units [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].
*   A Digital Terrain Model of New Zealand for topography [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].
*   U-Pb age data from zircons for key stratigraphic horizons [Milicich 2014, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
The geological framework model was built using a defined workflow in Leapfrog Geothermal software [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3]. The process included:
1.  Defining the region of interest.
2.  Importing borehole stratigraphy and GIS data.
3.  Building a structural network of faults, using QMAP fault locations where present and applying a uniform dip of 80° to the northwest or southwest for faults without surface expression [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].
4.  Building geological surfaces pinned to stratigraphic contact points from borehole data, with some surfaces constrained by outcrops [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].
5.  Adjusting surfaces manually in areas with no boreholes, constrained by geological morphology, with edits tracked for future updates [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].
A parallel model was also built independently by Mighty River Power Ltd. using MVS software. The two models were compared iteratively to evaluate interpretations, resolve ambiguities, and identify errors [Milicich 2014, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
*   The 3-D model provides new insights into the geological and structural framework, controls on deep-seated permeability, and system evolution [Milicich 2014, pp. 1-2].
*   The dominant fault structures are northeast-southwest striking, aligned with the modern TVZ rift system, and include active faults like the Onepu fault and Rotoitipaku fault zone [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-4].
*   A second set of northwest-southeast striking faults primarily displaces deeper units (>1 Ma) and is inferred to be a major pathway for fluid upflow [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-4].
*   U-Pb dating and 3-D visualization revealed that rhyolite bodies beneath the field represent dome extrusions and intrusions emplaced over restricted time periods, not individual flows [Milicich 2014, pp. 4-5].
*   The emplacement mechanism of rhyolite bodies switched from horizontal sills (Caxton Formation, ~0.36 Ma) to vertical dikes (Onepu Formation, ~0.15 Ma), reflecting a change in the orientation of principal stress axes during the development of the Whakatane Graben [Milicich 2014, pp. 5-5].
*   The parallel model development process between two software platforms led to a more robust geological framework by identifying errors and clarifying differences in interpretation [Milicich 2014, pp. 5-5].
*   The 3-D model serves as a management tool for visualizing development strategies, reducing drilling risk, and supporting adaptive field management by modeling structural fabric and permeable units [Milicich 2014, pp. 5-6].

## Limitations
*   The structural framework may be more complex than represented, as only faults with offsets >20 m in borehole geology could be confirmed [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-4].
*   Faults are represented as single planes, whereas offsets may actually be defined by 'damage zones' or multiple splays, which are difficult to identify in boreholes [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-4].
*   There is ambiguity in the categorisation of the Kawerau greywacke basement between the Waioeka facies of the Pahau terrane and the Waipapa composite terrane [Milicich 2014, pp. 4-5].
*   Manual edits to geological surfaces in areas with no boreholes are based on geological understanding and may require revision with new data [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   Faults without surface expression have a uniform dip of 80° to the northwest or southwest, consistent with regional TVZ structures [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].
*   Sub-horizontally emplaced ignimbrites serve as reliable marker horizons for defining apparent fault offsets [Milicich 2014, pp. 4-5].
*   The geological surfaces generated by the modelling software are constrained by factual borehole contact points where available [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   Fault orientation and dip
*   Stratigraphic unit ages (from U-Pb zircon dating)
*   Lithology and thickness of geological formations
*   Depth to greywacke basement
*   Emplacement geometry of intrusive bodies (sills vs. dikes)
*   Orientation of principal stress axes (σ3)

## Reusable Claims
*   3-D visualisation overcomes the distortion of spatial relationships inherent in 2-D representations, allowing geologists to resolve geospatial relationships of geological bodies [Milicich 2014, pp. 1-2].
*   The process of building and comparing parallel 3-D models using different software platforms is a valuable quality assurance tool that helps identify errors in data input and clarify differences in geological interpretation [Milicich 2014, pp. 5-5, 6-6].
*   Integrating U-Pb geochronology with 3-D visualisation allows for the reinterpretation of intrusive body emplacement mechanisms, providing insights into the evolving stress field of a geothermal system [Milicich 2014, pp. 5-5].
*   A 3-D geological framework model can be directly imported into numerical reservoir models, ensuring consistency between the geological conceptual model and simulations [Milicich 2014, pp. 5-6].
*   Recognising the 3-D extent of near-surface permeable volcanic units is critical for defining reinjection and well-targeting strategies, as these units can act as conduits for cool marginal fluids [Milicich 2014, pp. 5-6].

## Connections To Other Work
*   Builds upon and revises previous stratigraphic frameworks for Kawerau by Nairn (1977, 1981, 1982, 1986), Grindley (1986), Christenson (1987), and Allis et al. (1995) [Milicich 2014, pp. 2-3].
*   Uses fault locations from the Rotorua QMAP geological map (Leonard et al., 2010) [Milicich 2014, pp. 3-3].
*   Applies 3-D modelling techniques previously used in the petroleum and mining industries and recently in other NZ geothermal fields (e.g., Ohaaki, Wairakei) [Milicich 2014, pp. 1-2].
*   Integrates with numerical reservoir models and field management strategies [Milicich 2014, pp. 5-6].
