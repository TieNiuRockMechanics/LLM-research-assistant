# Damage Analysis of High-Temperature Rocks Subjected to LN2 Thermal Shock.

## One-line Summary
Three high‑temperature rocks (granite, shale, sandstone) were subjected to LN₂ thermal shock after slow heating to 25 °C, 150 °C or 260 °C; results show that LN₂ quenching enhances permeability and deteriorates mechanical properties, with inter‑granular cracking as the primary damage mechanism, while sandstone is less sensitive mainly because of its larger pore spaces and weaker mineral thermal expansion heterogeneity [Wu 2019, pp. 1-2].

## Research Question
How does LN₂ thermal shock damage high‑temperature rocks, and what causes the sensitivity difference among granite, shale, and sandstone?
> “current researches are mainly focused on the responses of room‑temperature rocks to LN₂ thermal shock, while the thermal shock effect on high‑temperature rocks and the damage difference between different rocks are still unclear. In the present paper, we aimed to study the effect of LN₂ thermal shock on three different high‑temperature rocks (sandstone, shale and granite).” [Wu 2019, pp. 2-3]

## Study Area / Data
- **Granite** – collected from Shandong province, China; density 2618 kg/m³, permeability 0.0067 × 10⁻³ µm² (at 2 MPa confining pressure); main minerals: quartz (28.3 %), plagioclase (41.9 %), K‑feldspar (15.7 %) [Wu 2019, pp. 3-4, 13‑14].
- **Shale** – cored from Sichuan basin, China; density 2520 kg/m³, permeability 0.00036 × 10⁻³ µm² (lowest); rich in clay (20.6 %, 95 % illite), quartz (44.1 %), dolomite (12.6 %), calcite (18.8 %) [Wu 2019, pp. 3-4, 13‑14].
- **Sandstone** – medium‑grain sandstone from an oil field in eastern China, formation depth ≈ 2500 m; density 2396 kg/m³, permeability 0.1457 × 10⁻³ µm² (highest); quartz (56.9 %), plagioclase (19.1 %) [Wu 2019, pp. 3-4, 13‑14].
- All samples were cut into cylinders (⌀ 25 mm × 50 mm); shale cores perpendicular to bedding. Intact samples had similar P‑wave velocities to reduce heterogeneity effects [Wu 2019, pp. 3-4].
- Thermal treatments: slow heating (5 °C/min) to 25 °C, 150 °C or 260 °C, held for 10 h, then quenched by LN₂ (−196.8 °C) or cooled in air (as a weak‑shock benchmark) [Wu 2019, pp. 4].

## Methods
1. **Sample screening** – P‑wave velocity measured with an ultrasonic pulse device (samples coated with Vaseline); only those with similar Vₚ were used to keep comparable initial properties [Wu 2019, pp. 4].
2. **Thermal loading** – Muffle furnace, heating rate 5 °C/min, target temperatures 25, 150, 260 °C, hold 10 h; then LN₂ quenching or air cooling [Wu 2019, pp. 4].
3. **Permeability tests** – Transient‑pulse method with N₂, net confining pressures 2 MPa and 40 MPa (pore pressure 7 MPa and 30 MPa, ΔPₒ = 70 kPa); calculated using the pulse‑decay equation [Wu 2019, pp. 4-6].
4. **Mechanical tests** – Uniaxial and triaxial compression (confining pressure 40 MPa) on a TAW‑1000 electro‑hydraulic servo machine, displacement control at 0.05 mm/min; axial and lateral displacements recorded [Wu 2019, pp. 6].
5. **Micro‑scale analysis** – XRD (Rigatu MiniFlexII) for mineral composition; SEM (FEI Quanta 200F, up to ×200,000) for micro‑crack observation [Wu 2019, pp. 6].

## Key Findings
1. LN₂ thermal shock significantly improves permeability and deteriorates mechanical properties (strength, Young’s modulus) of granite and shale, while sandstone shows much weaker response [Wu 2019, pp. 1-2, 18].
2. Higher rock temperature intensifies the thermal shock effect – both permeability enhancement and strength reduction increase with initial rock temperature (from 25 °C to 260 °C) [Wu 2019, pp. 6-8, 10‑11, 18].
3. Inter‑granular cracks, mainly at quartz grain boundaries, are the primary damage mode; intra‑granular cracks also form but contribute less due to their smaller size [Wu 2019, pp. 12-13, 18].
4. Sandstone’s low sensitivity to LN₂ thermal shock is attributed to its larger original pore spaces (hundreds of microns) that accommodate thermal deformation, and to lower heterogeneity of mineral thermal expansion (smaller variation coefficient VC) [Wu 2019, pp. 14, 18].
5. Bedding planes facilitate crack propagation in shale during thermal shock, making shale more susceptible than sandstone [Wu 2019, pp. 16-18].
6. Confining pressure reduces the apparent thermal‑shock‑induced permeability growth rate ΔK and strength reduction rate Δσc, but the increasing trend with temperature remains [Wu 2019, pp. 8, 10‑11].

## Limitations
- Only a single LN₂ shock was applied; cyclic thermal shock (known to intensify damage) was not examined in this study [Wu 2019, pp. 2].
- The temperature range was limited to 25–260 °C; high‑temperature reservoirs may exceed this range.
- Only three rock types were compared; the conclusions may not directly translate to other lithologies.
- The study used dry samples and a simplified mineral expansion model (quartz vs. non‑quartz groups with assumed coefficients), which ignores complexities of real fluid‑saturated reservoirs [Wu 2019, pp. 13-14].
- Results are laboratory‑scale; field application may involve different stress, fluid, and cooling conditions not tested here.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **Air cooling as baseline** – Because air cooling induces negligibly weak thermal shock, the difference between LN₂‑cooled and air‑cooled properties isolates the thermal shock effect [Wu 2019, pp. 4, 6‑8].
- **Identical heating treatment** – All samples were heated together; any differences between cooling methods are attributed to the cooling process [Wu 2019, pp. 6].
- **Sample homogeneity** – Only samples with similar P‑wave velocities were selected, assuming that similar Vₚ implies comparable initial physical/mechanical properties [Wu 2019, pp. 4].
- **Mineral thermal expansion model** – Minerals were divided into quartz and “non‑quartz” groups; the average non‑quartz coefficient was taken as 6.8 × 10⁻⁶/°C, quartz as 20.45 × 10⁻⁶/°C [Wu 2019, pp. 13-14].
- **Confining pressures** – The 2 MPa and 40 MPa net confining pressures correspond roughly to shallow and deep reservoir conditions (≈3000 m) [Wu 2019, pp. 4-6].

## Reusable Claims
1. **For granite and shale**, LN₂ quenching after heating (150–260 °C) improves permeability significantly compared to air cooling, with ΔK reaching up to ~166 % for granite and ~79 % for shale under low confining pressure (2 MPa). The enhancement is temperature‑dependent and increases with initial rock temperature. [Wu 2019, pp. 8]
   - **Condition:** single LN₂ shock, sample dried, net confining pressure 2 MPa.
2. **Sandstone** shows negligible permeability enhancement (ΔK −4.29 to 3.87 %) and only minor strength reduction under the same thermal shock conditions, indicating lower sensitivity to LN₂ thermal shock. [Wu 2019, pp. 8]
   - **Condition:** medium‑grain sandstone with ~57 % quartz, original porosity hundreds of microns.
3. **Temperature amplification**: raising the rock temperature from 25 °C to 260 °C before LN₂ quenching magnifies mechanical deterioration (strength reduction up to ~25 % for granite) and permeability improvement. [Wu 2019, pp. 10-11, 18]
   - **Condition:** heating rate 5 °C/min, 10 h soak.
4. **Inter‑granular cracking at quartz‑feldspar boundaries** is the primary micro‑scale damage mechanism; intra‑granular cracks also form but are smaller and contribute less to overall property changes. [Wu 2019, pp. 12-13, 18]
   - **Condition:** rapid cooling (LN₂) of crystalline and sedimentary rocks.
5. **Mineral thermal expansion heterogeneity**, quantified by variation coefficient `VC`, correlates with thermal shock sensitivity: a higher `VC` (granite 0.580, shale 0.532) leads to greater damage than a low `VC` (sandstone 0.466). [Wu 2019, pp. 13-14]
   - **Condition:** simplified two‑group model; verified for the three tested lithologies.
6. **Large original pore spaces** (e.g., sandstone with hundreds of microns) provide room for thermal deformation, reducing local thermal stress and making the rock more resistant to LN₂ thermal shock. [Wu 2019, pp. 14, 16‑17]
   - **Condition:** pore size observed by SEM on intact samples.

## Connections To Other Work
- **Thermal shock via water quenching**: The literature (Kumari et al. 2017a,b; Shao et al. 2014; Wang et al. 2016b, 2017; Zhou et al. 2016) shows that water‑quenching thermal shock extends cracks and reduces strength in HDR reservoirs; the present study confirms analogous but more violent effects for LN₂ quenching [Wu 2019, pp. 2].
- **Previous LN₂ studies**: Wu et al. (2018) and Cai et al. (2014, 2015) investigated cryogenic LN₂ damage on room‑temperature rocks using SEM/NMR; this work extends the investigation to high‑temperature rocks (up to 260 °C) and compares three lithologies [Wu 2019, pp. 2-3].
- **Cyclic thermal shock**: Qin et al. (2017a,b, 2018a,b) demonstrated that cyclic LN₂ injection into coal generates larger fracture networks; the current study uses only a single shock and acknowledges that cyclic treatment could intensify damage [Wu 2019, pp. 2].
- **Rock heterogeneity and thermal cracking**: Johnson (1978) first highlighted mismatch in thermo‑physical properties; the paper quantifies this mismatch using a variation coefficient `VC` for the first time in LN₂ context [Wu 2019, pp. 2, 13‑14].

## Open Questions
