# Scaling properties within the Gulf of Corinth, Greece; comparison between offshore and onshore active faults.

## One-line Summary
This study analyzes the scaling properties of 136 active offshore and onshore normal faults in the Gulf of Corinth, finding both populations are bi-fractal with characteristic lengths at 5 km and 9 km that correlate to crustal structure and seismicity.

## Research Question
The purpose is to assess the characteristic scaling properties of active normal faults within the Gulf of Corinth and to compare the offshore and onshore fault arrays to understand fault growth, segmentation, and the different stages of fault network evolution [Zygouri 2008, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Gulf of Corinth, Greece, a 110-km-long by 30-km-wide active graben [Zygouri 2008, pp. 1-2]. The dataset comprises 136 well-determined active faults: 76 offshore faults (55%) and 60 onshore faults (45%) [Zygouri 2008, pp. 5-6]. Offshore fault data were obtained from a network of published high-resolution seismic reflection profiles [Zygouri 2008, pp. 5-6]. Onshore fault data were derived from high-accuracy mapping using aerial photographs, DTMs, and geological maps, with activity criteria including fault scarp steepness >25° and offset of Pleistocene sediments [Zygouri 2008, pp. 5-6].

## Methods
The analysis focused on fault length and vertical throw [Zygouri 2008, pp. 1-2]. Vertical throw was used as a proxy for displacement to homogenize the comparison between offshore and onshore datasets [Zygouri 2008, pp. 5-6]. Statistical properties were examined using logarithmic cumulative frequency plots to identify power-law (fractal) distributions [Zygouri 2008, pp. 9-10]. The fractal dimension (D) was calculated from the slope of these plots [Zygouri 2008, pp. 9-10]. Displacement-length (d/L) ratios were also analyzed [Zygouri 2008, pp. 12-13].

## Key Findings
Both offshore and onshore fault populations exhibit bi-fractal distributions for fault length and throw, suggesting a saturation state in deformation [Zygouri 2008, pp. 1-2, 9-10]. The fractal dimension D for faults between 2-5 km is similar for both populations (~0.98-1.01), but differs for faults >5 km (offshore D=1.94, onshore D=1.33) [Zygouri 2008, pp. 9-10]. Characteristic fault lengths are identified at approximately 5 km and 9 km [Zygouri 2008, pp. 1-2, 9-10]. Faults larger than 9 km appear to accumulate throw without significant lateral propagation [Zygouri 2008, pp. 1-2, 15-15]. The average d/L ratio is 0.029 for offshore faults and 0.045 for onshore faults [Zygouri 2008, pp. 12-13].

## Limitations
Potential measurement errors exist, especially for offshore faults due to seismic line spacing (error <500 m in length) and resolution (±10 m in throw) [Zygouri 2008, pp. 7-8]. Onshore fault parameters depend on map scale, with errors up to ±100 m for length and 2-20 m for throw [Zygouri 2008, pp. 6-7]. Erosion and sedimentation can affect the exposed length and throw of faults [Zygouri 2008, pp. 7-8]. Faults smaller than 1 km were excluded to avoid sampling bias [Zygouri 2008, pp. 7-8].

## Assumptions / Conditions
Active offshore faults are defined as those cutting and offsetting Quaternary sediments and producing a sea floor escarpment [Zygouri 2008, pp. 5-6]. Active onshore faults must meet criteria such as steep scarps (>25°) and offset of Pleistocene sediments [Zygouri 2008, pp. 5-6]. Vertical throw is used as a reliable proxy for displacement for comparison purposes [Zygouri 2008, pp. 5-6]. The analysis assumes that sedimentation rates near faults are typically less than fault slip-rates, so active faults are not buried [Zygouri 2008, pp. 7-8].

## Reusable Claims
- Both offshore and onshore fault populations in the Gulf of Corinth are bi-fractal, indicating a saturation state in deformation [Zygouri 2008, pp. 1-2, 9-10].
- Faults larger than 9 km in the Gulf of Corinth accumulate throw without significant lateral propagation, suggesting a shift in growth mechanism [Zygouri 2008, pp. 1-2, 15-15].
- The characteristic fault lengths of ~5 km and ~9 km in the Gulf of Corinth correlate with the underlying crustal mechanical layering at corresponding depths [Zygouri 2008, pp. 15-15].
- Offshore faults exhibit a lower average displacement-length ratio (0.029) compared to onshore faults (0.045), implying offshore faults lengthen faster while onshore faults evolve length and throw simultaneously [Zygouri 2008, pp. 12-13].
