# Development Program of Hot Dry Rock Geothermal Resource in the Yangbajing Basin of China.

## One-line Summary
A development program for hot dry rock (HDR) geothermal energy in Tibet’s Yangbajing Basin is proposed, estimating a resource of 5.4 × 10⁹ MW·a, and designing an artificial reservoir (3 × 10¹¹ m³) using natural fault zones with a vertical injection well and two inclined production wells, capable of supporting a 10 000 MW power station for ~100 years, with an initial 1 000 MW plant costing ~1 billion yuan and producing 8.64 × 10⁹ kWh annually (Feng 2012).

## Research Question
How can the vast HDR geothermal resource of the Yangbajing Basin, driven by a deep liquating magma chamber and complex tectonic faulting, be effectively extracted by leveraging the regional in‑situ stress field, temperature distribution, and natural fault architecture?

## Study Area / Data
- **Location:** Dangxiong–Yangbajing Basin, south‑east of Nyainqêntanglha, Tibet, China [Feng 2012, pp. 1-2].
- **Geophysical data:** artificial seismic (low‑velocity layer at 22 km depth), magneto‑electrotelluric exploration (low‑resistance layer, 5 Ω·m at ~5 km), deep seismic reflection (liquating rock mass at 13–22 km) [Feng 2012, pp. 2-2].
- **Stress measurements:** piezomagnetic stress‑relief method at four test points (Ybj1–Ybj4, depth 11–13 m) giving maximum horizontal principal stress 3.3–10.4 MPa, orientations N45°–N81°E [Feng 2012, pp. 3-5, Table 1].
- **Focal mechanism solutions:** confirm east–west tensile stress field; east‑west tensile forces produce normal fault‑type earthquakes, with the extensional regime inferred to >100 km depth [Xu et al. 2005, as cited in Feng 2012, pp. 3-5].
- **Temperature distribution:** finite‑element analysis of a vertical cross‑section (Fig. 3) over 180 km horizontally, 20 km vertically, based on a deep‑seated liquating rock mass >500 °C [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
- **Basin structure:** five large stepped normal faults (F1–F5), dips 55°–70° above ~6 km, transitioning to moderate‑dip (15°–20°) shear zones at greater depth, striking parallel to the magma chamber outline [Feng 2012, pp. 3-5, 5‑6].

## Methods
- **Temperature field analysis:** finite‑element method applied to the vertical cross‑section of the Dangxiong–Yangbajing Basin, using the deep‑seated magma temperature (>500 °C) to obtain the zonal temperature distribution and geothermal gradient [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
- **Resource evaluation:** volume of the deep‑seated liquating mass estimated as 180 000 km³ (7–18 km depth, average 500 °C); extractable resource calculated with lowest extractable temperature 150 °C and electrical efficiency 0.17 [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
- **Stress field characterization:** integration of shallow stress measurements (Table 1) and regional focal mechanism solutions to deduce that the minimum horizontal principal stress is normal to the basin strike (and to fault planes), while the maximum horizontal stress is parallel to the strike [Feng 2012, pp. 3-5].
- **Extraction scheme design:** the fault zone nearest the magma chamber (fault F1) serves as the artificial reservoir; a 9 000 m‑deep vertical injection well is placed at a low point on the downdip side, and two inclined production wells intersect faults F1–F5 to create a closed‑circuit system [Feng 2012, pp. 1-2, 5‑6; Fig. 7].
- **Seepage analysis:** coefficient of fissure seepage modelled as \( k_f = k_{f0} \exp[-\alpha(\sigma_n - \beta p)] \); permeability as a function of overburden stress (depth) derived from fault geometry [Feng 2012, pp. 3-5; Fig. 8].
- **Fluid field simulation:** a two‑month water‑injection scenario used to map equi‑flow distribution (Fig. 9), showing flow direction and gradient [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- **Reservoir and energy calculation:** artificial reservoir volume 3 × 10¹¹ m³; extractable thermal energy computed as \( Q = \rho V c T \) with \( \rho = 2700\ \mathrm{kg/m^3} \), \( c = 1000\ \mathrm{J/(kg\,°C)} \), and a thermal‑to‑electric conversion of 17 % [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- **Cost estimation:** drilling cost \( C = (H_v + H_{c1} + H_{c2}) \cdot P \), with \( H_v = 10\,000\ \mathrm{m} \), \( H_{c1} = H_{c2} = 12\,500\ \mathrm{m} \), \( P = 8\,000\ \mathrm{yuan/m} \); power station construction 616 yuan/kW; other costs 0.1 billion yuan [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].

## Key Findings
- The HDR geothermal resource of the Yangbajing region is estimated at **5.4 × 10⁹ MW·a**; at 17 % electrical efficiency this yields 0.92 × 10⁹ MW·a, sufficient for a 50 GW plant for ~1.8 × 10⁴ years [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
- Geothermal gradient above the magma chamber is **~45 °C/km** over a 20 km vertical column, extending ~180 km horizontally [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
- In‑situ stress measurements and focal mechanisms show the **minimum horizontal principal stress normal to the basin strike and fault planes**, while the maximum horizontal stress is parallel to the strike; early and modern stress fields are unchanged, favourable for fracture dilation [Feng 2012, pp. 3-5].
- The extraction design, using the natural fault system as an artificial reservoir, creates a **fractured rock volume of 3 × 10¹¹ m³** – 360 times the Cornwall (UK) HDR reservoir and 973 times the Soultz (France) reservoir – via one vertical injection well and two inclined production wells [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- Fluid‑flow simulation shows that injected water moves **predominantly upward along the fault dip** toward the production wells, with minimal downward migration [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- A **10 000 MW power station** could operate for ~100 years; an initial 1 000 MW facility would cost **~1 billion yuan** and generate 8.64 × 10⁹ kWh per year [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].

## Limitations
- Stress measurements are from very shallow depths (11–13 m); extrapolation to 5–15 km depth carries uncertainty, though focal mechanisms corroborate the orientation [Feng 2012, pp. 3-5].
- Temperature distribution relies on a finite‑element model with assumed magma chamber geometry and temperature; no deep borehole temperature validation is provided [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
- Resource evaluation does not quantify dynamic heat recharge from the magma chamber during extraction; the paper suggests resources could triple if recharge is considered, but this remains unconfirmed [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- The extraction design is a conceptual proposal; no pilot test or field validation has been performed [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- Induced seismicity, water availability, and environmental impacts are not discussed.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Lowest extractable temperature: 150 °C [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
- Electrical conversion efficiency: 0.17 (thermal use ratio 17 %) [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3, 5‑6].
- Average temperature of the deep‑seated liquating mass: 500 °C and volume ~180 000 km³ [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
- In‑situ stress field unchanged from early to modern times; present‑day measurements and focal mechanisms represent conditions at depth [Feng 2012, pp. 3-5].
- Seepage follows the exponential model (Equation 1) with appropriate parameters for the faults [Feng 2012, pp. 3-5].
- The 9 000 m injection well can be successfully drilled and cased to 8 500 m; the lower 500 m left open or with shock tubing [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- Fluid flow is dominated by fault zones and fractures; rock matrix contributes minor storage/flow [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- Drilling cost unit: 8 000 yuan/metre (2000s prices); power plant specific cost: 616 yuan/kW [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].

## Reusable Claims
1. The Yangbajing Basin contains an HDR resource of **5.4 × 10⁹ MW·a** based on a 180 000 km³ magma chamber at ~500 °C and a minimum extractable temperature of 150 °C [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
2. The geothermal gradient is **45 °C/km** over a 20 km vertical zone above the magma chamber [Feng 2012, pp. 2-3].
3. The **minimum horizontal principal stress is oriented normal to the strike of the Dangxiong–Yangbajing Basin and to the fault planes**, promoting fracture opening [Feng 2012, pp. 3-5].
4. A **natural fault system can be used as an artificial geothermal reservoir**, with a vertical injection well and two inclined production wells creating a closed‑circuit extraction system of volume 3 × 10¹¹ m³ [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
5. **Simulated 60‑day water injection shows fluid flow is directed updip along the faults toward production wells**, with limited downward migration [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
6. The proposed design could support a **10 000 MW power station for ~100 years**; an initial 1 000 MW plant would require ~**1 billion yuan** (2000s costs) and produce **8.64 × 10⁹ kWh per year** [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].

## Open Questions
- What is the actual **dynamic heat recharge** from the magma chamber during long‑term extraction? The paper notes a possible three‑fold resource increase, but this is not quantified [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- How will the faults respond to large‑scale, prolonged fluid circulation in terms of **induced seismicity and fault reactivation**? No risk analysis is presented.
- Can a 9 000 m well be drilled and maintained at 350–450 °C with **current technology**? The paper asserts feasibility but provides no detailed engineering assessment [Feng 2012, pp. 5-6].
- What are the **actual permeability and connectivity** of the deep fault system, and how might they evolve with thermal and chemical effects?
- **Water source and environmental impacts** for large‑volume injection are not discussed.
- How reliable is the assumption of **unchanged stress fields** at depth when only shallow (11–13 m) measurements are available, albeit supported by focal mechanisms?
