# Geology and in situ stress of the MH-2 borehole, Idaho, USA: Insights into western Snake River Plain structure from geothermal exploration drilling.

## One-line Summary
The MH-2 borehole in the western Snake River Plain encountered artesian geothermal fluids at 1745 m depth, and integrated geological and stress analyses reveal a complex structural environment with oblique normal faulting that creates permeability for fluid flow.

## Research Question
What is the structural environment that produces permeability for artesian flow in the MH-2 borehole, and what does it reveal about the tectonic setting and geothermal potential of the western Snake River Plain?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the MH-2 borehole, drilled to 1821 m total depth at Mountain Home Air Force Base in the western Snake River Plain, Idaho, USA [Kessler 2017, pp. 1-1]. Data include core from 1280 m to 1821 m, ultrasonic borehole televiewer (BHTV) image logs from 1286 m to 1596 m, wireline geophysical logs, laboratory rock density and unconfined compressive strength (UCS) measurements, and in situ stress indicators from borehole breakouts and drilling-induced tensile fractures [Kessler 2017, pp. 3-4, 4-5, 7-9, 17-18]. The zone of interest (ZOI) is defined as the lower 540 m from 1281 m to 1821 m [Kessler 2017, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
Methods include detailed core logging of lithology, fractures, faults, and shear slip indicators [Kessler 2017, pp. 3-4]. Borehole geophysical data, particularly ultrasonic BHTV logs, were used to image natural fractures, drilling-induced tensile fractures, and borehole breakouts [Kessler 2017, pp. 4-5]. Fracture orientation data were corrected for borehole sampling bias using a Terzaghi weighting factor [Kessler 2017, pp. 5-5, 20-22]. Stress orientations were determined from the azimuths of borehole breakouts and drilling-induced tensile fractures [Kessler 2017, pp. 9-10, 16-17]. Stress magnitudes were estimated using breakout widths, laboratory-derived rock strength (UCS), and assumed friction coefficients, applying Andersonian mechanics and Coulomb frictional faulting theory [Kessler 2017, pp. 16-18].

## Key Findings
The lower 540 m of the borehole consists of 42 discrete basalt flows, zones of hydrothermally altered basalt, reworked basaltic sediments, and basaltic breccia [Kessler 2017, pp. 5-7]. A total of 1701 natural fractures and 166 faults were logged in the core, with fracture density strongly correlated to lithology, being highest in stiff, brittle basalt units [Kessler 2017, pp. 7-9]. Fault dips average 45.9° with oblique slip vectors (mean rake 35.6°), indicating a mix of strike-slip and dip-slip motion [Kessler 2017, pp. 9-9, 14-16]. Borehole breakouts indicate a maximum horizontal stress (SH) orientation of 047° ± 7°, while drilling-induced tensile fractures indicate an SH orientation of 67° ± 21° [Kessler 2017, pp. 9-10, 16-17]. The in situ stress state is best explained by an oblique normal fault regime (SV > SH > Sh), with SH magnitudes varying with depth and approaching SV in some horizons, suggesting potential for mixed normal and strike-slip failure [Kessler 2017, pp. 16-18, 19-20]. Artesian hydrothermal fluids with a temperature of at least 150°C were encountered at 1745 m depth [Kessler 2017, pp. 1-1, 3-4].

## Limitations
The core is unoriented, so fracture strike cannot be determined directly from core alone [Kessler 2017, pp. 3-4]. BHTV logging was limited to 1286–1596 m due to high temperatures exceeding tool limits [Kessler 2017, pp. 4-5]. Stress magnitude calculations depend on assumed friction coefficients (μ = 0.6–1.0) and laboratory UCS values, which show high variability [Kessler 2017, pp. 17-18]. Pore pressure was assumed hydrostatic, but elevated pressures are likely at depth, introducing uncertainty in stress estimates [Kessler 2017, pp. 18-19]. The Terzaghi correction for fracture bias uses a maximum weighting factor of 15, which may undersample very steeply dipping fractures [Kessler 2017, pp. 20-22].

## Assumptions / Conditions
The rock mass is assumed to be critically stressed, meaning fractures and faults are at or near shear failure [Kessler 2017, pp. 16-17]. The stress state is assumed to be normal faulting (SV > SH > Sh) based on regional tectonics, with possible strike-slip components [Kessler 2017, pp. 16-18]. Pore pressure is assumed to follow a normal hydrostatic gradient (9.8 MPa/km) for calculations, though elevated pressures are inferred at depth [Kessler 2017, pp. 17-18, 18-19]. The coefficient of friction (μ) is assumed to be between 0.6 and 1.0 [Kessler 2017, pp. 17-18].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Maximum horizontal stress orientation (SH):** 047° ± 7° (from breakouts), 67° ± 21° (from drilling-induced fractures) [Kessler 2017, pp. 9-10, 16-17].
- **Stress magnitudes:** SV (vertical stress), SH (maximum horizontal stress), Sh (minimum horizontal stress); SH generally < SV but approaches SV in some horizons [Kessler 2017, pp. 16-18].
- **Fault dip:** Mean 45.9° ± 23° [Kessler 2017, pp. 9-9, 14-16].
- **Fault slip rake angle:** Mean 35.6° ± 31.8° [Kessler 2017, pp. 9-9, 14-16].
- **Fracture density:** Varies with lithology; up to 70 fractures per 10 m interval in brittle basalt [Kessler 2017, pp. 7-9].
- **Rock strength (UCS):** Highly variable (2.1–190.6 MPa) [Kessler 2017, pp. 17-18].
- **Pore pressure (Pp):** Assumed hydrostatic (9.8 MPa/km), but likely elevated at depth [Kessler 2017, pp. 17-18, 18-19].
- **Coefficient of friction (μ):** 0.6–1.0 [Kessler 2017, pp. 17-18].

## Reusable Claims
- The maximum horizontal stress (SH) in the MH-2 borehole is oriented 047° ± 7° based on borehole breakouts [Kessler 2017, pp. 9-10, 16-17].
- The in situ stress state at MH-2 is consistent with an oblique normal fault regime, where SH magnitudes approach SV in some horizons, allowing for mixed normal and strike-slip failure [Kessler 2017, pp. 16-18, 19-20].
- Fracture permeability in basalt-hosted geothermal systems is controlled by the orientation of fractures relative to the modern stress state and the presence of critically stressed faults [Kessler 2017, pp. 1-2, 19-20].
- The western Snake River Plain transition zone is characterized by complex structures developed in response to a transitional stress state between Snake River Plain and western Basin and Range regimes [Kessler 2017, pp. 1-1, 20-20].
- Elevated pore pressures at depth, as observed in MH-2, reduce effective stress and promote failure, potentially increasing fracture permeability in geothermal reservoirs [Kessler 2017, pp. 18-19, 19-20].
