# Three‑Dimensional Geologic Mapping to Assess Geothermal Potential: Examples from Nevada and Oregon.

## One-line Summary

A methodology for 3D geologic mapping and geothermal potential analysis is presented, using fault stress state, structural discontinuities, and host rock lithology to identify subsurface zones of geothermal fluid upwelling in structurally controlled systems in the Basin and Range province.

## Research Question

How can detailed 3D geologic mapping and quantitative analysis of fault systems be used to identify specific subsurface volumes most prospective for hosting geothermal upwelling in structurally controlled geothermal systems? [Siler 2019, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data

Four geothermal systems in the Basin and Range province were studied: Bradys, Nevada; Astor Pass, Nevada; Tuscarora, Nevada; and Neal Hot Springs, Oregon [Siler 2019, pp. 5-6]. These were selected because each has abundant surface and subsurface data allowing detailed 3D geologic characterization, and together they represent several important structural settings for geothermal fluid flow [Siler 2019, pp. 5-6].

- **Tuscarora, Nevada**: Located at the northern end of Independence Valley, an east-tilted half graben. Geothermal fluids are extracted from Paleozoic limestones and claystones. A 24 MWe capacity power plant was constructed in 2012 [Siler 2019, pp. 14-15]. Data included 1:24,000 scale geologic map, five geologic cross sections, downhole lithologic data from 12 wells (cuttings and core), and regional gravity anomalies [Siler 2019, pp. 9-10][Siler 2019, pp. 11-12].

- **Neal Hot Springs, Oregon**: Located ~25 km west of Vale, Oregon. Supports a 22 MWe capacity power station operating since 2012, with four wells producing ~141 °C geothermal fluids at 715 kg/s [Siler 2019, pp. 15-17]. Data included 1:24,000 scale geologic map, three geologic cross sections, interpretation of four seismic reflection profiles, 2D gravity data modeling, and lithologic data from core and cuttings from 24 wells [Siler 2019, pp. 15-17][Siler 2019, pp. 9-10].

- **Astor Pass, Nevada**: Lies at the intersection between two major fault zones [Siler 2019, pp. 5-6].

- **Bradys, Nevada**: Hosted by a step-over in a normal fault system [Siler 2019, pp. 5-6].

Regional heat flow in the northern Basin and Range ranges from ~85 to 110 mW/m², with geothermal gradients of ~35–40 °C/km, suggesting fluids circulate to minimum depths of ~3–5 km [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4].

## Methods

### 3D Geologic Mapping Workflow

The methodology integrates surficial and subsurface geologic and geophysical data into detailed 3D geologic maps [Siler 2019, pp. 1-2]. The workflow involves:

1. **Geologic mapping**: Detailed 1:24,000 scale or higher-resolution mapping delineating rock types, structure, hydrothermal alteration, and surface geothermal manifestations [Siler 2019, pp. 6-9].

2. **Downhole data**: Lithologic analysis from core and well cuttings provides key subsurface constraints. Penetration rate data, downhole geophysical logs, and loss-of-circulation zones are used as indicators of major lithologic changes and fractures/faults [Siler 2019, pp. 9-10].

3. **Geophysical data**: 2D seismic reflection data, gravity and magnetic data (including maximum horizontal gradients and 2D forward modeling), and magnetotelluric data are integrated [Siler 2019, pp. 9-10][Siler 2019, pp. 10-12].

4. **2D geologic cross sections**: Synthesized from all available data, selected orthogonal to structural grain, with iterative revision between mapped fault traces and cross sections [Siler 2019, pp. 10-12].

5. **3D fault map construction**: Digitization of fault traces from geologic maps, fault profiles from cross sections, and fault interpretations from subsurface well data. 'Hard' data (geologic map, cross sections, downhole data) and 'soft' data (added to constrain surfaces where hard data are insufficient) are distinguished [Siler 2019, pp. 12-14].

6. **3D stratigraphic map construction**: Digitization of stratigraphic contacts from cross sections, downhole data, and geologic maps, with soft data to retain strike, dip, thickness, and fault offset [Siler 2019, pp. 12-14].

7. **Uncertainty assessment**: 3D density of data is calculated by computing the distance from well data, surficial geophysical and map data, and cross sections throughout each 3D map [Siler 2019, pp. 14-15].

### Geothermal Potential Mapping

A cellular-based approach is used with 15 m cell sizes at Tuscarora and Neal Hot Springs [Siler 2019, pp. 17-18]. Three geologic proxies for permeability are evaluated:

1. **Fault stress state**: Slip tendency and dilation tendency are calculated for each 3D fault plane based on ambient stress conditions. Critically stressed faults near failure are conducive to fluid flow [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].

2. **Fault intersections and terminations**: Density of fault intersections and terminations per unit area is calculated in 3D space. A 'breakdown region' proximal to a fault termination is proportional to 10⁻² times the half-length of the fault [Siler 2019, pp. 20-22].

3. **Host rock lithology**: Relatively weak lithologies are less likely to host interconnected fault and fracture networks. Favorable lithologic intervals are identified from production data, pressure-temperature-spinner logs, and well construction data [Siler 2019, pp. 22-23].

Each proxy is normalized on a zero-to-one scale and summed to semi-quantitatively evaluate locations where positive indications for geothermal circulation are most highly collocated [Siler 2019, pp. 23-26].

## Key Findings

- **Tuscarora**: Geothermal upwelling is sourced from intersecting faults with opposing polarity in the axial part of an anticlinal accommodation zone. The densest zone of intersecting faults lies at ~3000 m depth, with a shallower zone at ~1200–1300 m depth corresponding to the production well bottom-hole locations. Fluids upwell along well-oriented NNE-striking fault segments and fault intersections [Siler 2019, pp. 25-27]. The north–south elongate zone of high intersection density extends ~1.25 km, consistent with tracer test data showing fluid flow between production wells and proximal injection wells but not to distal injection wells ~1.5 km to the north [Siler 2019, pp. 26-27].

- **Neal Hot Springs**: Geothermal upwelling is controlled by a complexly faulted, hard-linked left step-over between two overlapping west-dipping normal fault zones (the Neal fault and Sugarloaf Butte fault). The highest intersection density occurs along the southern margin of the step-over where many fault intersections lie between the two faults. The Neal fault bends ~10° westward at the southern margin, resulting in relatively higher slip and dilation tendency [Siler 2019, pp. 26-28]. Production wells intersect the Neal fault within middle Miocene mafic Columbia River Group volcanic section [Siler 2019, pp. 27-28].

- The most common structural settings for geothermal systems in the Great Basin are fault terminations, fault step-overs, fault intersections, accommodation zones, displacement transfer zones, and pull-aparts [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4].

- The most productive fluid upwelling zones are highly localized, limited to narrow zones tens to hundreds of meters wide, along a single fault or small subset of faults [Siler 2019, pp. 1-2][Siler 2019, pp. 4-5].

- Surface geothermal features may be located several kilometers away from the actual fluid upwelling zone [Siler 2019, pp. 5-6].

## Limitations

- The methodology assumes all fault zones have potential to act as fluid upwelling zones and all areas outside fault zones have no potential, which is inherently flawed since many formations can have matrix or fracture permeability serving as stratigraphic reservoirs [Siler 2019, pp. 17-18].
- Most fault segments along faults are impermeable to upflow, meaning the assumption that the whole fault system has potential to host upflow overestimates prospective area [Siler 2019, pp. 17-18].
- Slip and dilation tendencies do not incorporate frictional fault strength and therefore do not indicate the complete stress state of a fault [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- 3D geologic surfaces can take on unrealistic geometries where data are sparse or absent [Siler 2019, pp. 12-14].
- Fault surfaces are progressively smoother with increasing depth due to increasing distance from surficial input data, making slip and dilation tendency values more variable near the surface than at depth [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- The analyses were conducted with relatively robust data coverage that may only be available in well-explored and/or developed systems [Siler 2019, pp. 27-28].
- The normalized proxies are summed semi-quantitatively rather than quantitatively scaled relative to one another, because site-specific data cannot quantitatively constrain how different proxies relate to each other [Siler 2019, pp. 23-26].
- Seismic reflection data have limited ability to image steeply dipping faults and detailed stratigraphy beneath thick volcanics due to complex velocity structure and high acoustic attenuation [Siler 2019, pp. 9-10].
- High electrical conductivity in MT data does not necessarily correlate with modern fluid circulation systems, as basin sedimentary sequences can also show high conductivity [Siler 2019, pp. 10-12].
- Lithologic interpretations from mud logs or driller logs can have highly variable quality and reliability [Siler 2019, pp. 9-10].
- The Mesozoic granitic and metamorphic basement structural and lithologic characteristics at Neal Hot Springs are not well constrained [Siler 2019, pp. 15-17].

## Assumptions / Conditions

- Most Neogene faults in the tectonically active western USA are assumed to be relatively near a critically stressed state, such that slip and dilation tendencies serve as appropriate proxies for fluid flow potential [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- Stress magnitudes are assumed to increase linearly with depth [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- At Tuscarora, a representative Basin and Range stress field was applied with Shmin/Sv = 0.62 (calculated at Astor Pass and Dixie Valley) and Shmax = (Shmin + Sv)/2 (as suggested at Desert Peak) [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- At Neal Hot Springs, the same representative stress field ratios were applied [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- The methodology is most directly applicable to structurally (or fault-) controlled geothermal systems in extensional and transtensional domains [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4].
- Modern downwelling and recharge are not evaluated as they appear not to be an important aspect of Basin and Range geothermal systems [Siler 2019, pp. 4-5].
- The 'breakdown region' proximal to a fault termination is proportional to 10⁻² times the half-length of the fault [Siler 2019, pp. 20-22].

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Slip tendency**: Ratio of resolved shear stress to normal stress on a fault plane; quantitative indicator of fault reactivation potential [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- **Dilation tendency**: Quantitative indicator of the resolved stresses on faults related to fracture opening potential [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- **Fault intersection/termination density**: Number of fault intersections and terminations per unit area, calculated in 3D space [Siler 2019, pp. 20-22].
- **Shmin/Sv ratio**: 0.62, applied as representative Basin and Range stress ratio [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- **Shmax**: Calculated as (Shmin + Sv)/2 [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- **Cell size**: 15 m for Tuscarora and Neal Hot Springs 3D geothermal potential maps [Siler 2019, pp. 17-18].
- **Fault zone width**: Typically ~10s of meters, consistent with scaling relationships and production well data [Siler 2019, pp. 17-18].
- **Heat flow**: ~85–110 mW/m² in northern Basin and Range [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4].
- **Geothermal gradient**: ~35–40 °C/km [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4].

## Reusable Claims

- In the Basin and Range province, the occurrence of a geothermal system is primarily controlled by the localization of structurally controlled pathways for fluid upwelling from ~3–5 km depth, given regionally high heat flow [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4]. *Condition*: Applies to extensional and transtensional domains with high regional heat flow. *Limitation*: Does not account for systems where magmatic heat sources are the primary control.

- The most common structural settings hosting geothermal systems in the Great Basin are fault terminations, fault step-overs, fault intersections, accommodation zones, displacement transfer zones, and pull-aparts, which are sites of inherent structural complexity with high local density of interconnected faults and fractures [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4]. *Condition*: Based on survey of ~450 known systems. *Limitation*: Does not cover all possible structural settings worldwide.

- Surface geothermal features (fumaroles, hot springs, geothermal deposits) may be located several kilometers away from the actual fluid upwelling zone, as upwelling fluids may be trapped beneath impermeable layers, mix with shallow groundwater, or charge permeable stratigraphic horizons [Siler 2019, pp. 5-6]. *Condition*: Structurally controlled systems in the Basin and Range. *Limitation*: Geochemical signatures of thermal fluids can help distinguish upflow from outflow.

- Critically stressed faults near failure are more conducive to fluid flow, and slip/dilation tendency calculations can identify which fault segments are most likely to be critically stressed under ambient stress conditions [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20]. *Condition*: Assumes faults are near critically stressed state. *Limitation*: Slip and dilation tendencies do not incorporate frictional fault strength.

- Structural discontinuities (fault intersections and terminations) are associated with relatively high density of secondary faults and fractures and are known to host fluid flow [Siler 2019, pp. 20-22]. *Condition*: Applies to structurally controlled geothermal systems. *Limitation*: The breakdown region scaling (10⁻² times half-length) is an approximation.

- Relatively weak lithologic units (shales, unconsolidated sediments) do not support dense, interconnected fault and fracture networks to the same degree as more competent units, affecting permeability development where cut by fault zones [Siler 2019, pp. 22-23]. *Condition*: General rock mechanics principle. *Limitation*: Highly fractured volcanic rocks may be weaker when faulted relative to devitrified tuffs and lava flows.

## Connections To Other Work

- The methodology adapts existing techniques developed over several decades in oil and gas and minerals exploration (Calcagno et al. 2008; Houlding 2012; Lindsay et al. 2013) and builds upon previous 3D characterization efforts in geothermal systems (Jolie et al. 2015a, b; Siler et al. 2016a) [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4].
- Classification follows Moeck and Beardsmore (2014) and Moeck (2014) for structurally controlled geothermal systems [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4].
- Structural settings for geothermal systems build on Faulds et al. (2006, 2011) and Faulds and Hinz (2015) survey of ~450 Great Basin geothermal systems [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4].
- Fault stress analysis follows methods of Sibson (1994, 1996), Barton et al. (1995), Morris et al. (1996), and Ferrill et al. (1999) [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20].
- Fault intersection/termination density calculation follows Alberti (2011) [Siler 2019, pp. 20-22].
- Results from Bradys and Astor Pass have been published previously (Siler and Faulds 2013a; Jolie et al. 2015a, b, 2016; Siler et al. 2012, 2016a, b; Witter et al. 2016), whereas new results from Tuscarora and Neal Hot Springs are presented in detail [Siler 2019, pp. 5-6].
- The approach is applicable to structurally controlled geothermal systems worldwide including the East African Rift system, Turkey, the Rhine Graben, and Iceland [Siler 2019, pp. 2-4].

## Open Questions

- How can these methods be applied effectively in areas with sparse subsurface data, such as truly blind geothermal systems with no surface expression? [Siler 2019, pp. 27-28]
- Can the semi-quantitative summation of normalized proxies be improved to a fully quantitative weighting scheme with site-specific calibration data? [Siler 2019, pp. 23-26]
- How do the 3D geothermal potential results change with different assumed stress field parameters or fault zone widths?
- What is the relative contribution of stratigraphic reservoirs versus fault-hosted permeability in these systems, given the assumption that only faults conduct upwelling? [Siler 2019, pp. 17-18]
- How does the progressive smoothing of fault surfaces with depth affect the reliability of stress analysis results at depth? [Siler 2019, pp. 18-20]
