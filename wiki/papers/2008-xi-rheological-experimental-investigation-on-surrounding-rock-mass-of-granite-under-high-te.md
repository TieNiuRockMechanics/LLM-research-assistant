# Rheological Experimental Investigation on Surrounding Rock Mass of Granite under High Temperature and Hydrostatic Stress.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates the rheological properties and failure mechanisms of surrounding rock mass in granite with a borehole under high temperature and hydrostatic stress conditions, identifying critical stress and temperature thresholds for creep and failure.

## Research Question
What are the rheological properties, mechanisms, and failure conditions of the surrounding rock mass in granite under high temperature and hydrostatic stress, relevant to the stability of boreholes in hot dry rock (HDR) geothermal energy extraction?

## Study Area / Data
- **Rock Material:** Granite samples (commercial name "Luhui") from Pingyi, Shandong, China. The granite is described as gray-white, dense, and crack-free [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].
- **Sample Geometry:** Cylindrical samples with dimensions φ200 mm × 400 mm, containing a central borehole of φ40 mm [Xi 2008, pp. 1-2].
- **Mineral Composition:** Quantitative X-ray diffraction analysis showed the granite consists of illite (25%), quartz (28%), feldspar (43%), calcite (1%), siderite (1%), and other minerals (2%) [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].
- **Experimental Equipment:** A self-developed "20 MN servo-controlled triaxial rock testing machine with high temperature and high pressure" was used. Key specifications include a maximum axial pressure of 318 MPa, maximum lateral solid pressure of 250 MPa, maximum stable heating temperature of 600°C, and pressure hold time over 360 hours [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].

## Methods
- **Experimental Setup:** The study used the 20 MN servo-controlled triaxial testing machine. The machine uses solid pressure transmission for confining pressure, with independent control of axial and confining loads. Deformation was measured with a grating ruler (precision 5/1000 mm) and temperature with thermocouples [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].
- **Sample Preparation:** Granite was machined into φ200 mm × 400 mm cylinders (height-to-diameter ratio 2:1), and a φ40 mm borehole was drilled along the central axis [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].
- **Microscopic Analysis:** X-ray diffraction (X-RD) was used for mineral composition analysis. A Leica MPV-SP micro-photometer was used to observe the meso-structure of granite at different temperatures [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].
- **Experimental Procedure:** A stepwise loading and heating creep experiment was conducted following ISRM standards. The heating rate was controlled at 3-5°C/h. Upon reaching a set temperature (100°C, 200°C, ..., 600°C), the temperature was held constant while maintaining a set hydrostatic stress (equivalent to depths of 500, 1000, ..., 6000 m). Each temperature-stress state was maintained for 48 hours before proceeding to the next stage until sample failure. Temperature error was strictly controlled within ±1°C [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].
- **Theoretical Analysis:** The deformation of the surrounding rock was simplified to a thick-walled cylinder model under hydrostatic stress. A viscoelastic analysis using the generalized Kelvin model was performed to solve for the stress and strain fields [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].

## Key Findings
1.  **Creep Behavior:** Under hydrostatic stress below 5000 m depth and temperature below 600°C, the surrounding rock in granite primarily exhibits steady-state creep. When stress reaches 5000 m hydrostatic stress and temperature is 600°C, the creep becomes unstable [Xi 2008, pp. 1-2].
2.  **Thresholds:** The creep of the surrounding rock possesses both a **creep stress threshold** and a **creep temperature threshold** [Xi 2008, pp. 1-2].
    -   The **temperature threshold** for creep is between 400°C and 500°C for the same depth [Xi 2008, pp. 5-7].
    -   The **stress threshold** for creep is between 4000 m and 5000 m hydrostatic stress (100-125 MPa) for the same temperature [Xi 2008, pp. 5-7].
3.  **Creep Rate:** Creep rates increase significantly when thresholds are exceeded. For example, at 5000 m depth, the creep rate at 500°C is 1.87 times that at 400°C [Xi 2008, pp. 5-7].
4.  **Rheological Mechanism:** The primary mechanism for creep and the existence of thresholds is the dislocation and micro-cracking within the intergranular cement and grains under thermo-mechanical coupling. This is a composite process of thermal fracturing and stress-induced damage [Xi 2008, pp. 5-7].
5.  **Failure Conditions:** The critical conditions for rheological failure of the granite with a borehole are a hydrostatic stress of 5000-6000 m (125-150 MPa) and a temperature of 500°C-600°C. The failure modes are compression fracture, transpression, or a combination of both [Xi 2008, pp. 5-7].
6.  **Model Validation:** Theoretical creep strain calculations using the generalized Kelvin model showed good agreement with experimental values at 600°C for different depths [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].

## Limitations
- The experiments were conducted on a specific type of granite ("Luhui" from Shandong). Results may vary for other granite types or rock masses.
- The study focused on hydrostatic stress conditions (lateral pressure coefficient = 1), which is an idealized state for deep rock masses [Xi 2008, pp. 1-2].
- The maximum test temperature was 600°C and maximum simulated depth was 6000 m. Behavior beyond these limits is not confirmed.
- The borehole diameter (40 mm) and sample size (200 mm diameter) are specific to this experimental setup.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The deep rock mass is in a hydrostatic stress state (σ_x = σ_y = σ_z = γh, lateral pressure coefficient = 1) [Xi 2008, pp. 1-2].
- The granite is treated as an isotropic material for the theoretical analysis [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].
- The generalized Kelvin model is assumed to adequately describe the viscoelastic creep behavior of the granite under the tested conditions [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables:** Temperature (100°C to 600°C), Hydrostatic stress (equivalent to 500 m to 6000 m depth, or ~12.5 MPa to 150 MPa).
- **Dependent Variables:** Axial creep strain, creep rate (ε̇).
- **Material Parameters:** Initial shear modulus (G₀), long-term shear modulus (G∞), retardation time (T_ret) for the generalized Kelvin model [Xi 2008, pp. 2-5].
- **Thresholds:** Creep temperature threshold (400°C-500°C), Creep stress threshold (4000-5000 m, 100-125 MPa).

## Reusable Claims
- Under hydrostatic stress, the surrounding rock mass in granite exhibits a clear creep temperature threshold between 400°C and 500°C, beyond which creep rates increase by an order of magnitude [Xi 2008, pp. 5-7].
- The creep stress threshold for granite under high temperature is between 100 MPa and 125 MPa (equivalent to 4000-5000 m depth) [Xi 2008, pp. 5-7].
- The rheological failure of granite with a borehole under coupled thermo-mechanical loading is characterized by brittle fracture (compression or shear) occurring rapidly after the creep rate accelerates, rather than a prolonged tertiary creep stage [Xi 2008, pp. 5-7].
- The primary micro-mechanism driving creep and threshold behavior in granite is the initiation and propagation of micro-cracks at grain boundaries and within intergranular cement due to thermal stress and mechanical load [Xi 2008, pp. 5-7].
