# Indeterminacy of Displacement and Stress of Geologic Rock Mass System in the Critically Non-Stationary State: Implication on Prediction of Geo-Hazards.

## One-line Summary
In the critically non-stationary state preceding failure, displacement and stress in any unit of a geologic rock mass system exhibit extremely random variation in both direction and magnitude, making precise prediction of geo-hazards based on limited monitoring points scientifically and economically impossible.

## Research Question
What is the evolution characteristic of displacement and stress in a geologic rock mass system during the critically unstable (non-stationary) state, and what are the implications for predicting geo-hazards like earthquakes and landslides?

## Study Area / Data
The study uses numerical simulation models and references practical monitoring data from two geo-hazard events in China: a phosphate mine landslide in Hubei province (1982) and the Ms 7.2 Song-Pan earthquake in Sichuan province (1976) [Feng 2021, pp. 1-2, 16-18]. Additional reference is made to the 1964 Niigate earthquake in Japan [Feng 2021, pp. 18-20].

## Methods
The authors employed numerical simulation using an elastic-plastic finite element method. A plane-rectangle model containing sixteen units with different stress-strain properties was created to represent a geologic rock mass system. A constant displacement rate was applied to the model's top, and the evolution of stress and displacement was calculated through seventy loading steps [Feng 2021, pp. 3-8]. The simulation results were then compared with practical monitoring data from the cited landslide and earthquake cases.

## Key Findings
1.  The deformation of the geologic rock mass system model displays three phases: a linear deformation stage, a critically unstable (non-linear) state, and a failure state [Feng 2021, pp. 8-14].
2.  In the linear stage, displacement and stress change monotonously. In the failure state, they exhibit consistent change. However, in the critically unstable state, both the direction and magnitude of displacement and stress in any unit or node experience extremely random, irregular, and unsteady variation [Feng 2021, pp. 8-14, 14-16].
3.  This random variation is the typical, universal, and definite characteristic of geologic rock mass deformation before failure [Feng 2021, pp. 14-16, 18-20].
4.  Due to the huge dimension of geologic rock mass systems, it is scientifically and economically impossible to judge failure using an infinite number of monitoring points. Data from a limited number of stations cannot provide sufficient statistical characteristics to reflect the real activity of the entire mass [Feng 2021, pp. 1-2, 16-18].
5.  Practical cases (phosphate mine landslide, Song-Pan earthquake) confirm that monitoring parameters like displacement and groundwater level exhibit random changes before system failure, consistent with the numerical simulation results [Feng 2021, pp. 16-18].

## Limitations
1.  The numerical models used are very simple (plane-rectangle, 16 units) compared to the complexity of real geologic rock mass systems [Feng 2021, pp. 14-16].
2.  The study concludes that predicting failure based on limited monitoring points is impossible, but does not provide a new, reliable predictive criterion [Feng 2021, pp. 1-2, 18-20].
3.  The analysis relies on a few historical case studies for validation [Feng 2021, pp. 16-18].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The simplified numerical model (a structure of units with different mechanical properties) can represent the essential behavior of a real geologic rock mass system [Feng 2021, pp. 3-8, 14-16].
2.  The behavior observed in the critically unstable state is an intrinsic property of the system, not an artifact of the numerical model [Feng 2021, pp. 14-16].
3.  The practical monitoring data from the cited cases accurately reflects the pre-failure state of the geologic systems.

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Claim:** In the critically non-stationary state, both the direction and magnitude of displacement and stress in any unit of a geologic rock mass system exhibit extremely random variation, not monotonic change.
    **Condition:** Based on numerical simulation of a simplified composite model and field case studies of landslides and earthquakes.
    **Limitation:** The claim describes a characteristic pattern but does not provide a method for precise failure prediction.
2.  **Claim:** The random variation of monitoring parameters (e.g., displacement, stress, groundwater level) is a typical and universal precursor to geologic rock mass system failure.
    **Condition:** Observed in numerical models and real-world landslide and earthquake cases.
    **Limitation:** This randomness makes prediction based on trend extrapolation from limited points unreliable.
