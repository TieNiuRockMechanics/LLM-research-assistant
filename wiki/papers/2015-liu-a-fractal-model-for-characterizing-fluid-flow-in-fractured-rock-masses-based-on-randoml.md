# A Fractal Model for Characterizing Fluid Flow in Fractured Rock Masses Based on Randomly Distributed Rock Fracture Networks.

## One-line Summary
A fractal model incorporating geometric distribution (\(D_f\)) and flow-path tortuosity (\(D_T\)) is used to estimate equivalent permeability of 2‑D discrete fracture networks generated by Monte Carlo sampling of a power-law length distribution; tortuosity effects can alter flow volumes by up to ~19.5 % and must not be neglected.

## Research Question
How can the fractal nature of fracture-length distributions and the tortuosity of fluid flow in rough individual fractures be combined into a single predictive model for the equivalent permeability of two‑dimensional rock-fracture networks, and how sensitive is that permeability to the two fractal dimensions \(D_f\) and \(D_T\) and to the inherent randomness of the network? [Liu unknown-year, pp. 1-5]

## Study Area / Data
The study is purely numerical; all fracture‑network models are computer‑generated using the Monte Carlo method. The base parameter set is taken from a field‑mapping study of a real rock mass (Baghbanan & Jing 2007) [Liu unknown-year, pp. 10-12]:
- Minimum trace length \(l_{\text{min}} = 0.5\) m, maximum trace length \(l_{\text{max}} = 500\) m → ratio \(l_{\text{min}}/l_{\text{max}} = 0.001\) (meets the fractal condition).  
- Fractal dimension \(D_f\) range: 1.3 – 1.8 (values that yield connected networks and represent naturally occurring rock masses).  
- Tortuosity fractal dimension \(D_T\) range: 1.000 – 1.018 (derived from JRC 0 – 20).  
- Aperture range: 1 µm – 100 µm.  
- Fracture orientations and centre points are uniformly distributed; the model length is 5.0 m.  

No field data are directly used; comparisons are made with published power-law exponents from other sites and models. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 5-7, 10-12, 15-17]

## Methods
1. **Fractal length distribution** – A probability density function for trace length \(l\) is derived from the cumulative power‑law \(N(L \ge l) = (l_{\max}/l)^{D_f}\) (Eqs. 3‑6), leading to an explicit expression for \(l_i\) from a uniform random number \(R_i\) (Eq. 11). The necessary condition \(l_{\min}/l_{\max} \ll 1\) is satisfied by using a ratio \(\le 10^{-3}\). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 5-7]

2. **Aperture–length correlation** – Aperture is correlated with trace length assuming longer fractures have larger hydraulic apertures; a log‑normal distribution with zero mean logarithmic aperture and unit standard deviation is used, yielding an inverse‑error‑function relation (Eqs. 12‑14). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10]

3. **Tortuosity** – The actual flow-path length \(l_t\) through a rough fracture is related to the straight length \(l\) and the hydraulic aperture \(e\) by \(l_t = l^{1-D_T} e^{D_T}\) (Eq. 15), where \(D_T\) is the fractal dimension of the streamline (\(D_T = 1\) gives the parallel‑plate limit). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10]

4. **Flow through a single fracture** – The cubic law is modified by substituting Eq. 15, giving the flow rate \(q(i)\) as a function of \(D_f\), \(D_T\), \(l_{\min}\), and \(R_i\) (Eq. 16). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10]

5. **Network‑scale flow** – Discrete fracture networks (DFNs) are generated by the Monte Carlo method; dead‑end and isolated fractures are removed. Mass continuity is enforced at each fracture intersection (Eq. 18), steady‑state flow is assumed with a fixed horizontal hydraulic gradient, and the total outlet flow is used with Darcy’s law to compute the equivalent permeability \(K\) (Eqs. 19‑20). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 10-12]

6. **Model size determination** – A relation between \(D_f\) and mass density \(d_m\) (total fracture length per unit area) is empirically fitted (Eq. 21); the number of fractures in a 5 m × 5 m domain is then deduced (Table 2). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 12-15, 38-42]

7. **Validation** – The fractal dimension of each generated DFN is computed by box‑counting and compared with the theoretical \(D_f\); the power‑law exponent \(a\) of the fracture‑number‑vs.‑length distribution is compared with literature values. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 15-17, 22-25]

## Key Findings
- **Length‑distribution validation** – The fracture number obeys a power law \(n(l,0.5) = \alpha l^{-a}\) with exponent \(a\) linearly related to \(D_f\): \(a = -D_f + 3.49\). For \(D_f\) from 1 to 2, \(a\) ranges from 2.49 to 1.49, matching reported field and theoretical values (Table 4). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 15-17, 22-25]
- **Fractal dimension of networks** – For \(D_f \ge 1.5\), the box‑counting \(D_f\) agrees well with the input theoretical value; for smaller \(D_f\) the computed \(D_f\) is underestimated because many isolated fractures are deleted. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 15-17]
- **Flow patterns** – At low \(D_f\) (< 1.5) flow is concentrated in a few long fractures subparallel to the gradient; as \(D_f\) increases above ~1.5, flow becomes more homogeneous and many shorter fractures participate. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 17-20]
- **Permeability vs. \(D_f\)** – Equivalent permeability \(K\) varies over several orders of magnitude for low \(D_f\) due to stochastic variability; as \(D_f\) increases (> 1.6) the spread narrows. The mean \(K\) follows an exponential increase with \(D_f\) (Fig. 9). A mathematical expression linking \(K\) and \(D_f\) is plausible for large \(D_f\) but requires additional parameters (e.g., connectivity) for small \(D_f\). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 17-20]
- **Permeability vs. \(D_T\)** – \(K\) decreases exponentially with increasing \(D_T\); larger \(l_{\min}/l_{\max}\) ratios also reduce \(K\) because the total number of fractures diminishes. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 17-20]
- **Tortuosity effect** – The relative error \(\delta\) between flow volumes computed with (\(Q_{\text{tortuosity}}\)) and without (\(Q_{\text{cubic}}\)) tortuosity ranges from 17.64 % to 19.51 % at \(D_T = 1.018\) (JRC ≈ 20) and is insensitive to \(D_f\) or the random seed. Hence tortuosity must be included for accurate permeability estimates. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 17-20]

## Limitations
- The model is restricted to 2‑D fracture networks; 3‑D effects are not considered. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 20-22]
- Fracture orientations and centre points are assumed uniformly distributed, ignoring realistic clustering or preferred orientations. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 12-15]
- Scaling effects on equivalent permeability (variation of \(K\) with model size) were not evaluated; the 5 m domain may not represent the REV for all \(D_f\). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 20-22]
- The aperture–length correlation uses a fixed log‑normal distribution with zero mean and unit standard deviation; other relations (e.g., power‑law) could alter results. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10]
- The model is validated only against previously reported power‑law exponents, not directly against field permeability data. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 15-17, 22-25]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- \(l_{\min}/l_{\max} \le 10^{-3}\) is required for the fracture population to exhibit fractal scaling; the study uses 0.001. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 5-7]
- Steady‑state, incompressible fluid flow with constant horizontal hydraulic gradient; no source/sink terms inside the network. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 10-12]
- The parallel‑plate cubic law holds locally for each fracture, with the tortuosity correction applied macroscopically. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10]
- Fracture intersections are modelled as simple nodes with mass continuity; no flow resistance at intersections is considered. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 10-12]
- The relation \(l_t = l^{1-D_T} e^{D_T}\) (Eq. 15) is assumed to hold for single fractures; \(D_T=1\) gives the straight‑line (non‑tortuous) limit. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Fractal dimension of fracture network geometry** \(D_f\) (1.3–1.8) – controls length distribution and mass density. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 1-5, 12-15]
- **Fractal dimension of flow‑path tortuosity** \(D_T\) (1.000–1.018) – derived from JRC, controls extra path length. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10, 12-15]
- **Minimum and maximum trace lengths** \(l_{\min}\) (0.5 m), \(l_{\max}\) (500 m) – define the power‑law support; their ratio must be \(\le 10^{-3}\). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 5-7, 10-12]
- **Random number** \(R_i\) – seeds the length generation for each fracture, introducing stochastic variability. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 5-7, 17-20]
- **Aperture** \(e_i\) – correlated to trace length via inverse error function; range 1–100 µm. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10, 12-15]
- **Model size** \(L_n = 5.0\) m – governed by mass‑density relation; number of fractures \(N_t'\) computed accordingly. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 12-15, 38-42]

## Reusable Claims
1. **Fractal length distribution condition** – For a 2‑D fracture network to be treated as fractal, the ratio \(l_{\min}/l_{\max}\) must be \(\le 10^{-3}\); when this holds, the probability density function for trace length is \(f(l) = D_f \, l_{\min}^{D_f} \, l^{-(D_f+1)}\) / [ 2 ] (Eq. 6) and a random length can be generated as \(l_i = l_{\min} / (1 - R_i)^{1/D_f}\) or equivalently \(l_i = l_{\max} / (R_i)^{1/(D_f-2)}\) (Eq. 11). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 5-7]
2. **Power‑law exponent of number–length distribution** – In such networks, the count of fractures in a bin of width ±0.5 m follows \(n(l,0.5) \propto l^{-a}\), with \(a = -D_f + 3.49\) (valid for \(D_f = 1.3–1.8\) under the chosen parameters). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 15-17]
3. **Permeability sensitivity to tortuosity** – Ignoring tortuosity (i.e., using the parallel‑plate cubic law) can overestimate flow volume by as much as **17.6 %–19.5 %** when \(D_T = 1.018\) (corresponding to JRC ≈ 20). This error increases exponentially with \(D_T\) but is independent of the geometric fractal dimension \(D_f\). [Liu unknown-year, pp. 17-20]
4. **Permeability–\(D_f\) relationship** – For networks with \(D_f \ge 1.5\), the mean equivalent permeability follows an exponential law \(K \propto \exp(\beta D_f)\); for \(D_f < 1.5\) the large scatter makes a single predictive expression unreliable without additional connectivity measures. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 17-20]
5. **Flow localisation** – When \(D_f\) is small (<1.5), the majority of flow passes through a few long, boundary‑intersecting fractures; above ~1.5, flow becomes dispersed and shorter fractures form preferential pathways. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 17-20]

## Connections To Other Work
- The proposed fractal length distribution yields power‑law exponents \(a\) of 1.49–2.49, which are consistent with field and modelling studies: Dverstop & Anderson (1989) reported \(a=1.7\); Bour & Davy (1997) reported \(a=1.0–3.0\); De Dreuzy et al. (2001) found \(a=0.0–3.5\) [Liu unknown-year, pp. 15-17, 22-25].
- The flow‑localisation behaviour mirrors De Dreuzy et al. (2001‑2002): for small exponent \(a\) (or low \(D_f\)) the largest fractures dominate; for intermediate \(a\) the flow is more uniform [Liu unknown-year, pp. 17-20].
- The findings align with fractal permeability models for porous media (Yu & Cheng 2002) and tree‑like networks (Xu et al. 2006, 2008) that also treat two fractal dimensions (one for pore/fracture size distribution, one for tortuosity) [Liu unknown-year, pp. 1-5, 22-25].
- The parameter ranges were adopted from Baghbanan & Jing (2007), who studied correlated length–aperture distributions in fractured rock masses. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10, 10-12]

## Open Questions
- **Scaling effects** – How does the equivalent permeability vary with model size (REV) for different \(D_f\) and \(D_T\)? The present study used a fixed 5 m × 5 m domain; scaling analysis is needed. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 20-22]
- **Three‑dimensional extension** – Can the fractal model be generalised to 3‑D fracture networks where fracture shapes, orientation distributions, and connectivity are more complex? [Liu unknown-year, pp. 20-22]
- **Validation with real hydraulic data** – The model has only been compared with length‑distribution statistics; direct comparison with in‑situ permeability measurements would strengthen confidence.
- **Alternative aperture–length correlations** – The assumption of a log‑normal aperture distribution with fixed parameters may not hold for all rock types; sensitivity to this correlation should be explored. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 7-10]
- **Influence of fracture orientation** – The current model uses uniform orientations; the combined effect of fractal length distribution and realistic orientation clustering on permeability remains to be quantified. [Liu unknown-year, pp. 12-15]
