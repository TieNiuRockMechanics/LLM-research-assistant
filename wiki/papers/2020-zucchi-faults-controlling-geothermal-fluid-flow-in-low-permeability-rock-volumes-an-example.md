# Faults Controlling Geothermal Fluid Flow in Low Permeability Rock Volumes: An Example from the Exhumed Geothermal System of Eastern Elba Island (Northern Tyrrhenian Sea, Italy).

## One-line Summary
This study demonstrates that high-angle normal to oblique-slip faults acted as primary conduits for super-hot geothermal fluids within the low-permeability cap rocks of an exhumed geothermal system in Eastern Elba Island, controlling fluid flow, mineralization, and skarnoid formation.

## Research Question
How do faults control geothermal fluid circulation and fluid-rock interaction within the low-permeability cap rocks of a magma-driven geothermal system?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the exhumed Neogene geothermal system exposed along the seacoast in the Acquarilli and Norsi beaches of southeastern Elba Island, Italy [Zucchi 2020, pp. 1-2]. The area is located in the footwall of the Zuccale Fault, along a NE-striking high-angle fault system interpreted as part of a regional transfer zone [Zucchi 2020, pp. 2-4]. The host rocks are early Cretaceous clayey-carbonate sedimentary rocks (Argille a Palombini Fm) interbedded with tectonic slices of serpentinite, representing the cap rock of the paleo-geothermal system [Zucchi 2020, pp. 1-2, 4-5]. The dataset includes twenty-eight rock and mineralized fault/vein samples, ninety kinematic data points from fault zones, and integrated structural, petrographic, mineralogical, and fluid inclusion analyses [Zucchi 2020, pp. 2-4].

## Methods
The methodological approach integrated fieldwork, structural-kinematic analysis, petrographic and mineralogical studies (including SEM/EDS), and fluid inclusion analyses supported by Raman spectroscopy [Zucchi 2020, pp. 2-4]. Microthermometric analyses were performed on fluid inclusions in quartz and calcite to determine physical-chemical characteristics (temperature, salinity, P-T trapping conditions) of the paleo-geothermal fluids [Zucchi 2020, pp. 7-9, 20-21]. Phase-equilibrium curves and fluid inclusion isochores were computed using software (winTWQ, ISOC, HOKIEFLINCS, Q2) to estimate P-T conditions [Zucchi 2020, pp. 12-14, 20-21].

## Key Findings
1.  Two generations of NE- and NW-trending high-angle normal to oblique-slip faults were identified, with the first generation mineralized by Fe-amphibole + quartz + calcite ± chlorite ± epidote ± titanite, and the second by calcite + quartz [Zucchi 2020, pp. 5-7].
2.  Fluid inclusion data indicate at least two distinct paleo-geothermal fluids circulated through the fault zones at a maximum pressure of about 0.8 kbar [Zucchi 2020, pp. 1-2, 12-14].
    *   The first fluid was H₂O-rich, enriched in CO₂ (from decarbonation/dehydration of host rocks), with temperatures up to 495 °C and salinity of 1.0–6.4 wt.% NaClₑqᵤᵢᵥ. It represents residual intra-formational fluid mobilized during faulting [Zucchi 2020, pp. 1-2, 14-17].
    *   The second fluid was likely meteoric, CO₂-devoid, with lower temperatures (146.5–254 °C) and salinity of 1.9–2.9 wt.% NaClₑqᵤᵢᵥ, related to the final circulation event [Zucchi 2020, pp. 1-2, 14-17].
3.  Fluid-rock interaction produced skarnoids with three distinct mineralized zones (wollastonite-diopside, garnet, clinopyroxene-K-feldspar) in metacarbonate layers, and amphibole-rich vein networks in metapelitic layers [Zucchi 2020, pp. 5-7, 17-18].
4.  Geothermal fluid circulation occurred within restricted, fault-damaged volumes in an extensional setting, dissecting rocks previously affected by high-temperature contact metamorphism from a cooling magmatic intrusion (Porto Azzurro or Monte Capanne monzogranite) [Zucchi 2020, pp. 1-2, 19-20].

## Limitations
*   The study examines an exhumed geothermal system, which may not perfectly represent active, buried systems [Zucchi 2020, pp. 2-4].
*   Fluid inclusion preservation and interpretation can be complex, especially regarding CO₂ content and its effect on salinity calculations [Zucchi 2020, pp. 10-12].
*   The exact source magmatic body (Porto Azzurro vs. Monte Capanne) responsible for the thermal perturbation is not definitively confirmed [Zucchi 2020, pp. 17-18].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   The maximum pressure estimated for VL inclusions (775 bars) is assumed to be the maximum pressure for all subsequent LV inclusion trapping in an extensional setting [Zucchi 2020, pp. 12-14].
*   The VL inclusions record residual intra-formational fluid from metamorphic reactions that was later mobilized by faulting [Zucchi 2020, pp. 14-17].
*   The low-angle normal fault described by Viola et al. (2018) is interpreted here as part of the extensional tectonic regime, not a Pliocene thrust [Zucchi 2020, pp. 19-20].

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Temperature:** Ranges from up to 495 °C (first fluid) down to 146.5 °C (second fluid) [Zucchi 2020, pp. 1-2, 10-12].
*   **Salinity:** Ranges from 1.0 to 6.4 wt.% NaClₑqᵤᵢᵥ [Zucchi 2020, pp. 10-12].
*   **Pressure:** Maximum estimated at ~0.8 kbar (775 bars) [Zucchi 2020, pp. 1-2, 12-14].
*   **Fluid Composition:** H₂O-CO₂ (first fluid) evolving to H₂O (second fluid) [Zucchi 2020, pp. 14-17].
*   **Fault Kinematics:** Normal to oblique-slip (left-lateral and right-lateral) [Zucchi 2020, pp. 5-7].
*   **Mineral Assemblages:** Amphibole+quartz+calcite (Fault 1), Calcite+quartz (Fault 2), Wollastonite+diopside+garnet (Skarnoid) [Zucchi 2020, pp. 5-7].

## Reusable Claims
*   In low-permeability cap rocks, high-angle normal to oblique-slip faults can serve as the primary conduits for geothermal fluid flow, creating restricted, mineralized damage zones [Zucchi 2020, pp. 1-2, 19-20].
*   Super-hot (>400°C) geothermal fluids generated by metamorphic dehydration/decarbonation reactions can be stored and mobilized within fault zones in cap rocks if located near a cooling magmatic body [Zucchi 2020, pp. 19-20].
*   The evolution of a geothermal fluid from a hot, CO₂-bearing intra-formational type to a cooler, CO₂-depleted meteoric type can be recorded in successive generations of fault-hosted mineralization and fluid inclusions [Zucchi 2020, pp. 14-17].
*   Skarnoid formation in impure carbonate layers adjacent to pelitic layers is controlled by fluid-rock interaction and compositional gradients, producing distinct reaction zones [Zucchi 2020, pp. 17-18].

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study area is part of the Tuscan geothermal fields (Larderello, Mt. Amiata) and shares similarities with the exhumed system at Procchio-Spartaia in northern Elba, though at different P-T conditions [Zucchi 2020, pp. 2-4, 17-18].
*   The structural setting relates to regional extensional tectonics and transfer zones controlling magmatism in the Northern Apennines and Tuscan Magmatic Province [Zucchi 2020, pp. 2-4, 19-20].
*   The work contrasts with interpretations by Viola et al. (2018) regarding the timing and nature (extensional vs. compressional) of faulting in the area [Zucchi 2020, pp. 19-20].

## Open Questions
*   What is the precise role of pre-existing stratigraphic discontinuities versus fault-related fractures in controlling fluid flow pathways within the cap rock?
*   How does the progressive infiltration of meteoric water affect the long-term chemical evolution and heat content of the geothermal reservoir?
*   Can the findings from this exhumed analogue be quantitatively scaled to predict fluid flow in active, buried geothermal systems?
