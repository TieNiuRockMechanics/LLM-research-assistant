# Quantitative Structural–Geological Exploration of Fault-Controlled Geothermal Systems—A Case Study from the Basin-and-Range Province, Nevada (USA).

## One-line Summary
A 3D structural-geological model combined with slip- and dilation tendency analysis of the Brady's geothermal system demonstrates a method to identify favorable drilling targets in fault-controlled geothermal reservoirs.

## Research Question
How can a three-dimensional structural-geological model integrated with fault stress modeling be used to efficiently identify favorable drilling targets in a fault-controlled geothermal system?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Brady's geothermal system, located approximately 80 km northeast of Reno in the Basin-and-Range Province, Nevada, USA [Jolie 2015, pp. 1-2]. The system is part of the Hot Springs Mountains and is an extensional amagmatic geothermal system [Jolie 2015, pp. 1-2]. Data sources include detailed 1:24,000 geological mapping, four geological cross sections, two 2D seismic reflection profiles, gravity data, and analysis of drill cuttings and cores from 19 wells [Jolie 2015, pp. 2-4]. The known geothermal reservoir is located at a depth of 600 m–1,500 m below the surface within Oligocene ash-flow tuffs and Miocene lava flow formations [Jolie 2015, pp. 2-4].

## Methods
A three-dimensional structural-geological model was developed using earthVision 8.0.1 software [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-5]. The model dimensions were 7.2 km × 8.3 km × 3.0 km, with the base at 1,000 m below sea level [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-5]. Faults were grouped into hierarchical classes and modeled from high to low hierarchy [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-5]. The stress field was estimated using stress inversion of 37 fault slip measurements from the neighboring Desert Peak and Desert Queen areas, assuming similar principal stress orientations [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-5]. In situ stress ratios were estimated using Anderson's theory of faulting and the empirical approach of Jaeger et al. (2007) for different friction coefficients representing weak (μ=0.56), average (μ=0.85), and strong (μ=0.98) rock strength [Jolie 2015, pp. 5-7]. Slip tendency (Ts) and dilation tendency (Td) were calculated for all fault planes in the model to quantify their favorability for fluid flow [Jolie 2015, pp. 7-8].

## Key Findings
The 3D model comprises 61 discrete fault planes, 71 fault blocks, and eight geological units [Jolie 2015, pp. 7-8]. The determined stress field indicates a normal faulting regime (σv > σH,max = σ2 > σh,min = σ3) with some strike-slip effects [Jolie 2015, pp. 7-8]. The stress ratio R from surface data inversion is 0.53 [Jolie 2015, pp. 7-8]. Major geothermal surface manifestations correlate spatially with N- to NNE-oriented, steeply westwards dipping fault zones with dilation tendencies of 0.8–0.9 and slip tendencies of 0.3–0.35 [Jolie 2015, pp. 9-11]. Faults within the reservoir are primarily dominated by a high dilational trend rather than a high slip tendency, indicating they are not critically stressed under current conditions [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-13]. A fluid pressure increase of ~16 MPa would be required for fault reactivation along selected fault planes under average rock strength conditions [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-13].

## Limitations
The study assumes that the resolved stress regime from exposed faults in neighboring areas is also acting at reservoir depths at Brady's [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-5]. Stress field variations occur over relatively small distances, as indicated by disagreement between Brady's borehole breakout data and inverted surface data [Jolie 2015, pp. 7-8]. The methodology is expected to be effective in tectonically active regions where surface stress indicators reflect subsurface conditions [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-13]. The extent and recharge mechanisms of the geothermal system are not comprehensively understood [Jolie 2015, pp. 2-4].

## Assumptions / Conditions
Faults are treated as cohesionless with uniform properties, and individual rock mechanical properties of stratigraphic horizons are not considered [Jolie 2015, pp. 5-7]. The Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion is applied [Jolie 2015, pp. 5-7]. It is assumed that measured striae in faults occur parallel to the maximum shear stress acting on the fault plane and that the stress state has remained relatively uniform over time [Jolie 2015, pp. 4-5]. The friction coefficients of rocks are used to infer fault properties since fault friction coefficients are not known [Jolie 2015, pp. 5-7].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Principal stresses: σ1 (maximum), σ2 (intermediate), σ3 (minimum)
- Friction coefficient (μ): 0.56 (weak), 0.85 (average), 0.98 (strong)
- Slip tendency (Ts): ratio of shear stress to normal stress on a fault plane
- Dilation tendency (Td): normalized measure of likelihood of a fault to dilate
- Stress ratio R: (σ2 - σ1)/(σ3 - σ1)
- Pore fluid pressure (Pp)
- Depth: 1,463 m (for stimulation well 15-12)

## Reusable Claims
- Combining 3D structural-geological modeling with slip- and dilation tendency analysis can efficiently contribute to the identification of favorable drilling targets in fault-controlled geothermal systems [Jolie 2015, pp. 1-2].
- In the Brady's geothermal system, faults are primarily dominated by a high dilational trend rather than a high slip tendency, indicating that critically stressed faults are not present under current stress conditions [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-13].
- A fluid pressure increase of approximately 16 MPa would be required to induce fault reactivation along optimally oriented fault planes in the Brady's reservoir under average rock strength conditions [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-13].
- The spatial distribution of slip- and dilation tendency, rather than absolute values, is of primary interest in structural analysis for identifying fluid pathways [Jolie 2015, pp. 9-11].
- The applied methodology is expected to be effective in tectonically active regions such as the Basin-and-Range Province where stress indicators at the surface reflect the stress field in the subsurface [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-13].

## Connections To Other Work
The study extends to neighboring areas of Desert Peak and Desert Queen, where local surface-based fault slip data and borehole data demonstrate slight variations in the stress field among neighboring geothermal systems [Jolie 2015, pp. 11-13]. Similar approaches of slip- and dilation tendency analysis have been applied to other geothermal sites (e.g., Moeck et al., 2009b) [Jolie 2015, pp. 2-4]. The methodology can be useful for EGS stimulation experiments, such as those planned for well 15-12 at Brady's [Jolie 2015, pp. 2-4].

## Open Questions
The extent and recharge mechanisms of the Brady's geothermal system are not comprehensively understood [Jolie 2015, pp. 2-4]. Local recharges to the geothermal system are assumed to be minimal due to the arid climate, but this is not confirmed [Jolie 2015, pp. 2-4]. The interaction of fault density and fault stress conditions, as explored by fault intersection/tip density analysis (Siler and Faulds, 2013), may be a reasonable addition to the fault stress analysis but requires further investigation [Jolie 2015, pp. 9-11].
