# Laboratory Investigation on Physical and Mechanical Properties of Granite After Heating and Water‑Cooling Treatment.

## One-line Summary
Heating and rapid water-cooling treatment significantly deteriorates the physical and mechanical properties of granite, with changes governed by competing thermal hardening and cracking processes, and a critical temperature threshold around 500 °C.

## Research Question
What are the effects of heating and rapid water-cooling treatment on the physical and mechanical properties of granite, and how do these properties evolve with temperature?

## Study Area / Data
The granite samples were selected from the Dabie mountains area of Hubei Province, China [Zhang 2017, pp. 2-3]. The mineral composition is mainly feldspar, mica, and quartz, with average mass fractions of 35.43% albite, 24.51% potash feldspar, 28.77% mica, and 10.02% quartz [Zhang 2017, pp. 2-3]. The natural density is 2.61 g/cm³, and the initial average porosity is 1.19% [Zhang 2017, pp. 2-3]. Cylindrical samples were 37 mm in diameter and 74 mm in length [Zhang 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Methods
- **Thermal Treatment:** Samples were heated in a furnace at a rate of 5 °C/min to temperatures of 25, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, and 900 °C, held for 4 hours, then rapidly cooled by immersion in water at ambient temperature for 1 hour [Zhang 2017, pp. 2-3].
- **Porosity Measurement:** Open porosity was determined using a vacuum saturation technique based on dry and saturated weights [Zhang 2017, pp. 3-4].
- **Thermal Conductivity:** Measured using a system based on the transient state comparative method [Zhang 2017, pp. 4-6].
- **Ultrasonic Velocity:** P-wave velocity was measured using a high-power pulse emitter (Model TH204) with a resonant frequency of 2.5 MHz [Zhang 2017, pp. 6-7].
- **Gas Permeability:** Measured using the steady flow method with argon gas under a confining pressure of 5 MPa and an injection pressure of 1.5 MPa [Zhang 2017, pp. 7-8].
- **Mechanical Testing:** Triaxial compression tests were performed under strain-rate control (0.0002 min⁻¹) at confining pressures of 0, 5, 10, and 15 MPa [Zhang 2017, pp. 8-11].

## Key Findings
- **Porosity:** Increased from 1.19% at 25 °C to 6.13% at 900 °C, with a significant increase above 500 °C [Zhang 2017, pp. 3-4].
- **Thermal Conductivity:** Continuously decreased with increasing treatment temperature [Zhang 2017, pp. 4-6].
- **P-wave Velocity:** Decreased from 3343.25 m/s at 25 °C to 594.09 m/s at 900 °C, an 82% reduction [Zhang 2017, pp. 6-7].
- **Gas Permeability:** Increased from ~10⁻¹⁸ m² at 25 °C to ~10⁻¹⁵ m² at 900 °C, a three-order-of-magnitude increase [Zhang 2017, pp. 7-8].
- **Elastic Modulus & Cohesion:** Below 200 °C, these properties increased with temperature. Between 200 and 500 °C, they showed no obvious change. Above 500 °C, they decreased significantly [Zhang 2017, pp. 1-2, 13-15].
- **Internal Friction Angle:** Exhibited an inverse trend to cohesion, decreasing below 200 °C, remaining constant between 200-500 °C, and increasing above 500 °C [Zhang 2017, pp. 13-15].
- **Behavior Transition:** At 900 °C, the granite showed an obvious elastic–plastic behavior, indicating a transition from brittle to ductile [Zhang 2017, pp. 1-2].

## Limitations
- The confining pressures used (0-15 MPa) may be relatively low for some geothermal engineering applications [Zhang 2017, pp. 8-11].
- Further studies are needed to confirm the quartz α-β transition at 573 °C and the polymorphic transition at 800 °C in the studied granite samples [Zhang 2017, pp. 4-6].
- The Klinkenberg effect was not considered in gas permeability calculations due to the injection pressure level [Zhang 2017, pp. 7-8].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The mechanical strength of granite is described by the linear Mohr–Coulomb criterion [Zhang 2017, pp. 13-15].
- The fluid flow in permeability tests was assumed to be one-dimensional and steady-state [Zhang 2017, pp. 7-8].
- The low heating rate (5 °C/min) was used to minimize thermal gradients and ensure cracking was induced by temperature, not gradient [Zhang 2017, pp. 2-3].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variable:** Heating temperature (25, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 900 °C).
- **Cooling Method:** Rapid water-cooling (immersion for 1 hour).
- **Confining Pressure:** 0, 5, 10, 15 MPa in triaxial tests.
- **Measured Properties:** Porosity, thermal conductivity, P-wave velocity, gas permeability, elastic modulus, Poisson's ratio, peak stress, cohesion, internal friction angle.

## Reusable Claims
- **Condition:** For granite subjected to heating and rapid water-cooling treatment. **Limitation:** Below 500 °C, thermal hardening is the dominant process, leading to increases in elastic modulus and cohesion. Above 500 °C, thermal cracking dominates, causing significant decreases in these properties [Zhang 2017, pp. 15-16].
- **Condition:** For granite from the Dabie mountains. **Limitation:** The porosity increase is primarily due to microcracks from rapid cooling, mineral transformation (especially quartz α-β transition at ~573 °C), and weakening of mineral particles [Zhang 2017, pp. 1-2].
- **Condition:** For the tested granite. **Limitation:** The cooling rate significantly influences the degree of thermal cracking; rapid water-cooling induces more damage than slow air-cooling [Zhang 2017, pp. 15-16].
- **Condition:** For granite samples. **Limitation:** There is a clear correlation between porosity and other physical properties: permeability increases with porosity, while thermal conductivity and P-wave velocity decrease [Zhang 2017, pp. 15-16].
