# Fault-Controlled Circulation of Meteoric Water and Mantle Volatiles in the Coso and Beowawe Geothermal Systems, Basin and Range, USA.

## One-line Summary
This study uses hydrogeologic models to demonstrate that fault-controlled circulation of deeply penetrating meteoric water, rather than direct mantle fluid flux, is the dominant mechanism transporting mantle volatiles (like 3He) to shallow crustal levels in the Coso and Beowawe geothermal systems.

## Research Question
The study quantitatively tests the hypothesis that fault-controlled circulation of meteoric fluids is the dominant mechanism transporting mantle volatiles from the brittle-ductile boundary to the near-surface environment in the Great Basin, and evaluates the alternative scenario of focused mantle fluid flow up through deep faults [Person 2011, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on two contrasting geothermal systems in the Basin and Range province: the Coso geothermal field in eastern California (Walker Lane belt) and the Beowawe geothermal field in north-central Nevada (Miocene northern Nevada rift) [Person 2011, pp. 1-1]. Both are active systems with reservoir temperatures between 120 °C and 350 °C, elevated shallow heat flow, and fault-controlled groundwater discharge [Person 2011, pp. 1-1]. Coso is characterized by active normal faulting, bimodal volcanism, and high helium isotope ratios (R/Ra up to 7.08), while Beowawe lacks Quaternary magmatism and has a low R/Ra value (0.46) [Person 2011, pp. 1-2]. Data include detailed seismic reflection data, geologic mapping, temperature-depth profiles, and geochemical/isotopic tracer data (δ18O, δD, 3He/4He) [Person 2011, pp. 1-2, 2-3].

## Methods
Idealized cross-sectional hydrogeologic models were developed for each system using seismic and geologic data [Person 2011, pp. 2-3]. The models assigned permeability values consistent with field measurements, with a low-permeability value (10⁻¹⁸ m²) for units >350 °C to simulate the brittle-ductile transition zone [Person 2011, pp. 2-3]. The solution domains were discretized with triangular elements, with mesh refinement along faults [Person 2011, pp. 2-3]. For Coso, two scenarios were simulated: one with deep meteoric fluid circulation only, and one including a mantle fluid flux at the base of the Coso Wash fault [Person 2011, pp. 2-3]. For Beowawe, basal R/Ra values were varied between 8 and 2 [Person 2011, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
- At Coso, the model indicates a loop-style free convection system with flow cells extending to ~6 km depth, scavenging mantle helium that has diffused through the brittle-ductile zone [Person 2011, pp. 2-3]. Simulated R/Ra values (6.9–7.3) match the reported value of 7.08 [Person 2011, pp. 3-4].
- At Beowawe, the model shows a single-pass flow system where circulation is constrained above the brittle-ductile transition zone by high-permeability horizons, resulting in low R/Ra values (0.39–0.55) that match the reported value of 0.46 [Person 2011, pp. 3-4].
- Computed fluid δ18O compositions match field data, with heavier values at Coso due to higher temperature fluid-rock exchange and negligible shifts at Beowawe due to faster circulation and lower temperatures [Person 2011, pp. 3-4].
- Simulations including a deep magmatic fluid flux at Coso produced a mixing pattern not observed in field data [Person 2011, pp. 3-4].
- Fluid circulation in both systems is dominated by natural (density-driven) convection, not topography-driven flow [Person 2011, pp. 3-4].

## Limitations
The models are idealized representations based on available seismic and geologic data [Person 2011, pp. 2-3]. The study does not consider all possible mechanisms for mantle volatile transport (e.g., diffusion across the brittle-ductile boundary, transient brittle behavior) in quantitative detail [Person 2011, pp. 1-1]. The Beowawe model did not include a mantle fluid flux scenario [Person 2011, pp. 2-3].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The brittle-ductile transition zone is simulated by assigning a low permeability (10⁻¹⁸ m²) to all units and fault segments with temperatures >350 °C [Person 2011, pp. 2-3].
- Thermal and hydrologic properties, as well as reactive surface areas, are assigned to each stratigraphic unit as per Data Repository tables [Person 2011, pp. 2-3].
- For Coso, the mantle fluid flux scenario assumes a flux of 3.32·10⁻⁸ kg m⁻² s⁻¹ at the base of the Coso Wash fault [Person 2011, pp. 2-3].
- For Beowawe, basal R/Ra values are varied between 8 and 2 to test sensitivity [Person 2011, pp. 2-3].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Fault permeability: 10⁻¹² to 10⁻¹⁵ m² for deep permeable faults [Person 2011, pp. 1-1, 4-4].
- Reservoir temperatures: 120 °C to 350 °C [Person 2011, pp. 1-1].
- Vertical groundwater flow rates: ~0.3 m/yr along Coso Wash fault; 50–10 m/yr within Malpais fault [Person 2011, pp. 2-3].
- Helium isotope ratios (R/Ra): Reported values of 7.08 at Coso and 0.46 at Beowawe [Person 2011, pp. 1-2].
- Fluid δ18O shifts: 5‰–10‰ at Coso; <3‰ at Beowawe [Person 2011, pp. 1-2].

## Reusable Claims
- In extensional geothermal systems like Coso, deeply circulating meteoric water can scavenge mantle 3He that has diffused through the brittle-ductile transition zone, producing high near-surface R/Ra ratios without requiring significant direct mantle fluid flux [Person 2011, pp. 3-4, 4-4].
- The presence of stratiform high-permeability zones within an aquifer system (as at Beowawe) can prevent groundwater from reaching the brittle-ductile transition zone, resulting in low R/Ra ratios insensitive to deeper 3He inputs [Person 2011, pp. 3-4].
- Deeply penetrating, permeable faults (10⁻¹² to 10⁻¹⁵ m²) continuous to at least 5 km depth are critical components for generating thermal anomalies in modern Great Basin geothermal systems [Person 2011, pp. 4-4].
- Fluid circulation in these continental geothermal systems is driven by free (density-driven) convection rather than topography-driven flow [Person 2011, pp. 3-4].

## Connections To Other Work
The study references and builds upon work by Kennedy et al. (1997) and Kennedy and van Soest (2007) on mantle 3He transport along the San Andreas fault [Person 2011, pp. 1-1]. It also considers mechanisms proposed by Kulongoski et al. (2005) involving episodic overpressure and fault failure [Person 2011, pp. 1-1]. The models use permeability and thermal data from prior studies (e.g., Monastero et al., 2005; Person et al., 2008) [Person 2011, pp. 2-3, 4-4].

## Open Questions
- How do the mechanisms of mantle 3He transport (direct intrusion, diffusion, transient brittle behavior) vary in importance across different tectonic settings? [Person 2011, pp. 1-1]
- What is the precise role of fault-valve behavior and episodic overpressure in mantle-crust volatile transport in these systems? [Person 2011, pp. 1-1]
