# Characteristics and Controlling Factors of Archean Metamorphic Reservoirs in Bohai Sea Area.

## One-line Summary
This study systematically characterizes Archean metamorphic reservoirs in the Bohai Sea area and identifies six primary controlling factors, noting distinct formation mechanisms between shallow and deep buried hills.

## Research Question
The research aims to summarize the characteristics of Archean metamorphic buried hill reservoirs in the Bohai Sea area and to systematically analyze their main controlling factors, addressing a gap in previous studies that focused on specific structures or single reservoir types [Du 2021, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study area is the Bohai Sea area, located in the eastern part of the Bohai Bay Basin. The data are based on sidewall coring, core, and thin section observations from over 136 wells that penetrated Archean metamorphic rocks, supplemented by scanning electron microscopy and conventional physical property experiments [Du 2021, pp. 1-2, 3-6].

## Methods
The methodology involved systematic observation of rock samples to determine lithology and reservoir space types, analysis of physical property (porosity and permeability) data versus depth, integration of well logging curves to define reservoir zonation, and synthesis of regional tectonic evolution to identify controlling factors [Du 2021, pp. 3-6, 6-10].

## Key Findings
1.  The Archean metamorphic rocks are mainly gneiss, metamorphic granite, and migmatite, with local granulite and amphibolite, providing a lithological basis for large-scale reservoirs [Du 2021, pp. 1-2].
2.  Shallow buried hills (depth <3500 m) exhibit a vertical zonation from top to bottom: clay zone, sandy zone, sandy gravel zone, fracture zone, and bedrock zone. Deep buried hills (depth >3500 m) are zoned as weathered fracture zone, inner fracture zone, and bedrock zone [Du 2021, pp. 3-6, 6-10].
3.  Reservoir formation is controlled by six factors: tectonic stress (controls fracture formation), weathering and leaching (controls dissolution pores in weathering crust), paleogeomorphology (controls distribution of different reservoir types), rock type (controls development of fractures and pores), deep thermal fluids (has dual control), and burial depth (controls reservoir type structure) [Du 2021, pp. 1-2, 6-10].
4.  Controlling factors differ with burial depth: shallow buried hill reservoirs are greatly affected by weathering/leaching and paleogeomorphology, while deep buried hill reservoirs are mainly controlled by tectonics, followed by lithology [Du 2021, pp. 1-2, 10-12].

## Limitations
The study is focused on the Bohai Sea area, and the specific controlling factors and their relative importance may vary in other geological settings. The research is based on existing well data, which may not fully capture the heterogeneity of the entire region.

## Assumptions / Conditions
The analysis assumes that the well data from over 136 wells are representative of the broader Archean metamorphic rock distribution in the Bohai Sea area. The classification of buried hills into shallow and deep types is based on a depth threshold of 3500 m [Du 2021, pp. 3-6].

## Key Variables / Parameters
-   **Lithology**: Gneiss, metamorphic granite, migmatite, granulite, amphibolite.
-   **Physical Properties**: Porosity (0.2%–32.8%), Permeability (0.01–2128.4)×10⁻³ μm².
-   **Burial Depth**: Classified as shallow (<3500 m) and deep (>3500 m).
-   **Controlling Factors**: Tectonic stress intensity, weathering/leaching intensity, paleogeomorphology, felsic mineral content, deep thermal fluid activity.

## Reusable Claims
-   In the Bohai Sea area, Archean metamorphic buried hill reservoirs can be classified into shallow and deep types, each with a distinct vertical zonation pattern and set of primary controlling factors [Du 2021, pp. 3-6, 6-10].
-   The formation of effective fracture systems in deep metamorphic reservoirs requires a multi-stage tectonic history, specifically an early compressional phase (e.g., Indosinian) to create crushed zones, followed by later extensional or strike-slip phases (e.g., Yanshanian, Himalayan) to reactivate and open these fractures [Du 2021, pp. 6-10].
-   Rock type, specifically the content of brittle felsic minerals, is a critical factor controlling reservoir quality at a local scale, with higher felsic content correlating with better-developed fractures and higher porosity [Du 2021, pp. 10-12].
-   Deep thermal fluids have a dual effect on metamorphic reservoirs: they can precipitate minerals that occlude pores (destructive) or dissolve minerals like feldspar to create secondary porosity (constructive), with the net effect depending on fluid composition and existing fracture networks [Du 2021, pp. 10-12].
