# Micromechanics of Thermally Induced Cracking in Three Crustal Rocks.

## One-line Summary
This study uses scanning electron microscopy on ion-milled samples and fracture mechanics modeling to investigate the micromechanisms of thermally induced cracking in Westerly granite, Frederick diabase, and Oak Hall limestone, finding that crack density depends on temperature, thermal expansion properties, initial porosity, and grain size.

## Research Question
What are the micromechanisms of thermally induced cracking in crustal rocks, and how do factors like temperature, thermal expansion mismatch, thermal expansion anisotropy, initial crack porosity, and grain size control the crack density and propagation behavior? [Fredrich 1986, pp. 1-1]

## Study Area / Data
The study used samples of three petrologically distinct crustal rocks: Westerly granite (Rhode Island), Frederick diabase (Maryland), and Oak Hall limestone (Pennsylvania). [Fredrich 1986, pp. 2-2] Cylindrical cores (18 mm diameter, 10 mm length) were heated to various peak temperatures (Table 1). [Fredrich 1986, pp. 2-2]

## Methods
- **Sample Preparation & Heating:** Samples were dried in vacuo, then heated to peak temperatures at rates <2°C/min with minimal thermal gradients (<3°C/cm). [Fredrich 1986, pp. 2-2]
- **Microscopy:** Polished sections were ion-milled, sputter-coated, and examined using a scanning electron microscope (SEM) with backscatter and EDX capabilities. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 2-2]
- **Stereological Analysis:** For Westerly granite, crack surface area per unit volume (Sv) was quantified using stereological techniques (line intercept method) on SEM images. Grain boundary and intragranular cracks were counted separately. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 2-3]
- **Fracture Mechanics Modeling:** A square inclusion model was developed to analyze stress fields due to thermal expansion mismatch and anisotropy. Stress intensity factors (K1) for crack propagation were calculated. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 5-7]

## Key Findings
- **Westerly Granite:** Grain boundary cracking concentrated between 100-165°C, with most boundaries cracked by 250°C. Intragranular cracking increased drastically above 250°C. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 3-4]
- **Frederick Diabase:** Significant cracking only above 500°C. Partial melt observed at 1040°C, filling many cracks. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 4-5]
- **Oak Hall Limestone:** Negligible thermal cracking observed up to 500°C, contradicting model predictions. This is attributed to possible plastic flow relaxing internal stresses. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 4-5, 18-18]
- **Crack Density Relationship:** The thermally induced crack surface area per unit volume (Sv) in Westerly granite shows a quadratic dependence on temperature increase (ΔT). [Fredrich 1986, pp. 1-1, 13-15]
- **Propagation Behavior:** Grain boundary crack propagation due to thermal expansion mismatch is unstable, while intragranular cracking is stable and incremental. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 9-12]

## Limitations
- The fracture mechanics models involve idealizations regarding grain geometry and assume isotropic thermoelastic properties for the matrix. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 15-16]
- The models are deterministic and do not account for statistical distributions of microcrack dimensions and fracture toughness. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 15-16]
- The study did not consider the effects of thermal gradients, confining pressure, or pore fluids. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 16-17]
- The quadratic relationship for Sv assumes temperature-independent material properties, which may not hold, especially above the quartz α-β transition (573°C). [Fredrich 1986, pp. 13-15]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The thermoelastic modulus of the polycrystalline aggregate matrix is isotropic. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 7-9]
- Elastic modulus is assumed identical for the matrix and the inclusion in the model. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 7-9]
- Crack propagation follows linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) principles. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 5-7]
- The energetic balance model assumes the factor of 8 is a geometric constant characterizing strain energy distribution. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 13-15]

## Reusable Claims
- The thermally induced crack surface area per unit volume (Sv) in Westerly granite exhibits a quadratic dependence on the temperature increase (ΔT), which can be explained by an energetic balance between thermoelastic strain energy and fracture surface energy. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 13-15]
- Grain boundary cracking due to thermal expansion mismatch is predicted to be unstable, leading to propagation until arrested at geometric junctures, while intragranular cracking is stable and proceeds incrementally. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 9-12]
- The negligible thermal cracking observed in fine-grained Oak Hall limestone, despite significant thermal expansion anisotropy, suggests that internal stresses may be relaxed by plastic flow mechanisms such as twinning, crystal plasticity, or superplastic flow. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 18-18]
- The crack propagation temperature for a given initial crack length is higher in Frederick diabase than in Westerly granite due to smaller thermal expansion mismatch and finer grain size. [Fredrich 1986, pp. 17-18]
