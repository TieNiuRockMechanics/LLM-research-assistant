# How Close to Failure Is a Granite Rock Mass at a 5 km Depth?

## One-line Summary

At the 5 km deep Soultz geothermal site, the granite rock mass is not close to failure under natural conditions; large‑scale shear failure requires pore‑pressure increments > 10 % of the minimum principal stress, while the first microseismic events appear at much smaller pressure changes and reflect only elastic stress‑perturbation (similar to a Kaiser effect) \[Cornet 2007, pp. 1-2, 18‑19\].

## Research Question

*What is the mechanical significance of microseismicity induced by pore‑pressure variations – does it signal only small effective‑stress perturbations (a Kaiser‑like effect) or the onset of large‑scale failure of a granite rock mass at 5 km depth?* \[Cornet 2007, pp. 1-2\]

## Study Area / Data

- **Site:** Soultz‑sous‑Forêts, upper Rhine graben (France), experimental geothermal reservoir in granite at depths down to 5 km.  
- **Wells:** GPK1 (3600 m, deepened to 3600 m in 1992), EPS1 (2200 m), GPK2 (3800 m, deepened to 5 km in 1999), GPK3, GPK4.  
- **Data sources:**
  - Hydraulic tests: straddle‑packer stress measurements, small‑scale reconnaissance injections, large‑scale injections (1993 in GPK1, 2003 in GPK3) \[Cornet 2007, pp. 2-4\].
  - Borehole images: four‑arm caliper, acoustic televiewer, Ultrasonic Borehole Imager (UBI) logs in GPK1 and GPK2 \[Cornet 2007, pp. 6-8\].
  - Induced seismicity: downhole 3D‑accelerometers + hydrophone (CSMA network) and surface network (EOPGS); >10 000 events recorded during 1993 injection \[Cornet 2007, pp. 8-9\].
  - Additional constraints: shear‑wave splitting, focal mechanisms, multiplets, density‑based overburden estimate, pore‑pressure profile [Eq. (10)] \[Cornet 2007, pp. 9-11, 14, 15‑16\].

## Methods

- **Stress‑field evaluation:**
  - Minimum principal stress (Sₕ) from shut‑in/reopening pressures of hydraulic tests, especially from stabilized injection pressures during large‑scale injections when pressure becomes independent of flow rate (interpreted as fracture opening pressure) \[Cornet 2007, pp. 3-4, 13‑14\].
  - Vertical stress (Sᵥ) from integrated rock density using Eq. (2c) \[Cornet 2007, pp. 12-13\].
  - Maximum horizontal stress (S_H) direction from borehole breakout orientations, drilling‑induced tensile fractures, thermal borehole‑cross‑section elongations \[Cornet 2007, pp. 6-8\].
  - Magnitude of S_H constrained by sub‑equality with Sᵥ inferred from mixture of normal/strike‑slip focal mechanisms, shear‑wave splitting (five‑constant elastic model), and orientation of induced shear zones \[Cornet 2007, pp. 14-15\].
- **Induced‑seismicity analysis:**
  - Event location and cloud shape monitoring during 1993 injection \[Cornet 2007, pp. 4, 8‑9\].
  - Multiplet identification and relative relocation to infer rupture‑plane orientations and link small‑scale slips to macroscopic failure planes \[Cornet 2007, pp. 8-9, 16\].
  - Comparison of observed seismic‑cloud orientations with those predicted by Coulomb failure under known stress field.
- **Failure‑criterion derivation:**
  - Estimate cohesion and friction angle by requiring that macroscopic failure occurs only after a pore‑pressure increase of ~3–8 MPa (dependent on distance from wellbore and diffusivity) \[Cornet 2007, pp. 15-17\].
  - Combine with geometry of induced shear zones: e.g., 23‑24° inclination to maximum principal stress direction.

## Key Findings

1. **Stress state down to 5 km:**
   - Sₕ varies linearly with depth: Sₕ = 0.54 Sᵥ [Eq. (6)]; at 2900 m Sₕ ≈ 39.5 MPa, at 4550 m the ratio Sₕ/Sᵥ = 0.537 \[Cornet 2007, pp. 13-15\].
   - S_H is sub‑equal to Sᵥ (0.95 Sᵥ ≤ S_H ≤ 1.1 Sᵥ) within 2800–3600 m \[Cornet 2007, p. 15\].
   - Maximum horizontal stress direction: N170° ± 10°E, with a suggestion of slight clockwise rotation with depth \[Cornet 2007, pp. 12-13\].
2. **Induced‑seismicity phases during 1993 injection:**
   - Phase 1 (wellhead pressure < 4 MPa): no observable microseismicity.
   - Phase 2 (4–9 MPa): axisymmetric growth of seismic cloud, rock remains macroscopically elastic.
   - Phase 3 (> 9 MPa): planar localisation of seismicity → large‑scale failure; tensile fractures open when Sₕ is reached, otherwise shear planes form.
   \[Cornet 2007, pp. 15-16\]
3. **Failure criterion:**
   - Large‑scale shear failure requires pore‑pressure increase larger than ~10 % of the natural Sₕ (≈ 3–8 MPa overpressure) and is described by a Coulomb criterion with cohesion C = 1–6 MPa and friction angle 39‑44° [Eq. (11)] \[Cornet 2007, pp. 16-17\].
   - The natural state was far from failure; the linear stress‑versus‑depth profile is not controlled solely by frictional equilibrium on pre‑existing faults.
4. **Role of rheology vs. friction:**
   - Outside zones of stress heterogeneity, principal stress directions are stable; the rock mass behaves as a continuum. The observed linear stress variation with depth likely reflects long‑term visco‑elastic (possibly pressure‑solution‑controlled) relaxation, not just Byerlee’s law \[Cornet 2007, pp. 17-18\].

## Limitations

- **Time‑dependent borehole failure:** Compressive breakouts appear only after ~1 year, preventing quantitative stress‑magnitude estimates from breakout width; stress‑corrosion or pore‑pressure diffusion effects unknown \[Cornet 2007, p. 8\].
- **S_H magnitude uncertainty:** Only bounded by sub‑equality; direct measurement impossible with current methods, and thermal‑stress corrections carry > 20 % uncertainty \[Cornet 2007, pp. 14-15\].
- **Stress heterogeneity:** Local principal‑stress direction variations up to ~15‑20° exist on scale < 100 m; microseismic events sample heterogeneous zones preferentially, biasing stress inversions \[Cornet 2007, pp. 12-13\].
- **Pervasive drilling‑induced fractures:** May have compromised small‑scale straddle‑packer tests, leading to systematically low Sₕ values; those data excluded from final profile \[Cornet 2007, p. 13\].
- **Cohesion and friction estimation:** Depends on assumed pore‑pressure diffusion and geometry; cohesion values range from 1 to 8 MPa depending on friction angle \[Cornet 2007, pp. 16-17\].

## Assumptions / Conditions

- Stabilized injection pressure during large‑scale tests equals the magnitude of the minimum principal stress exactly, with negligible thermal stress influence.  
- The vertical direction is a principal stress direction over most of the depth range (borehole deviation < 5°).  
- The rock mass is isotropic in its elastic properties; shear‑wave anisotropy is stress‑induced.  
- No tensile strength for the granite is assumed for interpretation of drilling‑induced fractures and borehole cross‑section elongation \[Cornet 2007, p. 7\].  
- Pore pressure follows P_f = 0.9 + 0.0098 z MPa (Evans et al. 1998) \[Cornet 2007, p. 14\].  
- The long‑term rheology controls stress variation; frictional equilibrium is not required.  
- Stress‑heterogeneity zones are limited to a few tens of metres, and filtering out wavelengths < 20 m yields a regional mean direction with σ ≈ 3° \[Cornet 2007, p. 13\].

## Key Variables / Parameters

- **Stress‑depth relations:**
  - Sₕ = 0.54 [33.8 + 0.0255 (z − 1377)] MPa \[Eq. (6), Cornet 2007, p. 14\].
  - Sᵥ = 33.8 + 0.0255 (z − 1377) MPa \[Eq. (2c), Cornet 2007, p. 12\].
  - S_H ≈ (0.95 − 1.1) Sᵥ within 2800‑3600 m \[Cornet 2007, p. 15\].
- **Direction:** S_H azimuth N170° ± 10°E (possible clockwise rotation with depth).  
- **Pore pressure gradient:** 0.0098 MPa/m (brine density 1.07 g/cm³).  
- **Failure criterion** (1500‑3500 m):  
  τ_failure = (σₙ − P_f) tg(42° ± 4°) + (4 ± 3) MPa \[Eq. (11), Cornet 2007, p. 17\].  
- **Hydraulic diffusivity** intact rock: ~5 × 10⁻² m²/s (from Phase 2 cloud growth).  
- **Threshold for large‑scale shear failure:** pore‑pressure increase > ~10 % of in‑situ Sₕ (≈ 3‑8 MPa overpressure depending on location).  
- **Cohesion range:** 1‑6 MPa (for friction angles 39‑44°).  
- **Depth range of validity:** 1500‑3600 m for the complete stress model; deepest control at 4550 m for Sₕ.

## Reusable Claims

1. **Claim:** In the deep Soultz granite (≥ 5 km), the natural state of stress is far from frictional failure; pore‑pressure increases of more than 10 % of the minimum principal stress are required to initiate large‑scale shear failure.  
   *Condition:* Applies to intact granite rock mass without pre‑existing large‑scale faults optimally oriented for slip.  
   *Limitation:* Local zones of stress heterogeneity may fail at lower pore‑pressure increments.

2. **Claim:** The minimum horizontal stress scales linearly with vertical stress as Sₕ = 0.54 Sᵥ in the Soultz granite at depths 1.5‑4.5 km, independent of frictional equilibrium assumptions.  
   *Condition:* Valid only where the vertical direction is principal and rock density is as given.  
   *Limitation:* Tested only at Soultz; site‑specific coefficient.

3. **Claim:** The onset of injection‑induced microseismicity does **not** indicate macroscopic failure; rather, it records elastic response to small effective‑stress changes, analogous to the Kaiser effect. Only when the seismic cloud becomes structured (planar) does large‑scale failure initiate.  
   *Condition:* Applicable to low‑permeability crystalline rock with distributed small‑scale fractures.  
   *Limitation:* Threshold values (here ~3 MPa overpressure for onset, ~8 MPa for failure) depend on site stress and diffusivity.

4. **Claim:** The maximum horizontal stress can be constrained to be sub‑equal to the vertical stress (0.95 Sᵥ ≤ S_H ≤ 1.1 Sᵥ) by combining focal‑mechanism populations, shear‑wave splitting, and orientation of induced shear zones.  
   *Condition:* Requires availability of both normal and strike‑slip events, plus clear shear‑wave splitting data.  
   *Limitation:* Does not provide a precise single value.

5. **Claim:** A Coulomb failure criterion for fresh fracture formation in the Soultz granite requires a cohesion of 1‑6 MPa and an intrinsic friction angle of 39‑44°, as derived from the geometry of seismically defined shear planes and the overpressure needed to initiate macroscopic failure.  
   *Condition:* Valid for depths 1500‑3500 m under water‑injection conditions.  
   *Limitation:* Cohesion estimate depends on assumed pore‑pressure diffusion model.

6. **Claim:** Stress‑orientation heterogeneity exists around faults and fractures but decays within a few tens of metres; at scales > 100 m the rock mass behaves as a continuum, supporting long‑term visco‑elastic stress control rather than block‑based friction.  
   *Condition:* Observed in deep granite at Soultz; may apply to other crystalline sites.  
   *Limitation:* The exact visco‑elastic mechanism (e.g., pressure solution) remains unverified.

## Connections To Other Work

- **KTB (Germany):** Similar depth‑dependent clockwise rotation of S_H direction and linear stress profiles reported by Brudy & Zoback (1999); Soultz direction (N170° ± 10°E) is ~30° north of the NW European mean but consistent with deep KTB values (N166‑182°E) \[Cornet 2007, pp. 13-14\].
- **Bad Urach (Germany):** Breakout analysis gives N172° ± 7°E mean S_H direction in crystalline rocks at 1.9‑3.5 km, supporting regional trends \[Cornet 2007, p. 14\].
- **Rangely (Colorado) experiment:** Raleigh et al. (1976) found a ~50 % increase in pore pressure needed to reactivate a fault, analogous to Soultz where the rock mass was far from failure before injection \[Cornet 2007, p. 17\].
- **Cajon Pass (California):** Stress heterogeneities linked to local slip events but an overall steady trend; consistent with continuum behaviour outside fault zones \[Cornet 2007, p. 18\].
- **Le Mayet de Montagne (France):** Local stress heterogeneities shown to be consistent with near‑complete shear‑stress relief computed elastically, again supporting continuum models \[Cornet 2007, p. 18\].
- **Byerlee’s law vs. rheology:** The linear stress variation at Soultz cannot be solely explained by friction on pre‑existing faults; Brace & Kohlstedt (1980) upper limits and Evans et al. (1989) sedimentary examples suggest long‑term visco‑elastic control \[Cornet 2007, pp. 1-2, 17‑18\].

## Open Questions

- What mechanism (pressure solution, stress corrosion, creep) controls the long‑term visco‑elastic relaxation that produces the observed linear stress gradient?  
- How can time‑dependent breakout development be quantitatively modelled to extract S_H magnitude?  
- Does the apparent clockwise rotation of S_H with depth (from N165°E at 1.6 km to N185°E at 3.8 km) continue below 5 km?  
- What is the precise magnitude of S_H below 3.6 km, and can it be constrained better by larger‑magnitude seismic events?  
- How do local stress heterogeneities affect the upscaling of hydraulic and mechanical properties for reservoir stimulation?  
- Can the cohesion and friction angle estimates be refined with new pore‑pressure monitoring and more accurate event locations?
