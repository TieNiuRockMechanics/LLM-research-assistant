# Mechanical Properties and Failure Characteristics of Granite Treated with a Combined Water – Air Cooling Cycle.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates how cyclic combined water-air cooling at 100–600 °C affects the mechanical properties, thermal crack distribution, and failure mode of granite, identifying critical temperature thresholds for crack regionalization and structural peeling relevant to Enhanced Geothermal Systems.

## Research Question
How do temperature (100–600 °C) and number of heating–cooling cycles (1, 5, 9) influence the thermal crack distribution, uniaxial compressive strength, Young’s modulus, and failure characteristics of granite subjected to a combined water–air cooling process? [Chen 2022, pp. 1-3]

## Study Area / Data
- **Rock type:** Red‑white granite from Rizhao, Shandong Province, China. Average density 2.73 g/cm³, initial porosity 0.64 %. [Chen 2022, pp. 3-4]
- **Specimen geometry:** Cylinders 38 mm diameter × 76 mm height, prepared following ISRM (2007) suggestions. All specimens taken from the same direction of the same granite block to minimize heterogeneity/anisotropy. [Chen 2022, pp. 3-4]
- **Test matrix:** Six target temperatures (100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600 °C) × three cycle numbers (1, 5, 9 cycles). Sample labeling example: “100‑1” = 100 °C target, 1 cycle. [Chen 2022, pp. 3-4]

## Methods
1. **Combined water–air cooling cycle** [Chen 2022, pp. 3-4]:
   - Slow heating rate of 5 °C/min to target temperature, held for 4 h for uniform heating.
   - Sample quickly placed in a container of 25 °C water; water level = ½ specimen height.
   - After water cooling, sample dried at 25 °C for 24 h before next cycle.
   - Process repeated to achieve 1, 5, and 9 cycles.
2. **Uniaxial compression test** [Chen 2022, pp. 4-5]:
   - AG‑X250 Shimadzu mechanical testing system, displacement control at 0.01 mm/s (strain rate ≈ 0.00013 s⁻¹).
   - Axial strain corrected for machine and platen stiffness via Eq. (1): ε = ΔH·F/Kₘ·F/Kₚ / H.
3. **Acoustic emission (AE) monitoring** during loading to track dynamic failure. [Chen 2022, pp. 4]
4. **Crack characterization** [Chen 2022, pp. 5-6]:
   - **Macroscopic cracks:** AVIZO image software for noise reduction, threshold segmentation, and quantitative crack counting on specimen surfaces.
   - **Mesoscopic cracks:** Thin‑section microscopy at water‑cooling and air‑cooling positions; classification into intergranular and intragranular cracks.

## Key Findings
1. **Regional thermal crack distribution** [Chen 2022, pp. 5-6, 11-13]:
   - ≤ 200 °C: No visible thermal cracks.
   - 300–400 °C: Cracks mainly in water‑cooling zone, extending to the interface with more cycles.
   - ≥ 500 °C: Cracks widespread; near the water–air interface they become denser than in either the water‑or air‑cooling zones.
   - 600 °C × 9 cycles: granite fractured along the combined cooling interface before loading.
2. **Mechanical property degradation** [Chen 2022, pp. 6-9, 13]:
   - ≤ 300 °C: Peak axial stress and Young’s modulus decrease slowly and linearly (e.g., 6.3–14.5 % stress drop from 100 to 300 °C for 1–9 cycles).
   - > 400 °C: Stress and modulus decrease non‑linearly and substantially (e.g., 49.2–59.3 % stress drop from 400 to 600 °C for 5 cycles).
   - Young’s modulus reductions follow the same trend: slight linear decline → rapid non‑linear decline (up to 70.7 % loss for 5 cycles 400→600 °C).
3. **Failure mode transition** [Chen 2022, pp. 10-11, 13]:
   - At 400 °C: predominantly brittle tensile fracture.
   - At 500–600 °C, with more cycles: failure shifts from brittleness to ductility.
   - Splitting failure migrates from water‑cooling zone toward the water–air interface; above 500 °C, the interface becomes the weakest zone.
   - 600 °C marks the threshold where asperity‑like material fractures and peels off from the cooling interface.
4. **AE‑based loading stages** [Chen 2022, pp. 10-11]:
   - Crack‑closure, crack‑initiation/stable‑growth, and rapid‑growth/instability stages identified.
   - Increasing temperature and cycles lengthen the crack‑closure and rapid‑failure stages while shortening the stable‑growth phase (quantified by Rc, Rs‑p, Rr‑p ratios).

## Limitations
- Only one granite type (Rizhao, Shandong) was tested; results may not directly transfer to other lithologies. [Chen 2022, pp. 13]
- The influence of the proportion (height/area ratio) of air‑cooling to water‑cooling zones was not investigated; the water level was fixed at 50 % specimen height. [Chen 2022, pp. 13]
- Tests conducted under ambient‑pressure uniaxial loading; confining stress effects relevant to deep reservoirs were not addressed. (not explicitly stated, but implied by experimental setup)
- Drying after each cycle was performed, but residual water in partially closed cracks may still influence subsequent thermal shock mechanics. implied from methodology [Chen 2022, pp. 3-4]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Thermal damage is driven by differential thermal expansion of mineral constituents and by the temperature gradient between the water‑ and air‑cooled portions. [Chen 2022, pp. 11]
- Damage accumulation across cycles is a “memory‑cumulative” process, i.e., not simple linear superposition but dependent on prior damage state. [Chen 2022, pp. 13, 11]
- The heating rate of 5 °C/min and the 4 h holding time were sufficient to achieve uniform specimen temperature. [Chen 2022, pp. 3-4]
- The half‑height water immersion creates a simplified analogue for the cooling contrast experienced by asperities on fracture surfaces in EGS during cold‑water injection. [Chen 2022, pp. 1-3]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent variables:** Target temperature (100–600 °C); number of heating–cooling cycles (1, 5, 9); cooling mode (combined water–air, water level ½ height). [Chen 2022, pp. 3-4]
- **Measured outcomes:** Macroscopic thermal crack count (via AVIZO); mesoscopic crack type and density (thin‑section); uniaxial compressive peak axial stress; Young’s modulus; stress–strain curve shape; AE ringing counts; strain‑stage ratios (Rc, Rs‑p, Rr‑p); macroscopic failure pattern. [Chen 2022, pp. 5-11]
- **Fixed parameters:** Specimen dimensions H 76 mm × D 38 mm; initial density 2.73 g/cm³; initial porosity 0.64%; heating rate 5 °C/min; holding time 4 h; water temperature 25 °C; displacement loading rate 0.01 mm/s; drying time 24 h. [Chen 2022, pp. 3-4]

## Reusable Claims
- **CLAIM:** In granite subjected to a water‑air cooling boundary, macroscopic thermal cracks first appear in the water‑cooled zone at ~300 °C and propagate toward the interface with increasing thermal cycles. At ≥500 °C, crack density near the water–air interface surpasses that in the homogeneous cooling zones. [Chen 2022, pp. 5-6]
  - Conditions: 5 °C/min heating, 4 h holding, 25 °C water quench over half height, followed by 24 h drying per cycle.
  - Limitation: Observed in one Rizhao granite; may not generalise to all granitoids.
- **CLAIM:** Below 300 °C, peak uniaxial compressive strength and Young’s modulus decline slowly and linearly with temperature and cycle number; above 400 °C the decline becomes nonlinear and severe. [Chen 2022, pp. 6-9]
  - Conditions: Identical thermal treatment, uniaxial loading at ~0.00013 s⁻¹.
- **CLAIM:** At 500 °C, thermal stress from the temperature difference between air‑ and water‑cooled zones is sufficient to shift the dominant splitting failure locus from the water‑cooled region to the water–air interface. [Chen 2022, pp. 11, 13]
- **CLAIM:** 600 °C is the critical temperature where granite at the water–air cooling interface fractures and peels during combined cooling cycles, even before mechanical loading; this temperature corresponds to formation of wide transgranular cracks and the α→β quartz transition (~573 °C). [Chen 2022, pp. 6, 11-13]
- **CLAIM:** In enhanced geothermal systems, asperities on fracture walls (which experience water quenching while adjacent rock cools naturally) risk peeling at temperatures ≥600 °C, potentially degrading fracture flow aperture and heat transfer efficiency. [Chen 2022, pp. 13]

## Candidate Concepts
- [[Combined water–air cooling cycle]] – partial immersion quench creating two cooling zones.
- [[Memory-cumulative thermal damage]] – damage accumulation depending on prior damage state, not linear superposition.
- [[Thermal crack regionalization]] – zonal pattern of cracks (water‑cooling, air‑cooling, interface).
- [[Asperity peeling in EGS]] – thermal damage difference causing asperities to detach from fracture walls.
- [[α–β quartz transition damage threshold]] – quartz phase change at ~573 °C enhancing crack formation and ductility.
- [[Strain-stage ratios]] (Rc, Rs‑p, Rr‑p) – quantitative indices of loading-stage evolution with thermal damage.

## Candidate Methods
- [[AVIZO image analysis for thermal crack quantification]] – noise reduction, threshold segmentation, and crack counting on specimen photographs.
- [[Half-height water cooling test]] – experimental analogue for thermal gradient at an asperity‑rock interface.
- [[AE staging method]] – classification of stress–strain curves into crack-closure, stable-growth, and rapid-failure stages using acoustic emission ringing counts.
- [[Thin-section microcrack classification]] – identification of intergranular vs. intragranular cracks in water- and air-cooled positions.

- **Cooling rate effects:** Wang et al. [4–6] (high heating rate promotes tensile cracks; rapid cooling contraction stronger than slow cooling); Wu et al. [20] (cooling method differences significant only > 600 °C). [Chen 2022, pp. 2-3]

- **Quenching cycle effects:** Xu & Sun [31] (static tensile strength decreases with T and increases with quench cycles); Fan et al. [32] (spatial gradient of thermal damage peaks, then decreases above 600 °C, observed via different method). [Chen 2022, pp. 2-3]

## Open Questions
- What is the quantitative effect of different water‑air cooling height ratios on thermal damage and mechanical response? (Not studied here; fixed at ½ height.) [Chen 2022, pp. 13]
- How do confining pressure and true triaxial stress states alter the observed failure mode transitions and interface peeling?
- To what extent do mineralogical composition and grain‑size variations influence the threshold temperatures (300 °C, 500 °C, 600 °C) reported?
- What is the long‑term cyclic behavior (>> 9 cycles), and does the “memory‑cumulative damage” concept have a quantifiable critical damage index?
- Can the fracture/peeling temperature at 600 °C be mitigated or delayed by modifying water injection temperature or chemistry?
