# Genetic model of intraplate volcanic high-temperature geothermal system in Yanggao-Tianzhen Basin, Datong.

## One-line Summary
A trinity genetic model of “magmatic heat source – structural conduction – shallow local thermal anomaly” is proposed for the intraplate volcanic high‑temperature geothermal system in the Yanggao‑Tianzhen Basin, driven by the long‑range effect of plate subduction and collision [Sun 2025, pp. 1-1, 12‑15].

## Research Question
How are intraplate volcanic high‑temperature geothermal resources distributed and what genetic model can explain their formation under the influence of concealed volcanoes and deep fault systems in the Yanggao‑Tianzhen Basin, Datong volcanic area [Sun 2025, pp. 1-2]?

## Study Area / Data
- **Study area**: Yanggao‑Tianzhen Basin, adjacent to the Datong Quaternary Volcanic Group, North China Craton [Sun 2025, pp. 2-3].
- **Well data**: Deep geothermal wells GR1 (Tianzhen, 1624 m, 168 °C at 1620 m), YG1 (Yanggao, 2682 m), and shallow well P1 (178 m, wellhead 104 °C) [Sun 2025, pp. 2-2, 4‑4, 10‑12].
- **Geophysical data**: 2D seismic profiles, 3D magnetotelluric (MT) inversion resistivity volumes, 3D seismic curvature volumes [Sun 2025, pp. 3-4, 5‑6, 8‑9].
- **Hydrochemical data**: Major ions and stable isotopes (δ²H, δ¹⁸O) from shallow and deep geothermal water, cold groundwater, and spring water [Sun 2025, pp. 9-10].
- **Other constraints**: Lithology logs, surface temperature, geothermal gradient measurements, and previous petrological/geochemical findings on Datong volcanic rocks [Sun 2025, pp. 2-3, 4‑5, 6‑7].

## Methods
- **Temperature logging**: Steady‑state borehole temperature measurement using a platinum resistance single‑point sensor after thermal recovery [Sun 2025, pp. 4-4].
- **Geothermal gradient calculation**: Full‑well gradient derived from temperature–depth curves, segment gradients computed for different stratigraphic units [Sun 2025, pp. 4-5].
- **MT 3D resistivity inversion**: Extraction of low‑resistivity (0–1.5 Ω·m) anomalies to delineate molten/semi‑molten magma chambers; maximum curvature extraction for fault characterization [Sun 2025, pp. 5-6, 8‑9].
- **Seismic interpretation**: Identification of unconformities SB1 and SB2 from 2D profiles; curvature attribute analysis for fault continuity [Sun 2025, pp. 3-4, 8‑9].
- **Hydrochemical analysis**: Piper trilinear diagram, Gibbs diagram, and δ²H–δ¹⁸O plot against the Local Meteoric Water Line [Sun 2025, pp. 9-10].
- **Thermal equilibrium time calculation**: Conductive cooling model for a sphere in a semi‑infinite medium, Eq. (1), with corrections for convection, pressure, and crystallization heat [Sun 2025, pp. 7-8].

## Key Findings
- The basin comprises three stratigraphic sequences (Quaternary, Neogene‑Paleogene, Archean) separated by two regional unconformities (SB1, SB2); the Quaternary acts as a regional caprock [Sun 2025, pp. 1-1, 3‑4].
- Heat transfer is predominantly conductive; geothermal gradient is higher in the east than in the west. Shallow Quaternary strata show strong convection locally, while deeper Archean sections are dominated by conduction [Sun 2025, pp. 1-1, 4‑5].
- Low‑resistivity anomalies at 4000–8000 m depth represent concealed molten/semi‑molten magma chambers that serve as shallow heat sources; their distribution differs between the Yanggao area (clustered, ellipsoidal, shallow‑buried heating) and the Tianzhen area (belt‑like, with distinct magmatic conduits and crater‑like features) [Sun 2025, pp. 1-1, 5‑7].
- NE‑SW‑trending faults (Mesozoic) act as major heat‑conducting pathways; NW‑SE‑trending faults (Cenozoic) connect to shallow heat sources and influence groundwater circulation [Sun 2025, pp. 8-9].
- Hydrochemical facies: shallow geothermal water is HCO₃–Ca·Mg; deep geothermal water is HCO₃·Cl·SO₄–Na (and HCO₃·Cl–Na). Isotopic data (δ²H: –85.25‰ to –75.26‰; δ¹⁸O: –11.36‰ to –9.51‰) indicate a meteoric origin, with deep water exhibiting poor circulation and renewal [Sun 2025, pp. 1-1, 9‑10].
- The genetic model comprises three coupled elements: (1) shallow magma chambers (unerupted concealed volcanoes) as the heat source, (2) deep/large faults as the structural conduction framework, and (3) shallow sandstone reservoirs with local thermal anomalies – termed the “magmatic heat source – structural conduction – shallow local thermal anomaly” trinity model [Sun 2025, pp. 1-1, 12‑15].
- The intraplate high‑temperature geothermal system is activated by long‑range effects of plate subduction and collision, leading to craton destruction, lithospheric weakening, thermo‑chemical erosion, and multi‑stage magma upwelling that forms unerupted concealed volcanoes [Sun 2025, pp. 1-1, 14‑15].

## Limitations
- **Sparse drill control**: Only a few deep wells (GR1, YG1) constrain the thermal structure; conclusions on heat source geometry rely heavily on geophysical inversion [Sun 2025, pp. 4-5].
- **Non‑uniqueness of electrical data**: Fault characterization from MT curvature analysis carries inherent uncertainty [Sun 2025, pp. 8-9].
- **Simplified cooling model**: The spherical magma body assumes instantaneous emplacement, homogeneous properties, and neglects multi‑stage injection or hydrothermal cooling details [Sun 2025, pp. 7-8].
- **Hydrochemical snapshot**: Water sampling may not fully capture temporal variations in mixing ratios or deep circulation paths [Sun 2025, pp. 9-10].
- **No direct sampling of magma chambers**: Interpretations of molten state rely on resistivity and seismic velocity anomalies, not direct petrological confirmation [Sun 2025, pp. 5-7].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The low‑resistivity anomalies (resistivity ∼1 Ω·m) correspond to magma chambers in a molten or semi‑molten state; high‑resistivity columns indicate solidified intrusions [Sun 2025, pp. 5-6, 6‑7].
- The magma body is modeled as a once‑filled spherical basalt body placed in a semi‑infinite medium for cooling time estimation [Sun 2025, pp. 7-8].
- Initial magma temperature is 1060 °C; thermal diffusivity α = 0.012 cm²/s; average surface temperature 15 °C; average geothermal gradient 7.255 °C/100 m [Sun 2025, pp. 7-8].
- Country rock temperature at heat source depth is calculated by the linear geothermal gradient extrapolation (Tₛ = 15 + G × H) [Sun 2025, pp. 7-8].
- Thermal equilibrium is assumed when the temperature difference between magma and country rock is less than 1% of the initial difference [Sun 2025, pp. 7-8].
- The magnetotelluric interpretation assumes that low resistivity in volcanic areas is primarily caused by partial melting and fluid flow [Sun 2025, pp. 6-6].
- Isotopic composition of meteoric water is represented by the Local Meteoric Water Line; all water samples originate from atmospheric precipitation [Sun 2025, pp. 10-10].

## Reusable Claims
- **Unerupted concealed volcanoes** (molten/semi‑molten magma chambers) at depths of 4–11 km can serve as shallow high‑temperature heat sources for intraplate geothermal systems, provided they have not fully cooled and are connected by permeable fault networks. [Sun 2025, pp. 5-7]
- **The heat transfer mode in the Yanggao‑Tianzhen Basin is primarily conduction**, but local thermal convection occurs where faults intercept shallow magma chambers or where deep‑circulating meteoric water ascends along basin‑controlling faults; the shallow Quaternary clay acts as an effective caprock. [Sun 2025, pp. 4-5, 10‑12]
- **NE‑SW‑trending faults** form the dominant heat‑conducting framework, while **NW‑SE‑trending faults**, though shallower, facilitate shallow hydrothermal circulation and can delineate favorable geothermal target areas. [Sun 2025, pp. 8-9]
- **Hydrochemical signatures clearly differentiate shallow and deep circulation**: shallow waters are HCO₃–Ca·Mg with active renewal, deep waters are HCO₃·Cl·SO₄–Na with poor circulation; both originate from meteoric water, enabling the use of hydrochemistry to infer connectivity and residence time. [Sun 2025, pp. 9-10]
- **The “magmatic heat source – structural conduction – shallow local thermal anomaly” trinity model** provides a reproducible conceptual framework for exploring intraplate volcanic high‑temperature systems, where the critical combination is: (i) unerupted concealed volcano, (ii) deep/large faults acting as conduits, and (iii) shallow porous sandstone reservoirs heated through the “baking” effect of thermal conduction. [Sun 2025, pp. 12-15]
- **Long‑range effects of plate subduction/collision** (e.g., Paleo‑Pacific subduction) can activate intraplate high‑temperature geothermal systems by causing craton destruction, lithospheric thinning, and deep mantle upwelling that generates multi‑level magma chambers, even far from plate boundaries. [Sun 2025, pp. 14-15]
- **Cooling time calculations** show that a 30 km diameter basaltic magma body at 8 km depth would take 1.9–2.9 Ma to reach thermal equilibrium, yet sustained mantle heat and magma replenishment can maintain an open, long‑lived heat source. [Sun 2025, pp. 7-8]

## Connections To Other Work
- The study confirms earlier geophysical findings that shallow S‑wave low‑velocity anomalies and low‑resistivity bodies exist beneath the Datong area, likely representing molten magma chambers [Sun 2025, pp. 1-2] (Tang et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2018).
- It aligns with the recognition that the Datong Volcanic Group is a product of Cenozoic intraplate volcanism triggered by the destruction of the North China Craton and the eastward extrusion of the Tibetan Plateau [Sun 2025, pp. 2-3] (Gao et al., 2020; Pan et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2011).
- The proposed genetic model is compared with plate‑margin systems, such as the Japanese island arc, where subduction slab rollback and back‑arc spreading create a different heat‑source architecture; the intraplate system relies more on deep mantle upwelling and craton destruction [Sun 2025, pp. 14-15].
- Hydrochemical and isotopic evidence for meteoric origin of geothermal water and the role of deep‑circulating fault zones echoes similar cases in the North China Craton [Sun 2025, pp. 9-10] (e.g., Gibbs, 1970).
- The cooling time assessment builds on previous calculations (Zhou, 2021) but adds multi‑factor corrections [Sun 2025, pp. 7-8].
- The paper contributes to the growing body of work on intraplate high‑temperature geothermal exploration in China, following recent discoveries in Matouying (151 °C), Subei Basin (155 °C), and Dongying (167 °C) [Sun 2025, pp. 1-2].

## Open Questions
- Whether a true thermal convection geothermal system exists in the Xiong’er Mountain area (southern Tianzhen) along magmatic eruption channels, as inferred but not yet proven by drilling [Sun 2025, pp. 12-12].
- The exact geometry and connectivity of the multi‑level magma chambers beneath the basin and their temporal evolution, including the frequency and volume of magma replenishment from the mantle [Sun 2025, pp. 7-7].
- How fault‑fracture network permeability evolves under the coupled effects of high temperature, mineral alteration, and sealing, and its impact on geothermal production sustainability [Sun 2025, pp. 5-6].
- The nature of the transition from conductive to convective heat transfer in the shallow Archean granite section observed in well YG1 – i.e., whether it is fracture‑controlled or interface‑controlled [Sun 2025, pp. 4-4].
- The extent to which the identified concealed volcanic clusters are truly unerupted, or whether they represent older, now‑buried eruptive centers, and how this affects heat source longevity [Sun 2025, pp. 6-7].
- The role of deep mantle flow patterns (e.g., the gravity gradient zone) in focusing magma supply to the Datong area, and how this might guide exploration elsewhere in the North China Craton [Sun 2025, pp. 14-15].
