# Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS): A Review.

## One-line Summary
This paper provides a comprehensive review of Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) technology, covering its origins, development stages, performance optimization techniques, seismic response, and cost estimation software, highlighting its potential for large-scale electricity generation.

## Research Question
The main objective is to study the state of the art, technology, and research of EGS systems, analyzing different aspects such as development of a geothermal plant, different optimisation techniques, seismic response to fluid injection, as well as different software packages to estimate costs [Olasolo 2015, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The review synthesizes information from global EGS projects and research. Key sites include the first HDR project at Los Alamos Scientific Laboratory (LASL), USA [Olasolo 2015, pp. 1-2], the European HDR project at Soultz-sous-Forêts, France [Olasolo 2015, pp. 1-2], and the first privately-run commercial EGS plant at Habanero, Australia [Olasolo 2015, pp. 1-1]. Data encompasses thermal gradients from various regions [Olasolo 2015, pp. 2-3], reservoir properties, and seismic data from sites like Basel, Switzerland; The Geysers, USA; and Paralana, Australia [Olasolo 2015, pp. 8-9].

## Methods
The study is a literature review. It synthesizes existing research on EGS development, including site selection, reservoir creation via hydraulic stimulation, and reservoir operation [Olasolo 2015, pp. 4-5]. It reviews optimization techniques such as using dense fluids [Olasolo 2015, pp. 5-6] and CO2 as a working fluid [Olasolo 2015, pp. 6-7]. It also analyzes seismic monitoring methodologies [Olasolo 2015, pp. 8-9] and evaluates software packages for cost estimation [Olasolo 2015, pp. 9-10].

## Key Findings
- The Geodynamics plant in Habanero (Australia), which started up on 2 May 2013, is the first privately-run commercial EGS plant to produce electricity on a large scale [Olasolo 2015, pp. 1-1].
- Using dense fluids for hydraulic stimulation can increase and improve underground circulation pathways, reduce permeability, and improve mass flow and heat extraction ratios [Olasolo 2015, pp. 5-6].
- Using CO2 as a working fluid instead of water can result in a heat extraction ratio approximately 50% higher, with added efficiency at low temperatures and potential for geological CO2 sequestration [Olasolo 2015, pp. 6-7].
- Analysis of seismic data shows a clear relationship between fluid injection volume and the maximum observed magnitude of induced seismic events [Olasolo 2015, pp. 8-9].
- EURONAUT and GEOPHIRES are identified as suitable software packages for estimating and simulating costs, with GEOPHIRES capable of simulating electricity, direct-use heat, and combined heat and power (CHP) [Olasolo 2015, pp. 9-10].

## Limitations
- EGS technology is still in development, with very limited operational experience measured in months rather than years in many cases [Olasolo 2015, pp. 4-5].
- There are significant uncertainties surrounding the artificially created underground geothermal reservoir, which is where the biggest knowledge and technology gaps lie [Olasolo 2015, pp. 4-4].
- The seismic response to fluid injection is a widely questioned aspect by society [Olasolo 2015, pp. 1-2].
- Economic assessment is complex due to dependence on many physical and financial factors [Olasolo 2015, pp. 9-10].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The review assumes that suitable geological sites exist with high thermal gradients (e.g., 40-150 °C/km) at depths of 4-5 km [Olasolo 2015, pp. 2-3].
- The viability of EGS depends on technological advancements to create and operate artificial reservoirs efficiently and economically [Olasolo 2015, pp. 3-4].
- The analysis of seismic response assumes that seismic monitoring networks are of sufficient quality and configuration to accurately locate events [Olasolo 2015, pp. 8-9].

## Reusable Claims
- Using dense fluids (e.g., NaCl brine) for hydraulic stimulation in EGS can increase and improve underground circulation pathways and improve the mass flow and heat extraction ratio, while allowing control of fracturing pressure at depth to minimize seismic risk [Olasolo 2015, pp. 5-6].
- CO2 as a working fluid in EGS can provide a heat extraction ratio approximately 50% higher than water and is more efficient at low temperatures, but is inefficient at subcritical pressures [Olasolo 2015, pp. 6-7].
- The maximum observed magnitude of induced seismicity at EGS sites increases with the increasing volume of injected fluid [Olasolo 2015, pp. 8-9].
- Multi-well systems in EGS offer more operational flexibility and a larger heat exchange surface area compared to doublet systems, leading to better heat exploitation and longer reservoir lifetime [Olasolo 2015, pp. 9-10].
