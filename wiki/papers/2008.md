# ఃറీቔႨ ᄻ৳ѯ.

## One-line Summary
This paper characterizes natural fractures in a low‑matrix‑permeability reservoir, documenting four vertical fracture sets whose permeability is maximised along the direction of minimum opening stress.

## Research Question
How do structural fractures develop in a tight reservoir, what are their geometric and petrophysical characteristics, and how does the contemporary stress field control fracture opening and reservoir permeability?

## Study Area / Data
- Depth 1000–1500 m; 10‑bed reservoir (Beds 1– 10), main pay in Beds 6– 8.  
- Region indicated as «ൌဒ൩ (െႲն࿐ (ࣘ ,))ࣘ102249; ç ඳ , ༆ν 163517» (eastern basin setting).  
- Core and thin‑section data from multiple wells (Well 9, 19, 40, etc.).  
- Fracture observations are tied to structural position (Fig. 1).

## Methods
1. **Fracture classification** – Nelson [18] and Lorenz et al. [19,20] schemes (shear, extension, hybrid).  
2. **Geometric measurements** – length, aperture, spacing, density (cm/cm²), height from core and image logs (Section 2, Figs 2–4).  
3. **Permeability estimation** – Monte Carlo simulation for fracture‑controlled permeability [29] (Section 5).  
4. **Stress analysis** – opening stress calculated using the formula  
   `σ_opening = f(σ₁, σ₃, µ, H, θ, ρS, ρW, β)` (Table 1).  
5. **Paleostress reconstruction** – recognising that the four fracture sets formed during two distinct stress episodes (Section 4).

## Key Findings
1. **Fracture sets** – Four vertical sets are present: NE‑SW, NW‑SE, E‑W, S‑N. The NE‑SW and NW‑SE sets dominate in terms of density and permeability (Fig. 3).  
2. **Geometry** – Spacing mostly < 2.2 m, often within 60 cm; length 2–16 m (max > 20 m); height up to > 100 m in some cases but typically < 20 m (Fig. 4).  
   - Density average 0.32 cm/cm², with well‑to‑well variation (e.g., Well 9: 0.22; Well 19: 0.53; Well 40: 0.30).  
3. **Permeability enhancement** – Matrix permeability is very low (< 10 × 10⁻³ µm²). Fracture permeability increases sharply where fracture intensity is high.  
   - Monte Carlo simulation yields fracture permeability mainly (1.7–43.4) × 10⁻³ µm², with the bulk below 1.0 × 10⁻³ µm² (Section 5).  
4. **Stress control** – The minimum opening stress direction corresponds to the highest permeability direction. For instance, Well 40 shows NE‑SW opening stress 25–26.5 MPa, whereas S‑N is 28.9–30.6 MPa (Table 1).  
5. **Formation mechanism** – The four sets are explained by two stages of paleostress (Himalayan‑period compression, later stress regime) that produced conjugate shear and extension fractures (Section 4).
