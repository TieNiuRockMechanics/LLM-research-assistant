# Experimental Investigation on Thermal Cracking, Permeability under HTHP and Application for Geothermal Mining of HDR.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates the coupled effects of high temperature and high pressure (HTHP) on granite's thermal cracking, permeability evolution, and microstructure, demonstrating how thermal stimulation can enhance permeability for Hot Dry Rock (HDR) geothermal energy extraction.

## Research Question
How do changes in high temperature and high pressure influence the physical properties (specifically thermal cracking and permeability) of rocks, and how do these changes influence the sustainability of extracting HDR geothermal energy? [Zhao 2017, pp. 4-5]

## Study Area / Data
The experimental samples are three granite specimens (NO.2, NO.4, NO.7) of "Luhui granite" cored from Pingyi, Shandong province, China. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6] The granite is a strongly heterogeneous brittle and hard rock, mainly consisting of feldspar (43%), quartz (28%), and illite (25%). [Zhao 2017, pp. 11-12] Experiments were conducted using a 600℃ 20MN servo control rock triaxial testing machine at China University of Mining and Technology. [Zhao 2017, pp. 4-5]

## Methods
- **Permeability Tests:** Conducted under HTHP conditions using nitrogen as the flow gas. Samples were heated from room temperature to 500℃ at a rate of 5℃/h under constant confining pressures (75 MPa, 25 MPa, 12.5 MPa). Permeability was measured at plateau temperatures (50℃ increments) by monitoring gas flow rate. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- **Acoustic Emission (AE) Testing:** AE sensors were installed on the triaxial chamber to detect thermal cracking signals (event number, energy, duration, amplitude) during the heating process. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- **Micro-CT Analysis:** Samples processed under HTHP conditions were scanned using a μCT225kVFCB micro-CT system to analyze the evolution of microstructure and thermal cracks. The scanning cell size was 1.847 µm. [Zhao 2017, pp. 6-8]

## Key Findings
1.  Granite permeability shows a two-phase growth curve with temperature: a weak initial variation followed by a sharp increase after a "critical temperature." [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
2.  The critical temperature for permeability change decreases with increasing confining pressure, tending toward a steady value above 30 MPa. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
3.  AE monitoring revealed multiple intensive periods of thermal cracking during heating (e.g., 55-65℃, 110-230℃, 270-340℃, 400-500℃), with intensive cracking occurring before 200℃ under HTHP. [Zhao 2017, pp. 6-8]
4.  Micro-CT investigations show thermal cracking initiates at grain boundaries as inter-granular microcracks, develops into larger cracks, and eventually forms macrocracks and intra-granular cracks at 500℃, changing the texture to mylonitic. [Zhao 2017, pp. 6-8]
5.  Permeability of sample NO.7 increased by two orders of magnitude, from 4.7×10⁻⁸ to 6.03×10⁻⁶ D, after thermal treatment. [Zhao 2017, pp. 8-9]

## Limitations
- The study focuses exclusively on one type of granite ("Luhui granite"). [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- Experiments were conducted in a laboratory setting; direct field-scale validation is not provided. [Zhao 2017, pp. 8-9]
- The heating rate (5℃/h) and specific HTHP conditions may not perfectly replicate all geothermal reservoir environments. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The experimental conditions (HTHP) are designed to replicate a geothermal environment at depths of 500m to 3000m. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- Nitrogen gas flow is used to measure permeability, assuming it adequately represents fluid flow behavior in cracked rock. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- The granite samples are assumed to be representative of the HDR reservoir rock. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Temperature (T):** Ranged from room temperature to 500℃. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- **Confining Pressure (σ₃):** Applied at 12.5 MPa, 25 MPa, and 75 MPa. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- **Permeability (k):** Measured in Darcy (D), showing evolution with temperature. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- **Critical Temperature:** The temperature at which permeability begins to increase sharply; a function of confining pressure. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- **Acoustic Emission (AE) Response:** Product of vibration amplitude and time, indicating cracking intensity. [Zhao 2017, pp. 6-8]

## Reusable Claims
- Under HTHP conditions, the critical temperature for a sharp increase in granite permeability decreases with increasing confining pressure but stabilizes when confining pressure exceeds approximately 30 MPa. [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- Thermal cracking in granite under HTHP is a multi-period process, with intensive AE events indicating crack formation occurring in distinct temperature stages (e.g., 55-65℃, 110-230℃, 270-340℃, 400-500℃). [Zhao 2017, pp. 6-8]
- The mechanism of thermal damage in granite under HTHP involves both inter-granular thermal stress (dominant at lower temperatures) and intra-granular thermal stress (initiated at higher temperatures, e.g., 500℃). [Zhao 2017, pp. 6-8, 10-11]
- Thermal stimulation can enhance the permeability of an initially impermeable granite reservoir by orders of magnitude, a key mechanism for creating flow paths in HDR systems. [Zhao 2017, pp. 8-9]

## Connections To Other Work
- The study builds on prior work showing thermal cracking changes rock permeability, strength, and porosity. [Zhao 2017, pp. 4-5]
- It contrasts with findings by Chaki (2008) who identified 500℃ as a critical temperature under low confining pressure (0.8 MPa). [Zhao 2017, pp. 5-6]
- It contrasts with findings by Jones et al. (1997) where little damage occurred below 300℃, attributing the difference to high confining pressure effects. [Zhao 2017, pp. 6-8]
- The experimental results support and provide physical basis for numerical models of HDR systems, such as the THM coupled model by Zhao et al. (2015). [Zhao 2017, pp. 8-9]

## Open Questions
- How do the findings for "Luhui granite" translate to other granite types or crystalline rocks found in different HDR reservoirs?
- What is the long-term evolution of permeability and crack networks under cyclic thermal loading (heating and cooling) relevant to operational HDR systems?
- How can the laboratory-scale relationship between critical temperature and confining pressure be optimized for field-scale thermal stimulation design?
