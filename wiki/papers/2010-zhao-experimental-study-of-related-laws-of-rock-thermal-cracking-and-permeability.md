# Experimental Study of Related Laws of Rock Thermal Cracking and Permeability.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates the thermal cracking thresholds, multi-peak acoustic emission behavior, and corresponding permeability evolution in sandstone and granite under high-temperature triaxial stress conditions.

## Research Question
The research aims to determine whether a correlation exists between the thermal cracking characteristics of rock and its permeability evolution under high-temperature and high-pressure triaxial conditions, and to reveal the related laws and mechanisms [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The experiments used large cylindrical samples (φ200 mm × 400 mm) of two rock types:
*   **Yongcheng fine-grained feldspar sandstone** from Henan Province, China [Zhao 2010, pp. 2-5].
*   **"Luhui" granite** from Pingyi, Shandong Province, China [Zhao 2010, pp. 2-5].

## Methods
*   **Testing System:** Experiments were conducted using a self-developed "600 ℃ 20 MN servo-controlled rock triaxial testing machine with high temperature and high pressure" [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-2].
*   **Procedure:** Samples were subjected to a constant axial and confining pressure. Temperature was increased from room temperature to 600 ℃ in steps (e.g., 50, 100, 150... 600 ℃) at a rate of about 5 ℃/h. After reaching each set temperature, the system was held for 2 hours before testing [Zhao 2010, pp. 2-5].
*   **Measurements:**
    *   **Permeability:** Measured at each temperature step using nitrogen gas as the fluid, with flow rates recorded by soap film and rotor flowmeters [Zhao 2010, pp. 2-5].
    *   **Acoustic Emission (AE):** Monitored in real-time using sensors attached to the sample to record AE events, energy, duration, and amplitude during heating [Zhao 2010, pp. 2-5].

## Key Findings
1.  **Thermal Cracking Thresholds:** Both rock types exhibit a clear thermal cracking threshold temperature. For Yongcheng fine sandstone, the threshold is approximately 170 ℃. For Luhui granite, the threshold is approximately 65 ℃ [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
2.  **Multi-Period Cracking:** Beyond the threshold, thermal cracking is discontinuous and multi-periodic. From room temperature to 600 ℃, AE activity shows more than two peak intervals, indicating cracking is neither monotonically increasing nor decreasing [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
3.  **Permeability Evolution:** Rock permeability also exhibits multiple peak intervals that correspond synchronously with the peaks in thermal cracking (AE activity). Following an AE active period, a relative decrease in permeability occurs but remains at a high level. Overall, permeability increases with each successive cracking episode [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
4.  **Rock Composition Influence:** The differences in thermal cracking and permeability behavior between granite and sandstone are attributed to their distinct mineral compositions and resulting heterogeneity. Granite, with significant quartz, feldspar, and illite, shows stronger non-uniformity [Zhao 2010, pp. 5-7].

## Limitations
*   The study focused on only two specific rock types (a sandstone and a granite).
*   The experiments were conducted under specific, constant stress conditions (e.g., 6 MPa axial, 5 MPa confining for sandstone).
*   The maximum test temperature was 600 ℃.
*   The heating rate was slow (5 ℃/h) to ensure uniformity, which may not represent all field conditions.

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   The heating process was assumed to be uniform throughout the large sample.
*   The stress state (axial and confining pressure) was held constant during the temperature increase for each test.
*   Nitrogen gas flow was used as a proxy for measuring rock permeability.

## Key Variables / Parameters

*   **Acoustic Emission (AE) Event Count, Energy, Amplitude, Duration**
*   **Rock Permeability** (k)

*   **Pore Pressure** (varied in sandstone tests: 0.4, 0.8, 1.2 MPa)

## Reusable Claims
*   Under triaxial stress, the thermal cracking of granite and sandstone initiates at distinct threshold temperatures (e.g., ~65 ℃ for Luhui granite, ~170 ℃ for Yongcheng sandstone) [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-2].
*   The process of rock thermal cracking from room temperature to 600 ℃ is characterized by discontinuous, multi-periodic AE activity with more than two peak intervals, not a monotonic trend [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
*   Rock permeability evolution under heating is directly correlated with thermal cracking activity, exhibiting synchronous multi-peak behavior; permeability increases cumulatively with each major cracking episode [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-2, 5-7].
*   The heterogeneity in mineral composition (e.g., differing thermal expansion coefficients of quartz, feldspar, illite) is the fundamental cause of the observed multi-period thermal cracking behavior [Zhao 2010, pp. 5-7].

## Connections To Other Work
*   The granite's thermal cracking threshold (~65 ℃) is consistent with findings by Wang et al. (1989) on Westerly granite [Zhao 2010, pp. 5-7].
*   The study addresses a gap noted in prior research, which was often limited to small samples or post-heating measurements, by using large samples under real-time triaxial stress [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-2].
*   It builds on previous work by the authors on the development of the testing machine [Zhao 2010, pp. 2-5] and micro-CT studies of granite thermal cracking [Zhao 2010, pp. 5-7].

## Open Questions
