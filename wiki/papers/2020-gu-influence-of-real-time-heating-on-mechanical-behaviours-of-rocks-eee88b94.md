# Influence of Real-Time Heating on Mechanical Behaviours of Rocks.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the mechanical properties of sandstone, marble, and granite under real-time heating up to 800°C, finding that uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and elastic modulus vary significantly with temperature due to mechanisms like thermal cracking, sintering, and mineral decomposition.

## Research Question
How do the mechanical behaviours (specifically UCS and elastic modulus) of different rock types (igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic) change under real-time heating conditions, as opposed to pre-heated treatment, and what are the underlying mechanisms?

## Study Area / Data
Sandstone and marble samples were collected from Jvxian, Laizhou, and Wenshang in Shandong Province, China. Granite samples were also used. All samples were prepared as cylinders (ϕ50×100 mm) according to ISRM standards [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2].

## Methods
A servo-controlled compression apparatus (WAW-600) with a furnace and temperature controller was used. Samples were oven-dried for 24h at 105°C to remove moisture. They were heated at a rate of 2°C/min to target temperatures (25°C, 200°C, 400°C, 600°C, 800°C), held for 2 hours for equilibration, and then compressed at a rate of 0.5 MPa/s until failure. At least three samples were tested at each temperature point [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2].

## Key Findings
- **Sandstone**: UCS decreases from 117.16 MPa at 25°C to 107.3 MPa at 400°C, then increases sharply to 196.63 MPa at 800°C. The elastic modulus shows a general decreasing trend but has a slight increase between 400°C and 500°C [Gu 2020, pp. 2-4, 4-5].
- **Marble**: UCS decreases linearly to a minimum of 55.84 MPa at 600°C, then increases slightly to 64.45 MPa at 800°C. The elastic modulus decreases linearly from 10.03 GPa at 25°C to 2.5 GPa at 800°C [Gu 2020, pp. 2-4, 4-5].
- **Granite**: UCS decreases continuously with temperature, reaching 32.3 MPa at 800°C (a ~70% reduction from room temperature). The elastic modulus decreases from 10.47 GPa at 25°C to 3.32 GPa at 800°C [Gu 2020, pp. 2-4, 4-5].
- **General Trend**: The elastic modulus of all rock types decreases with temperature, indicating a weakening ability to resist deformation. Peak strain increases for all rocks, with marble showing a distinct transition from brittle to ductile failure between 600°C and 800°C [Gu 2020, pp. 2-4, 4-5, 5-8].
- **Mechanisms**: Below 400°C, thermal cracking reduces UCS for all rocks. Above 400°C, sandstone UCS increases due to a sintering reaction involving quartz, potassium feldspar, and clay minerals. Marble UCS increases slightly at 800°C due to dolomite decomposition and oxide filling. Granite lacks the mineral composition for sintering, so its UCS continuously declines [Gu 2020, pp. 4-5, 5-8, 8-9].

## Limitations
- The study focuses on three specific rock types from specific locations in China.
- The heating rate was fixed at 2°C/min; faster rates, which induce larger thermal stress, were not studied [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2].
- The mechanisms (sintering, decomposition) are inferred from mineral composition and observed reactions, not directly measured in situ during heating.
- The research does not address long-term creep behavior or cyclic heating effects.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Samples were oven-dried at 105°C for 24 hours to remove free moisture before testing [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2].
- A slow heating rate (2°C/min) was used to ensure homogeneous internal and external sample temperature, considered more representative of natural settings [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2].
- Samples were held at the target temperature for 2 hours to allow for thermal equilibration before compression [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2].
- The elastic modulus was calculated from a linear fit to the stress-strain curve between 30% and 70% of the UCS [Gu 2020, pp. 2-4].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variable**: Temperature (25°C, 200°C, 400°C, 600°C, 800°C).
- **Dependent Variables**: Uniaxial Compressive Strength (UCS), Elastic Modulus, Peak Strain.
- **Rock Type**: Sandstone (sedimentary), Marble (metamorphic), Granite (igneous).
- **Mineral Composition**: Quartz, potassium feldspar, anorthose, clay minerals, dolomite, etc.

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim**: For sandstone with a mineral composition similar to that studied (quartz, potassium feldspar, anorthose, clay minerals), UCS can increase significantly above 400°C under real-time heating due to a sintering reaction that enlarges contact areas between crystal grains.
  - **Condition**: Real-time heating at a slow rate (2°C/min). Mineral composition must include appropriate percentages of feldspar and clay minerals.
  - **Limitation**: Does not apply to sandstones with different mineral compositions (e.g., lacking clay minerals) [Gu 2020, pp. 4-5, 5-8].
- **Claim**: The elastic modulus of all tested rock types (sandstone, marble, granite) decreases under real-time heating, indicating a universal softening effect that reduces the rock's ability to resist deformation.
  - **Condition**: Temperature range from 25°C to 800°C.
  - **Limitation**: The magnitude of decrease varies significantly by rock type [Gu 2020, pp. 4-5].
- **Claim**: Marble UCS may show a slight recovery at 800°C due to the decomposition of carbonate minerals (e.g., dolomite), which produces oxide particles that fill intergranular spaces and increase friction.
  - **Condition**: Real-time heating to 800°C. Rock must be carbonate-rich (e.g., >90% dolomite).
  - **Limitation**: The increase is slight compared to the prior decrease; overall strength remains lower than at room temperature [Gu 2020, pp. 5-8, 8-9].

## Connections To Other Work
The study contrasts its "real-time heating" approach with previous research that typically used "heat treatment" (pre-heating samples to a temperature and then testing at room temperature) [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2]. It references numerous prior studies on sandstone [Gu 2020, pp. 1-2, 2-2], marble [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2], and granite [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2] under high temperature, noting that the literature generally shows significant property changes above 400°C–600°C [Gu 2020, pp. 2-2]. The sintering mechanism in sandstone is linked to ceramic manufacturing principles [Gu 2020, pp. 4-5].

## Open Questions
- How do other rock types (e.g., limestone, basalt) behave under identical real-time heating conditions?
- What is the effect of different heating rates (especially faster ones) on the mechanical properties and failure mechanisms?
- How do these real-time heating effects translate to long-term stability in engineering applications like underground coal gasification or geothermal reservoirs?
- Can the sintering effect in sandstone be reliably predicted based on mineral composition alone?
