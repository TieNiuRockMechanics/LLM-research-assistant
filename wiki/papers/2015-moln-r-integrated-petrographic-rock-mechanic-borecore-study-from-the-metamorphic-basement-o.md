# Integrated Petrographic – Rock Mechanic Borecore Study from the Metamorphic Basement of the Pannonian Basin, Hungary.

## One-line Summary
An integrated petrographic and rock mechanical analysis of borecores from a metamorphic basement fault zone in Hungary reveals distinct microstructural and mechanical properties that define a damage zone, transition zone, and fault core, with implications for fluid migration and compartmentalization.

## Research Question
The main aim was to present the results of an integrated petrographic, microstructure, and rock mechanical analysis to clarify the role of the main structural elements in the hydraulic system of both the fault zone and the whole fractured rock mass [Moln 2015, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study is located in the pre-Neogene basement of the Pannonian Basin, specifically the Mezősas-Furta Dome (MFD) [Moln 2015, pp. 2-3]. Data consists of 10 available borecores from well A-2, sampled in the depth interval between 3290 and 3297.85 meters below sea level with high core recovery [Moln 2015, pp. 3-4]. All rock specimens were affected by various degrees of brittle deformation and were not oriented [Moln 2015, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
- **Petrographic Image Analysis (PIA):** Conducted to define the geometric features of clasts within the fault rocks. Polished surfaces were cut parallel to the longitudinal axis of the borecores, and 2D images were taken. Clast edges were selected manually using Adobe Photoshop, and measurements were completed in ImageJ [Moln 2015, pp. 3-4].
- **Measured Clast Geometric Parameters:** Particle Size Distribution (PSD), Clast complexity (DR), Aspect Ratio (AR), Circularity (Circ), Orientation Angle (Angle), and Convexity (Conv) [Moln 2015, pp. 3-4].
- **Statistical Analysis:** Multivariate statistical analysis using discriminant functions (D1, D2) was applied to classify the fault rock samples [Moln 2015, pp. 4-5].
- **Rock Mechanical Tests:** Uniaxial compressive tests were performed on standard cylindrical samples (2:1 axis:diameter ratio, 50 mm diameter) following ISRM suggestions. Parameters derived include uniaxial compressive strength (σc), Young's modulus (E), Poisson's ratio (ν), crack propagation stress level (σcd), and brittleness coefficient (B = We/Wt) [Moln 2015, pp. 4-5].

## Key Findings
1.  The fault zone is mainly composed of cataclasites, with local coarse breccias and sporadic fault-gouge ribbons [Moln 2015, pp. 5-7].
2.  Clast geometric data (e.g., increasing PSD, decreasing orientation dispersion) correlate with increasing deformation intensity [Moln 2015, pp. 7-7].
3.  Rock mechanical tests reveal three distinct zones with depth:
    *   **Damage Zone (M1-3):** Extremely brittle nature, low uniaxial compressive strength (σc), and high brittleness (B) [Moln 2015, pp. 9-10].
    *   **Transition Zone (M4-6):** Higher σc, strain-hardening nature, and low brittleness, interpreted as the mechanically strongest part [Moln 2015, pp. 9-10].
    *   **Fault Core (M7-10):** Gouge-rich cataclasites with widespread plastic nature, locally pseudo-ductile microstructure, and lower destruction work (Wt) [Moln 2015, pp. 9-10].
4.  The fault zone, with an estimated total thickness of ~15 m, is interpreted as a significant migration pathway within the fractured crystalline reservoir. The distributed fault core may play a key role in compartmentalizing the local hydraulic system [Moln 2015, pp. 1-2, 10-11].

## Limitations
- The borecores were not oriented, limiting kinematic interpretation [Moln 2015, pp. 3-4].
- The number of available samples is low, and their representativeness of the whole rock mass is doubtful [Moln 2015, pp. 1-2].
- The spatial position and kinematic sense of the fault zone are unclear due to the absence of oriented borecores, high-resolution seismic sections, or nearby wells [Moln 2015, pp. 2-3].
- The structure of the fault zone below the sampled interval can only be estimated with limited reliability [Moln 2015, pp. 10-11].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The analyzed fault zone is a representative example of brittle fault zones in the crystalline basement of the Pannonian Basin [Moln 2015, pp. 1-2].
- The discriminant functions (D1, D2) used for fault rock classification, developed on similar lithologies from the nearby Szeghalom Dome, are applicable to the MFD samples [Moln 2015, pp. 4-5].
- The theoretical fault zone architecture defined by earlier works (e.g., [1, 2, 16]) is used to infer the downward continuation and symmetry of the fault zones [Moln 2015, pp. 10-11].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Clast Geometric Parameters:** Particle Size Distribution (PSD), Clast complexity (DR), Aspect Ratio (AR), Circularity (Circ), Orientation Angle (Angle), Convexity (Conv) [Moln 2015, pp. 3-4].
- **Rock Mechanical Parameters:** Uniaxial compressive strength (σc), Crack propagation stress level (σcd), Young's modulus (E), Poisson's ratio (ν), Destruction work (Wt), Elastic work (We), Brittleness coefficient (B = We/Wt) [Moln 2015, pp. 4-5].

## Reusable Claims
- In the studied fault zone, the brecciated damage zone exhibits low uniaxial compressive strength and high brittleness, suggesting it may have the best reservoir characteristics due to its weakly disaggregated structure and rigid rheology [Moln 2015, pp. 9-10, 10-11].
- The transition zone between the damage zone and fault core is mechanically the strongest part of the fault zone, characterized by strain-hardening behavior and higher uniaxial compressive strength [Moln 2015, pp. 9-10].
- The presence and spatial density of fault gouge ribbons strongly control the rock mechanical properties of fault rocks, leading to plasticity and weak elasticity in mature fault rock samples [Moln 2015, pp. 9-10].
- The fractal dimension of particle size distribution (PSD) increases with increasing deformation intensity, with values below 1.3 indicating damage zone breccias and values above 1.6 indicating fault gouge formation [Moln 2015, pp. 7-9].
- The fault core, with its clay-rich, ductile, and impermeable behavior, can contribute to the hydrodynamically compartmentalized behavior of the fractured metamorphic reservoir [Moln 2015, pp. 10-11].
