# The effects of thermal treatments on the fatigue crack growth of Beishan granite: an in situ observation study.

## One-line Summary
In situ SEM observations of thermally treated (50–600 °C) Beishan granite under cyclic three-point bending reveal a temperature-driven shift from forward crack propagation to opening of pre-existing thermal cracks, with crack growth rates fitting the variant Hartman–Schijve equation and parameters correlating with linear crack density changes.

## Research Question
How do thermal treatments (50–600 °C) affect the fatigue crack initiation, propagation, and growth rate of Beishan granite under cyclic loading, and how can microstructural changes (quantified by linear crack density) explain the observed mechanisms? [Wang 2021, pp. 2-2]

## Study Area / Data
- Rock type: Beishan granite from Gansu Province, China, sampling depth 634–636 m. [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- Mineral composition (XRD): plagioclase 55.65%, quartz 24.05%, potassium feldspar 16.95%, mica and clay 3.35%. [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- Density: 2.66 g/cm³, porosity: 0.31%. [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- Specimen geometry: three-point bending, 25 mm × 10 mm × 5 mm, straight notch 0.4 mm wide, 3 mm long at mid‑edge. [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- Experimental facility: JSM‑5410LV SEM with servo‑hydraulic loading system in vacuum (10⁻⁴ Pa). [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- Temperature range: 50, 100, 150, 200, 250, 300, 400, 500, 600 °C (heating rate 3–5 °C/min, held 1 h, then natural cooling). [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- Cyclic loading: sinusoidal, 2 Hz, lower limit 20% of monotonic peak load (Pₛ), upper limit increasing stepwise from 40% Pₛ by 5% Pₛ every 4000 cycles. [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- 29 cyclic loading tests successfully conducted; monotonic tests also performed at 10⁻⁴ mm/s to determine Pₛ. [Wang 2021, pp. 4-5, Table 1]

## Methods
- **Thermal pre‑treatment**: Heating in muffle furnace, natural cooling, then in situ SEM loading. [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- **Microstructural quantification**: Stereological method (Underwood 1972) – counting intersection points of cracks with a grid of parallel test lines (spacing 500 µm) on SEM images at ×35, calculating linear crack density Pₗ = Pₙ / Lₜ. [Wang 2021, pp. 4-5]
- **In situ observation**: Continuous recording of crack initiation and propagation near notch root at ×35 magnification; load–displacement data automatically recorded. [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- **Crack growth rate analysis**: Variant Hartman–Schijve equation:  
  da/dN = D [ (ΔK – ΔK_thr) / √(1 – K_max/A) ]^α  
  with K calculated by a formula from Zuo et al. (2017). [Wang 2021, pp. 9-11]
- **Fatigue life and crack length measurement**: SEM image analysis, crack length a = a₀ + Δa; maximum growth length Δa_max determined. [Wang 2021, pp. 6-9]
- **Temperature grouping for analysis**: Low (50–150 °C), intermediate (200–300 °C), high (400–600 °C). [Wang 2021, pp. 4-5]

## Key Findings
1. **Cracking pattern transition**: With rising temperature, fatigue cracking shifts from initiation and forward propagation of new cracks (low T) to crack bridging between pre‑existing and intergranular cracks (intermediate T), and eventually to continuous opening of pre‑existing thermal cracks (high T). [Wang 2021, pp. 4-6]
2. **Microstructural changes**: Linear crack density Pₗ increases exponentially with temperature, accelerating above 300 °C. [Wang 2021, pp. 4-5]
3. **Critical crack morphology**: Evolves from a single‑crack pattern (Fig. 9j) to a fracture network pattern (Fig. 9k) with wider crack apertures as temperature rises. [Wang 2021, pp. 6-9]
4. **Crack growth rates (50–250 °C)**: Experimental da/dN vs (ΔK–ΔK_thr)/√(1–K_max/A) fits the variant Hartman–Schijve equation well. [Wang 2021, pp. 9-11]
   - α (slope, sensitivity to ΔK) mostly 0–0.5, lower than in composites/alloys, increases with Pₗ. [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]
   - D fluctuates near 7.01 × 10⁻⁸, weakly related to Pₗ. [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]
   - ΔK_thr decreases exponentially with temperature (approx. 0.2→0.15 MPa·m¹/²) and with increasing Pₗ, indicating easier crack initiation at higher thermal damage. [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]
5. **Sudden crack length jumps**: Attributed to overload from stepped amplitude increase and to initial discontinuities (pre‑existing cracks, grain boundaries). Overloads trigger grain splitting, crack bridging, and opening; initial discontinuities provide pathways. This behaviour is opposite to ductile crack retardation. [Wang 2021, pp. 13-14]
6. **Thermal treatment effects**: Thermal cracking (inter‑, trans‑, intra‑granular) and weakening of grain boundary strength both influence fatigue mechanisms, with their respective roles varying with temperature range. [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]
7. **Comparison with monotonic loading**: Rock fragmentation, grain decohesion, and grain extrusion are distinct fatigue‑specific phenomena, enhanced by thermal treatment. [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]
8. **Engineering implications**: Higher temperature leads to more extensive fatigue damage (more cracks, wider apertures, larger damage zone), which can benefit hydraulic fracturing in hot dry rock but poses challenges for nuclear waste repository integrity under cyclic loads. [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]

## Limitations
- Small specimen size (25 mm × 10 mm × 5 mm) due to SEM chamber constraints; may not fully represent bulk rock behavior. [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- Unstable final fracture not captured by SEM. [Wang 2021, pp. 4-5]
- At T > 300 °C, simultaneous opening of multiple cracks made crack front localization and accurate Δa measurement impossible, limiting crack growth rate analysis to ≤250 °C. [Wang 2021, pp. 6-9]
- Only one rock type (Beishan granite) studied; generalizability to other lithologies not confirmed.
- da/dN curves for same‑T specimens did not collapse to a single master curve, indicating specimen‑specific microstructural variation not fully controlled. [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]
- Physical meaning of exponent α in variant Hartman–Schijve equation not fully revealed. [Wang 2021, pp. 13-14]
- Monotonic loading tests performed at a single rate (10⁻⁴ mm/s). [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- Temperature ramp rate and hold time fixed; effects of different heating/cooling paths not explored.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Thermal damage is primarily represented by linear crack density (Pₗ) measured on surface SEM images at ×35, assumed representative of bulk. [Wang 2021, pp. 4-5]
- The variant Hartman–Schijve equation (originally for metals/composites) applies to rock fatigue crack growth for the examined small cracks. [Wang 2021, pp. 9-11]
- Crack growth length Δa measured from notch root along the observed area; crack path assumed to be the dominant fatigue crack.
- The stepwise cyclic loading scheme (increasing amplitude every 4000 cycles) is considered representative of variable amplitude fatigue in rock engineering. [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- Temperature range 50–600 °C covers conditions in nuclear waste repositories (~200 °C) and geothermal reservoirs (up to 600 °C). [Wang 2021, pp. 2-4]
- The influence of temperature on fatigue is dominated by thermal cracking and grain boundary weakening, neglecting possible chemical/mineralogical alterations. [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]

## Reusable Claims
1. **Claim**: For Beishan granite, linear crack density (Pₗ) measured by stereology on SEM images increases exponentially with thermal treatment temperature, with sharp rise above 300 °C.  
   *Condition*: Heating at 3–5 °C/min, 1 h hold, natural cooling; SEM observation at ×35. [Wang 2021, pp. 4-5]
2. **Claim**: The fatigue cracking mode transitions from forward propagation of new cracks (≤150 °C) to crack bridging involving intergranular cracks (200–300 °C) and to opening of pre‑existing thermal cracks (≥400 °C).  
   *Condition*: Stepwise cyclic three‑point bending, 2 Hz, vacuum. [Wang 2021, pp. 4-6]
3. **Claim**: The variant Hartman–Schijve equation (da/dN = D[(ΔK–ΔK_thr)/√(1–K_max/A)]^α) can describe fatigue crack growth rates in thermally treated Beishan granite up to 250 °C.  
   *Condition*: Small cracks, K calculated via secant formula; ΔK_thr chosen to fit data. [Wang 2021, pp. 9-11]
4. **Claim**: Increasing thermal damage (higher Pₗ) lowers the threshold stress intensity factor range (ΔK_thr) for crack initiation and increases the sensitivity exponent α.  
   *Condition*: 50–250 °C range; ΔK_thr from ~0.2 to ~0.15 MPa·m¹/². [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]
5. **Claim**: Sudden crack length jumps during stepped cyclic loading are primarily due to overload effects, which trigger grain splitting, crack bridging, and opening; initial discontinuities provide secondary paths.  
   *Condition*: Beishan granite, stepped amplitude, brittle behavior. [Wang 2021, pp. 13-14]
6. **Claim**: Thermal treatment promotes grain decohesion and intergranular fracture during cyclic loading, particularly in the 200–300 °C range, and grain extrusion in high‑temperature range.  
   *Condition*: Observed in SEM during fatigue. [Wang 2021, pp. 5-6]
7. **Claim**: The crack growth rate magnitude (10⁻⁸–10⁻⁷ mm/cycle) and exponent α (≤0.5) for this granite are lower than typical values for alloys and composites.  
   *Condition*: As tested under the given loading protocol. [Wang 2021, pp. 11-13]
