# Experimental Study on Mechanical Characteristics of Granite under High Temperatures and Triaxial Stresses.

## One-line Summary
This study investigates the thermal deformation, failure characteristics, and thermo-mechanical parameters of large granite samples under high temperature and triaxial stress conditions using a specialized high-capacity testing machine.

## Research Question
The research addresses the need to understand the mechanical characteristics of granite at high temperature and high pressure, which is critical for applications such as extracting heat from hot dry rock (HDR) and the disposal of nuclear waste [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].

## Study Area / Data
The experimental data comes from tests on large cylindrical granite samples with a diameter of 200 mm and a height of 400 mm [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3]. The samples were tested under temperatures up to 600°C and a confining pressure of 25 MPa, which simulates a depth of approximately 1000 meters [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].

## Methods
The study utilized a 20 MN servo-controlled triaxial rock testing machine with high temperature and pressure capabilities developed by the authors [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3]. The experimental procedure involved: (1) applying a hydrostatic stress of 25 MPa; (2) heating the samples at a rate of 5°C/h; (3) maintaining the target temperature for 5 hours to ensure thermal equilibrium; and (4) conducting mechanical loading tests at specific temperatures [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3]. Thermal expansion was measured under stress, and failure modes were observed post-test [Wan 2008, pp. 3-6].

## Key Findings
1.  Under 3D hydrostatic stress, granite's thermal deformation course can be divided into three stages: a slow deformation stage at low temperature (room temperature to 120°C), a fast deformation stage at medium-high temperature (120°C to 450°C), and a slow deformation stage at high temperature (above 450°C) [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].
2.  The thermal expansion coefficient measured under stress is significantly lower than that measured without stress, indicating that stress-free measurements may overestimate the rock's thermal expansion [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].
3.  Granite exhibits volume expansion under low stress but transitions to volume shrinkage after exceeding a certain stress level under high temperature and triaxial conditions, a behavior different from that at normal temperatures [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].
4.  The failure mode of granite at high temperature is shear failure, and the rock transforms from brittle to ductile behavior under high confining pressure and high temperature [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].
5.  The elastic modulus of granite decreases with increasing temperature under confining pressure, following a three-stage process: a slow decrease (room temperature to 200°C), a fast decrease (200°C to 400°C), and a relatively constant stage (400°C to 600°C) [Wan 2008, pp. 3-6].

## Limitations
1.  Previous studies often used small samples (e.g., φ50 mm × 100 mm), which may not adequately represent the behavior of actual rock masses [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].
2.  The thermal expansion coefficient measured in a stress-free state may excessively magnify the rock's thermal expansion and lead to incorrect conclusions [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].
3.  The study's conclusions are specific to the tested granite type and the specific stress/temperature paths applied.

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  A confining pressure of 25 MPa is used to simulate the stress state at a depth of approximately 1000 meters [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].
2.  The heating rate is controlled at 5°C/h to approximate quasi-static thermal loading [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].
3.  Samples are maintained at target temperatures for 5 hours to achieve thermal equilibrium [Wan 2008, pp. 1-3].
