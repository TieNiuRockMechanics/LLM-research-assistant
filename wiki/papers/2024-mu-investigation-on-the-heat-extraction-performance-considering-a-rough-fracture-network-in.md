# Investigation on the Heat Extraction Performance Considering a Rough Fracture Network in Heterogeneous Reservoirs.

## One-line Summary
A 3D geothermal numerical model is built, using spatial frequency for reservoir heterogeneity and the Weierstrass-Mandelbrot fractal function for rough discrete fractures, to examine how fracture parameters and injection-production parameters affect heat extraction performance [Mu 2024, pp. 1-1].

## Research Question
How do geological-engineering parameters (fracture number, width, roughness coefficient, injection rate, injection temperature, and geothermal gradient) influence heat extraction performance in an enhanced geothermal system with a heterogeneous reservoir and a rough discrete fracture network? [Mu 2024, pp. 1-1, 1-3, 14-14].

## Study Area / Data
The study takes the hot dry rock (HDR) development in the Qiabuqia area of the Gonghe Basin, Qinghai Province, as an example [Mu 2024, pp. 6-6]. Physical property data were obtained from HDR coring experiments in the Qiabuqia geothermal field: rock density 1430–3710 kg/m³ (mean 2607 kg/m³), thermal conductivity 1.85–3.20 W/(m·K) (mean 2.51 W/(m·K)), heat capacity 640–880 J/(kg·K) (mean 754.4 J/(kg·K)), matrix porosity 3.85–6.05% (mean 5%), permeability 1.52×10⁻¹⁴–2.56×10⁻¹⁴ m² (mean 2×10⁻¹⁴ m²) [Mu 2024, pp. 7-7].

## Methods
- **Numerical model:** Finite element method (Comsol Multiphysics), staggered solution scheme, Newton‑Raphson iteration, backward difference time stepping [Mu 2024, pp. 6-6].
- **Heterogeneity:** Three‑dimensional synthetic material data generated by a truncated Fourier‑like sum of cosine waves with spatial frequencies controlled by a spectral index \( \beta \); amplitudes follow a Gaussian‑random‑weighted power‑law decay \( d(a,b,c) = g(a,b,c) / (a^2+b^2+c^2)^{\beta/2} \); phases are uniform random on \([-\pi/2, \pi/2]\) [Mu 2024, pp. 4-6].
- **Rough fractures:** Fracture surface height \( z(x,y) \) constructed from the Yan–Komvopoulos three‑dimensional W‑M fractal function (Eq. 22) with parameters: fractal dimension \( D \) (2<D<3), characteristic scale \( G \), sampling length \( L \), cutoff length \( L_s \), frequency index \( n \), and random phase \( \psi_{m,n} \) [Mu 2024, pp. 6-6].
- **Governing equations:** Mass conservation (Eq. 1, 3) with Darcy’s law (Eq. 2); fracture permeability via cubic law including roughness coefficient \( f_f \) (Eq. 4); energy balance with equivalent volumetric heat capacity and equivalent thermal conductivity (Eqs. 5–7); linear initial geothermal gradient (Eq. 8) [Mu 2024, pp. 3-4].
- **Model domain:** 1000 m × 1000 m × 1000 m, one injection well and one production well, rough discrete fractures of 600 m length; fixed, insulated external boundaries; injection‑production balance (injection rate = production rate) [Mu 2024, pp. 6-6, 8-8].
- **Validation:** Heat transfer along a single smooth fracture compared with analytical solution (Eq. 23); good agreement [Mu 2024, pp. 7-7].
- **Evaluation metrics:** Average production well temperature \( T_{\text{pro}} \) (Eq. 24), produced energy \( E_w \) (Eq. 25), effective electric power \( W_e \) (Eq. 26, conversion efficiency \( \eta=0.45 \), heat removal temperature \( T_{\text{rej}} \)) [Mu 2024, pp. 8-8].

## Key Findings
1. **Fracture number:** Increasing from 0 to 5 causes produced energy to drop 27.88 %, effective electric power 38.67 %; from 5 to 9 only a further 21.63 % and 26.29 % decrease; when the number reaches 7–9, the influence on low‑temperature area sweep gradually diminishes [Mu 2024, pp. 10-10, 14-14].
2. **Fracture width:** From 0.5 mm to 3 mm, average production temperature drops 6.08 %, produced energy 38.69 %, effective electric power 47.11 %; wider fractures intensify thermal breakthrough [Mu 2024, pp. 10-10, 14-14].
3. **Fracture roughness coefficient:** Raising the coefficient from 1.6 to 11.6 increases produced energy by 18.77 % and effective electric power by 24.46 %; it delays thermal breakthrough [Mu 2024, pp. 10-12, 14-14].
4. **Injection rate:** From 60 l/s to 160 l/s, average production temperature falls 6.06 %, but produced energy rises 57.42 % and effective electric power 33.95 %; higher rate accelerates thermal breakthrough [Mu 2024, pp. 12-12, 14-14].
5. **Injection temperature:** From 303 K to 353 K, produced energy decreases 48.16 %, effective electric power 38.10 %; injection below 333 K may cause wellbore scaling; 333–353 K is suggested as beneficial [Mu 2024, pp. 12-12, 14-14].
6. **Geothermal gradient:** From 0.01 K/m to 0.06 K/m, produced energy increases 16.73 %, effective electric power 21.51 %; reservoirs with >0.03 K/m show better heat extraction [Mu 2024, pp. 12-14, 14-14].

## Limitations
- The constitutive model of fracture width and its changes is not considered [Mu 2024, pp. 13-14].
- Non‑Darcy flow behavior in rough fractures is not analysed; the current model focuses on Darcy flow [Mu 2024, pp. 13-14].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Reservoir is a porous medium composed of matrix and discrete fractures; fluid flow is saturated single‑phase and follows Darcy’s law [Mu 2024, pp. 3-4].
- Heat transfer in rock mass is only heat conduction, no thermal radiation [Mu 2024, pp. 3-4].
- Fluid properties (water) change with temperature and pressure, embedded from property curves [Mu 2024, pp. 4-4].
- Initial reservoir temperature varies linearly with depth: \( T_{\text{ini}}(z) = T_g + \varsigma z \) [Mu 2024, pp. 4-4].
- Injection well: \( T(t)=T_{\text{inj}} \), \( Q_{\text{inj}}(t)=Q_0 \); production well: adiabatic heat flux, \( Q_{\text{pro}}=Q_0 \); vertical boundaries: specified flow direction and temperature conditions [Mu 2024, pp. 4-4].
- Numerical model size 1000 m × 1000 m × 1000 m, fracture length 600 m; external boundaries fixed, insulated, no flow [Mu 2024, pp. 6-6].
- Simulation time 80 years; injection‑production balance maintained [Mu 2024, pp. 8-8].
- Base case parameters: injection temperature 303 K, injection rate 120 l/s, geothermal gradient 0.03 K/m, fracture width 0.002 m, roughness coefficient 1.6, fractal dimension 2.1, characteristic scale 1e‑4, grid accuracy 50 [Mu 2024, pp. 4-5 (Table 2)].

- **W‑M fractal parameters:** fractal dimension \( D \), characteristic scale \( G \), sampling length \( L \), cutoff length \( L_s \), frequency index \( n \), random phase \( \psi_{m,n} \) [Mu 2024, pp. 6-6].

## Reusable Claims
- In a heterogeneous EGS reservoir with rough discrete fractures, increasing the fracture number beyond 7–9 no longer expands the dominant cold‑fluid pathways significantly, and further decreases in production temperature and energy output become marginal [Mu 2024, pp. 10-10, 14-14].
- Wider fractures (0.5→3 mm) intensify thermal breakthrough because of higher fracture permeability; produced energy can drop by ~39% and effective electric power by ~47% under the studied conditions [Mu 2024, pp. 10-10, 14-14].
- A larger fracture roughness coefficient reduces fracture permeability (cubic law with roughness factor) and delays the cold front, improving heat extraction performance; a +10 increase in roughness coefficient (1.6→11.6) yields ~24.5% more effective electric power [Mu 2024, pp. 10-12, 14-14].
- Higher injection rates (60→160 l/s) simultaneously increase produced energy (~57%) and effective electric power (~34%) but accelerate thermal breakthrough; the optimal rate must balance operational lifespan and heat extraction efficiency [Mu 2024, pp. 12-12, 14-14].
- Raising injection temperature reduces the production–injection temperature difference, thus lowering produced energy and effective electric power; injection temperatures of 333–353 K are suggested to avoid wellbore scaling and maintain acceptable extraction efficiency [Mu 2024, pp. 12-12, 14-14].
- Reservoirs with a geothermal gradient >0.03 K/m show better heat extraction performance; an increase from 0.01 to 0.06 K/m raises effective electric power by ~21.5% under the model conditions [Mu 2024, pp. 12-14, 14-14].

## Connections To Other Work
- Previous numerical studies often represented fractures as ideal smooth surfaces and assumed homogeneous reservoirs [Mu 2024, pp. 1-3, 3-3]. This work explicitly incorporates rough fracture geometry (W‑M function) and reservoir heterogeneity (spatial frequency) [Mu 2024, pp. 1-1, 3-3].
- Compared with earlier models (Table 1): e.g., Yao et al. [16] used curved rough surface for a single fracture; Li et al. [10] used smooth straight lines; Zheng et al. [18] used partially rough surfaces; none combined global rough surfaces with heterogeneity as done here [Mu 2024, pp. 3-3, 4-4].
- The influence of fracture network density has been studied with dual‑porosity methods (Gelet et al. [8]) and with discrete fracture models (Zhang et al. [9]); the present model uses discrete rough fractures [Mu 2024, pp. 1-1, 3-3].
- Fracture roughness effects on flow and heat transfer were previously evaluated qualitatively by He et al. [11]; this study quantifies the effect using the roughness coefficient in the cubic law [Mu 2024, pp. 3-4, 10-12].
- Fractal‑like tree networks (Zhang et al. [12]) and well‑fracture‑reservoir combined models (Cao et al. [13]) have been explored; this model focuses on stochastic rough discrete fractures generated by the W‑M fractal function [Mu 2024, pp. 6-6].
- The use of a heterogeneous reservoir is consistent with some recent works (Gao et al. [22], Zhang et al. [24], Han et al. [25]), but this study integrates heterogeneity with rough discrete fractures [Mu 2024, pp. 3-4].
