# Stable Isotope and Anthropogenic Tracer Signature of Waters in an Andean Geothermal System.

## One-line Summary
This study uses stable isotopes and anthropogenic tracers to determine that thermal waters in the Lonquimay-Tolhuaca Volcanic Complex are of meteoric origin, recharge at ~1000 m elevation, and mix with a small fraction (2-22%) of modern water, with fault-fracture networks controlling residence times and ascent pathways.

## Research Question
What are the origin, residence times, and evolution of thermal waters in the Lonquimay-Tolhuaca Volcanic Complex (LTVC) of the southern Chilean Andes, and how do fault-fracture networks control their circulation, mixing, and geochemical signature?

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on the Lonquimay-Tolhuaca Volcanic Complex (LTVC) in the Southern Volcanic Zone (SVZ) of Chile (~38°S). The area is characterized by the interplay between the NNE-trending Liquiñe-Ofqui Fault System (LOFS) and WNW-striking Andean Transverse Faults (ATF) [Moreno 2021, pp. 1-2]. A total of 20 water samples from naturally discharging springs (12 thermal, 8 cold) and 6 surface water samples were collected between 2016 and 2018 [Moreno 2021, pp. 4-4]. Thermal waters discharge at temperatures from 31°C to 96°C, while cold springs range from 8°C to 13°C [Moreno 2021, pp. 4-5]. The subsurface geology includes the Cura-Mallín Group (volcano-sedimentary Guapitrío and sedimentary Río Pedregoso Formations) and the overlying Malleco Formation [Moreno 2021, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
Water samples were analyzed for major ion geochemistry, stable isotope ratios (δ²H, δ¹⁸O, δ¹³C_TDIC), and dissolved anthropogenic tracers (CFC-11, CFC-12, CFC-113, SF₆) [Moreno 2021, pp. 1-1]. Major cations were analyzed by ICP-OES and anions by ion chromatography; bicarbonate alkalinity was measured by titration [Moreno 2021, pp. 4-4]. Stable isotopes were analyzed by OA-ICOS laser absorption (δ²H, δ¹⁸O) and mass spectrometry (δ¹³C) [Moreno 2021, pp. 4-4]. Anthropogenic tracers were sampled following specific protocols to avoid contamination and analyzed by purge and trap gas chromatography [Moreno 2021, pp. 4-4]. A Rayleigh-fractionation model was applied to precipitation isotope data to estimate recharge elevation [Moreno 2021, pp. 6-7]. Geochemical modeling using PHREEQC was used to simulate the effects of CO₂ degassing and calcite precipitation on δ¹³C values [Moreno 2021, pp. 7-8]. Binary mixing models were applied to anthropogenic tracer data to quantify mixing between old and modern water [Moreno 2021, pp. 9-10].

## Key Findings
1.  **Recharge Source and Elevation:** Thermal and cold waters are of meteoric origin, plotting near the Local Meteoric Water Line (LMWL) [Moreno 2021, pp. 5-6]. Recharge for most thermal springs occurs at average elevations near ~1000 m a.s.l., corresponding to valley margins and lower mountain flanks [Moreno 2021, pp. 6-7].
2.  **Carbon Sources:** The bulk of dissolved inorganic carbon (TDIC) is derived from the addition of soil CO₂ to infiltrating rainwater. Samples near volcanic centers (e.g., MA(5), VA(20)) show an additional magmatic CO₂ input [Moreno 2021, pp. 7-7].
3.  **Subsurface Processes:** Thermal waters show no significant oxygen isotope shift, suggesting insufficient temperatures for exchange or high water/rock ratios [Moreno 2021, pp. 6-6]. CO₂ degassing and calcite precipitation during ascent modify the δ¹³C signature of thermal waters [Moreno 2021, pp. 7-8].
4.  **Residence Times and Mixing:** All geothermal samples contain low but detectable concentrations of anthropogenic tracers, indicating the addition of a small fraction (2–22%) of modern meteoric water [Moreno 2021, pp. 1-1, 9-10]. Discharge temperature correlates with water age, with thermal waters being older than cold waters [Moreno 2021, pp. 1-1]. Binary mixing between an old, tracer-free fluid and a modern component best describes the age distribution of thermal springs [Moreno 2021, pp. 9-10].
5.  **Structural Control:** Fault-fracture meshes, particularly favorably oriented segments of the LOFS, exert a first-order control on residence times, ascent rates, and mixing, allowing focused, rapid ascent of hydrothermal fluids with limited mixing [Moreno 2021, pp. 1-1, 10-11].

## Limitations
*   The scarcity of year-round, high-altitude precipitation isotope data in southern Chile required the use of a Rayleigh-fractionation model with boundary conditions from a single weather station (Temuco) [Moreno 2021, pp. 6-6].
*   Potential bacterial degradation of CFCs (especially CFC-11) under anaerobic conditions may affect apparent ages in some samples (e.g., VL(10), AM(13), CB(18)) [Moreno 2021, pp. 10-10].
*   The study does not provide direct measurements of reservoir temperature or permeability, relying on surface manifestations and geochemical modeling.
*   Samples JM(7) and BT(19) were excluded from tracer analysis due to potential boiling effects [Moreno 2021, pp. 5-6].

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   The Rayleigh-fractionation model assumes topography is the dominant control on precipitation isotope composition and that the isotopic signature of water is not significantly altered during subsurface circulation [Moreno 2021, pp. 6-7].
*   Anthropogenic tracer dating assumes equilibrium with the atmosphere at recharge (Henry's Law), known atmospheric histories, and that tracers are conservative (no degradation or contamination) [Moreno 2021, pp. 8-9].
*   The binary mixing model assumes mixing between two distinct end-members: a pre-1940, tracer-free fluid and a modern meteoric component [Moreno 2021, pp. 9-10].
*   Recharge temperature is approximated as mean annual air temperature corrected for altitude using a standard lapse rate [Moreno 2021, pp. 8-9].

## Reusable Claims
*   **Condition:** In the Andean Cordillera of the SVZ (~38°S), where topography strongly controls precipitation distribution. **Claim:** Geothermal system recharge occurs locally at elevations around 1000 m a.s.l., corresponding to valley margins and lower mountain flanks [Moreno 2021, pp. 6-7].
*   **Condition:** For thermal springs in the LTVC with discharge temperatures between 31°C and 96°C. **Claim:** The modern meteoric water fraction, determined by binary mixing of anthropogenic tracers, ranges from 2% to 22% [Moreno 2021, pp. 9-10].
*   **Condition:** In fractured geothermal systems with low primary host rock permeability. **Claim:** Focused conduit flow along high vertical permeability fault-fracture networks allows hydrothermal fluid to ascend rapidly with limited mixing with shallow groundwater, maintaining elevated temperatures [Moreno 2021, pp. 10-11].
*   **Condition:** For hydrothermal fluids ascending from depth. **Claim:** CO₂ degassing and calcite precipitation at temperatures below 200°C can significantly enrich the δ¹³C signature of the fluid [Moreno 2021, pp. 7-8].

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study builds on and confirms findings from previous work in the SVZ regarding the role of the LOFS and ATF in controlling geothermal fluid circulation and composition [Moreno 2021, pp. 1-2, 11-12].
*   The binary mixing model result (2-22% modern water) is in excellent agreement with helium isotope data from Tardani et al. (2016) for the same area [Moreno 2021, pp. 9-10].
*   The conceptual model of fluid circulation is consistent with mechanisms described in Wrage et al. (2017) and the structural controls outlined in Pérez-Flores et al. (2016, 2017) [Moreno 2021, pp. 11-12].
*   The use of CFCs and SF₆ as tracers in geothermal systems follows methodologies established by Plummer et al. (2006) and Busenberg and Plummer (2006) [Moreno 2021, pp. 2-3].

## Open Questions
*   What are the precise mechanisms and rates of mixing between deep hydrothermal fluids and shallow groundwater at the fault-fracture interface?
*   How do seasonal variations in recharge volume and temperature affect the long-term geochemical evolution of the system?
*   What is the exact role of the different ATF orientations in modulating fluid pathways and reservoir temperatures compared to the LOFS?
*   Can the anthropogenic tracer approach be refined to better account for potential degradation in reducing geothermal environments?
