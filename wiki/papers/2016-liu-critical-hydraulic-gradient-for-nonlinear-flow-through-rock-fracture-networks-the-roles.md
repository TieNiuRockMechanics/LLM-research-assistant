# Critical Hydraulic Gradient for Nonlinear Flow through Rock Fracture Networks: The Roles of Aperture, Surface Roughness, and Number of Intersections.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally and numerically investigates the critical hydraulic gradient \( J_c \) for the onset of nonlinear flow in discrete fracture networks (DFNs), showing that \( J_c \) is most sensitive to mechanical aperture, followed by the number of intersections and surface roughness, and develops empirical expressions to predict Forchheimer’s law coefficients and \( J_c \) [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2], [Liu 2016, pp. 7-8], [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12].

## Research Question
What are the roles of mechanical aperture, surface roughness, and the number of intersections in determining the critical hydraulic gradient for the transition from linear to nonlinear fluid flow in discrete fracture networks (DFNs)? [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2] The study seeks a criterion based on the hydraulic gradient \( J \) rather than the Reynolds number \( Re \) because assessing localized \( Re \) values in a DFN is a "tough task" and \( J \) is typically a known parameter in practice [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2]. The transition mechanism itself was already known from single fractures and intersections, but the quantitative link to macro DFN properties had not been established [Liu 2016, pp. 2-2].

## Study Area / Data
- **Experimental models**: Two crossed-fracture models with a single intersection (50 cm side length, 5 mm thickness), manufactured from glass plates. One model had smooth fracture surfaces; the other had rough surfaces generated by pre-designed cutting, resulting in partially unmated walls. Actual apertures (estimated via CCD camera imaging and image processing) averaged ~1.23 mm (smooth) and ~1.17 mm (rough). Roughness in the rough model quantified by \( Z_2 \) (0.14–0.43, corresponding to JRC 4.47–20.30). Flow tests were performed with distilled water under one-inlet/two-outlet configurations, with intersecting angles of 60°, 120°, and 180° [Liu 2016, pp. 2-2], [Liu 2016, pp. 2-4].
- **Parametric numerical DFN models**: 40 two‑dimensional DFN models (50 cm × 50 cm). Two orthogonal sets of fractures intersecting at 60° with parameters: mechanical aperture \( h_M \) = 0.5–10 mm; number of intersections \( N_i \) = 1–12; joint roughness coefficient JRC = 0–20; hydraulic gradient \( J \) = 10⁻⁸–10⁰ [Liu 2016, pp. 6-7].
- **Validation DFN models**: Larger DFNs (side lengths 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0 m) extracted from a 5 m × 5 m parent network (smooth and rough versions). Fracture length followed a power law (\( a = 1.7 \)), aperture correlated with length (\( h_M = 0.0025 l^{0.5} \)), and orientation followed the Fisher distribution (\( K = 8 \)). Mean \( h_M \) was ~4.0–4.5 mm [Liu 2016, pp. 9-11].

## Methods
- **Flow experiments**: High-precision syringe pump (±0.001 mL/min), differential pressure gauge (±10 Pa), and electronic balances (±0.01 g). Flow rate incrementally varied from 1 to 100 mL/min. Fracture geometry was captured with a CCD camera and processed into binary images; polylines with 0.01 mm segments were drawn in AutoCAD to reproduce the geometry for numerical models [Liu 2016, pp. 2-4], [Liu 2016, pp. 4-5].
- **Numerical simulations**: Two‑dimensional finite‑volume method (ANSYS FLUENT) directly solving the Navier–Stokes equations. No‑slip boundary conditions on all walls. Ten meshing layers were chosen (further refinement changed the flow rate by < 1 %). Identical flow rates as in the tests were applied, and pressure drops recorded [Liu 2016, pp. 4-5].
- **Flow regime quantification**: \( J \)–\( Q \) data were fitted to Forchheimer’s law, \( J = AQ + BQ^2 \). Nonlinearity factor \( E = BQ^2 / (AQ + BQ^2) \), and the critical hydraulic gradient \( J_c \) was defined at \( E = 0.01 \), i.e., when the nonlinear term contributes 1 % of the total pressure drop [Liu 2016, pp. 5-6].
- **Parametric study and regression**: 40 simulations with controlled \( h_M \), \( N_i \), and JRC. Multi‑variable regression yielded empirical expressions for \( A \), \( B \), and \( J_c \) (Eqs. 8–10) [Liu 2016, pp. 7-8].

## Key Findings
1.  **Experimental validation**: For both smooth and rough single‑intersection models, the \( J \)–\( Q \) relationship is well described by Forchheimer’s law (\( R^2 > 0.99 \)). At low flow rates, the relationship is linear and follows the cubic law [Liu 2016, pp. 5-5].
2.  **Role of roughness and intersections**: The rough model exhibited \( A \) and \( B \) coefficients 2–5 times larger than the smooth model, indicating lower effective permeability and stronger nonlinearity. The intersecting angle had a much smaller effect [Liu 2016, pp. 5-6].
3.  **Critical hydraulic gradient (\( J_c \))**:
    - \( J_c \) of the rough single‑intersection model (~10⁻²) was smaller than that of the smooth model, corresponding to \( Re \) ≈ 10.5–13.8 at the inlet [Liu 2016, pp. 5-6].
    - In parametric DFNs, \( J_c \) varies by approximately five orders of magnitude when \( h_M \) changes from 0.5 mm to 10 mm.
    - \( J_c \) is most sensitive to mechanical aperture, followed by the number of intersections and surface roughness [Liu 2016, pp. 7-8].
4.  **Empirical equations**: Multi‑variate regression yielded:
    - \( A = (\lambda h_M)^{-0.5} \cdot \exp \{310.5(\lambda h_M)^{-0.008} - 0.001\mathrm{JRC} - 0.7 N_i^{0.25} - 303\} \)
    - \( B = (\lambda h_M)^{-2} \cdot \exp \{719(\lambda h_M)^{-0.002} - 0.01\mathrm{JRC} - 5 N_i^{0.15} - 700.4\} \)
    - \( J_c = (\lambda h_M)^{-0.5} \cdot \exp \{300.8(\lambda h_M)^{-0.01} - 0.03\mathrm{JRC} - 0.3 N_i^{0.5} - 303.3\} \)
    where \( \lambda \) (mm⁻¹) accounts for aperture variation among fractures. For uniform fractures, \( \lambda = 1 \); for the validation DFNs, \( \lambda = 0.8 \) gave the best fit [Liu 2016, pp. 7-8], [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12].
5.  **Validation of equations**: When applied to DFNs with correlated length–aperture distributions, the equations predicted \( A \), \( B \), and \( J_c \) with high correlation (\( R^2 = 0.99, 0.69, 0.95 \), respectively) [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12].
6.  **Flow regimes in DFNs**: For the validation models, linear regime (\( J < 10^{-6} \)), weak inertial regime (\( 10^{-6} < J < 10^{-4} \)), and strong inertial regime (\( J > 10^{-4} \)) were identified. The inﬂection point in the \( Q/J \)–\( J \) relationship is consistent with the Barree–Conway model, not predicted by Forchheimer’s law alone [Liu 2016, pp. 9-11].

## Limitations
- The study used two‑dimensional DFN models, and the presented empirical equations “are therefore not directly applicable to three dimensional DFNs” [Liu 2016, pp. 7-8].
- The models considered first‑order roughness (primary asperities) only, lacking micro‑roughness typical of some natural fractures (glass conchoidal surfaces) [Liu 2016, pp. 2-4].
- The equations were derived from DFNs with relatively simple geometries. Their validity was only tested on DFNs with correlated length–aperture distributions and specific statistical parameters [Liu 2016, pp. 7-8], [Liu 2016, pp. 9-11].
- The no‑slip boundary condition on top/bottom walls was considered negligible because the fracture width (5 mm) was ~5 times the aperture (~1 mm), but this may not hold for all fracture geometries [Liu 2016, pp. 4-5].
- Eddy micro‑structures at high Re were not resolved in detail because the mesh refinement was limited to 10 layers as a “compromise between computational time and accuracy” [Liu 2016, pp. 4-5].
- The empirical expressions (Eqs. 8–10) may only be suitable for shallow rock masses; for deep‑seated rocks with apertures < 0.1 mm, the required \( J_c \) would be “extremely large … that barely exists in nature” [Liu 2016, pp. 7-8].

## Assumptions / Conditions
- **General**: Fluid is incompressible Newtonian water (density 998.2 kg/m³, viscosity 0.001 Pa·s at 20 °C). Flow is laminar; nonlinearity arises solely from the convective acceleration term \( (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{u} \) in the Navier–Stokes equations. No‑slip boundaries on all fracture walls. The cubic law (Eq. 4) is the limit of Forchheimer’s law when the nonlinear term vanishes. A single critical \( E = 0.01 \) defines \( J_c \) for all cases [Liu 2016, pp. 4-5], [Liu 2016, pp. 5-6].
- **Experiments**: Mechanical aperture does not change with hydraulic pressure. Inlet and outlets are on an identical horizontal plane; pressure at the exit of outlet tank is zero, but the differential gauge captures the true pressure drop across fractures [Liu 2016, pp. 2-4].
- **Parametric models**: Two‑dimensional, 50 cm × 50 cm, constant aperture per fracture. Roughness of a fracture wall follows a Gaussian distribution of node shifts. JRC is calculated from \( Z_2 \) using Eq. 2. Uniform hydraulic pressure imposed on top/bottom boundaries; left/right impermeable. Steady state when inflow equals outflow [Liu 2016, pp. 6-7].
- **Validation DFNs**: Fracture length follows a power‑law (exponent \( a = 1.7 \)); orientations follow a Fisher distribution (\( K = 8 \)); mechanical aperture correlates with length as \( h_M = 0.0025 l^{0.5} \). Surface roughness is only applied in the rough model version (otherwise smooth). Mean \( h_M \) and mean JRC are used in predictions. \( \lambda = 0.8 \) mm⁻¹ was fitted to these DFNs; the value “may be subjected to change when a different algorithm for generating aperture distributions … is employed” [Liu 2016, pp. 9-11].

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent variables**:
  - \( h_M \): Mechanical aperture (mm) – arithmetic average point‑to‑point distance between fracture walls.
  - \( N_i \): Number of fracture intersections in the DFN.
  - JRC: Joint Roughness Coefficient, a widely used roughness index linked to \( Z_2 \) via Eq. 2 [Liu 2016, pp. 2-4], [Liu 2016, pp. 6-7].
- **Dependent variables**:
  - \( J_c \): Critical hydraulic gradient (dimensionless), defined at \( E = 0.01 \).
  - \( A \) (s/m³) and \( B \) (s²/m⁶): Coefficients in Forchheimer’s law \( J = AQ + BQ^2 \); \( A \) inversely related to permeability [Liu 2016, pp. 5-6].
- **Other parameters**:
  - \( \lambda \): Reduction factor for \( h_M \) accounting for aperture variability among fractures (mm⁻¹; = 1 when all fractures have identical aperture; = 0.8 for the validation DFNs) [Liu 2016, pp. 7-8], [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12].
  - \( E \): Nonlinearity factor, fraction of pressure drop due to the \( BQ^2 \) term [Liu 2016, pp. 5-6].
  - \( Re \): Reynolds number (not used for the DFN‑scale criterion but shown to correlate with \( J_c \)) [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2], [Liu 2016, pp. 5-6].
  - Intersecting angle: Considered in the experimental models (60°, 120°, 180°) but found to have a “much smaller impact” than roughness and aperture [Liu 2016, pp. 5-6].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **“Forchheimer’s law … can well quantif[y]” the \( J \)–\( Q \) relationship in fractured rock systems, from single intersections to DFNs** [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2], [Liu 2016, pp. 5-5].  
    *Condition/Limitation*: Valid for the laminar nonlinear regime; at sufficiently low \( J \), the \( BQ^2 \) term becomes negligible and the cubic law is recovered. The fit quality (\( R^2 > 0.99 \)) was demonstrated for 2D geometries with apertures ≥ 0.5 mm and \( Re \) up to at least ~100.
2.  **“Larger apertures, rougher fracture surfaces, and a greater number of intersections … result in the onset of nonlinear flow at a lower \( J_c \)”** [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2], [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12].  
    *Condition/Limitation*: Confirmed for 2D DFNs with \( h_M \)=0.5–10 mm, JRC=0–20, \( N_i \)=1–12. The hierarchy of sensitivity is \( h_M \) > \( N_i \) > JRC, attributed to the cubic dependence of \( Re \) on aperture.
3.  **“When the imposed hydraulic gradient on a DFN is below the predicted critical hydraulic gradient, application of the cubic law … would give reasonable solutions”** [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12].  
    *Condition/Limitation*: This statement assumes that necessary aperture modifications (e.g., from \( h_M \) to \( h_H \)) for surface roughness are applied. The threshold \( J_c \) must be estimated via Eq. 10.
4.  **“When the hydraulic gradient is larger than the critical value, one may apply Forchheimer’s law … using predicted values of \( A \) and \( B \) based on the proposed equations”** [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12].  
    *Condition/Limitation*: The equations (Eqs. 8–9) are empirical and were derived from a specific set of 2D DFN simulations with uniform aperture per fracture and constant intersection angle (60°). They require a calibrated \( \lambda \) factor when aperture varies among fractures (e.g., \( \lambda = 0.8 \) for the tested length–aperture correlated DFNs). Direct transferability to 3D networks is not confirmed.
5.  **“A factor (\( E \)) … represents the proportional contribution of the nonlinear term to the total pressure drop”** and can be used with a chosen threshold to define \( J_c \) [Liu 2016, pp. 5-6].  
    *Condition/Limitation*: The \( E = 0.01 \) threshold adopted here relies on the high precision of the apparatus (pressure ± 10 Pa, flow ± 0.001 mL/min). Coarser experimental setups may warrant a higher threshold (e.g., \( E = 0.1 \)).
6.  **The transition in DFNs proceeds through “linear regime,” “weak inertial regime,” and “strong inertial regime”** based on the \( Q/J \)–\( J \) relationship, not solely predicted by Forchheimer’s law [Liu 2016, pp. 9-11].  
    *Condition/Limitation*: The specific \( J \) boundaries (\( 10^{-6}, 10^{-4} \)) observed for the validation DFNs (mean \( h_M \) ~4 mm) will shift depending on geometric characteristics. The presence of an inflection point and plateau in \( Q/J \) agrees conceptually with the Barree–Conway model.

## Connections To Other Work
- The cubic law is widely adopted in DFN simulations often disregarding hydraulic gradient magnitude (e.g., works assuming \( J = 1 \), \( J = 0.1 \), or unknown constants) [Liu 2016, pp. 2-2], [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12].
- The critical Reynolds number concept for single fractures is well studied, with reported ranges from 0.001 to 100 depending on roughness and stress [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2].
- Nonlinear mixing and streamline routing behavior at fracture intersections have been reported for \( 1 < Re < 100 \) [34, 44, 60 in references], motivating this DFN-scale study [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2].
- The relationship \( Q/J \) vs. \( J \) showing a transition to a plateau and then decrease is “consistent with the model put forward by Barree and Conway [4]” [Liu 2016, pp. 9-11].
- The empirical estimation of JRC from \( Z_2 \) follows the equations of Myers (1962) and Tse and Cruden (1979) [Liu 2016, pp. 2-4].
- The reduction factor \( \lambda \) conceptually accounts for effects analogous to those discussed in fracture‑network permeability studies where shorter fractures with smaller apertures act as “necks” [17, 18 in references] [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12].

## Open Questions
- How should the presented 2D empirical equations (Eqs. 8–10) be upscaled or modified for 3D DFNs? The authors state they are “not directly applicable” [Liu 2016, pp. 7-8].
- What is the precise relationship between the hydraulic gradient \( J_c \) and the local Reynolds numbers within individual fractures of a large DFN? The study uses a global \( J \) criterion for convenience, but the underlying physics is tied to local \( Re \) [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2].
- How does the critical gradient change under applied normal and shear stresses, which affect both aperture and roughness? (Mentioned as important in single‑fracture literature but not examined here) [Liu 2016, pp. 1-2].
- What is the general transferability of the calibrated \( \lambda \) value (0.8) and the regression constants in Eqs. 8–10 to DFNs with other statistical distributions (e.g., different length–aperture scaling, different orientation distributions, variable intersection angles)?
- For very large DFNs (hundreds or thousands of fractures), can the NS equations be replaced by Forchheimer’s law at the single‑fracture level using the predicted coefficients, and what are the error bounds? The paper suggests this as a computationally feasible approach [Liu 2016, pp. 11-12], but a direct validation at such scales is not provided.
