# Seismic Response of the Fractured and Faulted Granite of Soultz-sous-Forêts (France) to 5 km Deep Massive Water Injections.

## One-line Summary
The seismic response to massive water injections at ~5 km depth in the Soultz-sous-Forêts granite differs markedly between wells, with GPK2 reactivating a dense 3D fracture network and GPK3 activating large pre-existing faults, leading to poor hydraulic performance and increased seismic risk.

## Research Question
How do highly fractured and faulted zones within the granitic basement of the Soultz-sous-Forêts Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) site mechanically react to hydraulic stimulations, and to what extent do the results align with the initial HDR concept of creating a dense 3D fracture network for fluid exchange? [Dorbath 2009, pp. 2-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study is located at the Soultz-sous-Forêts EGS site in the Upper Rhine Graben, France, approximately 5 km from its major western boundary fault. The granitic basement lies at a depth of 1.4 km below Permian to Cainozoic sediments. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 2-2] Data were collected during hydraulic stimulations of three deep wells: GPK2 (stimulated in 2000), GPK3 (stimulated in 2003), and GPK4 (stimulated in two stages in 2004 and 2005). The wells are drilled to approximately 5000 m True Vertical Depth (TVD). [Dorbath 2009, pp. 1-2] Seismic monitoring utilized both downhole (accelerometers in wells 4550, 4601, OPS4) and surface networks (temporary and permanent stations operated by EOST). [Dorbath 2009, pp. 3-4]

## Methods
Induced microseismicity was monitored using downhole and surface seismic networks. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 3-4] Hypocentre locations were calculated using the HYPOINVERSE algorithm with all available stations, including the downhole system. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 4-5] Magnitudes were calculated from coda duration, calibrated to match seismic moment magnitude (Mw) determined from waveform analysis of a broadband instrument. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 5-6] The frequency-magnitude distribution was analyzed using the Gutenberg-Richter relation to determine b-values. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 6-8] Hydraulic parameters (pressure, flow rate) were recorded during injections. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 5-6]

## Key Findings
- **GPK2 (2000):** Stimulation induced over 700 events with M>1.0, forming a dense, homogeneous cloud without apparent structure. The b-value was >1.2, and medium-size earthquakes (M<2) contributed >80% of the cumulative seismic moment. Injectivity increased by a factor of 20. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 1-2]
- **GPK3 (2003):** Stimulation induced about 250 events with M>1.0, but with a greater proportion of large events (up to M2.9). Hypocentres formed clear structures identified as large faults. The b-value was ~0.9, and large events (M>2.0) accounted for the greater part of the cumulative seismic moment. The already high injectivity remained unchanged. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 1-2]
- **GPK4 (2004/2005):** Stimulation produced even fewer induced events, making interpretation difficult. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 1-2]
- The differences between GPK2 and GPK3 are attributed to the presence of large faults cut by GPK3 or in its vicinity, which were reached by injected water. Once a seismic event occurs on a fault, a sequence of earthquakes is triggered, behaving largely independent of the injected flow rate. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 1-2]
- Evidence suggests that creeping could be a major, if not the main, source of deformation. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 1-2]

## Limitations
- Location uncertainties for seismic events are estimated at ~50-70 m horizontally and ~60-100 m vertically. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 4-5]
- The dataset for GPK4 stimulation is limited, making reliable statistics difficult. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 11-13]
- The study focuses on events with M>1.0 to image main structures; finer-scale analysis requires more sophisticated methods (e.g., 4-D tomography) which are out of scope. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 4-5]
- No UBI logs are available for the open-hole section of GPK2 below 3880 m due to a cave. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 2-3]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The 1-D velocity model derived from sonic logs and calibration shots is assumed accurate for hypocentre location. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 4-5]
- Seismicity is assumed to indicate reactivation of pre-existing fractures and faults. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 2-2]
- The relation D = 2b (fractal dimension D, b-value) is adopted from Aki (1981). [Dorbath 2009, pp. 13-15]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Magnitude (M):** Used to classify seismic events. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 4-5]
- **b-value:** Slope of the Gutenberg-Richter frequency-magnitude distribution. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 6-8]
- **Injectivity:** Measured in L s⁻¹ MPa⁻¹, indicating hydraulic performance. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 13-15]
- **Flow Rate:** Injection rate in L s⁻¹. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 5-6]
- **Wellhead Pressure:** Injection pressure in MPa. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 5-6]
- **Cumulative Seismic Moment:** Total seismic energy release in Nm. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 5-6]

## Reusable Claims
- In fractured granite without large faults, hydraulic stimulation can reactivate a dense 3D network of fractures, characterized by a high b-value (>1.2) and a dominance of medium-size earthquakes in the cumulative seismic moment. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 1-2, pp. 6-8]
- When stimulation intersects large pre-existing faults, seismicity is dominated by fewer, larger events on those structures, resulting in a lower b-value (~0.9) and poor enhancement of injectivity. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 1-2, pp. 11-11]
- Once large faults are activated by fluid injection, their seismic behavior can become largely independent of the injection flow rate and pressure. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 13-15]
- Aseismic slip (creeping) on fractures may be a significant, if not dominant, deformation mechanism during hydraulic stimulation, leading to well casing damage. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 13-15]
- For future EGS development, wells should be drilled in zones free of large faults to avoid poor hydraulic performance and seismic risk to the population. [Dorbath 2009, pp. 1-2]

- Compares b-values with those from other tectonic and volcanic regions (e.g., San Andreas Fault, Ethiopian Rift, Mount St. Helens). [Dorbath 2009, pp. 6-8]

## Open Questions
