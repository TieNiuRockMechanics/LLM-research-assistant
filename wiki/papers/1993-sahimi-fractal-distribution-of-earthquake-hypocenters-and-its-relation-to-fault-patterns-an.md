# Fractal Distribution of Earthquake Hypocenters and Its Relation to Fault Patterns and Percolation.

## One-line Summary
Percolation theory is proposed as a unified framework explaining the fractal geometry of fault patterns in rock and the spatial distribution of earthquake hypocenters.

## Research Question
How can a single theoretical framework explain both the observed fractal geometry of fault patterns in heterogeneous rock and the spatial distribution of earthquake hypocenters?

## Study Area / Data
The study analyzes fracture network data from the Geysers geothermal field in northeast California, Yucca Mountain in Nevada, and the Monterey Formation in Central California [Sahimi 1993, pp. 1-2]. Seismic data from four regions in Southern California were used: San Andreas-Elsinore (SA-EL), Parkfield, Whittier Upland, and an unnamed fourth region [Sahimi 1993, pp. 3-4].

## Methods
Fracture networks were mapped using the pavement method on rock outcrops [Sahimi 1993, pp. 1-2]. The fractal dimension was estimated by counting the minimum number of boxes or cubes needed to cover the data set at various length scales [Sahimi 1993, pp. 2-3]. A discrete model of fracture was employed using a triangular network of springs with elastic constants and failure thresholds drawn from broad distributions to simulate macroscopically heterogeneous rock [Sahimi 1993, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
At large length scales (order of a kilometer or more), fault patterns are percolation fractals with a fractal dimension D=1.9 in 2D and D=2.5 in 3D [Sahimi 1993, pp. 1-2]. At small length scales (several meters), the fractal dimension is about 1.6-1.7 [Sahimi 1993, pp. 1-2]. The 3D spatial distribution of earthquake hypocenters from four Southern California regions has a fractal dimension of about 1.8 [Sahimi 1993, pp. 2-3]. This value matches the fractal dimension of the backbone of 3D percolation clusters [Sahimi 1993, pp. 2-3]. A discrete spring network model for heterogeneous rock produces fracture networks with a fractal dimension of 1.85, supporting the percolation analogy [Sahimi 1993, pp. 2-3].

## Assumptions / Conditions
At large length scales, rock is macroscopically heterogeneous, and crack nucleation and growth occur at randomly selected regions, making the process analogous to random percolation [Sahimi 1993, pp. 1-2]. Earthquake hypocenters are distributed on the active backbone of the regional fault network [Sahimi 1993, pp. 2-3].

## Reusable Claims
- In macroscopically heterogeneous rock, the geometry of large-scale fault networks is a percolation fractal with dimension D=1.9 (2D) and D=2.5 (3D).
- The spatial distribution of earthquake hypocenters in 3D has a fractal dimension of approximately 1.8, which is identical to the fractal dimension of the backbone of a 3D percolation cluster.
- A discrete model with spatially varying elastic properties can generate fracture networks with fractal dimensions consistent with percolation theory.
