# An In-Depth Seismological Analysis Revealing a Causal Link between the 2017 MW 5.5 Pohang Earthquake and EGS Project.

## One-line Summary
The 2017 MW 5.5 Pohang earthquake was triggered by fluid injection from the Pohang Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) project, which reactivated a critically stressed fault zone, leading to a self-sustained rupture that released tectonically accumulated strain energy.

## Research Question
What is the relationship between the seismic activity near the Pohang EGS site and the hydraulic stimulation experiments conducted as part of the project, and can a causal link to the 2017 MW 5.5 Pohang earthquake be established? [Woo 2017, pp. 2-3]

## Study Area / Data
- **Geological Setting**: The study area is the Pohang basin in the southeastern part of the Cretaceous Gyeongsang basin, South Korea. The geology consists of Quaternary alluvium overlying Tertiary sandstone and mudstone, with basement rock of granitic rocks and gneisses. The region is characterized by NE- to NNE-trending normal faults and NW-trending transfer faults. [Woo 2017, pp. 2-3]
- **Seismic Data**: Data were used from three seismic networks: 1) Permanent networks (KMA, KIGAM, KHNP) with broadband, short-period, and accelerometer stations; 2) A temporary EGS project network with surface seismometers, borehole seismometers, a borehole geophone array in well PX-2, and vertical seismic profiles; 3) A temporary short-period network installed after the third stimulation. [Woo 2017, pp. 3-4]
- **Injection Data**: Detailed information on fluid injections from the EGS project was collected to correlate with seismicity. [Woo 2017, pp. 3-4]

## Methods
- **Velocity Model Construction**: Developed a local 1-D seismic velocity model for the EGS site based on geological strata from drilling cores and seismological observations. [Woo 2017, pp. 3-4]
- **Earthquake Detection**: Used a template-matching method on continuous waveforms from station PHA2 (2009 to mainshock) to detect small events. 39 events were used as templates, leading to an initial catalog of 520 earthquakes. [Woo 2017, pp. 4-6]
- **Hypocenter Determination**: A three-step process: 1) Initial locations using Hypoellipse; 2) Relative locations using the double-difference method (HypoDD); 3) Final locations by shifting relative locations based on the absolute location of a key event (August 13, 2017, MW 1.2) determined using a borehole array in PX-2. [Woo 2017, pp. 6-8]
- **Magnitude Determination**: Local magnitude (ML) and moment magnitude (MW) were determined. ML was estimated using station corrections and peak amplitudes. MW was computed in time and frequency domains using seismic moments. [Woo 2017, pp. 8-9]
- **Focal Mechanism Solutions**: Constrained from P-wave polarities using HASH software. 53 solutions were obtained and classified by fault type. Stress inversion was performed using Michael’s method. [Woo 2017, pp. 9-10]
- **Clustering Analysis**: Two hierarchical clustering analyses were applied: one using the Kagan angle between focal mechanisms, and another using waveform similarity. [Woo 2017, pp. 10-12]

## Key Findings
- No seismic activity was detected near the EGS site until November 2015, when a large quantity of heavy drilling mud was lost from well PX-2 at a depth of ~3,800 m. [Woo 2017, pp. 1-2]
- Earthquakes during PX-2 stimulations migrated sequentially to the southwest along a fault plane, leading to the location of the mainshock. [Woo 2017, pp. 1-2, 13-14]
- The delineated fault plane for PX-2-related events (G2) crossed the PX-2 well at approximately 3,800 m depth, corresponding to the interval of mud loss and the breakage of the well's casing due to the mainshock rupture. [Woo 2017, pp. 12-13]
- The fault plane for G2 events had a strike/dip of N214°/43°NW, similar to the mainshock focal mechanism (N214°/51°NW). [Woo 2017, pp. 10-12]
- The seismic moment of the MW 5.5 mainshock far exceeded the maximum expected moment based on scaling laws relating maximum magnitude to injected volume (Mmax-V). [Woo 2017, pp. 14-15]
- The seismic energy released by the mainshock was approximately 50 times larger than the cumulative hydraulic energy injected. [Woo 2017, pp. 14-15]
- The b-value of the frequency-magnitude distribution was ~0.66, lower than commonly observed in the region. [Woo 2017, pp. 8-9]

## Limitations
- The accuracy of final hypocenters depended on the absolute location of the key event, which had potential uncertainties from borehole sensor orientation. [Woo 2017, pp. 12-13]
- The 1-D velocity model did not account for local anisotropy. [Woo 2017, pp. 3-4]
- Magnitude determinations for some events relied on empirical relationships and peak amplitudes at a single station (PHA2), with estimated uncertainties of ±0.2 magnitude units. [Woo 2017, pp. 8-9]
- The template-matching method could not detect the mainshock due to large magnitude differences. [Woo 2017, pp. 4-6]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The fault zone reactivated by fluid injection was critically stressed prior to injection. [Woo 2017, pp. 14-15]
- The key event's location, determined from the PX-2 borehole array, is the most reliable and used to anchor all final locations. [Woo 2017, pp. 7-8]
- The G2 seismicity delineates a pre-existing fault plane that was reactivated. [Woo 2017, pp. 10-12]
- The MW 5.5 event was a self-sustained rupture where the majority of seismic energy was tectonically released. [Woo 2017, pp. 14-15]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Injection Volume**: Cumulative net injected volume for PX-1 and PX-2 wells. [Woo 2017, pp. 14-15]
- **Magnitude**: Local (ML) and moment (MW) magnitudes of induced and triggered events. [Woo 2017, pp. 8-9]
- **Stress Drop**: Estimated at 5.6 MPa for the mainshock. [Woo 2017, pp. 8-9]
- **b-value**: ~0.66 from frequency-magnitude distribution. [Woo 2017, pp. 8-9]
- **Seismogenic Index (Σ)**: Estimated at -1.69 for all events. [Woo 2017, pp. 8-9]
- **Fault Geometry**: Strike, dip, and rake of fault planes and focal mechanisms. [Woo 2017, pp. 9-10]

## Reusable Claims
- Fluid injection from an EGS project can reactivate a critically stressed fault zone, leading to a triggered earthquake whose magnitude significantly exceeds predictions from injection volume scaling laws. [Woo 2017, pp. 14-15]
- The spatiotemporal migration of seismicity along a fault plane during sequential hydraulic stimulations can indicate the location of a future large triggered event. [Woo 2017, pp. 13-14]
- Heavy mud loss during drilling into a fault zone can perturb the local stress field and induce microseismicity prior to formal stimulation. [Woo 2017, pp. 12-13]
- Large-magnitude induced/triggered earthquakes often occur during shut-in or bleed-off periods rather than during active fluid injection. [Woo 2017, pp. 13-14]
- The energy released by a large triggered earthquake can be orders of magnitude greater than the hydraulic energy input, requiring an additional tectonic energy source. [Woo 2017, pp. 14-15]
