# Study on Impact Grinding Law of Granite under the Conditions of High Temperature and High Pressure.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates the impact grinding laws of granite under high temperature (up to 500°C) and high confining pressure (100 MPa), finding that drilling speed generally increases with temperature but efficiency is limited above ~150°C due to plastic deformation, while specific energy consumption decreases significantly with increasing temperature.

## Research Question
The research aims to systematically study the impact fragmentation laws of granite under high-temperature and high-pressure states to provide a theoretical basis for the development of efficient drill bits and improved drilling methods for geothermal wells [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-3].

## Study Area / Data
The experiments were conducted using large cylindrical samples of Luhui granite (density 2.71 g/cm³) sourced from Pingyi, Shandong, China. The samples were processed to a diameter of 200 mm and a height of 400 mm [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-3].

## Methods
The study utilized the "20MN servo-controlled high-temperature and high-pressure rock triaxial testing machine" at China University of Mining and Technology. Experiments were performed on four granite samples under a constant confining pressure of 100 MPa (approximating a depth of 4000 m) at different temperature levels: room temperature (~20°C), 150°C, 300°C, and 500°C. For each sample, orthogonal tests were conducted with varying drilling weights (WOB) and impact frequencies (powers) to measure drilling speed and specific energy consumption. The impact piston mass was 40 kg with a 15 mm stroke, and the drill bit rotation speed was constant at 5 r/min [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-3].

## Key Findings
1.  Under high confining pressure (100 MPa), drilling speed generally increases with temperature. However, when the temperature exceeds approximately 150°C, the number of rock fractures increases, and the rock exhibits certain plastic deformation characteristics, which is not conducive to the full utilization of impact energy. Therefore, impact drilling is suitable for drilling hard rock layers at lower temperatures (not exceeding about 150°C) [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-3].
2.  Under high confining pressure, with fixed drilling parameters, the specific energy consumption of impact drilling decreases as temperature increases. In this experiment, the specific energy consumption at 500°C was about 50% lower than at 20°C [Zhao 2010, pp. 3-5].
3.  In a high-temperature and high-pressure environment, within a certain range of WOB and impact power, drilling speed increases with an increase in WOB or impact power. Specific energy consumption generally decreases with an increase in WOB [Zhao 2010, pp. 3-5].
4.  At 500°C, the drilling speed was approximately double that at room temperature under the tested conditions, attributed to a significant reduction in rock strength and the drill bit acting in a cutting mode under high WOB [Zhao 2010, pp. 3-5].

## Limitations
1.  The study notes that at 150°C, some series showed higher specific energy consumption than at 20°C, which could be due to sample inhomogeneity or increased rock shear strength and torque work at that temperature, requiring further experimental research [Zhao 2010, pp. 3-5].
2.  The analysis of energy consumption included a comparison between calculations with and without torque work, acknowledging that the values differ from theoretical ones but still reflect the trend with temperature [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-3].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The confining pressure was held constant at 100 MPa for all tests, simulating a depth of approximately 4000 meters [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-3].
2.  The drill bit rotation speed was constant at 5 r/min, and the impact piston mass (40 kg) and stroke (15 mm) were fixed throughout the experiments [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-3].
3.  The influence of the drill bit's rotation angle was not considered in this test, as it was assumed to have little effect on drilling speed within a certain range [Zhao 2010, pp. 1-3].
