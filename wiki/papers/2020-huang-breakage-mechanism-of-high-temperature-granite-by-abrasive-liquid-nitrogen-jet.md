# Breakage Mechanism of High-Temperature Granite by Abrasive Liquid Nitrogen Jet.

## One-line Summary
Abrasive liquid nitrogen jetting efficiently breaks high-temperature granite by combining thermal stress-induced damage, abrasive impact, and fluid wedge effects, producing larger and more irregular breakage volumes than abrasive water or nitrogen jets.

## Research Question
The study aims to further explore the rock-breaking mechanism of abrasive liquid nitrogen jets on high-temperature granite, analyzing macroscopic and microscopic breakage characteristics to understand the process for applications in hot dry rock development [Huang 2020, pp. 1-1].

## Study Area / Data
The experimental data comes from laboratory tests on granite samples, a common rock type in hot dry rock reservoirs. The samples were 100 mm × 100 mm × 100 mm cubes with a density of 2710 kg/m³, Young's modulus of 25.71 GPa, Poisson's ratio of 0.166, uniaxial tensile strength of 8.020 MPa, and uniaxial compressive strength of 112.3 MPa [Huang 2020, pp. 1-3]. Samples were heated to specified temperatures (e.g., 200°C, 300°C) using a muffle furnace with a staged heating rate of 10°C/h and held for 48 hours to ensure uniform heating [Huang 2020, pp. 1-3].

## Methods
The study employed a custom-designed abrasive liquid nitrogen jet experimental apparatus capable of operating at -196°C with a maximum pressure of 40 MPa [Huang 2020, pp. 1-3]. The system included a gas pressurization system and an abrasive mixing system using a pre-mixed method with a cone-straight nozzle (diameter 2.5 mm) [Huang 2020, pp. 1-3]. Key experimental parameters were a stand-off distance of 10 mm, jetting time of 1 minute, abrasive size of 40 mesh, and abrasive volume flow rate of 5 L/min [Huang 2020, pp. 1-3]. Control experiments were conducted with abrasive water jets and abrasive nitrogen jets under comparable conditions [Huang 2020, pp. 1-3]. Microscopic analysis was performed using a Quanta 200F field emission environmental scanning electron microscope (SEM) on cores extracted from near the breakage pits after the samples returned to room temperature [Huang 2020, pp. 3-6]. Numerical simulation using ANSYS software coupled the fluid calculation module (FLUENT) and structural mechanics module (TRANSIENT STRUCTURE) to model the thermal-hydraulic-mechanical coupling process [Huang 2020, pp. 6-9].

## Key Findings
1.  **Macroscopic Breakage:** Abrasive liquid nitrogen jets formed perforation cavities with irregular shapes, uneven surfaces, and visible cracks. The breakage volume was significantly larger than that from abrasive water jets and abrasive nitrogen jets [Huang 2020, pp. 1-1, pp. 3-6]. Under high pressure (30 MPa) or high rock temperature (300°C), the rock often underwent longitudinal splitting [Huang 2020, pp. 3-6].
2.  **Microscopic Breakage:** SEM revealed that the primary fracture mode was brittle fracture under tensile and shear actions, manifested as transgranular and intergranular fractures. Numerous micro-cracks were generated in the perforation wall and nearby areas due to low-temperature action and impact loads [Huang 2020, pp. 1-1, pp. 3-6].
3.  **Breakage Mechanism:** The process involves four coupled factors: (1) Low-temperature liquid nitrogen causes damage to the rock's physical properties (increased permeability, decreased strength). (2) Thermal stress from the large temperature difference induces and extends cracks. (3) High-speed abrasive particle impact loads cause crushing and generate stress waves. (4) Fluid wedge effect, where low-viscosity liquid nitrogen penetrates cracks, transmits pressure, and extends fractures [Huang 2020, pp. 6-9, pp. 9-10].
4.  **Comparative Performance:** Nitrogen abrasive jets produced the deepest perforations due to the highest particle exit velocity, while water abrasive jets produced the most regular, smooth cavities due to higher fluid viscosity constraining particles. Liquid nitrogen jets on hot rock produced the largest cavity volume due to combined thermal and impact effects [Huang 2020, pp. 3-6].

## Limitations
1.  The experiments used a specific type of granite; results may vary for other rock types.
2.  The nitrogen abrasive jet control experiment was limited to a lower pressure (13 MPa) due to gas cylinder constraints, which may not be a fully equivalent comparison [Huang 2020, pp. 1-3].
3.  The study focuses on laboratory-scale single-jet impact; scaling to field conditions (e.g., multiple jets, confining pressure, fluid flow) requires further investigation.
4.  The numerical model assumed the rock as a linear elastic body, which may not capture all nonlinear damage behaviors [Huang 2020, pp. 6-9].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The granite samples are representative of hot dry rock reservoirs.
2.  The staged heating method (10°C/h, held for 48h) does not significantly alter the granite's structure and strength, so observed damage is primarily from LN2 cooling and abrasive impact [Huang 2020, pp. 1-3].
3.  In the numerical model, the rock is treated as a linear elastic material with a large Young's modulus [Huang 2020, pp. 6-9].
4.  The local thermal non-equilibrium model is valid for the high local seepage velocity and large temperature difference between fluid and rock [Huang 2020, pp. 6-9].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For high-temperature granite (e.g., 200-300°C). **Claim:** Abrasive liquid nitrogen jetting produces significantly larger breakage volumes and more complex fracture networks than abrasive water jetting due to synergistic thermal stress and impact mechanisms. **Limitation:** Demonstrated in laboratory-scale single-jet experiments.
2.  **Condition:** During abrasive liquid nitrogen jet impact on hot rock. **Claim:** The primary microscopic fracture modes are transgranular and intergranular brittle fractures driven by tensile and shear stresses from thermal shock and particle impact. **Limitation:** Based on SEM analysis of specific granite samples.
3.  **Condition:** In the design of jetting for geothermal reservoir stimulation. **Claim:** Using abrasive liquid nitrogen jets to create perforations can generate extensive micro-cracks in the wall, which is expected to lower the fracture initiation pressure during subsequent high-pressure liquid nitrogen fracturing. **Limitation:** This is a proposed application based on experimental observations, not directly tested in the study.
