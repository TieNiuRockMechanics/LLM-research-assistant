# Heat-Flow Regimes and the Hydrate Stability Zone of a Transient, Thermogenic, Fault-Controlled Hydrate System (Woolsey Mound Northern Gulf of Mexico).

## One-line Summary
This study uses heat-flow measurements and a compositional simulator to model the dynamic hydrate stability zone at Woolsey Mound, finding that fault-controlled fluid migration and thermogenic gas composition significantly influence its depth and extent.

## Research Question
How do heat-flow regimes, fault activity, and thermogenic fluid migration influence the depth and dynamics of the base of the hydrate stability zone (BHSZ) at a complex, fault-controlled site like Woolsey Mound? [Macelloni 2015, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study focuses on Woolsey Mound, located in Mississippi Canyon Block 118 (MC118) on the northern Gulf of Mexico continental slope, at approximately 890 m water depth. The site is a one-kilometer-diameter carbonate/hydrate mound characterized by three main crater complexes (SE, SW, NW) associated with master faults (yellow, magenta, blue/red) that intersect the seafloor. The mound is underlain by a major salt body whose uppermost extent is within 400 m of the seafloor. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 2-2]
Data include: 1) A heat-flow survey conducted in March 2012 at 15 sites using a 5.5-m Lister-type heat-flow probe. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 2-5] 2) Seismic and acoustic data defining subsurface structures, fault locations, and the depth to the salt body. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 2-5] 3) Geochemical data from recovered gas hydrate and vent gas samples. A hydrate sample from 2002 contained ~70% CH4, 7.5% ethane, 15.9% propane, 4.4% i-butane, and 1.1% n-butane. A 2011 sample averaged 79% methane, 11% ethane, 9% propane, and 1% butane. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 5-5]

## Methods
1.  **Heat-Flow Survey:** In situ measurements were taken using a multi-sensor probe. Thermal conductivity was measured via a heat pulse, and the geothermal gradient was determined using Bullard plots (temperature vs. integrated thermal resistance). Corrections were applied for water column temperature gradients. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 8-10]
2.  **Compositional Hydrate Simulator:** A new simulator was developed to model hydrate formation/dissociation for multi-component (Structure II) hydrates. It uses mathematical fits to the CSMHYD program to compute equilibrium pressure and hydrate composition as functions of temperature, salinity, and free gas composition (parameterized by H and G). The BHSZ depth was computed iteratively at each site by finding where liquid pressure equals equilibrium pressure. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 5-5, 10-11]
3.  **BHSZ Computation:** Six cases were modeled, varying gas composition (pure methane; two thermogenic mixtures) and salinity profile (uniform seawater salinity; linear gradient from seawater at seafloor to saturation at salt depth). [Macelloni 2015, pp. 6-7]

## Key Findings
1.  **Anomalous Heat-Flow:** Measured heat-flow within the mound area (43.8 to 80.1 mW/m², avg. 63.5 mW/m²) is significantly higher than in surrounding areas (27.7 to 35.8 mW/m²), indicating a thermal anomaly of 26-39 mW/m². [Macelloni 2015, pp. 5-6]
2.  **Fault-Controlled Heat-Flow:** Heat-flow is highest directly at the seafloor expression of master faults and decreases on the flanks. The blue fault (NW complex) has the highest average heat-flow (75 mW/m²), followed by the magenta (SW, 64.5 mW/m²) and yellow (SE, 50 mW/m²) faults. This pattern is attributed to focused advective fluid flow along faults. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 5-6, 6-7]
3.  **Dynamic BHSZ:** The base of the hydrate stability zone is not a simple boundary but a dynamic zone where hydrate, free gas, and dissolved gas can coexist. Its depth varies laterally and is controlled by fault activity. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 7-8]
4.  **Impact of Gas Composition:** The BHSZ depth is highly sensitive to gas composition. Models using pure methane (Structure I) yield a shallower BHSZ inconsistent with geological evidence. Models using the measured thermogenic gas composition (Structure II) produce a thicker HSZ, which best fits the observed seismic and geological constraints, particularly at the SE complex. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 7-8]
5.  **Salinity Effect:** High salinity from the underlying salt body inhibits hydrate formation, shoaling the BHSZ. However, the presence of heavier hydrocarbons (ethane, propane) counteracts this effect, extending the HSZ downward. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 7-8]
6.  **Temporal Variability:** Differences in heat-flow and venting activity among faults suggest that hydrate formation and dissociation vary temporally, potentially temporarily sealing conduits. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 8-8]

## Limitations
1.  The assumption of a linear salinity gradient from seawater at the seafloor to saturation at the salt body is a simplification. Transport of heat and salinity likely occurs primarily along faults in a time-variant manner, requiring a nonlinear model. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 6-7]
2.  The study lacks dense, near-seafloor heat-flow transects to fully understand thermal diffusivity mechanisms around seeps. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 6-7]
3.  The compositional simulator is limited to a three-component hydrocarbon system (CH4, C2H6, C3H8). [Macelloni 2015, pp. 5-5]
4.  The analysis is based on a limited number of hydrate and gas samples. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 5-5]

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  The geothermal gradient is steady-state for the purpose of BHSZ calculation. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 10-11]
2.  Pore water salinity increases linearly with depth from seawater salinity (0.035) at the seafloor to saturated salinity at the depth of the salt body. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 6-7]
3.  The hydrate system is in thermodynamic equilibrium at the computed BHSZ depth. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 10-11]
4.  The measured heat-flow values are representative of the conductive component, though advective flow is acknowledged as the primary modifier. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 6-7]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Heat Flow (Q):** Measured in mW/m², ranges from 27.7 to 80.1. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 5-6]
*   **Geothermal Gradient (dT/dz):** Derived from heat-flow and thermal conductivity, ranges from ~37.65 to 76.82 °C/km. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 10-11]
*   **Salinity (S):** Assumed to vary linearly from 0.035 at seafloor to saturated (Sm) at salt depth. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 6-7]
*   **Gas Composition:** Parameterized by H (proportion of non-methane gas) and G (proportion of propane in non-methane gas). [Macelloni 2015, pp. 5-5]
*   **Temperature (T), Pressure (P):** Primary state variables for hydrate stability. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 10-11]
*   **Depth to Salt Body (D):** Derived from seismic data, varies by site. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 10-11]

## Reusable Claims
1.  In fault-controlled hydrate systems, heat-flow measurements can identify active fluid conduits, with peak values directly over fault traces. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 5-6, 6-7]
2.  The presence of thermogenic gases heavier than methane (ethane, propane) promotes the formation of Structure II hydrates, which expands the vertical extent of the hydrate stability zone and mitigates the inhibiting effect of high salinity from nearby salt bodies. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 7-8]
3.  The base of the hydrate stability zone in complex, fault-controlled settings is not a single, static boundary but a dynamic zone that can wax and wane, particularly along active conduits where it may shoal close to the seafloor. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 7-8]
4.  Gas hydrate formation and dissociation can vary temporally along faults, potentially acting as a temporary seal that modulates the migration of thermogenic fluids from depth. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 8-8]

## Connections To Other Work
*   The study builds on previous work at Woolsey Mound by the Gulf of Mexico Hydrates Research Consortium (GOMHRC) since 2005. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 2-2]
*   It addresses the lack of clear BSRs in the Gulf of Mexico by using direct heat-flow measurements, contrasting with BSR-derived heat-flow models used elsewhere (e.g., Black Sea, Svalbard, Hydrate Ridge). [Macelloni 2015, pp. 2-2]
*   The findings on salt inhibition of hydrates align with work by Ruppel et al. (2005) and Taylor et al. (2000). [Macelloni 2015, pp. 2-2, 6-7]
*   The concept of a dynamic, multi-phase BHSZ is supported by the modeling of Liu and Fleming (2007). [Macelloni 2015, pp. 2-2]
*   The hypothesis of fault sealing by hydrates is discussed in relation to time-lapse seismic observations (Macelloni et al., 2012) and earlier concepts (e.g., Wood et al., 2002). [Macelloni 2015, pp. 8-8]

## Open Questions
1.  What is the precise, time-variant salinity distribution along and away from the fault conduits? [Macelloni 2015, pp. 6-7]
2.  How do the dynamics of hydrate formation/dissociation and fault sealing operate over human-relevant timescales? Permanent monitoring is needed. [Macelloni 2015, pp. 8-8]
3.  What is the full spatial extent and connectivity of the fracture porosity hosting hydrates, as indicated by the high-frequency scattering (HFS) seismic anomaly? [Macelloni 2015, pp. 7-8]
4.  How does the process of "fractional crystallization" of hydrates from rising multi-component fluids operate in detail? [Macelloni 2015, pp. 7-8]
