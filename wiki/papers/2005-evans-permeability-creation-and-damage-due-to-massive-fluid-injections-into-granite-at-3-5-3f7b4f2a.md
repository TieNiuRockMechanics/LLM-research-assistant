# Permeability Creation and Damage Due to Massive Fluid Injections into Granite at 3.5 km at Soultz: 2. Critical Stress and Fracture Strength.

## One-line Summary
This study analyzes stress conditions on fractures in a 3.5 km deep granite borehole at Soultz to evaluate whether permeable fractures are critically stressed and to constrain fracture strength, finding that critical stressing is necessary but not sufficient for permeability, with impermeable fractures requiring significant cohesion to remain stable during massive fluid injections.

## Research Question
The paper evaluates whether the prestimulation and poststimulation sets of permeable fractures are more favorably oriented for failure than nonpermeable fractures, and places constraints on fracture strength to understand the conditions for permeability development [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].

## Study Area / Data
The study is located at the Soultz-sous-Forêts Hot Dry Rock (HDR) project site in the Rhinegraben, France. Data come from borehole GPK1, which is 3.6 km deep with an open hole section below 2850 m in granite. The analysis uses two datasets of approximately 500 natural fractures imaged on a Schlumberger Ultrasonic Borehole Imager (UBI) log: one for fractures in their natural state (18 permeable) and another after two major injections that increased permeable fractures to over 95 [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].

## Methods
The analysis resolves shear and effective normal stress components acting across fracture populations and plots the results on Mohr circles. Stresses are normalized by vertical stress. The study considers the standard stress state, varies stress parameters (SHmax orientation, SHmax magnitude, Shmin magnitude), and evaluates poroelastic stress effects during injections using an oblate ellipsoidal model [Evans 2005, pp. 5-9].

## Key Findings
1.  Permeable fractures are critically stressed, supporting shear stress levels verging on failure under a Coulomb friction law with a coefficient of 0.6–1.0 [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].
2.  The vast majority of impermeable fractures are also critically stressed, indicating that critical stressing is a necessary but not sufficient condition for permeability [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].
3.  To prevent failure of impermeable fractures during injections requires a cohesion of at least 6.5 MPa to augment a maximum allowable friction coefficient of 1.0 [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].
4.  Permeable fractures largely occur in hydrothermally altered cataclastic shear zones and contain illite, suggesting they may be relatively weak [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].
5.  The strength of the shear zones appears significantly greater than the illite-rich fractures composing them, imposing constraints on their internal architecture [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].
6.  An upper bound on the large-scale poroelastic stress induced during reservoir pressurization is DS1 = DS2 = 3.6 MPa and DS3 = 2.7 MPa [Evans 2005, pp. 12-13].

## Limitations
1.  Knowledge of the stress state is limited and debated, particularly the SHmax magnitude profile [Evans 2005, pp. 3-4].
2.  The alteration profile derived from cuttings has limited resolution, prohibiting fracture-scale correlation [Evans 2005, pp. 9-12].
3.  The poroelastic stress calculation assumes a uniform pressure increase within an idealized ellipsoidal volume, which is an approximation [Evans 2005, pp. 9-9].
4.  The analysis cannot evaluate the hypothesis on a fracture-by-fracture basis without core [Evans 2005, pp. 9-12].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  One principal stress is vertical [Evans 2005, pp. 3-4].
2.  The standard stress state (linear trends for SHmax, Shmin, SV, Pp) is used as a starting point, with SHmax orientation from drilling-induced tension fractures (N170°E) considered definitive [Evans 2005, pp. 3-4].
3.  The condition SHmax = SV is considered a better working hypothesis for the SHmax profile than the standard stress state [Evans 2005, pp. 4-5].
4.  Fracture strength is evaluated using a Coulomb friction criterion with coefficients between 0.6 and 1.0 [Evans 2005, pp. 5-6].
5.  The effective stress law for shear failure uses pore pressure equal to ambient formation pressure, modified by a 9 MPa overpressure during injections [Evans 2005, pp. 6-7].
6.  The upper bound on poroelastic stress assumes the 9 MPa overpressure extends to the limits of the microseismic cloud [Evans 2005, pp. 9-9].

## Reusable Claims
1.  In the Soultz granite reservoir, critical stressing (shear stress near frictional failure) is a necessary but not sufficient condition for fracture permeability to develop [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].
2.  To remain stable during massive fluid injections with ~9 MPa overpressure, impermeable fractures in the Soultz granite require a cohesive strength component of at least 6.5 MPa, supplementing a maximum friction coefficient of 1.0 [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].
3.  The strength of large-scale cataclastic shear zones in granite can exceed the frictional strength of their constituent illite-rich fractures due to load transfer across intact rock bridges and jogs [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2].
4.  The maximum permissible value of SHmax in the Soultz basement, consistent with a crustal strength limited by a Coulomb friction law (μ=1.0), is 1.21 times the vertical stress [Evans 2005, pp. 12-13].

## Connections To Other Work
This work builds on the concept of critically stressed fractures introduced by Barton et al. (1995) and extends it to a deep granite reservoir subject to massive fluid injections. It references companion paper Evans et al. (2005a) for borehole observations and permeability data. The analysis of poroelastic stress uses methods from Segall and Fitzgerald (1998). The discussion of shear zone architecture references numerical modeling by Zhang and Sanderson (2001) [Evans 2005, pp. 1-2, 9-9, 12-13].

## Open Questions
1.  What is the precise internal architecture of the cataclastic shear zones that allows them to be strong despite containing weak, illite-rich fractures?
2.  How does the distribution and coverage of illite on fracture surfaces affect the bulk frictional strength of individual fractures?
3.  What is the exact role of cohesion in the strength of the calcite-filled, mode 1 fractures?
