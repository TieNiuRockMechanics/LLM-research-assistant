# Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS): A Review.

## One-line Summary
This review paper examines the state-of-the-art, technology, and research of Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS), covering plant development, optimisation techniques using dense fluids and CO₂ as a working fluid, the seismic response to fluid injection, and software packages for cost estimation. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 1-2]

## Research Question
The main objective is to study the state of art, technology and research of EGS systems, analysing different aspects such as development of a geothermal plant, different optimisation techniques, seismic response to fluid injection, as well as different software packages to estimate costs. Additionally, it is intended to collect in one document a large part of the existing literature in recent years. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
*   The review synthesises findings from multiple global EGS sites and projects.
*   Historical context is provided by the first HDR project at **Los Alamos Scientific Laboratory (LASL), USA**, which began in the 1970s and achieved a thermal capacity of around 10 MW by 1986. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 1-1]
*   The European benchmark is the **Soultz-sous-Forêts** project in the upper Rhine Valley, France, started in 1987. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 1-2]
*   The first privately-run commercial EGS plant for large-scale electricity production is the Geodynamics plant in **Habanero, Australia**, which came online on 2 May 2013. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 1-1]
*   Seismic data from various EGS stimulations is analysed, including sites at Soultz-sous-Forêts (France), **Basel (Switzerland)**, Icelandic sites, Latera (Italy), Gross Schönebeck (Germany), Berlin (El Salvador), The Geysers (USA), Cooper Basin and Paralana (Australia), and Bouillante (Guadeloupe). [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-8]
*   Thermal gradient data points are noted for the western USA (minimum 250°C at 5 km depth), southern Australia (temperatures of 273°C and 283°C measured), and the Soultz-sous-Forêts and Landau regions (up to 100°C/km in the first 1000 meters). [Olasolo 2016, pp. 2-3, 3-4]

## Methods
The paper is a review that synthesises literature on various EGS technologies, using a mix of historical project data, simulation studies, and field experiment results.
*   **Plant Development**: Describes a sequential process of finding a site, creating the reservoir via hydraulic stimulation, and operating the reservoir. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 4-4, 4-5]
*   **Performance Optimisation**: Reviews two specific techniques: (1) using dense fluids for hydraulic stimulation [Olasolo 2016, pp. 5-6], and (2) using CO₂ as a working fluid [Olasolo 2016, pp. 5-6]. Simulation studies for CO₂-EGS use two- and three-dimensional models to examine heat extraction and mass flow ratios. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 6-7, 7-8]
*   **Seismic Response**: Analyses the relationship between injected fluid volume and the maximum magnitude of induced seismicity, and discusses the influence of seismic network configuration on event detection. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-8, 8-9]
*   **Cost Software**: Describes the capabilities and evolution of the EURONAUT and GEOPHIRES software packages. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 9-9, 9-10]

## Key Findings
*   **EGS Plant Operation**: The key to financial viability in EGS technology lies in being able to produce energy over long periods with no need to make expensive repairs (such as the unplanned drilling of additional wells). [Olasolo 2016, pp. 10-10]
*   **Hydraulic Stimulation with Dense Fluids**: Using dense fluids makes it possible to (1) increase and improve underground circulation pathways; (2) reduce the permeability of those pathways; and (3) improve the mass flow ratio and the heat extraction ratio. It also helps to prevent simultaneous unwanted fractures in areas closer to the surface and minimises the risk of micro-seismic events. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 10-10]
*   **CO₂ as a Working Fluid**:
    *   At low pressures, CO₂ is inefficient and is not considered a viable alternative to water. However, it is much more efficient at medium and high pressures. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 6-7, 10-10]
    *   The heat extraction ratio for CO₂ is calculated to be approximately 50% higher than for water, and it is more efficient at low temperatures. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 6-6, 10-10]
    *   In plants operated with water, the dissolution of minerals and strong chemical reactions can cause short circulation and pathway clogging. CO₂ causes no significant dissolution/precipitation problems. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 6-6]
    *   If both injection and production wells are open over the whole depth of the reservoir, mass flow ratios increase substantially with depth, causing faster temperature decrease in deeper layers. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 7-8]
    *   If the production well is open only at its upper levels, an increase is forced in the mass flow ratio in those upper levels, which can maintain a constant heat extraction ratio over the first 25 years of the plant's lifetime. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-8, 10-10]
    *   In simulation, there is minimal sensitivity to the scale of discretisation (coarse vs. fine), so a "reasonable" grid size is recommended for fractured reservoir models. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 6-7]
*   **Seismic Response to Injection**: A clear relationship exists between fluid injection and seismic response. The maximum observed magnitude of seismic events increases with increasing volume of injected fluid. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-8, 10-10]
    *   Today's EGS reservoirs, developed between 2 and 5 km depth, do not exhibit a relationship between the number of recorded events or the maximum magnitude and reservoir depth, though depth must be considered in relation to injection pressure and crustal stress state. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 9-9, 10-11]
    *   Cyclic treatment, where injection is interrupted frequently, is suggested as a safer alternative to conventional injection to reduce induced seismicity. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-8]
    *   Deep borehole seismic sensors can detect events at lower magnitude thresholds than surface networks; for example, in Basel, a borehole network detected events down to Mw 0.1 compared to Mw 0.9 for surface stations. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-9]
*   **Cost Software**:
    *   GEOPHIRES and EURONAUT are considered the most suitable software packages for estimating and simulating costs. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 10-11]
    *   EURONAUT (European) is a modular program based on studies at Soultz-sous-Forêts, focused on flexibility and the ability to assimilate and grow. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 9-9]
    *   GEOPHIRES (American) is the newest package and has the unique feature of being capable of simulating not only electricity generation but also direct-use-heat and Combined Heat and Power (CHP). [Olasolo 2016, pp. 10-10]

## Limitations
*   **Plant Operation Experience**: EGS systems are so new that operational experience is measured in months rather than years in many cases. This creates uncertainty regarding the specific technology requirements for the operational stage. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 4-5]
*   **Site Definition**: A lack of experience in developing EGS plants creates a problem in determining what constitutes a "suitable" site. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 4-4]
*   **Modelling of CO₂-EGS**: The two-dimensional model ignores the effects of vertical flow and any potential dependency of horizontal flow on depth, which can be significant for CO₂ due to its large density changes. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 6-7]
*   **Seismic Monitoring**: Economic reasons often prevent extensive down-hole monitoring systems. The completeness and accuracy of event locations are primarily influenced by the quality and configuration of the seismic network, velocity model quality, and applied algorithms. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-9]
*   **Cost Models**: The predecessor to EURONAUT, the HDRec model, was written in an obsolete programming language (Fortran 77), making it increasingly difficult to incorporate changes and improvements. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 9-9]

## Assumptions / Conditions
*   **Hydraulic Stimulation**: Controlling the density of the working fluid, e.g., by adding NaCl, is assumed to be the best way of controlling the working pressure in the area to be fractured. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 5-6]
*   **CO₂ Performance**: For subcritical pressures, CO₂ is inefficient and is not a viable alternative to water. It is much more efficient at medium and high pressures. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 6-7]
*   **Reservoir Depth**: A primary assumption for EGS is finding a suitable location where the rock temperature at depths of 4–5 km is as high as possible. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 4-4]
*   **Economic Modelling**: EURONAUT is implemented on the basis of studies conducted at the EGS plant in Soultz-sous-Forêts, and its economic estimation is rooted in calculating via discontinuous cash flows. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 9-9]

## Key Variables / Parameters
*   **Fluid Density**: Adjusted using solid solvents (e.g., NaCl) to control hydraulic stimulation pressure. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 5-6]
*   **Working Fluid Type**: (Water vs. CO₂). Switching to CO₂ impacts heat extraction rates, chemical reactivity (no ionic dissolution), and seismic/flow behaviour. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 5-6, 6-6, 6-7]
*   **Injected Fluid Volume**: Directly related to the maximum observed magnitude of induced seismicity. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-8]
*   **Well Configuration**: Opening a production well only at the upper levels versus the whole depth changes mass flow ratio distribution and prevents early cooling of the reservoir. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 7-8, 8-8]
*   **Thermal Gradient**: Key site suitability variable; mean gradient is 25-30°C/km, but EGS sites have gradients of 40°C/km up to 150°C/km. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 2-3, 3-4]

## Reusable Claims
1.  Using dense fluids for hydraulic stimulation in EGS increases the downhole pressure gradient, enabling targeted fracturing at depth and reducing the risk of unwanted shallower fractures. The claim is supported by field tests at Soultz-sous-Forêts where brine injection increased the pressure gradient by 0.6-0.7 MPa/400m compared to water. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 6-6]
2.  CO₂ as a working fluid in EGS can provide a heat extraction ratio approximately 50% higher than water and eliminates problems of mineral dissolution/precipitation, but is only efficient at medium to high (supercritical) pressures. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 6-6, 10-10]
3.  The maximum observed magnitude of induced seismicity at EGS sites increases with the increasing volume of injected fluid. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-8]
4.  In CO₂-EGS, opening a production well only in the uppermost part of the reservoir can maintain a constant heat extraction ratio for the first 25 years of plant life by preventing early thermal breakthrough from deeper, faster-flowing layers. This is based on numerical simulations by Pruess. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 7-8, 8-8, 10-10]
5.  Cyclic hydraulic stimulation, where injection is interrupted frequently, is a safer alternative to conventional step-wise injection as it can reduce induced seismicity. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-8]
6.  Multi-well systems provide substantially larger heat exchange surface area and operational flexibility compared to doublet systems, leading to better exploitation of stored heat and a longer lifetime for the geothermal reservoir. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 9-10]

## Connections To Other Work
*   **Original HDR Model**: The EURONAUT and GEOPHIRES software packages trace their lineage back to the HDR economic model developed from work at the Los Alamos National Laboratory (Fenton Hill HDR Project) and the MIT-HDR model. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 9-9]
*   **MIT's "The Future of Geothermal Energy" (2006)**: A foundational report that provides a list of steps for setting up an EGS plant and predicts a dramatic increase in energy production from EGS once commercial operations are established. The GEOPHIRES software is a direct descendent of the models used in this report. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 3-4, 10-10]
*   **GEISER Project**: The analysis of seismic response in this review draws heavily on work done within the framework of the European Union project GEISER, which investigated various sites to understand and mitigate induced seismicity. [Olasolo 2016, pp. 8-8]

## Open Questions
*   What are the optimal operational techniques for an EGS plant (e.g., Doublet vs. Multi-well systems, specific electricity conversion cycles) given the paucity of long-term operational experience? [Olasolo 2016, pp. 4-5]
*   What is the specific technology requirement for the development stage of EGS reservoirs, given that operational experience is "measured in months rather than years"? [Olasolo 2016, pp. 4-5]
*   How can the EGS industry quantify future energy balances, given that prior to the 2013 Habanero plant startup there were only research plants, making hypothesis-making difficult? [Olasolo 2016, pp. 2-3]
*   Can ambient seismic noise analysis be reliably and consistently used to characterise EGS reservoirs in the absence of induced seismicity? [Olasolo 2016, pp. 9-9]
