# The Effect of Rapid Cooling on the Thermal Diffusivity of Granite.

## One-line Summary
Rapid cooling (water quenching) of hornblende granite and alkaline granite after cyclic heating significantly reduces thermal diffusivity, with the largest decrease occurring above 400 °C and after 5 – 15 cycles, driven by increased inter- and intragranular cracking and porosity.

## Research Question
How do temperature level and number of rapid cooling (water‑quenching) cycles affect the thermal diffusivity of hornblende granite and alkaline granite, and what micro‑structural mechanisms control the observed changes?

## Study Area / Data
- **Hornblende granite**: sourced from Shandong, China; average density 2.76 g/cm³ at room temperature; cylindrical specimens ~50 × 100 mm.
- **Alkaline granite**: sourced from Xinjiang, China; density 2.73 g/cm³; cuboid specimens 100 × 50 × 20 mm.
- Mineral compositions are given in Table 1.
- Temperature targets for hornblende granite: 250 °C, 350 °C, 450 °C, 550 °C, 650 °C; for alkaline granite: 250 °C, 350 °C, 450 °C.
- Quenching cycles for hornblende granite: 1, 5, 10, 15, 20 times; for alkaline granite: 1, 5, 10, 15 times.
- Cooling medium: water at 20 °C.

## Methods
- Heating protocol: heating rate 10 °C/min, held at target temperature for ~1 h, then immediate quenching in water (20 °C) and natural drying.
- Thermal diffusivity and thermal conductivity measured at room temperature using a Hot Disk TPS‑500 (Sweden); measurement ranges: 0.02–40 mm²/s and 0.03–100 W/(m·K), test time ~2 min.
- Comparison with literature data on intercrystalline crack width (Etienne and Poupert, 1989), intragranular and grain‑boundary microcrack width and porosity (Nasseri et al., 2007), and thermal diffusivity of quartz and feldspar (Höfer and Schilling, 2002; Kanamori et al., 1968).

## Key Findings
1. Thermal diffusivity of both granites decreases with increasing temperature at a given cycle number, with an abrupt drop after about 400 °C.
2. At constant temperature, thermal diffusivity decreases with cycle number. For hornblende granite, the change is most pronounced between 1 and 5 cycles; for alkaline granite, between 5 and 15 cycles.
3. The observed reductions are linked to increases in intercrystalline, intragranular, and grain‑boundary crack widths and total porosity, which accelerate above 400–450 °C.
4. The thermal diffusivity behavior of the bulk granite is strongly influenced by quartz, whose thermal diffusivity drops steeply with heating until the α–β quartz transition (~573 °C), where a slight increase occurs. Feldspar thermal diffusivity increases only slightly, so its influence is likely minor.
5. A positive linear correlation exists between thermal diffusivity (k, 10⁻⁶ m²/s) and thermal conductivity (λ, W/(m·K)):
   - Hornblende granite: λ = 0.168 + 0.896 k, R² = 0.863
   - Alkaline granite: λ = 1.346 k – 0.435, R² = 0.745
6. The trend of increasing thermal diffusivity with thermal conductivity is consistent with data from seafloor sediments (Tanikawa et al., 2016), though absolute values differ due to mineralogical and pore‑structure differences.

## Limitations
- All thermal‑property measurements were made at room temperature after cyclic treatment; high‑temperature in‑situ diffusivity was not directly measured.
- Only two granite types (hornblende and alkaline) were tested; generalisation to other granitoids requires caution.
- Pore structure and crack evolution were inferred from literature data on other granites (Westerly, Remiremont, Senones), not measured directly on the tested samples.
- The number of cycles was limited to 20 for hornblende granite and 15 for alkaline granite; longer‑term cyclic effects beyond these counts remain unexamined.
- Phase transformation effects (e.g., quartz α–β) were discussed based on referenced studies, but no mineralogical analysis was performed post‑treatment on the samples.

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Heating rate: 10 °C/min.
- Soaking time: 1 hour at target temperature.
- Cooling: immediate immersion in water at 20 °C; specimens air‑dried naturally.
- Thermal diffusivity measurements performed at room temperature using Hot Disk TPS‑500.
- Linear regression fits assume a simple linear relationship between thermal conductivity and thermal diffusivity across the tested temperature and cycle ranges.
- Comparisons with literature data assume comparable crack‑evolution behaviour between the tested granites and the reference granites (e.g., Westerly, Remiremont).

- **Quartz α–β phase transition temperature** (~573 °C).

## Reusable Claims
- Rapid water‑quenching from high temperatures, even for a single cycle, reduces the thermal diffusivity of granite, with the reduction becoming more pronounced as the peak temperature exceeds 400 °C. [Sun 2019, Figs. 3, 4, 5; Tables 2, 3].
- Repeated thermal cycling amplifies the diffusivity decline, but the sensitivity varies with rock type: hornblende granite shows the greatest change between 1 and 5 cycles, while alkaline granite shows it between 5 and 15 cycles. [Sun 2019, Fig. 3, Tables 2, 3].
- The loss of thermal diffusivity is primarily driven by the creation and widening of intercrystalline, intragranular, and grain‑boundary microcracks, and by a concomitant increase in porosity, processes that escalate above ~400 – 450 °C. [Sun 2019, Figs. 4–6; discussion Sec. 4.1].
- Among the major rock‑forming minerals, quartz exerts a dominant control on the thermal diffusivity of granite during heating: its diffusivity drops sharply with temperature and undergoes a small recovery at the α–β quartz transition, whereas feldspar diffusivity changes only slightly. [Sun 2019, Fig. 7; discussion Sec. 4.2].
- Thermal conductivity and thermal diffusivity of quenched granites are linearly correlated, allowing one to be estimated from the other with moderate to high R² (0.745–0.863). [Sun 2019, Eqs. (1), (2); Figs. 8, 9].
- The empirical linear relationships between thermal conductivity and diffusivity are specific to the granite types studied and cannot be directly transferred to other lithologies without verification. [Sun 2019, discussion Sec. 4.3].

## Open Questions
- How do the thermal diffusivity changes observed at room temperature after quenching relate to the in‑situ thermal properties of rocks at high temperature in a geothermal reservoir?
- What are the precise crack aperture and connectivity changes in the tested granites after different cycle numbers, and how do they quantitatively map to the measured diffusivity?
- Can the empirical λ–k relationships be extended to other granite types and other thermal–mechanical loading paths (e.g., slow cooling, intermediate cooling rates)?
- What is the role of mineral‑specific thermal expansion anisotropy and grain‑scale thermal stresses in determining the cycle‑sensitive deterioration of thermal diffusivity?
- How does the presence of pore fluids (e.g., in a fully saturated geothermal reservoir) alter the crack development and thermal transport during repeated quenching?
- What is the long‑term evolution of thermal diffusivity beyond 20 cycles, and does it approach an asymptotic value?
