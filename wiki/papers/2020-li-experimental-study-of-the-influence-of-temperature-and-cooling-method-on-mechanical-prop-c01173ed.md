# Experimental Study of the Influence of Temperature and Cooling Method on Mechanical Properties of Granite: Implication for Geothermal Mining.

## One-line Summary
This study experimentally investigates how heating temperature (20-600°C) and subsequent cooling method (natural vs. water cooling) affect the mechanical properties and microstructure of granite, finding that both factors significantly degrade rock strength and integrity, with water cooling causing more severe damage.

## Research Question
How do different heating temperatures and cooling methods (natural cooling vs. rapid water cooling) influence the macroscopic mechanical properties, microscopic structure, and physical properties (e.g., P-wave velocity) of granite, and what are the implications for the stability of artificial reservoirs in geothermal mining? [Li 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study uses granite samples. The mineral composition is: Quartz (40.6%), Potassium feldspar (16.0%), Plagioclase (23%), Mica (5.5%), Other minerals (8.0%). [Li 2020, pp. 2-3] Samples were cylindrical with a diameter of 50 mm and length of 100 mm (aspect ratio of 2), prepared according to ISRM suggested methods. [Li 2020, pp. 2-3] A total of 52 test specimens were used, divided into seven groups heated to different temperatures (20, 100, 200, 300, 400, 500, and 600°C). [Li 2020, pp. 2-3]

## Methods
- **Heat Treatment:** Samples were heated in a smart muffle furnace at a rate of 4°C/min to target temperatures (100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600°C) and held for 8 hours. [Li 2020, pp. 2-3]
- **Cooling Methods:**
    - **Natural Cooling:** Samples were left in the furnace to cool to room temperature (20°C). [Li 2020, pp. 2-3]
    - **Water Cooling:** After heating, samples were immediately removed from the furnace and immersed in room-temperature water until cooled. [Li 2020, pp. 3-4]
- **Testing:**
    - **Non-destructive Testing:** An NM-4B non-metal ultrasonic detector measured P-wave velocity before and after treatment. [Li 2020, pp. 2-3]
    - **Uniaxial Compression Tests:** Conducted on a rock testing machine with a displacement control loading rate of 0.02 mm/min. [Li 2020, pp. 3-4]
    - **Microscopic Analysis:** Cross-sectional morphology of fractured samples was analyzed using a rock microscope (SMART-POL). [Li 2020, pp. 3-4]

## Key Findings
1.  **Physical Properties:** P-wave velocity, elastic modulus, and uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) decrease with increasing heating temperature under both cooling modes. The reductions are greater under water cooling than natural cooling. [Li 2020, pp. 11-12]
2.  **Stress-Strain Behavior:** The stress-strain curve characteristics differ with temperature and cooling method. A double-peak phenomenon near the peak stress occurs in the 200-300°C range for natural cooling and above 300°C for water cooling. [Li 2020, pp. 11-12]
3.  **Mechanical Properties:**
    - **Elastic Modulus:** Under natural cooling, it increases from 20°C to 100°C (7.21 to 8.31 GPa) then decreases. Under water cooling, it decreases monotonically from 7.21 GPa at 20°C to 0.19 GPa at 600°C (a 97.36% decrease). [Li 2020, pp. 5-7]
    - **Uniaxial Compressive Strength (UCS):** Under natural cooling, UCS peaks at 100°C (146.95 MPa, 2% higher than at 20°C) then decreases slowly. Under water cooling, UCS decreases rapidly from 144.01 MPa at 20°C to 35.37 MPa at 600°C (a 75% decrease). [Li 2020, pp. 5-7]
    - **Peak Strain:** Increases with temperature under water cooling. Under natural cooling, it reaches a minimum at 100°C before increasing. [Li 2020, pp. 7-9]
4.  **Microscopic Damage:** Water cooling induces more severe internal damage, micro-cracks, and macroscopic fractures than natural cooling, especially above 400°C. At 600°C with water cooling, trans-granular cracks expand and grains break up. [Li 2020, pp. 7-9]
5.  **Mechanism:** Damage is attributed to thermal cracking from differential mineral expansion/contraction. Water cooling causes a faster temperature drop, leading to more uncoordinated shrinkage and more extensive cracking than natural cooling. [Li 2020, pp. 11-12]

## Limitations
- The study focuses on a single type of granite. Results may vary for different rock types or granite compositions. [Li 2020, pp. 2-3]
- The temperature range (up to 600°C) may not cover all conditions in deep geothermal reservoirs. [Li 2020, pp. 2-3]
- The cooling methods represent idealized scenarios; actual reservoir conditions may involve more complex thermal-hydraulic-mechanical-chemical (THMC) processes. [Li 2020, pp. 1-2]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- Granite samples are homogeneous and representative of the rock mass. [Li 2020, pp. 2-3]
- The heating and cooling protocols simulate the thermal shock experienced by hot dry rock during cold water injection in geothermal mining. [Li 2020, pp. 1-2]
- The uniaxial compression test conditions (static load, specific loading rate) are appropriate for evaluating mechanical property changes. [Li 2020, pp. 3-4]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- **Independent Variables:** Heating Temperature (20, 100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600°C), Cooling Method (Natural, Water).
- **Dependent Variables:** P-wave Velocity, Elastic Modulus, Uniaxial Compressive Strength (UCS), Peak Strain, Stress-Strain Curve Characteristics, Microscopic Crack Density.

## Reusable Claims
- **Claim:** Water cooling from high temperatures causes greater internal damage and a more significant reduction in mechanical properties (UCS, elastic modulus) in granite than natural cooling. [Li 2020, pp. 11-12]
    - **Condition:** For granite heated to temperatures between 200°C and 600°C.
    - **Limitation:** Specific to the granite composition and sample geometry tested.
- **Claim:** The elastic modulus of granite can initially increase when heated to 100°C and then naturally cooled, before decreasing at higher temperatures. [Li 2020, pp. 5-7]
    - **Condition:** Under natural cooling conditions.
    - **Limitation:** This trend may be specific to certain granite types and may not be universal.
- **Claim:** A double-peak phenomenon in the stress-strain curve of granite occurs at lower temperatures (200-300°C) under natural cooling compared to water cooling (>300°C). [Li 2020, pp. 11-12]
    - **Condition:** For the granite and temperature ranges studied.
    - **Limitation:** The exact temperature thresholds for this phenomenon may vary with rock type.
