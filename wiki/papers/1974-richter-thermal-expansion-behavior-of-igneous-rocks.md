# Thermal Expansion Behavior of Igneous Rocks.

## One-line Summary
The thermal expansion of igneous rocks is controlled by crack porosity, heating rate, previous maximum temperature, mineral composition, and crystal orientation, with precise measurements requiring low heating rates to avoid inducing new fractures.

## Research Question
What are the primary factors controlling the thermal expansion behavior of igneous rocks, and how do experimental conditions (e.g., heating rate, cycling) affect measured values?

## Study Area / Data
The study analyzed several terrestrial igneous rocks: Westerly granite, Barre granite, Cape Neddick gabbro, Fairfax diabase, Frederick diabase, and Ries granite. It also included data on lunar samples (e.g., 62295, 65015, 68415) and rocks from the Ries meteorite impact crater [Richter 1974, pp. 3-4, 8-9].

## Methods
Thermal expansion was measured using a modified Brinkmann TD-IX differential dilatometer with a fused silica measuring system and an LVDT transducer. The system was mounted vertically, surrounded by a water-jacketed shield, and connected to a digital data acquisition system. Samples were cylinders (1 cm diameter, 4.5-5.0 cm long) cut with water-cooled diamond drills. The recommended operational procedure involved changing temperature at 1-2°C/min for 30 minutes, then holding constant for 60 minutes, with data averaged over the last 40 minutes of the hold period. The dilatometer was calibrated using monocrystalline quartz parallel to the c-axis [Richter 1974, pp. 2-3].

## Key Findings
1.  For heating rates ≤2°C/min and maximum temperatures ≤250°C, thermal expansion curves are reproducible, may show small hysteresis but no permanent strain, and the coefficient of thermal expansion (α) is approximately that calculated from single-crystal values of constituent minerals [Richter 1974, pp. 1-2].
2.  For highly cracked rocks, the measured α is significantly less than the calculated value [Richter 1974, pp. 1-2].
3.  For heating rates >2°C/min or temperatures >350°C, new cracks and permanent strain are produced, systematically decreasing α. Recycling to the same temperature produces additional cracks until a steady state is reached after 2-5 cycles [Richter 1974, pp. 1-2].
4.  A "cracking temperature" (T_c) is defined as the minimum cycling temperature at which a sample first attains a permanent strain of 2 × 10⁻⁴ [Richter 1974, pp. 4-5].
5.  The observed thermal expansion depends inversely on microcrack porosity. Once permanent strain exceeds 3 × 10⁻⁴, the coefficient of thermal expansion is reduced [Richter 1974, pp. 8-9].
6.  Grain size appears to influence the cracking temperature; the finer-grained Frederick diabase (T_c ~560°C) cracked at a higher temperature than the coarser-grained Cape Neddick gabbro (T_c ~330°C) [Richter 1974, pp. 8-9].

## Limitations
1.  Anisotropy of thermal expansion was not investigated; linear expansion was measured along one dimension only, and volume expansion was taken as three times the linear expansion [Richter 1974, pp. 3-4].
2.  The effect of grain size was noted but not quantitatively discussed due to insufficient data [Richter 1974, pp. 8-9].
3.  The cause of the discrepancy between measured and calculated α for Westerly granite was not understood [Richter 1974, pp. 4-5].
4.  The cause of the initial hysteresis loop observed in some samples (e.g., Cape Neddick gabbro) on the first cycle to 250°C was not definitively identified [Richter 1974, pp. 5-5].

## Assumptions / Conditions
1.  Turner's equation for aggregate expansion assumes: (1) isotropic distribution of phases, (2) expansion independent of grain size/shape, (3) no internal disruptions, and (4) approximately equal bulk moduli of components [Richter 1974, pp. 4-5].
2.  Volume expansion is assumed to be three times the linear expansion measured along the sample's long axis [Richter 1974, pp. 3-4].
3.  The "cracking temperature" (T_c) is a rough measure of minimum grain boundary strength [Richter 1974, pp. 4-5].

## Reusable Claims
1.  **Condition:** For precise measurement of the intrinsic thermal expansion of igneous rocks. **Limitation:** Heating and cooling rates must not exceed 2°C/min to avoid inducing new cracks from thermal gradients in specimens of ~6 mm diameter. [Richter 1974, pp. 3-4, 8-9]
2.  **Condition:** When estimating the thermal expansion of an uncracked, polyphase igneous rock aggregate. **Limitation:** Turner's equation provides a good approximation if the rock's microstructure is isotropic and no internal disruptions occur during heating. [Richter 1974, pp. 4-5]
3.  **Condition:** For rocks subjected to thermal cycling. **Limitation:** The coefficient of thermal expansion decreases systematically once the permanent strain exceeds approximately 3 × 10⁻⁴, and a steady-state expansion is reached after 2-5 cycles to the same maximum temperature. [Richter 1974, pp. 7-8, 8-9]
4.  **Condition:** For rocks with high initial microcrack porosity (e.g., shocked rocks). **Limitation:** The measured thermal expansion will be significantly lower than the value calculated from single-crystal properties, as grains expand into existing pore space. [Richter 1974, pp. 3-4, 8-9]

## Connections To Other Work
This study builds on and seeks to explain discrepancies in earlier work by Bartlett [1], Adie [2], Wheeler [3], Griffith [4], and Hockman and Kessler [6]. It incorporates findings from lunar rock analysis by Baldridge et al. [10] and Todd et al. [11], and uses acoustic emission data from Todd [12] to justify low heating rates. The experimental setup is a modification of that described by Baldridge et al. [10] [Richter 1974, pp. 1-2, 2-3].
