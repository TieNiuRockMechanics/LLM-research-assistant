# Evolution of Dilatant Fracture Networks in a Normal Fault — Evidence from 4D Model Experiments.

## One-line Summary
Physical 4D model experiments using X-ray CT scanning reveal that dilatant fracture networks in normal faults evolve into self-similar, percolating systems with a fractal dimension of approximately 1.91, with fracture volume increasing non-linearly until antithetic faults form.

## Research Question
How do dilatant fracture networks in normal fault zones connect in 3D during progressive deformation, and what is the geometry of such networks? [Holland 2011, pp. 2-2]

## Study Area / Data
The study uses a physical analog model. The model consists of a 15 cm thick package of fine-grained cohesive hemihydrate powder (CaSO4·½H2O) with a tensile strength increasing with depth from 10 to 100 Pa. The model was deformed in a wooden box containing a 60° dipping normal fault. Data consists of ten successive X-ray computer tomographic (CT) volume scans acquired at 0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 10, 15, 20, and 25 mm offset from the basement fault. [Holland 2011, pp. 2-2]

## Methods
The physical model was prepared and deformed at room temperature, slowly, in a dry state without confining pressure. At each deformation stage, the model was imaged using a CT scanner with a spatial resolution of 500 μm. The radiological density data was processed by cropping volumes of interest and applying a calibrated threshold filter to segment open void voxels from powder voxels, resolving apertures down to 130 ± 30 μm. Clusters of less than four interconnected void voxels were removed. The fracture volumes were analyzed for interconnectivity (allowing face-to-face and edge-to-edge bridging), volume, vertical extent, and fractal characteristics using a 3D box-counting algorithm. [Holland 2011, pp. 2-3]

## Key Findings
- Dilatant jogs initiate along the evolving fault plane and coalesce into a self-similar percolating volume with a fractal dimension (Fd) of 1.91. [Holland 2011, pp. 1-2]
- The first vertically continuous open fracture at the model scale forms between 5 mm and 7 mm offset. [Holland 2011, pp. 2-3]
- Fracture volume increases non-linearly with progressive displacement. The rate of volume increase changes after 7 mm offset, associated with the formation of an antithetic fault at 10 mm offset. [Holland 2011, pp. 3-5]
- The number of isolated fractures increases until ~5 mm offset, then drops due to coalescence at ~7 mm offset. [Holland 2011, pp. 3-5]
- The 3D box-counting analysis shows the fractal dimension evolves from ~0 (individual pores) to ~1 (segmented lines) to ~2 (patched plane) and stabilizes around Fd=2 with further offset, indicating convergence towards a stable geometric system. [Holland 2011, pp. 3-5]
- The evolution is interpreted to be controlled by changes in the stress field: progressive displacement causes divergence from the master fault orientation, decreasing normal stress on the fault and promoting fracture growth, until antithetic fault formation increases normal stress and reduces the rate of fracture volume creation. [Holland 2011, pp. 1-2, 5-6]

## Limitations
- The model material's fracture width (aperture) is not scaled with the same ratio as length and stress. [Holland 2011, pp. 1-2]
- The physical model does not involve chemical processes such as cementation or fault healing. [Holland 2011, pp. 1-2]
- The experiment is conducted in a dry state at room temperature without confining pressure. [Holland 2011, pp. 2-2]
- The analysis excludes the upper 20% of the model to avoid near-surface structures. [Holland 2011, pp. 2-2]

## Assumptions / Conditions
- The processes and evolution of fracture geometries in the model are robust and apply to a wide range of normal fault zones in nature, despite scaling differences. [Holland 2011, pp. 1-2]
- The model assumes no elevated pore pressures and does not consider fault-valve processes or invasion percolation. [Holland 2011, pp. 1-2]
- The strength of the powder is caused by electrostatic bonded clusters due to small particle size. [Holland 2011, pp. 2-2]

## Key Variables / Parameters
- Basement fault offset (displacement): 0 to 25 mm.
- Fracture volume (open void space).
- Number of interconnected fracture clusters.
- Fractal dimension (Fd) of the fracture network.
- Normal stress on the master fault plane (inferred).
- Model material tensile strength: 10 to 100 Pa (increasing with depth). [Holland 2011, pp. 2-2]

## Reusable Claims
- In scaled physical models of normal faults, dilatant fracture networks evolve into self-similar, percolating systems with a fractal dimension of approximately 1.91 after a critical displacement. [Holland 2011, pp. 1-2, 2-3]
- The volume of dilatant fractures in a normal fault zone increases non-linearly with progressive displacement, with a change in rate associated with the formation of antithetic faults. [Holland 2011, pp. 3-5]
- The evolution of a dilatant fracture network in a normal fault can be driven by geometric evolution and associated stress changes alone, without the need for high fluid pressures or chemical processes. [Holland 2011, pp. 1-2, 5-6]
